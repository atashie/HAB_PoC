# models-and-methods

This category covers the statistical/machine-learning and mechanistic/process-based models used to forecast, nowcast, and explain freshwater cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (cyanoHABs), plus the data-fusion strategies (satellite + in-situ + weather) that feed them and the validation practices (skill metrics, baselines, leakage, spatiotemporal cross-validation) used to judge whether they work. It spans single-lake case studies (Taihu, Erie, Müggelsee, Yilong, Winnipeg, Daecheong) through national-scale operational systems (EPA/NOAA CyAN-based forecasts), and includes both convergent findings (feature importance, model families, generalizability limits) and a live, peer-reviewed methodological dispute over how much of one influential paper's reported skill is real. Because almost every model in this sub-literature is correlational/associational rather than causal, driver and "importance" claims below are flagged as such throughout.

## What's well-established

### Statistical/ML early-warning models routinely report strong in-sample or same-system skill, built mostly from in-situ variables
A large and consistent body of single-reservoir and single-river studies reports high apparent skill for short-horizon (days-to-a-week) cyanobacteria/chlorophyll-a forecasts built from dense in-situ water-quality, hydrodynamic, and meteorological monitoring — without any satellite input. An ANN outperformed an SVM for predicting a South Korean reservoir's algal alert level, with 6–7 day sampling identified as an efficient early-warning interval [ACAD-001]. A gradient-boosting/CNN-LSTM ensemble reached R²=0.89–0.93 forecasting weekly cyanobacteria cell counts from water temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen, nutrients, and precipitation alone [ACAD-043]. A random-forest-plus-boosting ensemble reached 78.7% test accuracy predicting alert levels from ~9 years of meteorological/hydrodynamic/water-quality data, outperforming both a 1D-CNN and an ANN [ACAD-117]. Similar in-situ-only pipelines report AUC=0.948 (CatBoost, Yilong Lake, China) [ACAD-060] and 84.7%/78.7% train/test accuracy (ensemble, South Korea) [ACAD-117]. Water temperature is the single most frequently cited top predictor across these studies [ACAD-043; ACAD-048; ACAD-069; ACAD-117; ACAD-128], though these are model-importance/correlational findings, not demonstrated causal mechanisms. A large-scale (102-site, 3-year, China-wide) explainable-deep-learning study fusing in-situ monitoring with ERA5 weather reanalysis found broadly similar results at national scale: an LSTM reached mean NSE=0.48 (median 0.52), beating 8 conventional ML baselines, with water temperature the top SHAP driver nationally (11.7% global importance) — but the authors' own reported spread (29.4% of sites "excellent," 30.4% "poor," NSE<0.3) shows this skill is highly site-dependent, not uniform [ACAD-048].

### Tree-based/gradient-boosted models match or occasionally exceed deep learning in single-site comparisons, though aggregate cross-study reviews report deep learning ahead on median performance
Across several independent single-site comparisons, ensemble tree methods (random forest, XGBoost, CatBoost, LightGBM) matched or outperformed deep-learning architectures (ANN, LSTM, 1D-CNN) on cyanoHAB prediction from in-situ data. An ensemble of random forest + boosting beat both 1D-CNN and ANN on a South Korean drinking-water reservoir [ACAD-117]. Rule-based/tree-based models (XGBoost, and a hybrid evolutionary rule-learner "HEA") generalized to unseen data better than an LSTM-with-attention model in an 11-year Lake Müggelsee case study, with the LSTM failing to generalize at all on daily-frequency data [ACAD-037] — though this specific result is now under direct peer-reviewed challenge (see Contested, below). A 2026 review synthesizing 168 studies (1997–2025) reports the opposite headline pattern at the aggregate/cross-study level — deep learning (particularly LSTM) showing the strongest median reported performance (R²=0.89, >90% accuracy "in many applications") — but that review's own text also flags "limited transferability across water bodies" as a persistent gap, cautioning against treating any single in-study accuracy figure as a general benchmark [ACAD-068]. CatBoost, a gradient-boosted method, was also the best of 10 models compared for Yilong Lake (AUC=0.948) [ACAD-060]. Taken together, the pattern across single-study, single-site comparisons in this pack leans toward tree/ensemble methods being competitive-to-superior for this data regime, even though the largest cross-study review reports deep learning ahead in aggregate.

### Satellite-plus-in-situ/weather fusion for cyanoHAB forecasting has been demonstrated at national operational scale, with an honest, temporally-validated accuracy/precision trade-off
The most directly comparable published analog to a remote-sensing-plus-in-situ HAB tool is a hierarchical Bayesian spatiotemporal (INLA) model that fuses the Sentinel-3 OLCI cyanobacteria index with weekly water-surface temperature, precipitation, and static lake morphology (depth, area) across 2,192 U.S. lakes [ACAD-050; FED-017]. Trained on 2017–2020 and tested on a fully independent, out-of-sample 2021 year (not a random split), it reached 90% accuracy, 88% sensitivity, 91% specificity, AUC=0.95, but only 49% precision — outperforming six compared ML/neural-network models (SVC, random forest ~0.84–0.85 accuracy; DNN, LSTM, RNN, GRU) on the same task [ACAD-050; FED-017]. (Caveat, per the source's own dossier: ACAD-050 contains an unresolved internal arithmetic discrepancy — its stated total lake-week observation count does not match the sum of its reported train/validation/test subsets — so these headline metrics should be cited with that data-provenance uncertainty noted, pending author clarification.) The authors and EPA are explicit that the model currently overpredicts positive bloom events (a conservative, health-protective bias) given the low true prevalence of bloom-weeks (~9–10%), and that precision (42–49%) is modest despite high accuracy/specificity — a textbook base-rate/precision caveat that should accompany any citation of the "90% accuracy" headline [ACAD-050; FED-017; FED-012; FED-016]. A closely related, geographically narrower fusion study (103 Florida lakes, same satellite lineage, R-INLA with SPDE spatial + AR1 temporal random effects) reached AUC=0.95/88% sensitivity/88% specificity on a holdout, dropping to AUC=0.89/82%/82% on a genuine one-week-ahead forecast test — and explicitly showed via nested-model DIC comparison that adding spatial structure produced by far the largest single improvement in model fit (DIC ~21,300 → ~7,600) [ACAD-092]. Both fusion studies independently flag the same operational bound: because week-to-week bloom state is strongly autocorrelated (AR1 alpha=0.90 in the Florida study), a meaningful share of apparent near-term skill may reflect persistence of current conditions rather than new information from the added covariates — and the Florida authors explicitly do not expect their model to remain accurate beyond about two weeks [ACAD-092]. A companion EPA statistical analysis of spatial (not temporal) variation across ~2,192 lakes found each 0.01 CI_cyano/km² increase in the satellite cyanobacteria index raised the odds of exceeding six bloom/toxin thresholds by 23–54%, and flagged 162 lakes at ≥75% probability of exceeding a lower (0.2 µg/L) microcystin threshold nationally [FED-041].

### The field converges on a three-family taxonomy — data-driven/ML, process-based/mechanistic, and hybrid — with hybrids reported as generally strongest but least mature
Multiple independent reviews organize HAB prediction methods the same way: statistical/ML models (ANN, RF, SVM, LSTM) that learn empirical relationships from historical data; process-based/mechanistic models that simulate the physical/biological mechanisms (nutrient uptake, growth, transport) explicitly; and hybrid models combining both [ACAD-003; ACAD-084; ACAD-093; ACAD-071]. A 2025 open-access review explicitly framed as unifying the previously separate remote-sensing-identification and predictive-modeling literatures uses this same three-way split and further names the shared bottlenecks across all three families: choosing optimal input variables, model transferability across diverse water bodies, and interpretability of complex ML models — recommending adaptive hybrid models and explainable/interpretable AI (XAI) as the way forward [ACAD-084]. Where hybrids are tested directly, they are reported as the top performer: a modular deep-learning surrogate for a Delft3D process-based model (Daecheong Lake, South Korea) raised total-cyanobacteria-count NSE from 0.644 (trial-and-error calibration) to 0.930 (surrogate-based optimization) in the calibration year, and 0.520→0.867 in the validation year, while cutting parameter-optimization compute time by 87.5–96.4% [ACAD-104]. The 168-study cross-review reports hybrid process-based+ML models as the single highest-accuracy family among reviewed studies (NSE up to 0.991) [ACAD-068], and a separate review of algal-bloom forecasting broadly likewise reports that hybrid methods "showed improved skill" over single-paradigm approaches for chlorophyll forecasting in the studies it surveys [ACAD-093]. However, reviews are consistent that hybrid frameworks remain comparatively scarce in the literature relative to single-paradigm models, and that this is itself an identified gap rather than a solved capability [ACAD-093; ACAD-084]. A related, more unusual data-driven variant addresses data scarcity directly: because CNNs need more densely-gridded spatio-temporal data than routine field or satellite sampling can supply, one proof-of-concept study trained a CNN entirely on **SYNTHETIC** cyanobacteria (Microcystis) fields generated by a 3D hydrodynamic-water-quality river model, rather than on directly observed data [ACAD-148]. It reached NSE=0.87 nowcasting Microcystis biomass, degrading to NSE=0.58 at longer lead times, with skill rising from 0.70 to 0.84 as training-grid observation density rose from 20% to 100%, and holding at an "acceptable" NSE=0.76 under 10% injected noise [ACAD-148] — but because both the training data and the evaluation target come from the same underlying simulation, these NSE values measure agreement with that model's own internal dynamics, not validated agreement with independently observed field or satellite cyanobacteria data, a distinction the source itself does not resolve.

### Operational federal deployment lags peer-reviewed validation: deployed systems fuse the same core data streams but disclose forecast skill far less rigorously than the papers that justify them
Beyond the peer-reviewed INLA fusion literature above, NOAA runs several operational, non-peer-reviewed Lake Erie HAB forecast products that illustrate what "deployed" data fusion looks like in practice. A seasonal Severity Index (bloom biomass integrated over a sustained 30-day window, normalized to the lowest observed year 2005 and highest observed year 2011) is issued ahead of each bloom season from Maumee River phosphorus loading and hydrological/weather forecasts, followed by near-daily in-season bulletins (minimum 96-hour bloom position and vertical-mixing forecasts) once in-situ sampling confirms Microcystis presence, fusing Sentinel-3 OLCI/true-color satellite imagery with 3D hydrodynamic model currents, river discharge, and toxin sampling [FED-024; FED-064; FED-068]. This system explicitly separates the satellite/biomass signal from toxin risk — bulletins do not themselves forecast microcystin concentrations, which come from separate weekly in-situ sampling flagged against Ohio's public-health advisory level — and its own FAQ states forecast lead time is capped at 3–4 days because "bloom transport, mixing, and decline cannot be reliably modeled further out" [FED-064; FED-025]. No source describing this operational system states a quantitative accuracy, skill score, or uncertainty figure for the forecasts themselves, despite targeted searches for such language, and one product page explicitly disclaims that "predicted bloom concentrations are guidance only and may not reflect the true, on-the-ground bloom condition" [FED-063; FED-026]. The system has also evolved discretely over time — a discontinued "Experimental" 5-day HAB Tracker (2017–2019 archive) was retired and folded into the current operational successor, and a 3D hydrodynamic sub-surface model was only added starting the 2020 season [FED-025; FED-026] — and NOAA's broader (non-Erie) HAB forecasting effort, ongoing since 1999, has designated seven national problem regions of which only the Florida/Texas Gulf coast has a mature operational forecast and Lake Erie has reached operational status, leaving five regions still developmental [FED-020]. The same disclosure gap appears, more sharply, in the commercial sector: at least one vendor platform (BlueGreen Water Technologies' "BGi") claims to fuse satellite and in-situ data via an AI model trained on 5+ years of environmental/weather data to identify HAB risk "days to weeks in advance" across three continents [PVT-001], but discloses no accuracy metric, validation sample, study-site list, or comparison baseline — a vendor capability claim that should not be weighted the same as the peer-reviewed fusion studies above, which do disclose out-of-sample accuracy, precision, and explicit false-positive-bias caveats [PVT-001; cf. ACAD-050; FED-017; ACAD-092].

### For flowing-water (riverine) systems specifically, process-based/mechanistic models — not ML — are the dominant historical approach, and the underlying monitoring data are typically sparse
A USGS-authored systematic review of 162 river/flowing-water HAB forecasting articles spanning 1975–2024 found process-based (mechanistic) models were used in 59% of studies, versus 37% for data-driven/statistical-ML approaches [ACAD-004]. The reviewed literature is heavily site-specific (93% of articles a single-location application) and geographically concentrated (South Korea 26%, Europe 25%, U.S. 21%, China 12%), with underlying observational datasets typically only about 5 years long, sampled weekly-to-monthly, at 1–10 sites — a real constraint on what any river-HAB model, ML or mechanistic, can be validated against [ACAD-004]. This review's scope explicitly excludes standing lakes, so its 59%/37% split should not be assumed to describe lake-focused HAB modeling, where the balance in this pack's other sources leans more toward ML/statistical approaches for lentic systems [ACAD-004]. Within lake mechanistic modeling specifically, coupled hydrodynamic-biomass models have also demonstrated strong short-term skill with sufficient in-situ instrumentation: a 3-day-ahead Lake Taihu forecast system (18 automatic buoys + boat surveys feeding a finite-volume hydrodynamic-biomass model) exceeded 80% forecast accuracy against independent remote-sensing and boat-survey data across two full bloom seasons [ACAD-052], and three different transport-model formulations (Lagrangian, Eulerian, hybrid) validated against three years of Sentinel-3 OLCI imagery for Lake Erie CHABs reached comparable hindcast skill without one dominating, at horizons of 24–240 hours [ACAD-024].

### Model explainability tools converge on nutrient/temperature/optical variables as top drivers (an associational, not causal, finding), and class-imbalance correction measurably improves rare-bloom-event detection at a sometimes-real accuracy cost
SHAP (Shapley Additive exPlanations) is the dominant post-hoc explainability method across this literature, applied to identify feature importance in CatBoost [ACAD-060], random forest/XGBoost [ACAD-128], LSTM [ACAD-048], and integrated CNN models [ACAD-126]. Across studies, water temperature, chlorophyll-a/phycocyanin, and nutrient concentrations (TN, TP, CODMn) recur as top-ranked drivers [ACAD-048; ACAD-060; ACAD-128], and the 102-site China study found water temperature's relationship to predicted bloom risk is threshold-like (a site-specific inflection point ranging 9.5–27.1°C, mean 20.9°C) rather than linear [ACAD-048]. One study explicitly used explainability to surface a genuine geographic anomaly for governance: longitude was the single most important feature in a U.S. cyanobacteria-severity classifier, but the authors caution this reflects a regionally-learned (US-specific) pattern that may not transfer internationally, rather than a mechanistic finding [ACAD-008]. Every source making an importance/driver claim in this literature is working from a statistical association within a fitted model, not a manipulated experiment — the authors of the 102-site China study explicitly self-flag that strong temporal autocorrelation among variables can let a model reach high predictive skill without having learned genuine environmental relationships, a caution that generalizes across the whole SHAP-based driver literature [ACAD-048]. A related, equally pervasive technique concern is class imbalance: because true bloom/high-alert weeks are a minority class in nearly every dataset in this literature (e.g., 10.1% of lake-weeks nationally in the INLA study [ACAD-050; FED-017]; 8.4%/13.6% for the two non-normal alert levels in a South Korean reservoir [ACAD-069]), several studies test resampling explicitly. In a 345-record, 3-class South Korean reservoir dataset, SMOTE-ENN resampling raised a Random Forest's minority-class accuracy from 50.0%/50.0% (L1/L2, no resampling) to 85.7%/100% [ACAD-069]. In an 8-reservoir South Korean study, applying SMOTE improved both RF and XGBoost classification performance for the minority bloom-occurrence class (pre-SMOTE minority-class AUC=0.63, recall=0.34, F-measure=0.39), with XGBoost more stable than RF specifically under imbalance [ACAD-128]. However, the trade-off is not free: in the same South Korean reservoir study, an ANN's minority-class recall rose from 64.3% to 71.4% after resampling, but its overall test accuracy fell by 9.8 percentage points in the same comparison [ACAD-069] — a concrete illustration that a resampling-driven minority-class gain is not automatically a net win once overall performance is considered.

## Contested / uncertain / poorly-established

**Does the influential Lake Müggelsee "intelligible ML" result (tree/rule-based models generalize better than deep learning) hold up under methodological scrutiny?** Recknagel et al. (2025) reported that XGBoost and a rule-based hybrid evolutionary algorithm (HEA) generalized to unseen data better than an LSTM-with-attention model, forecasting cyanobacteria 5 days ahead from 11 years of high-frequency Lake Müggelsee data, and proposed this as a general principle for "intelligible" CyanoHAB modeling [ACAD-037]. A peer-reviewed Comment published in the same journal (Water Research, 2026) directly disputes the reliability of this result on methodological grounds: it argues that cross-year interpolation of weekly biovolume measurements up to hourly/daily resolution can introduce temporal (look-ahead-style) leakage and artificially smooth the very dynamics the model is meant to predict; that the original evaluation leaned on R²/RMSE rather than threshold-exceedance skill at the operationally relevant 4 mm³/L hazard level; that the model's heavy reliance on one sensor variable (phycocyanin) was never benchmarked against a simple persistence baseline or a no-phycocyanin ablation; and that mixed single-station-predictor/multi-station-target data aggregation could bias the learned relationships [ACAD-110]. The commenters do not claim the original paper's contribution is invalidated, only that its "operational credibility" needs leakage-robust validation, event-based metrics, and stronger baselines to be fully trusted [ACAD-110]. This is a rare case in the pack of a specific, named methodological rebuttal (not just a general caution) aimed at a specific quantitative result — readers should treat ACAD-037's generalizability claim as contested pending resolution, not settled.

**Does deep learning or tree/ensemble learning have the edge for cyanoHAB prediction?** The evidence pulls in different directions depending on scale of comparison. Single-site, head-to-head studies in this pack mostly favor tree/ensemble methods: random forest+boosting beat 1D-CNN and ANN in a South Korean reservoir [ACAD-117]; XGBoost/HEA beat LSTM-with-attention at Lake Müggelsee (now contested, see above) [ACAD-037; ACAD-110]; an ANN beat an SVM in one reservoir [ACAD-001]. But the largest cross-study synthesis (168 studies, 1997–2025) reports deep learning, especially LSTM, with the strongest aggregate reported performance (median R²=0.89) [ACAD-068], and a 102-site China-wide study found its LSTM beat 8 conventional ML baselines including random forest [ACAD-048]. These are not strictly irreconcilable (different data volumes, sites, and evaluation designs could favor different model families), but no source in the pack directly reconciles the single-study-favors-trees vs. aggregate-review-favors-deep-learning pattern, and the review's own admission of "limited transferability across water bodies" [ACAD-068] cautions against resolving this by simply trusting the bigger number.

**Does fusing a remote-sensing signal add value over in-situ-only models, or can in-situ-only models already achieve comparable skill?** Several strong-performing in-situ-only models in this pack (R²=0.89–0.93 [ACAD-043]; AUC=0.948 [ACAD-060]; NSE mean 0.48–0.95 [ACAD-048]) report skill in the same range as, or exceeding, the headline accuracy of the satellite-fused INLA model (90% accuracy) [ACAD-050; FED-017] — but these are not apples-to-apples comparisons: different lakes, different target definitions (continuous NSE/R² vs. binary threshold-exceedance classification), different train/test designs (some not clearly out-of-time), and no source in the pack runs a controlled ablation removing the satellite signal from a fused model on the same dataset to isolate its marginal contribution to lake-level cyanoHAB forecasting specifically. The one source in the pack that does run such an ablation (removing individual fused data sources) is in a different problem setting — a severity-classification competition dataset, not lake-week bloom forecasting — and found all three modalities (optical satellite, climate, elevation) contributed positively, with performance degrading when any one was removed [ACAD-008]. Whether that finding transfers to the CyAN-style lake-forecasting problem is untested in this pack.

**How much of the INLA fusion model's near-term skill is genuine added value versus simple persistence?** The Florida spatio-temporal fusion study explicitly self-identifies this open question: given AR1 alpha=0.90 (strong week-to-week autocorrelation in bloom state), the authors state that "a substantial share of apparent near-term predictive accuracy could reflect simple persistence of current conditions rather than genuinely new information from the covariates" [ACAD-092]. Neither ACAD-092 nor the closely related national-scale study [ACAD-050; FED-017] reports a persistence-only baseline comparison to quantify this directly — a gap the sources themselves flag but do not close.

**Is process-based or data-driven modeling the "more common" or "better" approach — and does that even mean the same thing across water-body types?** For rivers, mechanistic models dominate the literature by volume (59% vs. 37%) [ACAD-004]. For lakes, the balance in this pack tilts toward statistical/ML approaches by sheer count of sources, though the 2020 review of climate-scale HAB modeling argues statistical models are the more common near-term forecasting tool generally, while process-based models are structurally better suited (in principle) to project beyond the historical range because they encode mechanism rather than fit to it — while cautioning process-based models are themselves complex, hard to calibrate, and can still fail if climate change causes previously-unrepresented processes to emerge [ACAD-071]. This is a case where "common" and "better for a given purpose" answer different questions, and the pack does not resolve which criterion should dominate a design choice.

**Does higher-frequency in-situ monitoring produce better forecasts?** The Lake Müggelsee study (now under methodological challenge, see above) reported daily-frequency data produced slightly better forecasting results than hourly-frequency data for the same 5-day-ahead cyanobacteria target, and recommended daily as sufficient/preferable [ACAD-037] — a counterintuitive result (more data being worse) that the disputing Comment does not directly address, leaving it unresolved whether this reflects a genuine signal-to-noise trade-off or an artifact of the same preprocessing/interpolation choices under methodological dispute [ACAD-037; ACAD-110].

**Single-source / abstract-only claims requiring extra caution.** Several quantitative claims in this section rest on a single study accessed at abstract level only, without independent replication in this pack: the 90%/49% accuracy-precision figures for the INLA national model trace to one paper (indexed twice, ACAD-050/FED-017, but it is one underlying study); the Lake Taihu 3-day, >80%-accuracy hydrodynamic forecast rests on one 2014 study with no stated confidence interval or baseline comparison [ACAD-052]; the Daecheong Lake surrogate-model NSE gains rest on one abstract-only 2025 paper with a single calibration/validation year pair [ACAD-104]; and the wind-speed-as-non-predictor finding in a South Korean reservoir explicitly contradicts (without refuting) prior ocean/marine HAB literature the authors themselves cite, attributing the difference to spatial scale rather than treating it as a general finding [ACAD-069].

**EPA's headline "90% accuracy" claim appears on a plain-language communications page with an internal arithmetic discrepancy that is not resolved in this pack.** The peer-reviewed methods paper underlying EPA's public 90% accuracy claim [FED-016] reports 432,030 total lake-week observations across the full 2017–2021 study period, yet separately reports training (308,204) + validation (132,688) subsets for 2017–2020 alone that sum to ~440,892, plus 113,984 for the 2021 test year — together exceeding the stated 432,030 total by a wide margin. This arithmetic mismatch is not reconciled anywhere in the accessible text of either the peer-reviewed paper [ACAD-050] or its EPA science-communication companion piece [FED-016; FED-017], and should be flagged rather than silently resolved if this study's sample sizes are cited downstream.

**Operational forecast systems disclose skill far less rigorously than the peer-reviewed literature they draw on — a "validation gap" between deployed products and the papers that justify them.** The peer-reviewed INLA fusion papers report specific, out-of-sample accuracy/precision/AUC figures with stated caveats (overprediction bias, ~2-week usable horizon, cannot detect toxins) [ACAD-050; FED-017; ACAD-092]. By contrast, the operational NOAA Lake Erie forecast system's own public-facing pages state no accuracy, skill score, or uncertainty figure anywhere, despite targeted searches for such language across five separate NOAA pages describing the same system [FED-020; FED-024; FED-025; FED-026; FED-063; FED-064; FED-068] — one page's only acknowledgment of uncertainty is a blanket disclaimer that output is "guidance only" [FED-063]. Even basic operational parameters are inconsistently stated across NOAA's own linked pages for the same system: the bloom/forecast season window is given as "June until October" on one page and "July to October" on another, and the in-season outlook horizon is stated as "5-day outlooks" on one page but a "96 hours" (4-day) minimum on another [FED-068]. This is not necessarily evidence the operational system is unskilled — it more likely reflects that public communications pages are not written to the same evidentiary standard as peer-reviewed methods papers — but it means operational-system claims in this pack cannot be assigned a quantitative skill figure and should not be conflated with the validated peer-reviewed fusion literature above.

## Gaps & open questions

- **No controlled, same-dataset ablation in this pack isolates the marginal value of a satellite/remote-sensing signal for lake-level cyanoHAB forecasting.** The closest available ablation (ACAD-008) is from a different problem framing (severity classification on a competition dataset), not the CyAN-style weekly bloom-forecasting task that is this project's own framing.
- **No source in the pack reports a persistence-only baseline for the leading satellite-fusion models**, despite both major fusion studies [ACAD-050/FED-017; ACAD-092] explicitly flagging strong temporal autocorrelation as a likely major (unquantified) contributor to their own reported skill.
- **Deep-learning-vs-tree-ensemble superiority is unresolved and may be scale/context-dependent** — single-site studies mostly favor trees/ensembles; the largest cross-study review favors deep learning in aggregate; no source bridges the two findings.
- **The Lake Müggelsee "intelligible ML" generalizability claim is under active, unresolved peer-reviewed dispute** [ACAD-037 vs. ACAD-110] — any downstream use of its conclusions (rule-based models generalize best; daily beats hourly sampling) should note this is contested, not settled.
- **Very few sources report uncertainty intervals or formal baselines (naive/climatology/persistence) alongside headline metrics** — several single-lake mechanistic and ML studies (e.g., ACAD-052's >80% accuracy; ACAD-104's NSE gains) report point estimates with no confidence interval or explicit baseline comparator.
- **Toxin (cyanotoxin) prediction remains structurally separate from biomass/chlorophyll forecasting** — the mechanistic microcystin-transport models [ACAD-051; FED-056] forecast the fate of toxin already measured in-situ, not its onset or production from first principles, and the statistical/ML models in this section almost universally target biomass, cell count, or chlorophyll-a as a proxy endpoint rather than toxin concentration directly (consistent with the parallel finding in ACAD-004 that only 5% of river-HAB studies actually modeled toxins as the endpoint, despite 23% being toxin-motivated).
- **Explainable-AI uptake remains low relative to model volume** — only 20% of 168 reviewed ML studies used XAI techniques [ACAD-068], meaning the SHAP-based driver convergence described above, while consistent where it has been tested, has only been tested in a minority of the broader modeling literature.
- **Geographic and system-type concentration limits generalizability inference across this whole section** — flowing-water HAB modeling findings are drawn from a literature dominated by South Korea, Europe, the U.S., and China [ACAD-004]; standing-lake findings in this pack likewise cluster in South Korea, China, and a handful of U.S./Florida/Great-Lakes systems, so claims about "what works" should not be assumed portable to unstudied regions or lake types without explicit testing.
- **Deployed operational forecast systems in this pack disclose no quantitative skill metric for their own outputs**, unlike the peer-reviewed literature underpinning similar approaches — meaning there is currently no way, from the sources gathered here, to independently verify how well the NOAA Lake Erie operational bulletins actually perform against real outcomes, only that the peer-reviewed methods they draw on (satellite-hydrodynamic fusion, INLA-style spatiotemporal models) have been validated in other, more academically-documented contexts [FED-020; FED-024; FED-025; FED-026; FED-063; FED-064; FED-068; cf. ACAD-050; FED-017; ACAD-092].
- **Synthetic-training-data approaches (ACAD-148) remain a single, abstract-only proof-of-concept in this pack** — no source tests whether a CNN trained on simulated hydrodynamic-model fields, once validated against that same model's internal dynamics, would retain its reported skill when checked against independently observed field or satellite cyanobacteria data.

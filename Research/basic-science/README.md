# basic-science

This category covers the biological and physicochemical foundations of freshwater cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (HABs): which taxa dominate and why, how nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus) and their ratios drive bloom formation, how temperature/stratification/hydrology create favorable conditions, what cyanotoxins are produced and their health consequences, how climate change is reshaping bloom frequency and timing (and how contested that attribution is), and the succession/physiological traits (buoyancy regulation, nitrogen fixation) that let cyanobacteria outcompete other phytoplankton. The evidence pack for this category spans 74 sources — peer-reviewed primary research and meta-analyses, federal agency guidance (EPA, CDC, USGS), and NGO/WHO technical references — of which 70 are cited directly below; the remaining four are institutional/program pages or generic reviews that added no findings not already better-supported elsewhere.

## What's well-established

### Bloom biology & dominant taxa
Cyanobacterial blooms are increasing in frequency, magnitude, and duration globally, with eutrophication and climate change identified as the two main catalysts [ACAD-032]. A handful of genera recur across the literature as the dominant bloom-formers: *Microcystis*, *Anabaena*/*Dolichospermum*, *Planktothrix*, *Nodularia*, *Trichodesmium*, and *Cylindrospermopsis* (*Raphidiopsis*) *raciborskii* [ACAD-032]. Cyanobacteria are an ancient lineage — persisting roughly 2.5–3.5 billion years through geochemical and climatic change — which multiple reviews use to frame their adaptive resilience [ACAD-021; ACAD-028]. Cyanobacteria possess efficient CO2-concentrating mechanisms (CCMs) that confer a competitive advantage under the carbon-limited, high-pH conditions typical of dense blooms, and rising atmospheric CO2 is described as intensifying blooms in eutrophic/hypertrophic lakes via this pathway [ACAD-019; ACAD-032]. Dense blooms can themselves drive sustained high-pH "basification" — a 2015 Lake Erie *Microcystis* bloom held the water column at a daily average pH of ~9.2 for about a month, and Lake Santa Olalla (Spain) sustained a mean pH of 9.5 over two years [ACAD-019]. Bacterial community composition differs by dominant cyanobacterial genus, and cyanophages, parasitic chytrid fungi, and CRISPR-based antiviral defenses all influence bloom community dynamics and contribute to bloom decline [ACAD-032]. Zooplankton grazing resistance is inconsistent and taxon/history-dependent rather than a fixed rule — some *Daphnia* populations feed on toxic *Microcystis*, others avoid it, apparently depending on prior co-evolutionary exposure [NGO-005]; a documented 35-year "arms race" between toxic *Microcystis* and *Daphnia* in Lake Constance illustrates this co-evolution [ACAD-032]. Interspecies competition among cyanobacteria is itself condition-dependent rather than fixed: in laboratory co-culture of native and alien Lithuanian lake strains, nutrient (phosphorus/nitrogen) concentration appeared to have the strongest effect on competition outcomes, ahead of temperature and then competitor species identity (a tentative ranking from the study), and the same "invader" species did not uniformly dominate across all tested conditions [ACAD-064]. Community composition can also shift with water clarity: in two hypereutrophic Polish lakes, *Planktothrix agardhii* biomass related to transparency and nutrients differently at each site, and the invasive *Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii* co-occurred with it rather than simply displacing it [ACAD-143]. Underscoring how broad and applied this field has become, review-level syntheses increasingly frame cyanoHABs as a multi-sector risk-management problem — spanning fish, pets, livestock, ecotourism, and real-estate value, not just water chemistry — while flagging cyanoHAB occurrence as difficult to predict because it is "spontaneous yet sporadic" [ACAD-031]. A 2023 Annual Review synthesis frames the field's central explanatory approach as tracing bloom initiation, outbreak, and persistence to the ecophysiological traits of bloom-forming cyanobacteria interacting with environmental processes, attributing intensification of the overall cyanoHAB phenomenon to the coupled effects of eutrophication, rising atmospheric CO2, and warming together, rather than any one factor alone [ACAD-058].

### Nutrient drivers and the N-vs-P limitation debate
There is broad agreement that nutrient enrichment (eutrophication) is a primary driver of cyanobacterial dominance and bloom risk, correlating positively with bloom occurrence in a global meta-analysis of 27 studies [ACAD-005]. The classic paradigm — that phosphorus alone controls freshwater eutrophication because N-fixing cyanobacteria can meet their own nitrogen demand — rests on landmark whole-lake evidence: a 37-year (1969–2005) whole-ecosystem experiment at Lake 227 (Experimental Lakes Area, Canada) found that cutting nitrogen input to zero while holding phosphorus constant did NOT reduce eutrophication, because N-fixing cyanobacteria compensated and biomass tracked phosphorus supply instead [ACAD-044]. However, a large and growing body of more recent work argues phosphorus-only management is insufficient wherever non-N2-fixing genera (e.g., *Microcystis*, *Planktothrix*) dominate and systems are N-limited or N+P co-limited [ACAD-027; ACAD-028; ACAD-029; ACAD-035; ACAD-036; ACAD-087; ACAD-130] (see Contested section below — this is an active, unresolved debate, not settled consensus). Cyanobacteria's competitive advantage over other algae strengthens at higher water temperatures, commonly cited as above ~25°C [ACAD-002; ACAD-028; ACAD-032; ACAD-056; ACAD-057], though the physiological optimum varies by genus (e.g., *Microcystis* spp. 27.5–32°C [ACAD-019]). Beyond bulk nutrient concentration, cyanobacteria practice "luxury" phosphorus storage as intracellular polyphosphate — accumulating far beyond immediate metabolic need, sufficient in some cases to support 8–16 further cell divisions after external phosphorus is exhausted [NGO-005; ACAD-089] — which decouples short-term bloom persistence from the water column's currently-measured nutrient concentration. Internal ("legacy") phosphorus stored in lake sediments, released back into the water column, can substantially undercut nutrient-management efforts and slow recovery even after external loads are cut, with recovery in some shallow eutrophic lakes taking decades [ACAD-063; ACAD-139; ACAD-142; ACAD-019].

### Temperature, stratification & hydrology
Warming water temperature is one of the most consistently cited physiological drivers of cyanobacterial dominance: growth rates for bloom-forming cyanobacteria typically peak above 25–35°C, exceeding the optimal ranges of competing diatoms and dinoflagellates (~15–25°C) [ACAD-002; ACAD-028; ACAD-032; ACAD-056; ACAD-057; ACAD-125]. Enhanced thermal stratification is repeatedly identified as a key physical precondition that lets buoyant, gas-vesicle-regulating cyanobacteria concentrate near the surface and outcompete algae that cannot regulate their position in the water column [ACAD-021; NGO-005; FED-021]. In six Connecticut drinking-water reservoirs, historical "high-risk" blooms (>70,000 cells/mL) correlated with a specific combination of warm surface water, cool bottom water, and strong thermal resistance to mixing — a correlation, not a demonstrated causal mechanism — and mid-century (2041–2070) projections under RCP8.5 show stratification beginning 2–4 weeks earlier and lasting 2–4 weeks longer, alongside 48±8 more days/year with surface water above 25°C [ACAD-053]. A 32-year (1987–2018), 20-reservoir U.S. study similarly found bloom severity rising alongside progressively earlier/longer summer warming, earlier stratification onset, and lengthening deep-water hypoxia — while total nitrogen, phosphorus, and watershed land cover stayed largely stable, suggesting the bloom increase in these systems is not simply explained by increased bulk nutrient loading [ACAD-125]. Precipitation and runoff mobilize and transport nutrients from land to water, and nutrient loading correlates with precipitation/runoff volume across numerous studies [ACAD-002], but the relationship is not one-directional: extreme rainfall can also flush/dilute a system, and a study of >1,200 continental-U.S. lake observations found precipitation's relationship to bloom-relevant nutrients is "mixed" — increasing nutrients via runoff in some cases, decreasing them via flushing in others [ACAD-049]. A single-storm case study at Conestogo Lake (Ontario) documented one mechanism in detail: bioavailable phosphorus rose more than 27-fold after an extreme rainfall/flood event, preceding an atypically early (~5 weeks ahead of normal), toxin-producing bloom about two weeks later [ACAD-120]. The 2015 Lake Erie bloom is repeatedly cited as an "ideal" bloom scenario illustrating the combined mechanism: heavy early-season rainfall delivering a large phosphorus pulse, followed by warm, calm, stratified conditions extending water residence time [ACAD-002]. Water residence time (hydraulic flushing) matters, but shortening it is not by itself an adequate control lever: a case study of a lake engineered to a ~3-day residence time still saw substantial bloom risk and rapid (3-day) algal growth in bioassays when its inflow was already eutrophic, with phosphorus addition producing the highest growth rates [ACAD-134]. Ice cover and its loss are an under-appreciated hydrological driver at high latitudes: an 18-month field campaign in three oligotrophic Canadian lakes found potentially toxic cyanobacteria proliferating across all seasons — including persistence of a non-photosynthetic basal lineage through ice cover and immediate recolonization at ice-off — alongside nearly 40 years of ice-phenology records showing significantly longer ice-free periods, complicating the assumption that cyanoHAB risk is confined to warm, visibly-blooming summer eutrophic systems [ACAD-115].

### Cyanotoxins & health
Cyanobacterial toxins fall into four toxicological classes despite structural diversity: neurotoxins, hepatotoxins, cytotoxins, and dermatoxins [ACAD-074]. Microcystins are the most chemically diverse and most commonly reported class worldwide — more than 200–250 known congeners, with microcystin-LR the most potent and most-studied, acting by inhibiting protein phosphatases 1 and 2A [ACAD-032; ACAD-074; ACAD-061; NGO-002; FED-065]. The best-documented human fatality event is the 1996 Caruaru, Brazil hemodialysis incident, in which microcystin-contaminated water caused acute liver failure and death in over 50 patients [ACAD-032; NGO-006]. Cylindrospermopsin caused a well-documented 1979 mass poisoning at Palm Island, Australia, hospitalizing well over 100 people (139–148, reported inconsistently across sources) with hepatoenteritis after an algicide-treated bloom released the toxin into a drinking-water reservoir [ACAD-061; ACAD-074]. Regulatory bodies have converged on broadly similar (though not identical) numeric guideline values for microcystins: EPA's non-enforceable 10-day drinking-water Health Advisories are 0.3 µg/L (infants/pre-school) and 1.6 µg/L (school-age/adult) [FED-013; FED-015], while WHO's 2020 provisional guideline values are 1 µg/L (lifetime drinking water), 12 µg/L (short-term drinking water), and 24 µg/L (recreational water) [NGO-002] — international/state values otherwise cluster between 0.04 and 1.5 µg/L depending on jurisdiction and exposure basis [FED-013]. As of EPA's 2015 assessment, no enforceable federal water-quality criteria existed for any cyanotoxin under the Safe Drinking Water Act or Clean Water Act [FED-013]. Visible bloom presence, color, and cell density are not reliable stand-ins for toxin risk: unsafe toxin levels can occur without a visible bloom, and toxin production is highly variable, species- and condition-dependent, so only laboratory testing confirms cyanotoxin presence [FED-032; FED-055; FED-073]. EPA's 2022 National Lakes Assessment found microcystin detected in half of U.S. lakes nationally, though only a small fraction (2%) exceeded the recreational health criterion, and detection rose sharply since the prior survey cycle even as nutrient levels changed little [FED-027]. Domestic animals — especially dogs — are documented sentinels of HAB risk because they will swim in or drink from visibly scummy water that people avoid; across three independent U.S. surveillance sources, dogs die from cyanotoxin poisoning far more often than humans, and toxin attribution patterns differ starkly by data source (microcystin-dominated at UC Davis' veterinary hospital vs. anatoxin-dominated in media/literature reports), while biochemical confirmation is rare across the board (4–13%) [ACAD-017]. CDC's national OHHABS surveillance system (2016–2018, 18 states) similarly found <5% of human or animal illness cases were laboratory-confirmed, with 89% of reported animal cases fatal (mostly wildlife/birds) versus zero human deaths among 389 reported human cases [FED-038; FED-004].

### Climate change & long-term trends
Multiple independent lines of evidence point toward increasing bloom frequency, magnitude, and duration globally over recent decades [ACAD-032; ACAD-057; ACAD-056; ACAD-021]. A 20-year (2003–2022), 4,085-lake global satellite study found 71.11% of lakes showed increasing algal cover trends (average +0.032%/year), versus 28.89% decreasing, with the relative importance of drivers dominated by anthropogenic factors (population density 27.63%, GDP 21.37%) for bloom *intensity*, while temperature and wind better explain shifts in bloom *timing* — these two dimensions change largely independently of one another [ACAD-012]. A separate 2026 Nature Communications study of 607 large shallow lakes argues short-term climate extremes (heatwaves, extreme precipitation) — not gradual warming — are the dominant drivers of global bloom variability, proposing a physiological "thermo-alkaline cascade" mechanism (heat/alkaline stress → oxidative stress → intracellular phosphorus storage → vertical migration to access sediment phosphorus) that can intensify blooms even in low-nutrient lakes [ACAD-022]. National-scale U.S. modeling projects mean cyanobacterial bloom occurrence roughly doubling to tripling by mid- and late-century (from ~7 days/year/waterbody currently to 16–23 days by 2050 and 18–39 days by 2090), with the increase driven primarily by rising temperature but partially offset by nutrient-loading changes [ACAD-002; ACAD-020; ACAD-057]. In Lake Taihu, China, a 20-year satellite/meteorological fusion study found bloom season lengthened from 238 to 302 days (a 27% increase) between the first and second decade of record, with a dose-response relationship of roughly +5,377 km² of cumulative bloom area per +1°C of annual mean temperature — though the authors explicitly caution that temperature alone does not fully explain interannual variability, since years with similar temperatures had markedly different bloom extents [ACAD-138]. Cyanobacteria's competitive advantage over other algae is well-established to increase steeply with temperature across large observational surveys, e.g., a 143-lake latitudinal transect from subarctic to tropical zones, and >100 lake sediment records show cyanobacterial dominance increasing over roughly the past 200 years in Northern Hemisphere temperate/subarctic lakes, consistent with Anthropocene acceleration [ACAD-032]. Despite this weight of evidence, **whether climate warming or nutrient enrichment dominates the observed global increase is explicitly stated to be scientifically unresolved** by a major 2024 Nature Reviews synthesis [ACAD-056; ACAD-057] — see Contested section. A 2023 review synthesizing this same terrain for U.S. inland waters frames the two drivers as complementary rather than competing at the level of general causal structure: excess nutrients from agricultural/urban runoff are treated as the primary, root-cause driver, with warming air/water temperatures and intensifying precipitation extremes described as factors that shift bloom frequency and duration on top of that nutrient baseline [ACAD-095].

### Succession & physiology (buoyancy, N-fixation)
The foundational PEG (Plankton Ecology Group) model of temperate lake plankton succession (1986), tested against 24 lakes, describes phytoplankton succession as predictable and directional (though disturbable by irregular physical events), including a mechanism directly relevant to late-season cyanobacterial dominance: a sequence of nutrient limitations (phosphate, then silica, then nitrogen) in which nitrogen depletion favors nitrogen-fixing filamentous cyanobacteria [ACAD-094]. Not all bloom-forming cyanobacteria fix nitrogen, and this distinction shapes ecological strategy: genera like *Aphanizomenon*, *Cylindrospermopsis*, *Dolichospermum*, and *Nodularia* can fix atmospheric N2 via specialized oxygen-free heterocytes, while non-diazotrophic genera such as *Microcystis* and *Planktothrix* depend on external nitrogen supply [ACAD-028; NGO-005]. Even among N-fixers, dependence on external nitrogen varies sharply by species: *Dolichospermum flos-aquae* reaches high biomass regardless of nitrogen supply, but *Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii* biomass is 15–20× lower when it must rely on its own fixation versus external nitrogen — meaning its blooms in nature would be expected mainly under high-nitrogen conditions despite its fixation capacity [ACAD-096]. Field metagenomic/metatranscriptomic sequencing of the 2014 western Lake Erie *Microcystis* bloom found coexisting *Dolichospermum*/*Aphanizomenon*-like ("ADA") taxa carried the large majority of actively-transcribed nitrogen-fixation genes recovered from the bloom community, implying these lower-abundance N-fixers — not the dominant non-diazotrophic *Microcystis* — were the community's main active nitrogen fixers at the time, though the authors state this potential influence on bloom ecology and water quality has not been quantified [ACAD-147]. Buoyancy regulation via gas vesicles is the classical mechanism enabling surface scum formation and is regulated primarily by light (more vesicles under low irradiance, vesicle collapse under high turgor pressure in high light), producing diel vertical migration [NGO-005]. A 2024 laboratory study identified an alternative, much faster (hours-scale, vs. the conventional days-to-weeks buoyancy timescale) non-buoyancy flotation mechanism in a specific *Microcystis* strain, driven by photosynthetic micro-bubble trapping plus EPS-mediated cell aggregation — a species-specific finding not yet tested in natural mixed populations [ACAD-080]. Extracellular polysaccharide (EPS) production also differs by strain and is linked to colony formation: a bloom-derived colonial *Microcystis* strain produced significantly more EPS and carried more gas-vesicle synthesis genes than three unicellular reference strains, and larger colony size is associated with faster surface migration and greater resistance to stressors (heavy metals, high light, grazing) [ACAD-119]. Turbulent mixing itself can select for particular photosynthetic trait compositions within a population (a modeling result), and populations with wider trait diversity form severe surface scum up to 4× faster than less-diverse populations [ACAD-090].

## Contested / uncertain / poorly-established

**Does phosphorus-only nutrient management remain sufficient, or is dual N+P control now necessary?** This is the single most contested question in the pack, spanning decades and dozens of sources. On one side: the landmark 37-year Lake 227 whole-ecosystem experiment concludes eutrophication cannot be controlled by reducing nitrogen alone, because N-fixing cyanobacteria compensate, and explicitly recommends phosphorus-focused management for most freshwater lakes (while itself cautioning this should not be extrapolated to estuaries) [ACAD-044]. On the other side, a large and growing body of work argues dual N+P reduction is now necessary: a 2009 Science commentary argued this outright and was itself immediately and formally rebutted in the same journal by Schindler & Hecky, who maintained P-only control is sufficient and better supported by long-term whole-lake evidence [ACAD-027]. More recent field/experimental evidence tips toward dual control in specific systems: bioassays in the San Francisco Bay Delta found phosphorus consistently replete while nitrogen was the more commonly limiting nutrient, arguing static N:P threshold rules don't hold there [ACAD-035]; Lake Erie field bioassays found phosphorus limits the spring diatom bloom but phosphorus AND nitrogen co-limit the summer cyanobacterial bloom, with dilution experiments suggesting the existing 40% P-only Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement target is insufficient and should be paired with an equal N cut [ACAD-036; ACAD-087]; and in NYC's Central Park/Prospect Park lakes, nitrogen — not phosphorus — was found to be the consistently limiting nutrient for cyanobacteria, the reverse of the traditional paradigm, attributed partly to P-rich, N-poor municipal drinking water used to top up the lakes [ACAD-130]. Review-level syntheses [ACAD-028; ACAD-029] and a Nature Reviews synthesis [ACAD-056] treat the shift toward dual N+P management as increasingly necessary given non-diazotrophic bloom genera and N-limited/co-limited systems, while flagging that the debate remains active. Complicating any nutrient-reduction strategy further: a cited 2022 *Science* modeling study (Hellweger et al., discussed within ACAD-019 and ACAD-036) found that a planned 40% phosphorus-only reduction in Lake Erie could actually make the bloom's toxin (microcystin) content 15–25% *higher* even while cutting biomass ~20% — a direct, still-debated counterexample to "nutrient reduction is unambiguously good," which the authors of ACAD-036 argue is oversimplified but do not resolve.

**Does the classical N:P ratio predict cyanobacterial dominance?** A widely-cited 2001 paper compiling 99 temperate lakes concluded the N:P-ratio model is flawed, and that absolute nutrient concentration and standing biomass predict bloom risk better than the ratio itself [ACAD-078] — directly complicating a management heuristic embedded in older eutrophication practice. Consistent with this, field mesocosm work found *Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii* dominating under BOTH very low (7:1) and very high (122:1) N:P ratios, the opposite of what classical stoichiometry theory predicts (that N-fixers should be favored only at low N:P) [ACAD-112]. The San Francisco Bay Delta study likewise argues a static ~15 N:P threshold is unreliable there given dynamic sediment-water exchange [ACAD-035].

**Is climate warming or nutrient enrichment the dominant driver of the global HAB increase?** A major 2024 Nature Reviews Earth & Environment synthesis states this is explicitly, currently unresolved in the field [ACAD-056; ACAD-057] — a direct rebuttal of any single-driver narrative. The same review documents cases on both sides: Lake Erie saw a ~50% phosphorus load cut (1974–late 1980s) yet still experienced a HAB resurgence since the 1990s, attributed to climate warming and legacy nutrients [ACAD-056]; Lake Balaton's 2019 bloom was NOT explained by external loading but by increased stratification and internal phosphorus loading [ACAD-056]; yet a 32-year, 20-reservoir U.S. study found bloom severity rising while total N, total P, and land cover stayed essentially flat, again implicating temperature/hydrology over bulk nutrient change [ACAD-125]. Complicating the "warming = more/worse blooms" narrative from a different angle, a global meta-analysis of 27 studies found "meteorological conditions" pooled as *negatively* correlated with cyanobacterial blooms at global scale — an aggregate sign that the source's own tension note flags as surprising against the wider warming-favors-cyanobacteria literature, though no effect size or confidence interval was retrievable from the abstract to weigh it properly [ACAD-005].

**Is the reported global increase in bloom frequency itself real, or an artifact of more monitoring?** A rigorous global statistical analysis (Hallegraeff et al. 2021) found NO statistically significant global increasing trend in marine/coastal HAB events (1985–2018) once adjusted for regional differences in monitoring/sampling effort (proxied via OBIS records) — the raw, unadjusted trend WAS significant, but adjustment could even flip the sign of regional trends, and the perceived global increase correlates strongly with a ~16-fold rise in global aquaculture production (a proxy for monitoring intensity) over the same period [ACAD-075]. This directly complicates broad "HABs are increasing globally" claims common elsewhere in the literature — though this analysis is centered on the marine/coastal HAEDAT database, and its applicability to purely freshwater cyanoHAB records specifically is not established within this source. Separately, a formal 2021 Nature "Matters Arising" rebuttal challenges the influential Ho, Michalak & Pahlevan (2019) satellite finding that 68% of 71 large global lakes showed increasing bloom intensity 1982–2012: the rebuttal argues single-band NIR reflectance is confounded by sediment plumes (potentially affecting up to 82% of the same 71-lake sample) and that Landsat 5's infrequent revisits and a masking algorithm bug undermine the original trend claim [ACAD-026; ACAD-101]. In the same vein, one Nature Reviews synthesis itself flags an internal contradiction: satellite observations show a modest bloom-frequency rise while in-situ chlorophyll-a data from 323 U.S. water bodies show levels decreasing in more lakes than increasing — the review states this "highlights that observational approaches could also influence the observed trends" [ACAD-057].

**Does temperature increase or decrease bloom toxicity?** Evidence is genuinely split and appears non-monotonic. One field/lab paired study found extracellular microcystin release from *Planktothrix agardhii* peaks at 20–25°C and then declines sharply by 30°C — a thermal optimum, not a "warmer = more toxic" linear relationship [ACAD-059]. Consistent with a cooler-can-be-more-toxic pattern, ACAD-019 reports lower ambient temperatures can favor higher cellular microcystin quota even as warming raises overall N-rich toxin production capacity — "warmer = more toxic" is stated as not simply true. Yet ACAD-049's Bayesian model-averaging analysis of >1,200 U.S. lake observations found higher spring air temperatures correlated with *reduced* microcystin concentrations (an association the authors do not claim is causal). And within the cyanotoxin-regulation literature broadly, effects of nitrate, iron, and light on toxin production reverse direction between species/strains in ways the reviewing authors attribute partly to non-standardized experimental methods rather than one consistent biological rule [ACAD-061].

**Do R. raciborskii's warm-adapted origins constrain where it can bloom?** A 2023 review reports *R. raciborskii* dominance and blooms have been observed in relatively cool water (10–15°C) in China, with marked strain-to-strain variation in cold tolerance, complicating a simple "climate warming enables poleward spread" narrative for this specific taxon — cold-tolerant intraspecific variation, not just warming, appears to be a key enabler of its temperate-zone range expansion [ACAD-081].

**Single-source or abstract-only findings warranting caution.** Several notable claims rest on a single study or could only be evaluated from an abstract (no full-text access), and should be weighted accordingly: the pH-dependent gas-vesicle mRNA-stability mechanism in *Microcystis* rests on one FEBS Letters paper accessed only via abstract, with most mechanistic detail (buffer, strain, pH values tested) drawn from secondary/unverified sources [ACAD-131]; the reversal of the classical Pit/Pst phosphate-transporter affinity relationship in ACAD-089 is flagged by the source's own reviewers as running counter to how these systems are typically described in the literature; the sulfate-molybdenum mechanism proposed to explain N-fixer vs. non-fixer dominance is explicitly self-described by its own authors as "questionable," since a contradicting study found no molybdenum limitation of N-fixers in a different eutrophic saline-lake system [ACAD-073]; and the 41-study/27-study global meta-analysis on driving-force categories was accessible only via abstract, with no effect-size, p-value, or confidence interval retrievable for any individual driver-bloom relationship [ACAD-005].

## Gaps & open questions

- **No consensus mechanism reconciling nutrient-centric and climate-centric bloom models.** Whether warming, nutrient loading, or their interaction dominates the global bloom trend remains explicitly unresolved per a major 2024 synthesis, and individual case studies point in different directions depending on the lake [ACAD-056; ACAD-057; ACAD-125].
- **Toxin production cannot yet be reliably predicted from biomass, cell density, or environmental drivers.** Multiple sources state cyanotoxin regulation shows contradictory, species/strain-dependent responses to nitrogen, phosphorus, light, iron, and temperature, often attributed to non-standardized experimental methods rather than one biological rule; current science explicitly cannot reliably predict how a given bloom's species composition and toxicity will change over time [ACAD-061; ACAD-019].
- **Internal ("legacy") sediment phosphorus loading is poorly quantified at scale.** Its magnitude relative to external loading is uncertain outside a handful of well-studied lakes, and whether it is important in the many thousands of under-studied reservoirs is an explicitly flagged data gap [ACAD-063; ACAD-142; ACAD-139].
- **Vertical/subsurface bloom distribution is poorly understood.** Both satellite and field monitoring focus overwhelmingly on surface water, risking false "bloom-free" assessments for deep-chlorophyll-maximum or metalimnetic bloom layers [ACAD-056; NGO-005].
- **HAB research is geographically and taxonomically skewed.** An estimated ~65% of HAB-relevant literature focuses on just 20 inland lakes, even though remote sensing has captured bloom events in over 20,000 lakes globally, with Africa, parts of Asia, and South America notably under-represented [ACAD-057].
- **True human/animal illness burden from cyanotoxins is unknown.** Surveillance systems are voluntary, passive, and demonstrably under-ascertain cases; biochemical/laboratory confirmation occurs in only a small minority (4–13%) of reported cases across independent U.S. data sources, and whether rising reported case counts reflect true increases or better detection/reporting cannot currently be distinguished [ACAD-017; FED-038; ACAD-123].
- **No unified physical-biological model exists for wave/wind-driven bloom transport and aerosolization.** A 2025 review frames this as a major, currently unresolved gap, proposing untested hypotheses rather than validated mechanisms [ACAD-144].
- **Whether nutrient-ratio (N:P) frameworks have any remaining predictive value is unresolved** given direct counter-examples of taxa dominating across the full range of tested ratios [ACAD-078; ACAD-112; ACAD-035].
- **Anatoxin-a and saxitoxin toxicology/regulation lag far behind microcystin.** WHO states the anatoxin-a database is inadequate even for a provisional guideline value, and gene-cluster regulation for both anatoxins and saxitoxins remains comparatively understudied [ACAD-146; ACAD-061; ACAD-074].

# in-situ-and-weather-data

This category covers the public in-situ water-quality data ecosystem used to characterize and pair with remote-sensing HAB signals: multi-agency aggregators (Water Quality Portal, NWIS), harmonized chlorophyll/pigment and cyanotoxin compilations, national probability surveys (NLA/NARS), high-frequency sensor and passive-sampler networks, and the gridded/station-based weather and climate datasets (gridMET, PRISM, Daymet, NLDAS, GHCN-Daily) typically used as driver covariates. It also covers what is known about sampling design, coverage biases, detection limits, and the practical difficulty of joining in-situ records to satellite observations in space and time.

## What's well-established

### In-situ water-quality data infrastructure is mature, multi-agency, but not seamlessly interoperable
The Water Quality Portal (WQP) is a joint USGS/EPA/National Water Quality Monitoring Council service that federates two backend systems — USGS's National Water Information System (NWIS, ~1.5 million monitoring sites) and EPA's Water Quality Exchange (WQX) Data Warehouse — and serves over 430 million water-quality result records contributed by 400+ (one EPA page states "over 1,500") state, federal, tribal, and local agencies [FED-043; FED-044; FED-045; FED-076]. The two backends refresh on different schedules (WQX weekly on Thursday evenings; NWIS historically every 24 hours) and, critically, site identifiers are **not harmonized** between them, so the same physical location can appear under different, non-matching IDs across the two systems [FED-044; FED-045]. As of the default (WQX2.2) interface, USGS-sourced records stopped updating after March 11, 2024; newer USGS data requires a separate beta WQX3.0 interface, a practical trap for any "live" pull of recent driver data [FED-044; FED-045]. USGS provides both R (`dataRetrieval`, v2.7.25/v2.7.23 as documented) and Python (`dataretrieval-python`, v1.2.0) open-source packages to programmatically query NWIS, WQP, NGWMN, and NLDI data, easing reproducible acquisition, though both packages are in an active transition away from legacy functions (e.g., `get_qwdata()`, `nwis.get_*`) toward newer `waterdata`/`read_waterdata_*` APIs, which is a reproducibility risk for code written against older tutorials [FED-018; FED-023; FED-049; FED-058].

### Harmonized national chlorophyll-a datasets exist, but are explicitly framed as rough proxies, not ground truth
A 2024 USGS-authored, peer-reviewed compilation (also released as a USGS ScienceBase data product) harmonizes discrete, lab-analyzed chlorophyll-a and pheophytin measurements from ~84,000 U.S. sites (83,829 after filtering) and over 1,374,000 pigment measurements spanning 2005–2022, built by merging WQP with previously unpublished USGS NWQL records via the `dataRetrieval` package [ACAD-018; FED-001; FED-002]. The authors are explicit about interchangeability limits: subtracting pheophytin from uncorrected chlorophyll-a does **not** equal corrected chlorophyll-a, and the WQP metadata field cannot reliably distinguish two different lab procedures both labeled "corrected chlorophyll a" (EPA Method 445 acidification vs. a modified fluorometric method) [ACAD-018; FED-001; FED-002]. Phytoplankton (µg/L, suspended) and periphyton (mg/m², benthic) chlorophyll are not directly comparable because they represent fundamentally different sampling bases [ACAD-018; FED-001]. The dataset retains rather than discards extreme values (~6,000 phytoplankton records, 0.48%, exceed 2,000 µg/L; some independently verified as real algal-bloom "scums") and retains ~40% of NWIS-sourced records that were still flagged "preliminary" at compilation time — both deliberate choices the authors flag for user caution [ACAD-018; FED-001; FED-002]. Only 437 of ~1.37 million records carry an explicit "algal bloom" event tag, so this is fundamentally a chlorophyll compilation, not a labeled bloom/no-bloom dataset [FED-002]. A separate, differently-scoped USGS/EDI product, "AquaMatch Chlorophyll a Data from Water Quality Portal" (~1970–2024), uses a three-tier (restrictive/narrowed/inclusive) data-quality scheme and is explicitly a component of a forthcoming "AquaSat v2" pipeline meant to expose the individual steps used to build satellite-to-in-situ matchups — but this release itself contains **no** satellite data; a separate pipeline (Ross et al. 2019) must be applied to add Landsat reflectances [FED-005]. Across all these releases, co-located monitoring sites are not aggregated into single physical-location records, so raw site counts likely overstate true independent spatial coverage [ACAD-018; FED-001; FED-002].

### National cyanotoxin surveys show cyanobacteria are geographically widespread, but proxy indicators (chlorophyll, cell counts) systematically over-predict toxin-based risk relative to direct measurement
EPA's National Lakes Assessment (NLA) 2007 cyanotoxin survey (1,161 lakes; Loftin et al. 2016) found cyanobacteria present in 98% of samples (dominant in 76%), yet direct toxin detection was far lower and inconsistent across classes: microcystins 32%, saxitoxins 7.7%, cylindrospermopsins 4.0% of samples, with triple co-occurrence in only 0.32% of positive samples [ACAD-034]. WHO recreational-risk thresholds were exceeded in just 1.1% of samples using measured microcystin, versus 27% using cyanobacterial-abundance proxies and 44% using total chlorophyll-a — the three WHO metrics fully agreed in only 27% of samples [ACAD-034]. The follow-on NLA 2022 survey (981 probability-sampled lakes, representing 268,020 lakes nationally, margin of error ≈±5% at 95% confidence) similarly found microcystin detectable in an estimated 50% of lake extent (up sharply from 21% in 2017) while only 2% (~5,360 lakes) exceeded EPA's 8 ppb recreational criterion; cylindrospermopsin was detected in 12% of lakes but exceeded its 15 ppb criterion in zero lakes [FED-027; FED-028]. Notably, national nutrient and biological indicators showed "little change" 2017→2022 even as microcystin detection rose by roughly 30 percentage points — an unexplained divergence the source itself does not resolve [FED-027; FED-028]. EPA's regulatory guidance reinforces the same pattern from the policy side: visible bloom presence is not a reliable indicator of toxin absence, and toxin production is "highly variable," depending on species and environmental conditions rather than being a fixed bloom property [FED-032; FED-034]. NLA only tracks two cyanotoxins (microcystins, cylindrospermopsin) and explicitly disclaims usefulness for site-specific/local management decisions, being designed for national/regional statistical estimation only [FED-028].

### High-frequency in-situ sensors and passive samplers add temporal resolution but reveal method-dependent, lake-specific, and spatially patchy performance
A two-year (2021–2022), 10-buoy study in western Lake Erie found water temperature highly spatially synchronous across the network, while dissolved oxygen, turbidity, chlorophyll-a, and phycocyanin were asynchronous, with asynchrony increasing with buoy separation distance; predicted buoy spacing needed for even "moderately strong" (r=0.5) correlation was ~108 km for temperature versus only ~18–46 km for the bio-optical/chemical variables — implying a single buoy should not be assumed representative of conditions elsewhere in a lake [ACAD-066]. A separate USGS pilot (SIR 2024-5010) deploying multiparameter sondes, nutrient optical sensors, and fluorometers at three Finger Lakes (2019–2020) found sensor-measured chlorophyll fluorescence only moderately correlated with lab chlorophyll-a (Pearson r 0.578–0.774, varying by lake), phycocyanin fluorescence was **not** significantly related to cyanobacterial biovolume (turbidity was a better indicator), and nitrate sensors performed well overall but underestimated at one of two lakes tested [FED-054]. Multivariate regression models built from multiple sensor channels did not outperform simple univariate fluorescence models, and the study attributes weak signal partly to the absence of cyanoHAB events at the specific monitoring locations during the study period, cautioning that Finger Lakes cyanoHABs are often spatially isolated and ephemeral [FED-054]. For toxin-specific passive sampling, a 2019 USGS/NYSDEC SPATT (solid-phase adsorption toxin tracking) deployment across three Finger Lakes found ELISA and LC-MS/MS both detected microcystins in 100% of analyzed samples versus 63% for LC-MS, but mean concentrations differed by up to ~8× across methods on the same extracts, even though the methods correlated strongly (r=0.90–0.95) [FED-053]. SPATT extracts registered many toxin detections (including ~80% ELISA-positive for anatoxins) absent from paired discrete grab samples, but extract concentrations cannot be readily converted to ambient in-lake concentrations because of variable flow and multi-day integration [FED-053]. The Global Lake Ecological Observatory Network (GLEON), a grassroots international sensor-network research collaboration founded in 2005, exists partly to address the general limnological problem that ecological model outputs have historically been validated against only a limited number of directly comparable in-situ measurements in both space and time — a Cyanobacteria working group is actively investigating (as an open hypothesis, not an established finding) whether repeatable pre/post-bloom patterns exist in physical buoy variables [ACAD-103; NGO-004; NGO-011].

### Weather and climate data products for pairing with water-quality signals are mature and diverse, but each has resolution, homogenization, and latency caveats
Multiple U.S. gridded/station weather products are available as driver covariates. **gridMET** (~4 km/1/24° resolution, CONUS, 1979–present, updated daily) blends PRISM's spatial detail with NLDAS-2's temporal detail and provides temperature, precipitation, radiation, humidity, wind, and derived indices (evapotranspiration, drought, fire danger), but cannot resolve microclimates finer than its native grid, should not be used to infer precipitation trends (due to input inhomogeneities), and its most recent 60 days are preliminary [ACAD-054; FED-059]. **PRISM** offers 800 m (climatological normals) to 4 km (operational monthly/daily) gridded temperature/precipitation/dewpoint back to 1895 (monthly) or 1981 (daily), with documented mean absolute errors of 5% (precipitation, eastern US) to 10% (western US), and is explicitly not homogenized, so should not be used for century-scale trend analysis [ACAD-132]. **Daymet** provides a 1 km grid of 7 daily variables (temperature, precipitation, vapor pressure, radiation, snow water equivalent, day length) for North America since 1980, with regionally uneven cross-validation accuracy (lowest error in the densely-instrumented eastern US) and documented humidity-estimation biases in arid environments [FED-050]. **NLDAS** delivers hourly, 1/8°-resolution atmospheric forcing (7 variables) since 1979 with ~4-day operational latency, but the source explicitly states NLDAS is "not intended to be used as an independent dataset for monitoring climate variability and change," and documents specific precipitation-input artifacts including a discontinuity at 1 January 2012 [FED-067]. **GHCN-Daily**, the underlying station network for many of these gridded products, holds records from 100,000+ global stations (180 countries), is quality-controlled (an automated system flags ~0.3% of ~2 billion values, with an estimated 98–99% true-error rate) but is explicitly **not** bias-adjusted/homogenized for instrument or observing-practice changes, unlike its monthly counterpart GHCN-Monthly [ACAD-107; FED-019; FED-030]. Station density is geographically uneven, with dense coverage in North America/Eurasia/Australia and comparatively sparse coverage in Africa, Antarctica, and South America [FED-019].

### Systematic sampling and reporting biases run through nearly every in-situ monitoring channel
A 2008 statistical study comparing six state agencies' (MI, WI, IA, OH, ME, NH) lake-monitoring programs against the full census of lakes in each state found larger lakes consistently more likely to be sampled than smaller ones, and sampling probability also varies with surrounding land use/cover, though a significant lake-size × land-use interaction prevented isolating a clean land-use effect; the authors conclude non-probability-based agency survey data must be used cautiously when generalized to the full lake population [ACAD-079]. This directly complicates treating Water Quality Portal or state DEQ records as a representative sample of "all lakes." EPA's own National Aquatic Resource Surveys (including NLA) address this specific bias by design, using probability (random, statistically representative) site selection rather than convenience/agency-run sampling, precisely so that results from sampled sites can be extrapolated to the full population of waters without needing to sample every waterbody [FED-078]. Separately, a multi-year field-scale study in the Lake Erie basin found phosphorus fractions respond in opposite directions to the same conservation practice (no-till cut total phosphorus but nearly doubled soluble phosphorus loss in surface runoff), directly contradicting the older planning assumption that controlling sediment loss automatically controls phosphorus loss — a caution relevant to any driver analysis treating "erosion control" as a proxy for "nutrient-loading control" [ACAD-076]. On the chlorophyll-compilation side specifically, sampling effort is geographically and temporally uneven: sample density, frequency, and even analytical methods vary greatly by state (Florida has the most phytoplankton samples, Indiana the most periphyton samples), and sampling is concentrated in warmer months [ACAD-018]. Sampling-design standards for the driver (nutrient-runoff) side of the equation exist but are narrowly scoped: USDA NRCS's CEMA 201 standard for edge-of-field water-quality monitoring requires a scientifically defensible paired-field or above-and-below-treatment design, a 9-parameter nutrient/sediment analyte panel, and oversight by a Qualified Individual with at least 2 prior projects' experience — but it governs only field-runoff monitoring for conservation-practice evaluation, not lake/HAB outcome monitoring itself [FED-051].

### State-level and CDC surveillance systems supply confirmed bloom/illness observations but are opportunistic, not systematic, coverage
CDC's One Health Harmful Algal Bloom System (OHHABS), launched 2016, is described by CDC as the only national public health system collecting both HAB occurrence and associated human/animal illness data, but reporting is voluntary at the state/territorial health-department level (not from individual clinicians or the public directly), and the "About" page itself provides no completeness, validation, or prevalence statistics [FED-004]. New York's NYHABS system similarly combines public/agency reporting with DEC human verification (confirmation requires visual observation, photos, and/or water sampling) but explicitly states its live map "is not a complete, exhaustive inventory of all HABs statewide," since it depends on reports actually being submitted [SLG-003]. Florida's public-facing dashboards (Algal Bloom Dashboard; Protecting Florida Together) integrate DEP/FWC monitoring and citizen reports but the fetched landing pages themselves carry no quantitative thresholds, cell counts, or toxin concentrations — the underlying data lives inside interactive map applications not captured by static text extraction [SLG-001; SLG-007]. NOAA's operational Lake Erie HAB Forecast/Tracker likewise depends on in-situ inputs it does not itself generate: it only begins issuing bulletins after weekly in-situ sampling (surface grabs at ~0.75 m depth) confirms *Microcystis* presence, and toxin (microcystin) values are compared against Ohio's Elevated Recreational Public Health Advisory level separately from the satellite-derived chlorophyll signal [FED-025].

### River, stream, and cross-water-body monitoring is comparatively thin, and toxin sources can lack a surface signature entirely
Most of the in-situ infrastructure above targets lakes/reservoirs, but a growing body of work documents cyanotoxins in flowing and transitional waters that a lakes-only monitoring design would miss. USGS's own program page states toxic cyanoHABs have been implicated in illness in at least 43 states, that cyanobacteria-producing HABs occur in large rivers (not just lakes/reservoirs, per an 11-site 2017 USGS study), and that microcystins were detected in 39% of assessed small streams in the southeastern US — though a companion USGS feature story's own headline ("One-Third of Streams") does not match its 39% body-text figure, an internal inconsistency worth flagging rather than resolving [FED-031]. A synthesis paper on monitoring across the "freshwater-to-marine continuum" reinforces this gap with concrete numbers: a California statewide stream assessment found >90% of stream kilometers supported potential toxin-producing cyanobacteria genera and 23% supported toxin-producing species (microcystins detected at 33% of sites), and a separate systematic study of southeastern US wadeable streams detected microcystins in 39% of streams — independently consistent with FED-031's figure [ACAD-127; FED-031]. The same synthesis documents that benthic (bottom-attached) cyanobacterial mats — which lack the strong water-surface spectral signature that satellite/remote-sensing HAB detection depends on — were themselves a microcystin source at nearly all sampled river sites in a California case study, and that toxins produced far upstream (e.g., Klamath River headwater reservoirs) can be transported over 300 km downstream and exceed public-health thresholds annually, meaning downstream-only or lake-only monitoring can miss the source [ACAD-127]. It also reports that passive samplers (SPATT) detected toxins far more often than paired discrete grab samples in San Francisco Bay (microcystins in 76% of SPATT vs. 50% of grab samples; domoic acid 97% vs. 11%) even as peer-reviewed SPATT usage grew roughly 14-fold over the past decade — but SPATT remains limited for regulatory use because relating its integrated concentrations back to numeric health thresholds is difficult [ACAD-127], a limitation independently corroborated by the Finger Lakes SPATT study's own inability to convert extract concentrations to ambient values [FED-053]. On the streamflow/groundwater side, USGS's National Water Quality Network (established 2013; 102 surface-water sites, 258 groundwater locations) is authorized in part under the Harmful Algal Blooms and Hypoxia Research and Control Act of 1998, yet its own published constituent list (nutrients, sediment, general chemistry, contaminants) names no bloom-specific parameter — no chlorophyll-a, cyanobacterial cell counts, or cyanotoxins — so it functions as a nutrient/contaminant-trend network adjacent to, but not a direct source of, HAB-specific in-situ data [FED-075].

## Contested / uncertain / poorly-established

- **Does chlorophyll-a/cyanobacterial abundance meaningfully predict toxin (health) risk, or systematically overstate it?** Multiple independent national surveys agree proxy indicators over-predict toxin-based risk: NLA 2007 found chlorophyll-a-based WHO risk exceedance (44%) and cyanobacteria-abundance-based exceedance (27%) both far exceeded microcystin-based exceedance (1.1%) [ACAD-034]; NLA 2022 similarly showed microcystin detectable in 50% of lake extent but only 2% exceeding the health criterion [FED-028]; EPA's own 2019 guidance states visual/cell-density observation cannot substitute for toxin lab testing [FED-032; FED-034]; and NOAA's Lake Erie HAB Tracker explicitly does not predict toxin concentrations from its satellite-chlorophyll signal [FED-025; FED-071]. This is a consistent, well-replicated finding across sources, not a genuine two-sided dispute — but it is flagged here because it is the single most load-bearing tension for any project treating a chlorophyll/cyanobacteria proxy as a health-risk output.

- **Is phycocyanin fluorescence a reliable in-situ proxy for cyanobacterial abundance?** FED-054's three-lake Finger Lakes sensor study found phycocyanin fluorescence was **not** significantly related to cyanobacterial biovolume, with turbidity a better predictor — a finding that complicates the common treatment of phycocyanin sensors as a straightforward cyanobacteria proxy in monitoring practice. The report itself frames this as potentially lake/period-specific (no cyanoHAB events occurred at the monitored locations during the study), not a universal indictment, so this should be read as a single-study caution rather than a resolved contradiction of phycocyanin sensing generally [FED-054].

- **How much can a single buoy or fixed monitoring station be trusted to represent a lake/basin?** ACAD-066's western Lake Erie buoy-spacing analysis (a single region, single lake, two seasons) found predicted spacing for "moderately strong" correlation was only ~18–46 km for bio-optical variables, implying most real monitoring networks under-resolve spatial complexity, and the authors explicitly caution against generalizing to other large lakes without further testing. This tension is echoed qualitatively by GLEON's Cyanobacteria working group, which frames pre/post-bloom sensor signatures as an open hypothesis under active investigation, not an established capability [ACAD-066; ACAD-103; NGO-004]. The evidentiary base here is narrow (one lake, one study) even though its implications (do not extrapolate one buoy's readings elsewhere) are stated with high confidence by the authors.

- **How representative are agency-run/opportunistic in-situ monitoring networks of the true population of lakes?** ACAD-079 (abstract-only access, no quantitative results retrievable) found systematic size- and land-use-related sampling bias across six state programs, directly complicating the assumption that WQP/state DEQ records represent an unbiased lake sample — but this finding rests on a single 2008 study with no numeric effect sizes available in the accessible text, and it has not been independently replicated within this pack. Even granting that probability-based designs (like NLA/NARS) solve the population-representativeness problem ACAD-079 identifies, representativeness of the *population of lakes* is a distinct question from whether infrequent, multi-year survey snapshots can characterize a fast-changing, time-variant phenomenon such as a bloom — a distinction the probability-survey design literature itself does not claim to resolve [FED-078].

- **Does nutrient loading track cyanotoxin prevalence over time?** FED-027/FED-028 report that national nutrient and biological indicators changed "little" between the 2017 and 2022 NLA cycles even as microcystin detection rose by ~30 percentage points — a divergence the source does not explain. This sits in tension with the general driver literature's nutrient-centric framing (e.g., ACAD-076's phosphorus-focused Lake Erie work) without directly contradicting it; the honest position is that this single national before/after comparison does not resolve whether nutrients drive short-term toxin variability, and other unmeasured factors (temperature, weather in the sampled year, analytical sensitivity changes) are explicitly floated as alternative explanations by the source itself.

- **Single-source or abstract-only claims requiring extra caution:** ACAD-079's core quantitative results (effect sizes, per-state counts, p-values) are not available beyond the abstract text; ACAD-103's GLEON overview is abstract-only and pre-dates/omits any HAB-specific content; SLG-001's Florida dashboard is a landing-only fetch whose live map content (real thresholds, cell counts) could not be independently verified in text; FED-005's AquaMatch product is landing-only with no record/site counts retrievable, and its own metadata is internally inconsistent about release date and DOI status.

- **Do surface-oriented monitoring and remote sensing capture the toxin sources that matter, or systematically miss a meaningful share of them?** Multiple sources converge on "no, not fully" but from different angles: benthic cyanobacterial mats (which lack the water-surface spectral signature satellite detection relies on) were a microcystin source at nearly all sampled river sites in one California case study [ACAD-127]; EPA states outright that cyanotoxin exposure risk can exist even when no bloom is visibly apparent [FED-032; FED-034]; and NOAA cautions that a bloom's visible size/biomass is not a proxy for its toxicity, since a large bloom's toxins can be less concentrated than a smaller bloom's [FED-071]. None of these sources quantifies what fraction of total toxin exposure nationally comes from non-surface or non-visible sources — the direction of the effect is consistently reported, but its magnitude is not established in this pack.

- **Internal inconsistencies within federal sources themselves (not resolved, only flagged):** The USGS chlorophyll compilation's own landing page calls the 2005–2022 span a "17-year record" while its companion peer-reviewed paper calls the identical span an "18-year record," and the six per-pigment-type record counts in the paper sum to ~1,376,213 versus the paper's own rounded headline of "over 1,374,000" [FED-002]. EPA's own pages disagree on how many agencies contribute to WQP ("over 1,500" on one page vs. "more than 1,000" on a related page, vs. "more than 400" on WQP's own FAQ/User Guide) [FED-043; FED-044; FED-045; FED-076]. A USGS feature story's own headline ("One-Third of Streams") mismatches its body text ("39 percent") for the same southeastern-U.S. microcystin-in-streams statistic [FED-031].

## Gaps & open questions

- **No resolved, quantified relationship between nutrient-loading trends and short-term cyanotoxin-detection trends** at the national scale: NLA 2017→2022 showed nutrient/biological indicators nearly flat while microcystin detection rose ~30 percentage points, and the literature in this pack does not explain the divergence [FED-027; FED-028].
- **No pack source directly quantifies how well remote-sensing chlorophyll/cyanobacteria retrievals match co-located in-situ discrete samples in space and time** (a true satellite-in-situ "matchup" validation) — the closest are (a) FED-005/AquaMatch, which is a precursor in-situ-only step explicitly requiring a separate pipeline to add satellite data, and (b) ACAD-114's cloud-adjusted revisit-frequency analysis, which addresses satellite temporal cadence but not spatial/temporal co-registration error against in-situ ground truth.
- **Phycocyanin's reliability as a cyanobacteria proxy across lakes/seasons remains unresolved** beyond the single Finger Lakes study that found it uncorrelated with biovolume — no broader multi-lake replication is present in this pack [FED-054].
- **The spatial-representativeness limits found for one Lake Erie buoy network are not shown to generalize** to other lake types, sizes, or morphologies; the authors themselves call for testing on other large lakes [ACAD-066].
- **No source in this pack reports a validated, multi-toxin (mixture) predictive model outside a single small case study** (Kabetogama Lake, 3 sites, 2016–2017 data, multiple linear regression via Virtual Beach software) — described as addressing a gap where "no known models" previously predicted toxin mixtures rather than single toxins [FED-031].
- **The true completeness/under-ascertainment rate of voluntary bloom-illness surveillance (OHHABS, NYHABS, state dashboards) is not quantified anywhere in this pack** — sources describe the reporting mechanism and its voluntary/opportunistic nature but provide no completeness or capture-rate estimate [FED-004; SLG-001; SLG-003; SLG-007].
- **The magnitude of state-agency lake-monitoring selection bias (size, land use) is asserted directionally but not quantified** in accessible text — ACAD-079 is abstract-only, with no effect sizes, odds ratios, or per-state counts retrievable.
- **Whether edge-of-field/conservation-practice effects on phosphorus fractions (surface vs. tile, soluble vs. total) generalize beyond the single St. Joseph River watershed study** remains open; the authors themselves caution against generalizing this correlational, single-watershed dataset [ACAD-076].

# treatment-and-management

This category covers interventions and policy frameworks aimed at reducing harmful cyanobacterial blooms (HABs) and the human/ecological risk they pose: in-lake chemical treatments (copper algaecides, hydrogen peroxide), nutrient and watershed management (phosphorus and nitrogen load reduction, TMDLs, agricultural BMPs, alum/phosphorus-inactivation), physical and biological in-lake controls (mixing, flushing, biomanipulation), and the guideline values and advisory frameworks that translate cyanotoxin science into "safe/unsafe" decisions for drinking water and recreation. A recurring theme across the **chemical (and some physical) in-lake treatment** sub-topics is a **suppression-vs-toxin-release trade-off**: killing or lysing cyanobacterial cells is not the same as neutralizing the toxin they contain, and several sources show these two outcomes can diverge sharply. (This trade-off is specific to lysis-based treatments such as copper algaecides and hydrogen peroxide; it does not apply to non-lysis approaches such as alum/phosphorus inactivation, nutrient-load reduction, or biomanipulation.)

## What's well-established

### Copper-based algaecides: efficacy, dose-response, and the toxin-release trade-off
Copper sulfate and chelated-copper formulations (e.g., Cutrine Ultra, EarthTec, SeClear, Captain, Captain XTR, Algimycin PWF, Argos) are long-used, EPA-registered, and well-studied cyanocidal agents [NGO-001; FED-036]. Across multiple bench-scale and field studies, copper reliably reduces cyanobacterial cell density/chlorophyll/phycocyanin in a dose-dependent way [ACAD-015; ACAD-065; ACAD-118; ACAD-136]. However, killing cells by copper-induced lysis releases previously intracellular microcystin into the dissolved (more bioavailable) fraction rather than degrading it — total toxin mass is redistributed, not reduced, by treatment [ACAD-118; ACAD-015]. This creates a genuine trade-off: in one bench trial, a *quarter* dose of Cutrine Ultra (0.125 mg/L Cu) — the lowest dose tested for that product — was judged the single best option overall (55% cyanobacteria reduction, limited toxin release, minimal rebound, lowest cost), outperforming higher, EPA-maximum doses of other products on combined efficacy/toxin-release/rebound/cost criteria [ACAD-015]. Products and formulations differ meaningfully in how much toxin they release per unit of kill: chelated-copper products (Algimycin PWF, Argos, Captain XTR, Cutrine Ultra) showed less microcystin release at 1 day-after-treatment and lower residual toxin at 4 days than other effective treatments in a Lake Okeechobee laboratory trial, even though multiple product classes achieved comparable cyanobacteria control [ACAD-065]. EPA's federal registration caps copper algaecide application at a maximum of 1.0 mg/L (1 ppm) active copper because of toxicity to non-target aquatic life, with zooplankton markedly more copper-sensitive than phytoplankton [ACAD-015; FED-006; PVT-005; PVT-002; FED-036].

Copper dosing has traditionally relied on a decades-old, alkalinity-only ratio rule with no rigorous scientific derivation [ACAD-082]. Newer research finds pH and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) — not alkalinity — are the strongest predictors of algal copper toxicity in laboratory bioassays (r²=0.813), and a resulting multiple-linear-regression (MLR) dosing approach let a 28-day pond mesocosm trial cut copper dose by 60% (100 vs. 250 µg/L Cu) while achieving statistically equivalent (~95%) cyanobacterial control and causing less collateral harm to non-target green algae and zooplankton [ACAD-082]. A follow-up 42-day repeated-dosing field trial reinforced this: three repeated MLR-based doses (40–80 µg/L Cu) matched the initial efficacy of a much higher conventional alkalinity-based dose (350 µg/L Cu), and the conventional higher dose became progressively *less* effective with repeated application while the lower doses did not [ACAD-136]. Both MLR papers explicitly bound their own applicability (e.g., not recommended below 50 mg/L CaCO3 alkalinity; needs validation in other systems) [ACAD-082].

Copper's environmental persistence is a structural limitation: copper does not degrade and accumulates in sediment with repeated use, and repeated application may select for copper-resistant cyanobacteria [FED-006; PVT-002; ACAD-033]. "Newer" proprietary copper formulations are not uniformly less toxic to non-target organisms than legacy copper sulfate — in one chronic (21-day) Daphnia magna study, the novel formulation SeClear was *more* chronically toxic (lower/more potent EC50 for reproduction and body length: 0.274 mg Cu/L) than traditional CuSO4·5H2O (0.436 mg Cu/L) [ACAD-072]. Toxicity-modifying water chemistry (pH, DOC, alkalinity, hardness) should be assessed per-application rather than assumed from a product's general reputation [ACAD-072; ACAD-082]. Manufacturer-issued EPA labels for these products (e.g., Captain, SeClear, PAK 27) independently corroborate the same operational constraints found in the independent literature: caps on single-application and annual cumulative copper dose, minimum 14-day retreatment intervals, partial-waterbody treatment requirements to avoid dissolved-oxygen crashes, and explicit warnings against treating already-dense toxic blooms because of the cell-lysis/toxin-release risk [PVT-002; PVT-005; PVT-004; FED-006]. A non-copper granular algaecide (sodium carbonate peroxyhydrate-based PAK 27) is marketed as an alternative where copper-sensitive fish are present, though its comparative efficacy/toxin-release profile is not independently evaluated in this pack the way copper and H2O2 are [PVT-004].

### Hydrogen peroxide and other physical/curative treatments: efficacy and the same toxin-release problem
Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) produces a clear, dose-dependent kill of Microcystis (EC50s of 2.5–3.8 mg/L across chlorophyll-a, photosystem-II efficiency, and particle-count endpoints), with cyanobacteria generally more H2O2-sensitive than eukaryotic phytoplankton, giving H2O2 some target selectivity [ACAD-042; ACAD-033]. But as with copper, H2O2 does not degrade microcystin — even at the highest doses tested (8–20 mg/L), the large majority of total toxin remained detectable, and the *dissolved* fraction rose sharply because lysis moves toxin out of cells rather than destroying it [ACAD-042; ACAD-116]. Effective doses vary strongly by water-body chemistry: a Finnish field-mesocosm study found even 20 mg/L H2O2 (10× a dose reported elsewhere to have destroyed ~99% of microcystins) failed to destroy released toxin, which the authors attribute to water-matrix-dependent H2O2 consumption — underscoring that a single "effective dose" figure does not transfer across water bodies [ACAD-116]. H2O2 doses of 10 mg/L and higher harmed non-target zooplankton and moving phytoplankton in that same study, leading the authors to recommend doses closer to 2 mg/L as a safer trade-off, and to caution that treated water should be tested for microcystin before use for sensitive purposes (e.g., irrigation) [ACAD-116]. A "prophylactic treatment" concept — repeated low-dose algicide applications made *before* bloom development, when biomass and toxin pool are still small — has been proposed as a way to sidestep the large-lysis-event toxin spike associated with treating an already-dense bloom [ACAD-033]. Low-power commercial ultrasound transducers, by contrast, produced only minimal biomass inhibition and did not reduce total microcystin, while still increasing the dissolved (bioavailable) toxin fraction — a "worst of both worlds" outcome the authors attribute to acoustic intensity roughly four orders of magnitude below what is needed for cavitation-driven toxin degradation [ACAD-042]. Coagulant-plus-ballast treatments that remove intact, still-living cells (rather than lysing them) are recommended as preferable where feasible, because they do not release stored toxin [ACAD-042]. A novel engineering approach — a passive, slow-release H2O2 buoy tested at lab/pond scale — nearly eliminated cyanobacteria within about a week in Lake Erie water samples, but is explicitly an early-stage prototype requiring further scale-up work [NGO-013].

### Nutrient and watershed management: phosphorus, nitrogen, and agricultural BMPs
Long-term, sustainable CyanoHAB control is widely held to require external nutrient load reduction, not chemical/physical/biological suppression alone [ACAD-028; ACAD-070; NGO-002; NGO-007]. Historically phosphorus-only management is increasingly viewed as insufficient: given the rise of non-N2-fixing bloom genera (Microcystis, Planktothrix) that depend on external nitrogen, and evidence of nitrogen-limited or N+P co-limited systems, both N and P reductions are now considered necessary in many settings [ACAD-028]. This is not a universal consensus — one review frames dual N+P reduction as a claim in tension with, and complicating, the older phosphorus-only eutrophication-management paradigm [ACAD-028], and the U.S. Lake Erie phosphorus-reduction program itself targets phosphorus alone while flagging (via a cited study) that phosphorus reduction without nitrogen reduction might not reduce bloom *toxicity* even if it shrinks bloom *extent* [FED-039; FED-074].

At the federal/binational program scale, the U.S.–Canada Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement (Annex 4) set a 40% total-phosphorus load-reduction target for Lake Erie's western/central basins (2008 baseline, adopted 2016) [FED-003; FED-039; FED-074]. As of a 2026 status report, NOAA's Lake Erie bloom-severity rating has been "mild" every year since 2020, spring soluble phosphorus in the Maumee River has declined somewhat beyond what streamflow alone would predict, and 21 of 24 monitored tributaries show decreasing or steady total phosphorus loads — but the lake remains eutrophic overall, tributaries still routinely exceed targets, and the report itself cautions that basin-wide trends are hard to disentangle from year-to-year precipitation/discharge variability [FED-037]. Large, well-funded state/federal programs (Ohio's H2Ohio, $250M since 2019, 2,600+ farmers, 1.85M acres; USDA NRCS conservation contracts; Great Lakes Water Authority wastewater P cuts >55%) are underway, but their success in producing lake-scale HAB reductions (as opposed to reported farm-level or plant-level metrics) is not independently demonstrated within this evidence pack [FED-037].

Agricultural best-management practices (BMPs) for reducing phosphorus export are the most direct field-tested lever on external loading. A synthesis of 15 U.S. row-crop studies (2000–2022) found subsurface fertilizer placement reduced mean plot-scale dissolved reactive phosphorus (DRP) by 88% relative to surface broadcasting, and that DRP rises steeply with application rate (0.19 mg/L unfertilized → 3.47 mg/L at "High" rate, p<0.001) [ACAD-041]. However, at field scale (as opposed to plot scale), effects were far more muted and in one case ran in the *opposite* direction from the plot-scale pattern, and the authors explicitly attribute the scale gap to plot studies capturing acute worst-case export versus field studies capturing chronic annual export [ACAD-041]. A parallel literature review of BMP effectiveness for the Canadian Lake Erie Basin similarly concludes effectiveness is "highly variable and site-specific," driven by local climate, slope, soil, and land-use conditions, cautioning against treating any single BMP effectiveness percentage as broadly transferable [ACAD-039]. In a real, intensively monitored case, two purpose-built wetland-restoration BMPs at hypereutrophic Lake Macatawa, MI produced no statistically significant upstream-vs-downstream or pre/post improvement in phosphorus, turbidity, or nitrate/ammonia after two years — the authors attribute this to the BMPs' minute footprint relative to the watershed (~0.097%), an anomalously dry monitoring year, and the likelihood that two years is simply too short a window for wetland maturation and lake response [ACAD-040]. A related USDA finding complicates the assumption that current-season nutrient reductions translate quickly into water-quality improvement: "legacy" phosphorus already accumulated in agricultural soils/sediments across seven studied U.S. watersheds is large enough to sustain high dissolved-P losses for decades without any new inputs, implying a lag between load-reduction action and observed improvement [FED-040].

### Alum (aluminum) treatment for internal phosphorus loading
Alum (aluminum sulfate ± sodium aluminate) treatment targets *internal* (sediment-derived) phosphorus loading rather than external inputs, and is well-documented as effective when internal loading is the dominant driver. A cross-lake synthesis of 21 U.S. lakes found alum reduced internal P-loading rate by ~two-thirds in polymictic lakes (persisting 5–11 years) and by 80% in dimictic (stratified) lakes (persisting an average of 13, up to 21, years); the authors judge ~10 years a reasonable longevity expectation for polymictic lakes and ~15 years for dimictic lakes [ACAD-038]. On Cape Cod, MA, ten lakes have been alum-treated since 1995; the best-documented case, Hamblin Pond, went ~18 years bloom-free after its first (1995) treatment, and a 2015 second treatment was characterized by the authors as a major success expected to persist roughly two more decades [ACAD-009]. A hypereutrophic-lake case cited elsewhere reports an 84 g/m² alum dose producing 70% chlorophyll-a reduction and 95% total-phosphate reduction [ACAD-033]. Average treatment cost was ~$150 (2016 USD) per g/m² of applied aluminum per hectare [ACAD-009]. Alum is explicitly **not a permanent fix and not universally effective**: it failed initially in 2 of 9 polymictic lakes in the 21-lake synthesis (apparently due to macrophyte interference), and effectiveness decline over time may reflect physical burial of the aluminum floc (documented at 20 cm sediment depth 16 years post-treatment at West Twin Lake, OH) rather than chemical exhaustion [ACAD-038]. Critically, alum does not address externally-driven water-quality problems: at West Twin Lake, OH, external nutrient diversion mattered more than internal-load control, and a Kezar Lake, NH case study shows a 71% cut in *external* phosphorus load did not stop blooms because of persistent internal loading — the reverse problem, illustrating that diagnosing which loading pathway (internal vs. external) dominates in a given lake is a prerequisite for choosing the right lever [ACAD-097; FED-077]. Dosing itself is not a settled, standardized calculation: different published Al-dosing methods applied to the same lake's sediment data can disagree by nearly an order of magnitude (45–306 g Al/m² across methods in one Thai lake study) [ACAD-102].

### Biomanipulation and other in-lake physical/biological controls
Biomanipulation — fish removal, piscivorous/planktivorous fish management, Daphnia/bivalve introduction, macrophyte re-introduction, or combinations — is a slower-acting, more context-sensitive nature-based alternative to chemical treatment. A review synthesizing 48 publications and 34 whole-lake/large-scale case studies found success or failure explained by fish-removal bottlenecks, stocking density, trophic-cascade effects, zooplankton grazing/diet shifts, macrophyte recovery, and nutrient/pH status — i.e., success is threshold- and context-dependent, and the authors state more data on cyanobacteria temporal dynamics before/after biomanipulation are needed before effectiveness can be properly evaluated [ACAD-016]. Physical controls (mixing, flushing) can reduce cyanobacterial biomass when water retention time is brought close to the cyanobacterial doubling time (on the order of days), but flushing has practical limits: it can simply relocate phosphorus downstream and is difficult to implement in large or irregularly shaped water bodies [ACAD-033; ACAD-070]. Deep, strong water-column mixing can suppress scum-forming cyanobacteria specifically, even though many cyanobacteria otherwise tolerate mixing well [NGO-007]. Popularly marketed "exotic" methods such as ultrasound and effective-microorganism products are explicitly flagged by a CYANOCOST editorial as lacking adequate scientific validation and not to be considered supported treatments [ACAD-111], consistent with the direct ultrasound failure documented experimentally [ACAD-042]. A whole-lake manipulation experiment demonstrated that halting nutrient loading, triggered by real-time statistical "resilience indicators" in chlorophyll/phycocyanin/dissolved-oxygen, reversed a developing cyanobacterial bloom within ~30 days — but the authors explicitly flag their manipulation as "idealized" (they could switch the nutrient driver on/off directly) relative to real management, where loading is diffuse/nonpoint and cannot simply be turned off, and where internal sediment loading can sustain eutrophication for decades after external loads are cut [ACAD-086]. All physical/chemical/biological suppression tactics, several sources agree, must be paired with external nutrient reduction to achieve durable control and can carry unintended ecosystem side effects (habitat alteration, biogeochemical/food-web change) when scaled to larger water bodies [ACAD-028; ACAD-070].

### Guideline values, advisory frameworks, and the toxin/biomass disconnect
The WHO's provisional drinking-water guideline value of 1 µg/L microcystin-LR (TDI 0.04 µg/kg bw/day), first set in 1998/2003 and reaffirmed with updated toxicology in 2020 (1 µg/L lifetime, 12 µg/L short-term, 24 µg/L recreational), anchors most national and state standards, with most of 17 countries surveyed adopting it, some as legally binding [ACAD-030; NGO-002; NGO-003]. The U.S. EPA issued non-enforceable drinking-water Health Advisories in 2015 (microcystins: 0.3 µg/L for infants/pre-schoolers, 1.6 µg/L for school-age/adults; cylindrospermopsin: 0.7 µg/L / 3.0 µg/L), derived from animal dose-response studies with 300–1000-fold uncertainty factors, and separate 2019 recreational-water criteria (8 µg/L microcystins, 15 µg/L cylindrospermopsin) [FED-013; FED-014; FED-015; FED-052; FED-032; FED-033]. Critically, there is **no U.S. federal enforceable standard** for cyanotoxins in either drinking or ambient water; regulatory uptake is fragmented, with only a small minority of states having drinking-water standards and roughly 21 of 50 having some recreational guidance [ACAD-030; FED-057; FED-013]. Actual state/tribal action thresholds for the same toxin and exposure route vary by roughly an order of magnitude or more (e.g., recreational microcystin "caution"/"warning" triggers ranging from 1 µg/L in California to an 8–2,000 µg/L single band in Nevada/Kansas), and some jurisdictions have no numeric threshold at all [FED-072; ACAD-030; FED-057]. This is compounded by a well-documented **decoupling between cyanobacteria biomass/cell-count and actual toxin concentration**: cyanobacteria cell-density or chlorophyll/phycocyanin proxies do not reliably predict toxin presence, because toxin production is strain- and condition-dependent, can occur at low cell densities, and can persist after a visible bloom is gone [FED-070; FED-032; SLG-005; ACAD-124]. Oregon's own retrospective data illustrate this directly: of 88 recreational advisories issued 2009–2014, 78 were triggered by cell-count exceedance and only 10 by measured toxin exceedance, and toxin-based monitoring demonstrably averted at least 13 advisories that cell-count-only monitoring would have triggered unnecessarily — while advisory duration was nearly identical either way (8.0 vs. 7.8 weeks) [ACAD-124]. In the opposite direction, the same Oregon dataset also documents blooms where measured microcystin reached 705 µg/L and 310 µg/L — tens to hundreds of times above the state's 10 µg/L human recreational guideline — showing cell-count screening did not systematically *underestimate* risk in those cases either [ACAD-124]. State/regulatory guidance therefore generally requires laboratory toxin confirmation, not visual/biomass observation alone, before issuing the most severe advisory tier [FED-032; SLG-002; SLG-005; SLG-006; SLG-009]. This dual cell-density/toxin-concentration alert-level structure traces back to WHO's original three-tier recreational framework (20,000 cells/mL or 10 µg/L chlorophyll-a for the lowest "Guidance Level," 100,000 cells/mL or 50 µg/L chlorophyll-a for the moderate tier, scum formation for the highest), which is still the template several current state frameworks build on [NGO-009; SLG-002; SLG-006]. States also set separate, often much stricter, "Do Not Drink" thresholds for finished drinking water than for recreational contact, and further distinguish sensitive sub-populations (infants, pregnant/nursing women, dialysis patients) and, in several states, dogs specifically, with dog-specific action levels commonly far below the corresponding human levels [SLG-004; FED-072; ACAD-124].

## Contested / uncertain / poorly-established

- **Does nutrient loading reliably predict bloom severity, or is the relationship inconsistent?** Several sources treat nutrient (P/N) load reduction as the most defensible long-term control lever [ACAD-028; ACAD-070; NGO-002; FED-039]. But a global, multi-decadal analysis cited within one review found that bloom exacerbation across most of 71 large lakes studied (1982–2012) "did not consistently track with previously hypothesized drivers," complicating nutrient reduction as a universal fix [ACAD-033]. A CYANOCOST editorial similarly cautions that evidence for a synergistic nutrient×temperature interaction is only "scant," despite the popular "blooms like it hot" framing it also cites approvingly [ACAD-111]. This is a correlational literature throughout — no source in this pack claims to have established a validated causal dose-response between nutrient load and bloom severity at lake scale; TMDL-style targets are built on simulation models correlating loading to algal-growth outcomes, not on causally validated field trials [FED-003].

- **Internal vs. external phosphorus loading as the dominant driver — highly site-specific, and government TMDLs may get the split wrong.** Some case studies (Cape Cod lakes, Hamblin Pond) find internal sediment loading dominant and alum treatment durably effective [ACAD-009; ACAD-038]. Others (West Twin Lake OH, Kezar Lake NH) find external loading dominant, such that internal-load control alone did not fix water quality, or external-load reduction alone did not stop blooms because of persistent internal loading [ACAD-038; FED-077]. A striking, source-internal finding: a real, MDEQ-issued TMDL's internal-loading estimate for Bear Lake, MI was found to be 3–7× too high relative to site-specific empirical measurement, which — left unchecked — would have misdirected management resources toward internal-load control rather than the external load the reanalysis identified as the more defensible lever [ACAD-097]. A Chautauqua Lake, NY TMDL's own model-derived internal-load estimate (55.1% of the South basin's total load) is explicitly a calculated *residual*, not a direct measurement [SLG-013]. Practitioners should not assume a government TMDL's internal/external split is ground truth without checking it against site-specific data.

- **Phosphorus-only vs. phosphorus-and-nitrogen control.** One review argues both N and P reduction are now needed given non-N2-fixing bloom genera and N-limited/co-limited systems [ACAD-028]. Yet the operational U.S. Lake Erie phosphorus-reduction strategy targets P alone, while its own cited literature (Gobler et al., as referenced in [FED-039] and [FED-074]) warns that P reduction without N reduction might shrink bloom *extent* without reducing bloom *toxicity* — a tension the plan itself surfaces but defers rather than resolves.

- **Copper algaecide dosing convention vs. new evidence.** The traditional, 40+ year old alkalinity-only copper dosing rule is being challenged by MLR (pH + DOC) dosing research showing it can be over-conservative on copper load while under-optimized for actual toxicity [ACAD-082; ACAD-136]. More strikingly, raising alkalinity was found to *increase* (not decrease) algal sensitivity to copper — the opposite of alkalinity's well-established *protective* effect on fish toxicity that the traditional formula is built around — meaning a dosing practice designed to be conservative for fish is not necessarily conservative for algal efficacy, and the mechanism is described only correlationally [ACAD-082]. This MLR approach is validated in only 14 data points at bioassay scale and explicitly bounded (not recommended below 50 mg/L CaCO3 alkalinity; not yet validated in lower-DOC/oligotrophic systems or a wider range of algal species) [ACAD-082].

- **Are "newer"/proprietary algaecide formulations less harmful to non-target organisms than legacy copper sulfate?** Marketing and some efficacy framing implies yes. Directly contradicting this, a chronic (21-day) Daphnia magna toxicity study found the novel formulation SeClear was *more* chronically toxic than traditional CuSO4·5H2O [ACAD-072]. This rests on a single peer-reviewed study but is a clear, quantified counter-finding to any blanket "newer = safer for non-target life" claim.

- **Treating cyanobacteria kill as a resolved bloom risk.** Multiple independent studies converge on the same structural warning: a treatment that visibly clears biomass (the kind of signal a chlorophyll/phycocyanin or satellite optical proxy would register as "bloom cleared") can coincide with, or even cause, a spike in dissolved, more bioavailable toxin — the opposite of what a biomass-only signal would suggest [ACAD-042; ACAD-118; ACAD-015; ACAD-116; FED-006; PVT-005; PVT-002]. This is consistent across chemical treatment classes (copper, H2O2) and is one of the most robust cross-source agreements in this category, though the *magnitude* of the effect (how much toxin release per unit of kill) varies by product and by water chemistry and is not fully generalizable from any single study.

- **Practitioner sentiment vs. controlled-trial evidence on treatment effectiveness.** A qualitative interview study of 31 U.S. water managers/researchers found many characterize surface-water chemical/physical treatments as "often the default response despite being ineffective, ecologically damaging, and not durable," and prefer prevention over treatment [ACAD-047]. This is *perception* data, not a controlled comparative efficacy result, and stands in some tension with the more favorable controlled-trial outcomes reported for specific products/protocols under specific conditions elsewhere in this pack (e.g., [ACAD-015; ACAD-082; ACAD-009]) — the discrepancy may reflect real-world deployment conditions (undersized doses, wrong timing, lack of monitoring triggers) differing from the controlled conditions of the trials, but this pack cannot confirm that explanation.

- **Wetland/BMP effectiveness timeframe.** A rigorous field study at Lake Macatawa found no detectable effect from purpose-built wetland BMPs after two years [ACAD-040], while national program-level narratives (H2Ohio, USDA NRCS) report farm-level/enrollment metrics as evidence of progress without an equivalent lake-scale, statistically-controlled before/after comparison in this pack [FED-037]. Whether 1–2 years is simply too short a window (as [ACAD-040] argues) or whether some BMP programs are underperforming even on longer timeframes is not resolved by the sources here.

- **Bibliographic/technical uncertainties flagged directly in the pack** (lower-stakes but worth noting for anyone citing these sources further): the biomanipulation review's journal volume/citation counts are inconsistently reported across indexing sources [ACAD-016]; a widely-repeated "494 → <10 µg/L in 9 days" microcystin figure for the Lake Okeechobee algaecide study could not be verified against the directly-fetched abstract text and is flagged low-confidence [ACAD-065]; and one H2O2 conference presentation has no retrievable methods or results at all, included here only for research-program context [ACAD-067].

## Gaps & open questions

- No source in this pack reports a randomized, controlled, multi-lake trial directly comparing chemical treatment, nutrient-load reduction, and biomanipulation head-to-head on lake-scale bloom/toxin outcomes; nearly all treatment-efficacy evidence is single-lake, single-product, laboratory/mesocosm-scale, or a retrospective before/after comparison, each with its own stated caveats about generalizability.
- The mechanism by which higher alkalinity increases (rather than decreases) algal sensitivity to copper is described only as an observed, unexplained correlation — no source offers a validated causal/biochemical explanation [ACAD-082].
- How much of any given chemical treatment's measured toxin-release differential (e.g., chelated vs. unchelated copper) is attributable to the chelating chemistry itself versus other confounded formulation/dosing differences is not isolated experimentally in this pack.
- The real-world scalability of resilience-indicator-triggered "early warning + halt nutrient loading" management is untested outside a small, fully-instrumented experimental whole-lake system where the researchers could switch the nutrient driver on/off directly — the authors themselves flag this as "idealized" relative to real, diffuse, nonpoint-source management situations [ACAD-086].
- No source in this pack quantifies how often, or by how much, legacy/internal phosphorus loading versus current external inputs dominates bloom risk across a broad, representative sample of lakes — each case study here is lake-specific, and several explicitly warn against generalizing their internal/external split to other systems [ACAD-097; FED-077; ACAD-038; SLG-013].
- Whether dual N+P nutrient reduction is required for durable, low-toxicity bloom control (versus P-only) remains an open, actively contested question that current major nutrient-management programs (e.g., Lake Erie) have not operationally resolved, only flagged for future research [ACAD-028; FED-039; FED-074].
- Cross-jurisdiction harmonization of cyanotoxin guideline values is unresolved: no source in this pack documents a scientific (rather than administrative/precautionary) basis for the wide (10–100×) variation in state/national action thresholds for ostensibly the same health endpoint [FED-072; ACAD-030; FED-057].
- The practical, field-scale efficacy of the "prophylactic treatment" (repeated low-dose, pre-bloom algicide) concept is proposed but not itself tested with a dedicated field trial in this pack — it is inferred from the mechanistic logic that pre-bloom biomass contains less toxin to release, not demonstrated directly [ACAD-033].
- Long-term ecosystem consequences of any given management choice (repeated copper accumulation and possible resistance selection; wetland/biomanipulation food-web shifts persisting years after intervention, as documented at Kezar Lake) are documented in isolated cases but not systematically studied across treatments or geographies [ACAD-033; FED-077].

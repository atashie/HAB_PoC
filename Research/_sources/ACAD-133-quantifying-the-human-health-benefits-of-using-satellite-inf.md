# Quantifying the Human Health Benefits of Using Satellite Information to Detect Cyanobacterial Harmful Algal Blooms and Manage Recreational Advisories in U.S. Lakes

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7446750/

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed "impact assessment" paper that builds an economic (cost-of-illness) framework to monetize the socioeconomic/human-health benefit of using the multiagency CyAN satellite system (EPA/NASA/USGS/NOAA) to detect cyanobacterial HABs and trigger recreational advisories, then applies that framework to a real 2017 cyanoHAB event on Utah Lake, Utah, comparing a satellite-informed "reference case" against a delayed-detection "counterfactual case."

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Satellite (CyAN) availability during the 2017 Utah Lake cyanoHAB event is estimated to have produced a central socioeconomic (human-health) benefit of approximately $370,000 (2017 USD), with a sensitivity-analysis range of $55,000–$1,057,000.
  - *evidence:* Derived by comparing modeled health-care costs avoided in the satellite-informed 'reference case' versus the delayed-detection 'counterfactual case'; range comes from varying delay time, exposure counts, symptom-case mix, and cost-per-case source. (Abstract; Section 5.5 (Estimate of Socioeconomic Benefits))
  - *quote:* "We find that in the counterfactual case, approximately $370,000 in health care costs were incurred as a result of cyanoHAB exposure... Our sensitivity analysis reveals a range in socioeconomic benefits of approximately $55,000 to $1,057,000 depending on our core assumptions and data choices."
- **[✓ verified]** The counterfactual (no-satellite-data) scenario assumes a ~7-day detection/advisory-posting delay, during which an estimated 8,000 people were exposed to the bloom at Utah Lake access points.
  - *evidence:* 7 days derived from time needed for symptom onset, self-attribution, complaint reporting, and advisory posting in the absence of remote detection; 8,000 derived from Utah Lake State Park visitor records plus Google Reviews-based estimates for other access points, adjusted for swim/boat/watersport participation rates. (Section 5.2 (Estimating Exposure to cyanoHAB))
  - *quote:* "Therefore, our final estimate of visitors that would have been exposed to the cyanoHAB in the counterfactual case is 8,000. ... We thus estimate that 7 days would serve as a reasonable exposure window."
- **[✓ verified]** Applying a 5% gastrointestinal (GI) symptom rate to the 8,000 exposed people yields roughly 200 mild GI cases and roughly 198–200 moderate-or-severe cases; of the moderate/severe group, about 30 are estimated to be severe.
  - *evidence:* 5% GI symptom rate taken from Stewart, Webb, Schluter, Fleming et al. (2006), a study of recreational users in Australia and Florida exposed to cyanobacterial toxins; the moderate/severe split further uses hospitalization/ED-visit rates from Hilborn et al. (2014). (Section 5.3 (Estimating Reduction in Human Health Impacts))
  - *quote:* "we project that approximately 200 people would have experienced mild symptoms and 200 people would have experienced moderate or severe symptoms... In 11 outbreaks that occurred between 2009 and 2010 in New York, Ohio, and Washington, the authors find that 61 people experienced symptoms, of which 3% required hospitalization and 12% went to some sort of emergency department... we estimate that of the 198 people who would have experienced moderate or severe symptoms, 30 would have experienced severe symptoms."
- **[✓ verified]** Cost per case of cyanoHAB-associated illness, adjusted to 2017 USD from DeFlorio-Barker et al. (2018), is $11.23 for mild cases, $264.38 for moderate cases, and $10,719.02 for severe cases (excluding mortality).
  - *evidence:* Primary cost-of-illness source is DeFlorio-Barker et al. (2018) on Americans exposed to contaminated recreational water; Henson et al. (2008, British Columbia) figures (mild $111.15, moderate $210.94, severe $905.80 in 2017 USD) are used as a secondary/validation source and in one sensitivity run. (Section 5.4 (Estimating Cost per Case of Illness))
  - *quote:* "Adjusted for inflation, mild, moderate, and severe cases cost $11.23, $264.38, and $10,719.02, respectively, in 2017 U.S. dollars."
- **[✓ verified]** CyAN estimates cyanobacterial cell density (cells/mL) via the Cyanobacteria Index algorithm using MERIS (historically, on Envisat) and, from 2016 onward, Sentinel-3 OLCI data — it is a proxy for bloom presence, not a direct toxin measurement.
  - *evidence:* Describes the remote-sensing pipeline underlying CyAN and clarifies that satellite output must still be paired with in-situ/lab toxin confirmation before a health advisory of a given severity is posted. (Section 2.2 (Background: the Cyanobacteria Assessment Network))
  - *quote:* "CyAN uses the Cyanobacteria Index algorithm as described in Lunetta et al. (2015), converted to an estimate of cyanobacterial cells per milliliter of water, derived from the European Space Agency Medium Resolution Imaging Spectrometer (MERIS) on the Envisat satellite and transferable to the Sentinel-3 Ocean Land Colour Instrument (OLCI) from 2016 onward."
- **[✓ verified]** National-scale cyanotoxin prevalence appears to be increasing: EPA's National Lakes Assessment detected microcystin in 39% of U.S. lakes in 2012, a 9.5 percentage-point increase from 2007.
  - *evidence:* Cited as background motivation for why satellite-based detection/monitoring capacity matters at national scale. (Section 2 (Background on cyanoHABs))
  - *quote:* "The National Lakes Assessment, a statistical survey of the condition of lakes, ponds, and reservoirs in the United States, detected microcystin in 39% of lakes in the United States in 2012—a 9.5% increase from 2007."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors estimate the CyAN program's ongoing annual operating cost at roughly $141,000/year, covering data storage/processing (~200 GB/year at $0.40/GB) and two GS-12-equivalent scientist salaries (~$70,000/year each) — but this excludes satellite launch and sensor manufacturing costs.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a rough program-cost benchmark to contextualize (not directly net against) the $370,000 estimated benefit; the authors do not compute a formal benefit-cost ratio in the extracted text. (Section 6 (Conclusion))
  - *quote:* "Assuming that about 200 Gigabytes of data need to be stored and processed per year...at a rate of $0.40 per Gigabyte and maintaining and updating the data repository requires two GS-12 equivalent scientists (at a rate of $70,000 per year), the cost for supplying a year's worth of satellite data is on the order of $141,000."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors state several explicit limitations of their own estimate: full advisory compliance was (unrealistically) assumed, pet/livestock harms were excluded, lost recreational-economy/tourism revenue was excluded, and respiratory-symptom costs were excluded as not cleanly comparable in the literature.
  - *evidence:* Self-reported scope limitations placed in the results/discussion, narrowing what the $370,000 figure actually represents. (Section 5.3 and Section 5.5 (Discussion of assumptions/limitations))
  - *quote:* "Third, we assumed full compliance with the posted recreational advisories, which is unlikely. ... Second, we did not consider social damages to pets and livestock as a result of the cyanoHAB. ... Finally, we did not calculate the costs to the local recreational economy associated with recreational advisories that limit lake access. ... we did not examine the societal benefits of avoiding respiratory symptoms because the cost estimates in the literature for treating this class of symptoms are often associated with illnesses such as lung disease or asthma, which are much more costly to treat than the illness associated with cyanoHAB exposure."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors explicitly caution that the Utah Lake dollar estimate is not generalizable: Utah Lake's unusually high visitor numbers likely make its benefit estimate higher than a 'typical' U.S. lake, and cross-lake variation in bloom/use characteristics prevents national extrapolation.
  - *evidence:* A direct external-validity caveat on the headline $370,000 figure. (Section 6 (Conclusion))
  - *quote:* "Utah Lake is a massive recreational destination with thousands of annual visitors. The benefits of satellite data found in this study are not representative of most lakes in the country and are likely higher than the typical lake experiencing a toxic cyanoHAB due to the number of affected persons. ... Significant variation in water use, bloom frequency, bloom spatial extent, bloom magnitude, toxin concentration, access to satellite data, and the degree to which satellite data inform management decisions prevents us from projecting our results to a national scale."
- **[✓ verified]** As of 2019, Utah shifted to permanent, year-round posted cyanoHAB warnings at Utah Lake regardless of detection timing, which the authors say reduces the future decision-relevant value of satellite-based early detection specifically at that lake.
  - *evidence:* A policy-context caveat: the value the paper measures is tied to a specific 2017 decision context (advisories timed to detection) that has since changed for this lake. (Section 6 (Conclusion))
  - *quote:* "As of 2019, environmental managers in Utah have decided to permanently post cyanoHAB warning advisories around Utah Lake, warning visitors not to swim or participate in water sports if they see signs of cyanobacteria. This decreases the potential value of future satellite detection of cyanoHABs, as it critically alters the decision context."

## Data / numbers
- Central estimate: ~$370,000 (2017 USD) in socioeconomic/human-health benefit from satellite-enabled early detection, 2017 Utah Lake cyanoHAB event
- Sensitivity range: $55,000–$1,057,000 (2017 USD) around the central estimate
- Counterfactual exposure window: 7 days (range tested in sensitivity analysis: 2–20 days)
- Estimated people exposed in counterfactual case: 8,000 (sensitivity analysis also tested 4,000 and 9,000)
- GI symptom rate applied: 5% of exposed recreators (from Stewart et al. 2006, Australia/Florida)
- Estimated case counts (counterfactual, 8,000 exposed): ~200 mild GI cases; ~198–200 moderate-or-severe GI cases, of which ~30 estimated severe
- Cost per case (2017 USD, DeFlorio-Barker et al. 2018 basis): mild $11.23; moderate $264.38; severe $10,719.02 (excludes death)
- Cost per case (2017 USD, Henson et al. 2008 British Columbia basis, secondary/sensitivity source): mild $111.15; moderate $210.94; severe $905.80
- Hilborn et al. (2014): 11 documented cyanoHAB outbreaks, 2009–2010, NY/OH/WA; 61 total illness cases; 3% hospitalized; 12% emergency department visits; 0 deaths
- National Lakes Assessment: microcystin detected in 39% of U.S. lakes (2012) vs. ~29.5% (2007), a 9.5 percentage-point increase
- Estimated CyAN annual operating cost: ~$141,000/year (~200 GB/year data storage/processing at $0.40/GB + 2 GS-12 scientists at ~$70,000/year each; excludes satellite/sensor capital costs)
- 2016 Utah Lake bloom (context, not the study event): 225 Utah Poison Control calls on July 15 peak day; ~300 additional calls over following weeks; ~3-day time-to-advisory; 23–32% of callers reporting symptoms (self-selected sample)
- 2014 Toledo, OH drinking-water advisory (context): ~500,000 residents affected, 3-day advisory, 110 CDC-documented illness cases
- 2013 Carroll Township, OH (context): 2,000 residents under a 'Do Not Drink' advisory
- Environmental Working Group (2019): over 500 algal bloom events reported in the U.S. in 2019 (not all cyanotoxin-confirmed or recreation-impacting)

## Methods
Builds a benefit-transfer / cost-of-illness "impact assessment" framework that monetizes the value of satellite-based cyanoHAB detection by comparing a satellite-informed "reference case" (immediate detection and advisory posting) to a "counterfactual case" (no satellite data, ~7-day detection/advisory delay estimated from symptom-onset and reporting time) for a documented 2017 Utah Lake cyanoHAB event first flagged by CyAN (Sentinel-3 OLCI / Cyanobacteria Index) on June 20, 2017. Exposure counts were estimated from Utah Lake State Park visitor records and Google Reviews of other access points, combined with activity-participation rates (swimming, boating, watersports). GI-illness incidence (5%) was taken from Stewart et al. (2006); illness-severity split (moderate vs. severe) used hospitalization/ED-visit rates from Hilborn et al.'s (2014) analysis of 11 U.S. cyanoHAB outbreaks (2009-2010, NY/OH/WA). Cost-of-illness figures came primarily from DeFlorio-Barker et al. (2018), cross-checked against Henson et al. (2008, British Columbia) as a secondary source and sensitivity scenario. A multi-parameter one-at-a-time sensitivity analysis (Table 3) varied the delay time, exposed population, case-severity mix, and cost source. The same descriptive framework (without full independent quantification) is also discussed for CyAN-informed advisory decisions in Ohio (NOAA's Lake Erie cyanoHAB Forecasting Bulletin) and California (SWAMP portal), and contrasted with Florida's Karenia brevis respiratory-illness cost literature (Hoagland et al. 2009). The paper states the framework works where a documented, dated bloom event with known advisory-timing counterfactual and available visitor/exposure data exist (as at Utah Lake), but that it does not attempt to translate to a national-scale benefit estimate given cross-lake heterogeneity in bloom frequency, extent, toxin levels, and management response.

## Stated limitations
The authors themselves state: (1) full compliance with posted recreational advisories was assumed, which they call "unlikely"; (2) social/economic damages to pets and livestock from cyanoHAB exposure were not quantified due to insufficient exposure data, despite documented fatal risk; (3) lost local recreational-economy/tourism revenue tied to advisory postings was not calculated; (4) respiratory-symptom costs were excluded because available literature conflates them with unrelated, more expensive chronic conditions (asthma/lung disease); (5) the ~$141,000/year CyAN "program cost" benchmark excludes satellite launch and sensor manufacturing costs, covering only data storage/repository maintenance and two analysts' salaries; (6) the $370,000 (and $55,000-$1,057,000 range) result is explicitly said to be "not representative of most lakes in the country" and "likely higher than the typical lake" because of Utah Lake's unusually high visitation; (7) "significant variation in water use, bloom frequency, bloom spatial extent, bloom magnitude, toxin concentration, access to satellite data, and the degree to which satellite data inform management decisions" prevents projecting the result to a national scale; (8) CyAN itself only estimates cyanobacterial cell density and "cannot detect toxins or determine if toxin concentrations pose human health risk," so lab/in-situ confirmation remains necessary; (9) as of 2019 Utah adopted permanent, year-round cyanoHAB advisories at Utah Lake regardless of detection timing, which the authors say "decreases the potential value of future satellite detection of cyanoHABs" at that specific lake going forward, i.e., the estimated benefit is tied to a 2017 decision context that has since partly changed.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper is one of the few sources that puts an explicit, monetized dollar figure on the value of earlier (satellite-based) HAB detection versus a delayed-detection counterfactual — directly relevant to the HAB PoC's "timing & targeting matter most" framing and to constructing a defensible product claim about the value of an early-warning tool. However, it also complicates any such claim in three ways relevant to this project: (1) the authors' own admission that the benefit is event- and lake-specific and "not representative of most lakes," which argues against extrapolating a single dollar figure as a general product claim; (2) the CyAN sensor's inherent limitation — it detects cyanobacterial cell density, not toxin concentration, so a satellite-only "risk readout" cannot on its own confirm human-health risk without in-situ/lab toxin follow-up, a caveat any tool built on remote-sensing fusion should carry explicitly; and (3) the paper's own note that where managers move to permanent, precautionary advisories (as Utah did in 2019), the incremental decision-value of early satellite detection can shrink — a structural tension for any tool whose value proposition rests on advisory-timing benefits, since that value is contingent on the receiving jurisdiction's existing advisory policy, not a fixed universal quantity.

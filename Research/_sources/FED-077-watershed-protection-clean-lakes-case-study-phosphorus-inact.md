# Watershed Protection: Clean Lakes Case Study: Phosphorus Inactivation and Wetland Manipulation Improve Kezar Lake, NH

**What it is.** A U.S. EPA Clean Lakes Program case study (EPA 841-F-95-002, EPA Office of Water, published circa 1995) describing a New Hampshire Department of Environmental Services-led restoration of Kezar Lake, NH, that combined an in-lake aluminum-salt (alum) phosphorus-inactivation treatment with upstream Chadwick Meadows wetland water-level manipulation, and that reports roughly 14 years (1980-1994) of before/after water-quality monitoring, costs, and outcomes, including an explicit account of the treatment's benefits fading within a few years.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[⚠ partial]** Eliminating most of the external (point-source) phosphorus load did not stop Kezar Lake's algal blooms, implicating internal (sediment) phosphorus loading as the sustaining cause and the rationale for an alum-based, sediment-targeting treatment.
  - *evidence:* The source states that closing/rerouting the wastewater facility eliminated 71% of the external phosphorus load, yet frames the persistent blooms and later treatment rationale around addressing phosphorus already resident in the sediments. (Background/history narrative (facility closure discussion))

  - *reviewer:* The source does not explicitly state that blooms persisted after external load elimination. While the timeline (facility closure 1981, treatment 1984) and treatment rationale (inactivating sediment phosphorus) imply this, the causal link is inferred rather than stated outright in the source text.
- **[✓ verified]** Kezar Lake is a small, shallow, dimictic, phosphorus-limited lake with a well-defined morphometry used to frame the whole restoration analysis.
  - *evidence:* The source gives a precise morphometric profile (depth, volume, flushing rate, elevation), attributing the lake-type characterization itself to an underlying technical report. (Lake and watershed characteristics section)

## Methods
Field-scale phosphorus inactivation via aluminum salts (aluminum sulfate plus sodium aluminate), applied with a custom-modified weed harvester rather than a barge, preceded by a copper sulfate algicide application intended to release phytoplankton-bound phosphorus; the source describes pilot jar/tank studies preceding a 10-ha lower-dose application and a larger 48-ha, 40 g Al/m3 application in 1984 (per Table 1). Paired with a watershed/wetland BMP: raising water levels at the 20-ha Chadwick Meadows wetland via flashboards installed in 1983, and planting wild rice (Zinzania/Zizania aquatica) in 1985-1986 alongside native blue-joint grass (Calamagrostis canadensis) to increase phosphorus sedimentation and vegetative uptake. Evaluation drew on roughly 14 years (1980-1994) of monitoring: monthly tributary and wetland-outlet phosphorus/flow measurements (1984-1988); lake sampling of dissolved oxygen, pH, alkalinity, dissolved aluminum, total phosphorus, chlorophyll-a, transparency, phytoplankton and zooplankton; sediment core analysis; hydrologic-budget and mass-balance modeling; and trophic-state classification via three separate models (State of New Hampshire, EPA, and Dillon-Rigler), continuing into a Phase III post-restoration monitoring effort (sediment-aluminum leaching assessment, fish-tissue aluminum comparisons in horned pout and yellow perch versus control lakes, macroinvertebrate diversity/density comparisons) and a citizen volunteer monitoring program after 1988. As reported by the source, the alum/harvester method 'worked' in that it produced multi-year (roughly 2-4 years, parameter-dependent per Table 2) improvements in dissolved oxygen, pH, alkalinity, total phosphorus, chlorophyll-a, transparency and phytoplankton, at lower per-hectare labor cost than the barge-based comparator lakes shown in Table 1, with no detected water-column impact from dissolved aluminum. The wetland-manipulation method is reported as largely underperforming its phosphorus-removal goal outside the summer growing season (working well only seasonally, and potentially reversing to a phosphorus source in high-flow periods), and the combined treatment's water-quality benefits are reported as time-limited rather than permanent, with four candidate (unconfirmed, hedged) mechanisms offered for the eventual partial rebound.

## Stated limitations
The source itself hedges its main result: "Initial success was realized following treatment, but within 4 years many parameters returned to near pretreatment levels, although this change may be due to meteorologic variability" - i.e., it does not claim the observed rebound is unambiguously attributable to treatment failure rather than climate. It explicitly reports the wetland-manipulation component as underperforming: "the overall effectiveness of Chadwick Meadows in removing phosphorus from Lion Brook was poor," with wetlands "might not attenuate or might even be a source of phosphorus in the fall and spring during periods of high flow" - i.e., the stated >80% removal benefit is explicitly bounded to the summer growing season only. The four proposed explanations for the water-quality rebound are presented in hedged language ("indicated the possibility," "may have migrated," "perhaps as a result of") rather than as confirmed mechanisms, and the source does not claim to have determined which (if any) is correct. The source also flags precipitation/climate as a rival, uncontrolled explanation for year-to-year water-quality swings in the post-restoration period (citing 1988, 1993 as anomalously wet/poor years and 1995 as an anomalously dry/clear year), explicitly complicating any simple attribution of outcomes to the treatment alone.

## Tensions with other findings
This case study complicates any HAB framing that treats current external nutrient loading (e.g., watershed land-use or point-source proxies, including those derivable from remote sensing/land-cover data) as sufficient to explain bloom risk: here a 71% cut in external phosphorus load did not stop blooms because of internal/legacy sediment phosphorus, implying models built only on contemporaneous external-loading signals could miss a dominant driver in P-legacy lakes. It also complicates a "treatment solves the problem" narrative: the alum treatment's benefits were explicitly time-limited (roughly 2-4 years by parameter per Table 2) rather than permanent, supporting a "bounded action window requiring re-monitoring/re-treatment" framing rather than a one-time fix - directly relevant to how a treatment-recommendation tool should calibrate its claims. Separately, the source cannot cleanly separate treatment effect from precipitation/climate effect on year-to-year water quality (explicitly naming 1988, 1993, and 1995 as climate-driven exceptions), which is the same correlation-vs-causation confound that recurs across HAB driver literature and argues for including weather/precipitation covariates in any treatment-evaluation model. Finally, it complicates a general assumption (sometimes implicit in watershed-BMP literature) that wetland restoration/manipulation reliably reduces nutrient loading: here the same wetland is reported as a poor net phosphorus sink overall, effective only seasonally, and a possible phosphorus source during high-flow periods - a caution against treating wetland BMPs as unconditionally beneficial.

# Drinking Water Health Advisory for the Cyanobacterial Microcystin Toxins (EPA 820R15100)

**What it is.** A non-regulatory EPA Drinking Water Health Advisory (Office of Water, Health and Ecological Criteria Division; EPA 820R15100, dated June 15, 2015) that derives Ten-day Health-Advisory concentration levels for microcystin toxins in drinking water from animal dose-response/toxicological data, and surveys microcystin chemistry, occurrence, environmental fate, health effects, risk characterization, analytical methods, and drinking-water treatment technologies.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** EPA set non-enforceable Ten-day Health Advisory levels for microcystins in drinking water: 0.3 µg/L for bottle-fed infants and young children of pre-school age, and 1.6 µg/L for school-age children through adults.
  - *evidence:* Both values use the same underlying toxicity data/Reference Dose but apply different age-specific body-weight and drinking-water-intake assumptions (infant time-weighted intake/body-weight ratios vs. adult mean body weight and 90th-percentile intake from EPA's 2011a Exposure Factors Handbook). (Executive Summary, p.1-2 (see also Sections 4.9-4.10 / Figure 4-2 per table of contents))
  - *quote:* "The Ten-day HA value for bottle-fed infants and young children of pre-school age is 0.3 µg/L and for school-age children through adults is 1.6 µg/L for microcystins."
- **[✓ verified]** The advisory's toxicity basis is a single critical study — Heinze (1999), a 28-day rat drinking-water study — identifying a LOAEL of 50 µg/kg/day for microcystin-LR based on increased liver weight, liver lesions with hemorrhage, and elevated serum liver enzymes.
  - *evidence:* No NOAEL was identified in this dataset, so EPA extrapolated from the LOAEL using an added uncertainty factor; effects are also tabulated in Table 3-1 (liver weights/serum enzymes) and Table 3-2 (histological evaluation). (Executive Summary, p.1; Tables 3-1 and 3-2, p.27; Section 3.2)
  - *quote:* "The lowest-observed-adverse-effect level (LOAEL) was determined to be 50 μg/kg/day, based on these effects."
- **[✓ verified]** EPA applied a composite (total) uncertainty factor of 1000 to the LOAEL to derive the Reference Dose.
  - *evidence:* Composed of four factors: 10x intraspecies variability, 10x interspecies (animal-to-human) extrapolation, ~3x for LOAEL-to-NOAEL extrapolation, and ~3x for database deficiencies. (Executive Summary, p.1-2; Section 3.2.3 (Uncertainty Factor Application))
  - *quote:* "This was based on a UF of 10 for intraspecies variability, a UF of 10 for interspecies variability, a UF of 3 (10½) for extrapolation from a LOAEL to no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL), and a UF of 3 (10½) to account for deficiencies in the database."
- **[⚠ partial]** EPA uses microcystin-LR as a surrogate toxicant for total microcystins (roughly 100 known congeners) in setting this advisory, since MC-LR is the most potent and most-studied congener.
  - *evidence:* Stated as an explicit modeling choice in the executive summary and given its own risk-characterization subsection (4.1). (Executive Summary, p.1; Section 4.1, p.31)
  - *quote:* "EPA is using microcystin-LR as a surrogate for other microcystin congeners. Therefore, the HA based on this critical study applies to total microcystins."
  - *reviewer:* Claim states MC-LR is 'the most potent congener' but source states it is 'one of the most potent congeners' — an overstatement of its relative potency rank.
- **[✓ verified]** EPA states there is inadequate information to assess the carcinogenic potential of microcystins under its 2005 cancer-risk-assessment guidelines.
  - *evidence:* Attributed to epidemiological studies limited by design/exposure measurement/confounding and the absence of long-term animal tumorigenicity dose-response studies; promotion studies showed increased GST-P-positive foci, but microcystin-LR alone showed no initiating activity in two promotion studies. (Executive Summary, p.2; Section 4.6 (Carcinogenicity Evaluation), p.33 per table of contents)
  - *quote:* "Applying the U.S. EPA (2005) Guidelines for Carcinogen Risk Assessment, there is inadequate information to assess carcinogenic potential of microcystins."
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystins in surface water are reported to have a half-life of roughly 4 to 14 days, and cyanobacterial blooms are typically seasonal (May through October), so EPA does not expect chronic (year-round) drinking-water exposure.
  - *evidence:* Half-life range is linked to sunlight exposure, natural organic matter, and bacterial presence, and is used to justify a short-term (Ten-day) rather than lifetime advisory framing. (Executive Summary, p.2)
  - *quote:* "Microcystins typically have a half-life of 4 days to 14 days in surface waters."
- **[✓ verified]** As of this document's publication, no U.S. federal water-quality criteria or enforceable regulations existed for cyanobacteria/cyanotoxins under the Safe Drinking Water Act or the Clean Water Act; microcystin-LR (with anatoxin-a and cylindrospermopsin) was on EPA's Contaminant Candidate List 3 (2009) and draft CCL4 (April 2015) as a candidate for future regulatory determination.
  - *evidence:* This Health Advisory is explicitly offered as non-regulatory, informal technical guidance to fill that regulatory gap. (Section 1.1, p.3)
  - *quote:* "Currently there are no U.S. federal water quality criteria, or regulations for cyanobacteria or cyanotoxins in drinking water under the SDWA or in ambient waters under the Clean Water Act (CWA)."
- **[✓ verified]** International and state drinking-water guideline values for microcystins (lifetime-exposure basis) cluster between 1.0 and 1.5 µg/L, anchored to a WHO provisional guideline of 1.0 µg/L microcystin-LR (TDI 0.04 µg/kg) adopted as a national-standard basis by 16 countries; among U.S. states, values found in the document range from 0.04 µg/L (Minnesota) to 1 µg/L (Ohio, Oregon).
  - *evidence:* Tables 1-1 and 1-2 tabulate international and state guideline values respectively; Australia (1.3 µg/L) and Canada (1.5 µg/L) adapted the WHO TDI using their own body-weight/consumption assumptions, reaching somewhat higher values. (Section 1.1, Tables 1-1 and 1-2, pp.4-5)
  - *quote:* "Values are similar across all countries, ranging between 1.0 and 1.5 μg/L based on lifetime exposures."
- **[✓ verified]** The main historical human health-effects evidence base for microcystins is acute recreational-exposure symptom reporting, plus a documented case series of intravenous exposure via microcystin-contaminated dialysate that caused acute liver failure and death in a large number of exposed patients; oral (ingestion) toxicity data in humans are limited and confounded.
  - *evidence:* Recreational-exposure symptoms listed: headache, sore throat, vomiting/nausea, stomach pain, dry cough, diarrhea, perioral blistering, pneumonia. Oral human data explicitly called limited and confounded by co-exposures and lack of quantitative exposure information. (Executive Summary, p.1)
  - *quote:* "Reports of human intravenous exposure to dialysate prepared with microcystin-contaminated water indicated acute liver failure and death in a large number of the exposed patients."
- **[✓ verified]** This Health Advisory is explicitly non-regulatory: it is not a legally enforceable standard and confers no legal rights or obligations.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as a caveat on the numeric advisory values, and reiterated generally for the EPA Health Advisory Program (all HAs 'subject to change as new information becomes available'). (Executive Summary, p.2; Section 1.0, p.3)
  - *quote:* "This HA is not a regulation, it is not legally enforceable, and it does not confer legal rights or impose legal obligations on any party."

## Data / numbers
- LOAEL = 50 µg/kg/day (microcystin-LR, oral, rat, Heinze 1999 28-day study) — critical-effect basis for the RfD
- Composite uncertainty factor = 1000 (10x intraspecies x 10x interspecies x ~3x LOAEL-to-NOAEL x ~3x database-deficiency)
- Ten-day Health Advisory = 0.3 µg/L, bottle-fed infants/pre-school children (EPA, 2015)
- Ten-day Health Advisory = 1.6 µg/L, school-age children through adults (EPA, 2015)
- WHO provisional guideline = 1.0 µg/L microcystin-LR, drinking water, lifetime exposure basis; underlying TDI = 0.04 µg/kg (WHO 1999, 2003)
- Australia guideline = 1.3 µg/L MC-LR toxicity equivalents (NHMRC/NRMMC, 2011)
- Canada guideline = 1.5 µg/L MC-LR (Health Canada, 2002)
- Minnesota state guideline = 0.04 µg/L MC-LR (MDH, 2012)
- Ohio state guideline = 1 µg/L microcystin (Ohio EPA, 2014)
- Oregon state guideline = 1 µg/L MC-LR (OHA, 2015)
- International guideline range = 1.0-1.5 µg/L (lifetime exposure), adopted in 16 countries following the WHO provisional guideline's release
- WHO recreational-water advisory guideline referenced for comparison = 10 µg/L microcystin (used as the threshold in the 2007 National Lakes Assessment comparison, per U.S. EPA 2009)
- Microcystin half-life in surface water = 4-14 days (depends on sunlight, natural organic matter, bacterial presence)
- Typical cyanobacterial bloom season = May through October
- ~100 microcystin congeners identified (structural variants)
- EPA Health Advisory Program covers 213 contaminants total (as of this document)
- 18 countries plus 3 U.S. states had drinking-water guideline values for microcystins as of this document (2015)

## Methods
EPA's standard non-cancer Health Advisory derivation: select a critical study (Heinze 1999, a 28-day rat oral/drinking-water bioassay) and critical effect (hepatic changes: increased liver weight, liver lesions/hemorrhage, elevated serum enzymes; Tables 3-1/3-2); identify a LOAEL (no NOAEL was available in this dataset); apply a composite uncertainty factor (1000x) to derive a Reference Dose (RfD); convert the RfD to age-specific drinking-water concentrations using EPA's Exposure Factors Handbook (2011a) body-weight and 90th-percentile drinking-water-intake distributions, yielding separate values for infants/pre-school children versus school-age-through-adult populations. Microcystin-LR is used throughout as a health-protective surrogate for total microcystins (~100 congeners). The source states this framework is applied for a short-term (Ten-day) rather than lifetime advisory because 7-30-day critical-study durations "typically" match that HA category and because bloom/exposure timing is seasonal rather than chronic. The same framework is explicitly NOT extended to a quantitative cancer risk estimate because available epidemiological and animal carcinogenicity data are judged inadequate. The table of contents also lists benchmark-dose (BMD) modeling, allometric scaling, and separate Analytical Methods (Sec. 5) and Drinking Water Treatment Technology chapters (Sec. 6: conventional treatment, adsorption, chemical oxidation, other filtration, combined technologies, point-of-use units) — but that body text falls outside the windowed excerpt supplied here, so no specific method-performance figures from those sections are reported in this dossier entry.

## Stated limitations
The source itself states: (1) human oral-toxicity data are "limited" and confounded "by: potential co-exposure to other contaminants; a lack of quantitative information; and other confounding factors"; (2) non-ingestion exposure routes (fish consumption, inhalation/dermal contact during recreation) "cannot be quantified at this time"; (3) chronic-toxicity animal studies "have not shown clinical signs of toxicity" but "are limited by study design and by the lack of quantitative data"; (4) no NOAEL was available for the critical effect, forcing a LOAEL-to-NOAEL extrapolation with an added uncertainty factor; (5) carcinogenic potential cannot be assessed — "inadequate information," epidemiological studies are "limited by their study design, poor measures of exposure, potential co-exposure to other contaminants, and the lack of control for confounding factors," and no long-term/lifetime animal dose-response studies exist; (6) the HA is explicitly non-regulatory — "not a regulation," "not legally enforceable," confers no legal rights/obligations, and "subject to change as new information becomes available."

## Tensions with other findings
The document frames bloom-favoring environmental conditions (nitrogen:phosphorus ratio, temperature, organic matter availability, light attenuation, pH) as factors that merely "play an important role" and "could encourage" toxin production — an explicitly correlational, non-quantified statement rather than a predictive or causal model; a HAB tool citing these as "drivers" should not overstate this into forecasting-grade causality. Second, the numeric guideline values surveyed here are not mutually comparable despite similar magnitudes: EPA's own Ten-day (short-term) HA (0.3/1.6 µg/L) rests on a different exposure duration than the WHO/Canada/Australia/state values (1.0-1.5 µg/L), which are lifetime-exposure-based, and both differ again from the WHO recreational-water guideline (10 µg/L) referenced via the 2007 National Lakes Assessment comparison — any secondary source quoting "the microcystin threshold" without specifying exposure route/duration/population risks conflating these distinct EPA/WHO/state figures. Third, the source states cyanotoxin exposure "is not expected to be chronic" because blooms are seasonal (May-October) in the datasets it relied on; this framing should be checked against, and could be complicated by, any source describing longer, more persistent, or shifting bloom seasons (e.g., in warmer climates or under nutrient loading trends) rather than assumed to hold universally.

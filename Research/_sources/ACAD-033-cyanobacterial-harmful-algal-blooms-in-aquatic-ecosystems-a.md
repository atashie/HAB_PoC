# Cyanobacterial Harmful Algal Blooms in Aquatic Ecosystems: A Comprehensive Outlook on Current and Emerging Mitigation and Control Approaches

**What it is.** A 2021 critical (narrative) literature review in the peer-reviewed journal Microorganisms (Sukenik & Kaplan) surveying current and emerging approaches to prevent, mitigate, and control cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (cyanoHABs), organized around three domains: prevention of bloom development, in-lake/reservoir treatment, and drinking/potable water treatment, and proposing a novel "prophylactic treatment" management concept.

## Data / numbers
- Global bloom trend dataset underlying the paper's claim of intensification: satellite-derived peak summertime cyanobacterial bloom intensity assessed across 71 large lakes worldwide, 1982–2012 (Ho et al., as cited)
- Copper sulfate field application dose: 1–4 g/m² (Lake Nanhu, China; encapsulated, slow-release form)
- Alum (aluminum sulfate) treatment of a hypereutrophic lake: dose as high as 84 g/m², producing 70% reduction in chlorophyll-a content and 95% reduction in total phosphate concentration
- Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) threshold lethal dose for Microcystis sp.: ≥4 mg/L (specific number given as '4 mg/L')
- Dual-dose H2O2 protocol: two consecutive sub-lethal H2O2 doses given 1–6 h apart were more lethal to Microcystis sp. strain MGK than one single dose of equivalent total concentration
- Lumichrome (metabolite of Aeromonas veronii) inhibitory concentration: as low as 4 nM severely inhibited growth of Microcystis sp. MGK
- Cylindrospermopsin (CYN): extracellular fraction may account for ~50% of total toxin (unlike other cyanotoxins, which are described as predominantly intracellular)
- Activated carbon used for toxin adsorption: specific surface area 400–1,500 m2/g; mesoporous carbon (pore size 2–50 nm) described as more effective than micro/macroporous carbon for microcystin adsorption
- ODTMA-bentonite nano-composite: reduced microcystins (MCs) in water to below 1 microgram/L via filtration
- Roodeplaat Dam, South Africa field trial: visible bloom reduction in satellite imagery by 10 days after application of a floating, H2O2-releasing composition
- Polyaluminum chloride coagulation: transiently reduced surface cyanobacterial chlorophyll-a by 90%, with floatation/recovery following shortly after
- Microcystin congener susceptibility order to chlorination (relative, no absolute rate given): MC-YR > MC-RR > MC-LR > MC-LA

## Methods
This is a critical (narrative, not systematic/PRISMA) literature review, not a primary data-collection study. The authors synthesize published findings across three domains they set as the paper's scope: (1) prevention of bloom development (early detection technologies, nutrient-load management, hydrological manipulation), (2) in-lake/reservoir treatments (physical/hydrophysical control, chemical algicides and oxidants, coagulation/flocculation, and emerging biological/allelopathic approaches), and (3) drinking/potable water treatment (intake management, on-line pretreatment, coagulation and filtration, membrane filtration, and toxin adsorption/oxidation). Evidence drawn on includes: a cited global satellite bloom-trend analysis (Ho et al., 71 lakes, 1982-2012); laboratory dose-response experiments on Microcystis sp. strain MGK for H2O2 and lumichrome; and multiple field/case-study deployments with before-after satellite or photographic imagery (Lake Tai and Lake Nanhu, China; Dianchi/Dian Lake, China; Roodeplaat Dam, South Africa; four small lakes in New York State). The authors state where approaches work well (e.g., peroxide-based treatments are called out as targeting cyanobacteria's differential oxidative-stress sensitivity 'while leaving no chemical residues') and where methods are unproven or fail at scale (e.g., advanced oxidation processes are said to need further work before practical application; nutrient-reduction's link to bloom intensity is called inconsistent across lakes; membrane toxin-rejection mechanisms are called not fully understood).

## Stated limitations
The review states numerous method-specific limitations rather than a single overarching limitations section: (1) Nutrient-load reduction — bloom exacerbation "did not consistently track with previously hypothesized drivers" across most lakes analyzed, and reduced N/P availability may favor N2-fixing, CYN-producing filamentous cyanobacteria. (2) Hydrological manipulation — relocates phosphorus downstream rather than eliminating it, may be infeasible for large/irregular water bodies, and requires very high inflow rates. (3) Biomass harvesting — risks contaminating groundwater with cyanotoxins from accumulated biomass and may simply free space for regrowth in nutrient-rich lakes. (4) Lanthanum-modified bentonite — P reduction is sometimes only transient, and lanthanum has documented adverse effects on plants, phytoplankton, zooplankton, and fish. (5) Dredging — cost currently restricts it to small water bodies. (6) Ultrasonic/hydrostatic-pressure treatment — damaged cells can repair themselves and potentially rebuild gas vesicles in the dark; non-target algae and zooplankton can be affected; energy consumption and effective distance are constraining. (7) Copper sulfate — accumulates in sediment and can re-release, can select for copper-resistant cyanobacteria, and faces growing public/regulatory resistance. (8) H2O2/peroxide treatments — the dose needed to control cyanobacteria also affects non-target bacterial phyla, sensitivity is highly variable across species/strains/growth phase, the PCD-like mechanism proposed is not fully established in cyanobacteria, and it remains an open question whether treated systems can be reset to a pre-toxic-bloom phytoplankton community. (9) QACs/surfactants — potential biocidal effects on non-target aquatic organisms. (10) Coagulation/flocculation — chlorophyll-a reductions can be transient (e.g., 90% reduction followed by rapid recovery), efficacy varies by species, and aluminum/ferric coagulants and alum can induce toxin release from Microcystis and Planktothrix. (11) Sludge disposal — cell lysis during storage/treatment releases intracellular toxins, requiring confirmation of no residual toxicity before disposal. (12) DWTP processes generally — conventional treatment can inefficiently remove soluble toxins and can increase toxin concentration by disintegrating cells; advanced technologies may be needed only intermittently, and capital/operational costs can restrict availability. (13) Chlorination/ozonation pretreatment — risk of cell lysis and by-product (e.g., THM) formation, restricting achievable disinfectant doses. (14) Sand filtration — essentially no removal of soluble cyanotoxins, and requires frequent backwashing because retained cells can release toxins. (15) Membrane (UF) filtration — shear-induced cell disintegration can leak toxin, and the mechanism of dissolved-toxin rejection by membranes is not fully understood, per the authors. (16) Advanced oxidation processes — described by a cited source (Zhang et al.) as needing further work before practical, large-scale application. (17) Biological/allelopathic control — active allelochemical compounds are largely unidentified, and any biocontrol consortium's stability is said to depend on continuous toxin exposure and stable environmental conditions.

## Tensions with other findings
This source complicates a common simplified HAB narrative — that nutrient (phosphorus/nitrogen) loading is the primary driver of bloom severity and that nutrient reduction is therefore a reliable universal mitigation — by citing a global, multi-decadal, multi-lake analysis (Ho et al., 71 lakes, 1982-2012) finding that bloom exacerbation "did not consistently track with previously hypothesized drivers" in most lakes studied. Any driver/effect claim about nutrients and bloom severity elsewhere in this literature review should be treated as correlational and lake-specific rather than as an established universal causal mechanism, per this source. Separately, this source also complicates any treatment-and-management narrative that presents specific interventions (lanthanum-modified clay/Phoslock, alum/ferric coagulation, chlorination, ozonation, membrane filtration) as unambiguously safe or effective: it documents that several of these can themselves cause cell lysis and cyanotoxin release, or have their own ecotoxicological costs (e.g., lanthanum bioaccumulation), a caveat that may be in tension with sources elsewhere in this review that frame those same treatments primarily by their nutrient- or biomass-removal efficacy without discussing toxin-release side effects.

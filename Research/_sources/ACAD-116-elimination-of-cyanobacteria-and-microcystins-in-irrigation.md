# Elimination of cyanobacteria and microcystins in irrigation water—effects of hydrogen peroxide treatment

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7048868/

**What it is.** A field-mesocosm study (Lake Köyliönjärvi, SW Finland, 2014) testing whether dosing cyanobacteria-laden lake water with hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) can eliminate cyanobacterial cells and their microcystin (MC) toxins so the water is safe for spray-irrigating a food crop. Four seasonal trials applied 0, 2, 5, and 20 mg L⁻¹ H2O2 in replicated 1000-L tanks, tracking phytoplankton, chlorophyll a, cell-bound and extracellular microcystins (by LC-MS/MS), non-target plankton viability, and microcystin residues in spray-irrigated spinach.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Hydrogen peroxide at all three tested doses (2, 5, 20 mg L⁻¹) effectively lysed a majority of the MC-producing cyanobacterial cells in lake water, with the fraction lysed varying by dose and by experiment/season.
  - *evidence:* Reported as a range across the four seasonal tank experiments and three active doses; the 5 mg L⁻¹ dose is separately singled out as achieving the highest lysis fraction observed. (Results/Discussion — cyanobacterial lysis efficiency)
  - *quote:* "effectively lysed a major part (70–88% in the different experiments) of the MC-producing cyanobacteria"
- **[✓ verified]** The study's central/primary hypothesis was only partially confirmed: H2O2 lysed cyanobacterial cells (moving MC from cell-bound to dissolved form) but did not destroy/degrade the released microcystins.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as the headline conclusion; this is the paper's key negative/mixed finding and the basis for its safety caveat about treated water. (Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "the treatment with H2O2 effectively lysed cyanobacterial cells causing MC to leak into the surrounding water but the H2O2 treatment did not destroy the released MCs"
- **[✓ verified]** Even at the highest dose tested (20 mg L⁻¹), residual cell-bound microcystin remained, which the authors attribute to either a H2O2-resistant cyanobacterial subpopulation or regrowth/re-establishment of a population.
  - *evidence:* Authors offer two alternative, non-adjudicated causal explanations for the residual signal — an explicit acknowledgment of mechanistic uncertainty rather than a confirmed cause. (Discussion — cyanobacterial lysis efficiency)
  - *quote:* "residual cell-bound MC was observed even at the highest applied dose 20 mg L⁻¹, pointing to either an H2O2-resistant cyanobacterial subpopulation or a re-establishment of a population (possibly from akinetes)"
- **[✓ verified]** No microcystins (MC-RR or MC-LR) were detected in any spinach samples irrigated with H2O2-treated, MC-containing water, and no phytotoxic effects were observed.
  - *evidence:* The authors themselves qualify this null result as likely due to the already-low ambient MC concentration in the source/treated water rather than as proof the treatment or crop is protective at higher toxin loads. (Results — spinach contamination)
  - *quote:* "No MC-RR or MC-LR were detected in any of the spinach samples"
- **[✓ verified]** H2O2 doses of 10 mg L⁻¹ and higher were detrimental to non-target moving plankton (zooplankton and moving phytoplankton), with effects already visible at 5 mg L⁻¹.
  - *evidence:* Based on a viability index (scale −1 to 3) scored by an experienced hydrobiologist ~2 hours after H2O2 application; this is a short-term endpoint only. (Results/Discussion — plankton viability)
  - *quote:* "Hydrogen peroxide at 10 mg L⁻¹ or higher had a detrimental effect on the moving phytoplankton and zooplankton"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors recommend doses close to 2 mg L⁻¹ as a safer trade-off between cyanobacterial lysis and harm to non-target organisms, at least in the short term.
  - *evidence:* Direct recommendation synthesizing the lysis-efficiency data against the plankton-toxicity data. (Discussion/Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "Doses close to 2 mg L⁻¹ seem safer at least from a short-term perspective"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors explicitly caution that H2O2-treated irrigation water should not be used for critical purposes without first testing its microcystin concentration, because released MCs may persist in it.
  - *evidence:* A direct, practice-facing recommendation following from the finding that lysis does not equal toxin destruction. (Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "As released MCs may be present in the hydrogen peroxide–treated water, the treated water should not be used for any critical purposes without testing of the MC concentration"
- **[✓ verified]** Baseline (pre-treatment) cell-bound microcystin concentrations in the study lake were relatively low and seasonally variable across the 2014 sampling season, rising toward autumn.
  - *evidence:* This establishes the toxin baseline against which all treatment effects and the spinach non-detection result must be interpreted — a low starting concentration limits how strong a test of accumulation/removal this experiment could be. (Results — initial water chemistry)
  - *quote:* "0.04 μg L⁻¹ in July to 2.4 μg L⁻¹ in September 2014"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors state that H2O2's effectiveness against cyanobacteria/microcystins is strongly dependent on the specific water's chemical matrix, making dose recommendations difficult to generalize from one water body to another.
  - *evidence:* An explicit bounding/limitation statement restricting the external validity of any single 'effective dose' finding, including their own. (Discussion — limitations on generalizability)
  - *quote:* "The outcome of the use of chemical oxidants is, however, strongly dependent upon the natural matrix of water making the extrapolation from one water source to another almost impossible"

## Data / numbers
- H2O2 doses tested: 0 (control), 2, 5, and 20 mg L⁻¹
- Cyanobacterial lysis: 70–88% of MC-producing cyanobacteria lysed across doses/experiments overall; 80–96% lysed specifically at 5 mg L⁻¹ H2O2
- Cell-bound (intracellular) MC in lake water before treatment: June 0.97 µg L⁻¹; July 0.037 µg L⁻¹ (season description gives 0.04 µg L⁻¹ for July); August 1.27 µg L⁻¹; September 2.4 µg L⁻¹ — overall 2014 season range '0.04 µg L⁻¹ in July to 2.4 µg L⁻¹ in September'
- Historical (2013) cell-bound MC peaks in same lake: 3.6 µg L⁻¹ (19 Aug 2013) and 6.2 µg L⁻¹ (26 Sep 2013)
- Comparator cited from elsewhere in Finland: cell-bound MC as high as 42 µg L⁻¹ recorded on Åland Islands
- Extracellular (dissolved) MC before treatment: usually <0.1 µg L⁻¹; up to 0.12–0.28 µg L⁻¹ in August 2014
- Extracellular MC after H2O2 treatment (September experiment, highest dose): approx. 3.8 µg L⁻¹
- MC:chlorophyll a ratio in lake water: below 10% (0.0043–0.081), versus 10–100% typical in cyanobacteria-dominated samples per cited literature
- Chlorophyll a in source water at experiment start: June 38.8 µg L⁻¹; July 8.7 µg L⁻¹; August 46.9 µg L⁻¹; September 29.8 µg L⁻¹
- Chlorophyll a in tank water at experiment end: June 24.3 µg L⁻¹; September 26.8 µg L⁻¹
- Water temperature (noon readings): lake surface 16.3–21.2 °C; tank water 14.3–22.3 °C
- Lake morphometry: mean depth 3 m, max depth 12.8 m, surface area 12.5 km², catchment area 123.7 km², fed by 26 agricultural ditches
- Historical lake nutrient/clarity data: total phosphorus up to 170 mg m⁻³; chlorophyll a up to 180 mg m⁻³; Secchi depth 0.3–0.5 m; ecological state described as 'poor since the 1980s'
- WHO provisional guideline value for MC-LR in drinking water: 1.0 µg L⁻¹; human tolerable daily intake 0.04 µg MC-LR kg⁻¹ body mass per day
- Analytical detection limits, cell-bound MC (µg L⁻¹): MC-dmRR 0.020; MC-RR 0.052; MC-dmLR 0.0051; MC-YR 0.0073; MC-LR 0.028
- Analytical detection limits, extracellular MC (µg L⁻¹): MC-dmRR 0.012; MC-RR 0.033; MC-dmLR 0.0032; MC-YR 0.0046; MC-LR 0.018
- Spinach MC quantitation limit: below 0.02 µg g⁻¹ fresh weight
- Spinach analytical recovery: nodularin internal standard 48.2 ± 4.8% (summer harvest, n=40) and 56.0 ± 5.3% (autumn harvest, n=40); spiked MC-RR recovery 47.6 ± 6.5% (n=24); spiked MC-LR recovery 47.2 ± 14.5% (n=24)
- Experimental design: twenty 1000-L tanks, 5 replicate tanks per H2O2 dose (0/2/5/20 mg L⁻¹), 4 seasonal experiments in 2014 lasting 3–14 days each
- External comparator cited by the authors: whole-lake treatment with 2 mg L⁻¹ H2O2 (different study/water body) reportedly destroyed ~99% of MC within a few days
- External comparators cited: zooplankton sensitive to H2O2 above 2.5 mg L⁻¹; cultured M. aeruginosa required >4 mg L⁻¹ H2O2 to kill; H2O2 half-life ~6 h at 2.5 mg L⁻¹ under 500 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹ irradiance without cyanobacteria; 4 mg L⁻¹ fully degraded within 5 h in organic-rich lake water (different study)
- Irrigation pump used: Saer FC25-2B, 2.2 kW, 3–8 m³ h⁻¹ capacity

## Methods
Outdoor field-mesocosm experiment: twenty 1000-L agricultural tanks filled with pumped water from Lake Köyliönjärvi (SW Finland), run as four seasonal trials in 2014 (starting 17 Jun, 9 Jul, 11 Aug, 8 Sep; incubations of 3–14 days), each trial dosing five replicate tanks per H2O2 concentration (0/2/5/20 mg L⁻¹, from a 35% stock, stirred in) under ambient outdoor temperature and light (no control of either). Endpoints measured: phytoplankton abundance/biovolume by the Utermöhl method on an inverted microscope; chlorophyll a via ethanol extraction and spectrophotometry (in-house modification of Sartory & Grobbelaar 1984); cell-bound and extracellular microcystins extracted separately (methanol extraction of filtered cells; solid-phase extraction of filtrate) and quantified by LC-MS/MS (Agilent 1200 LC + Bruker HCT Ultra iontrap MS, positive electrospray, targeted MRM transitions for MC-LR, MC-RR and other congeners); and non-target 'moving plankton' (Protozoa, Rotatoria, Cladocera, Copepoda, motile phytoplankton) viability scored on a −1-to-3 index by a hydrobiologist ~2 hours after dosing. Treated tank water was then used for two rounds of field spray-irrigation of spinach (crops harvested 24 Jul and 16 Sep 2014); spinach was analyzed for MC-LR/MC-RR by methanol extraction, SPE cleanup, and LC-MS/MS-MRM, using a nodularin internal standard plus spike-recovery experiments to check matrix effects. The approach works well for comparing lysis efficiency and cell-bound-vs-extracellular toxin partitioning across doses and seasons in one lake, and for a first-pass check of crop toxin uptake; it is explicitly not designed to establish long-term ecological safety (viability was scored at a single ~2-hour timepoint) and its spinach-matrix recoveries (47–56%) limit confidence in the near-zero spinach residue readings.

## Stated limitations
The authors state: (1) H2O2's effect on cyanobacteria/MC is "strongly dependent upon the natural matrix of water making the extrapolation from one water source to another almost impossible"; (2) released/extracellular MCs were "not efficiently degraded by H2O2 under the experimental conditions," and fully destroying them would require much higher H2O2 doses that would endanger non-target organisms; (3) the residual cell-bound MC seen even at 20 mg L⁻¹ could reflect either an H2O2-resistant cyanobacterial subpopulation or population re-establishment (e.g., from akinetes) — the paper does not distinguish between these; (4) "No long-term ecological effects of hydrogen peroxide application were studied in this paper" — plankton viability was scored only ~2 hours after dosing; (5) identifying which cyanobacterial species produced the MC was explicitly "beyond the goals of the present study"; (6) the absence of MC in spinach is attributed to the lake's already-low ambient MC concentrations, so the trial may not have been a strong enough toxin challenge to rule out uptake at higher exposure; (7) analytical recovery for spinach-matrix MC was only 47.2–56.0%, and internal-standard recovery likewise 48–56%, limiting confidence in near-zero spinach readings; (8) only 3 of the 5 prepared tanks per treatment were sampled at experiment end, and no formal statistical significance testing is reported; (9) plant-species-specific differences in MC uptake/decontamination potential are noted (citing other leafy crops) as a further generalization caveat.

## Tensions with other findings
The source's central tension is with treatment guidance drawn from other H2O2 field trials: it cites a prior whole-lake application of 2 mg L⁻¹ H2O2 (different study/water body) that reportedly destroyed ~99% of microcystins within days, whereas here even 20 mg L⁻¹ (10x that dose) failed to destroy released microcystins in Lake Köyliönjärvi water. The authors attribute this discrepancy to water-matrix-dependent H2O2 consumption (organic matter, radical scavengers), which argues against transferring a single "effective dose" across water bodies — a caution relevant to any treatment-and-management framing that proposes generic H2O2 dosing thresholds. The paper also complicates a common bloom-management assumption that killing/lysing cyanobacterial cells removes toxin hazard: lysis was efficient (70–96% of cells across doses), but this converted MC from cell-bound to dissolved form without destroying it, so treatment can transiently raise free (extracellular, more readily bioavailable) microcystin rather than eliminate the risk — an important nuance for any tool or recommendation that equates visible bloom knockdown with toxin safety.

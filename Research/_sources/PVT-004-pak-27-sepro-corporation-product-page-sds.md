# PAK 27 — SePRO Corporation Product Page / SDS

**What it is.** PAK 27 is a granular, non-copper algaecide sold by SePRO Corporation (EPA Reg. No. 68660-9; active ingredient sodium carbonate peroxyhydrate, reported at 85.0% by weight on SePRO-affiliated/distributor pages, not on the primary page itself), marketed for selective control of cyanobacteria plus broader-spectrum control of filamentous/planktonic green algae, diatoms, and Chara in lakes, ponds, drinking-water reservoirs, irrigation canals/ditches, aquaculture, and wastewater systems. The primary product page and its linked Label/SDS PDFs supply the mechanism-of-action and use-claim language but essentially no application-rate numbers, toxicology data, or EPA registration number in machine-readable form; those numeric data points had to be pieced together from SePRO's own retail/canal subpages and third-party distributor/reseller pages, several of which are mutually inconsistent (see tensions).

## Methods
This is a commercial product source, not a predictive model; "methods" here is the product's mechanism/dosing approach as described in the fetched pages. Mechanism: an oxidizing active ingredient (sodium carbonate peroxyhydrate, releasing H2O2 in water) that lyses algal/cyanobacterial cell membranes and degrades chlorophyll ("creates a powerful oxidation reaction that destroys algal cell membranes and chlorophyll, providing immediate algae control"). Dosing approach: one distributor page instructs that "Application Rates should be based upon actual cyanobacterial cell counts" rather than a single flat rate; the labeled rate itself is expressed in two different, unreconciled units across sources tied to this product (3-100 lbs/acre-foot per peroxygensolutions.com vs. 30-100 lbs per surface acre per SePRO's own retail/canal pages). Application method: "either manually, or by using most existing broadcast spreaders," including boat-mounted spreader systems noted as compatible. Retreatment: SePRO's retail page recommends waiting about two weeks between applications. No field-efficacy trial, comparative-performance data (e.g., vs. copper-based algaecides or an untreated baseline), or peer-reviewed validation of this specific product were present in any page I could access — the claims found are vendor marketing assertions plus a few regulatory-label facts (EPA Reg. No., active-ingredient percentage), not independently validated field results.

## Stated limitations
The accessible SePRO marketing pages (primary product page, retail/DIY storefronts, canals page) state essentially no explicit technical caveats: nothing on how water hardness, temperature, pH, sunlight, or existing vegetation might affect efficacy, and nothing on dissolved-oxygen depletion or fish-kill risk from decomposing algal biomass after treatment. The two documents that would normally carry SePRO's/EPA's formal precautionary and environmental-hazard language — the Label PDF and SDS PDF linked from the primary page, plus the EPA's own hosted copy of the label — returned only unreadable compressed/binary PDF content on every fetch attempt, so this entry cannot confirm the label's precautionary statements or the SDS's toxicological/ecological data sections. The only limitation-like statements actually found appear on third-party/retail pages rather than SePRO's own primary page: shipping/sales restrictions ("Cannot ship to WA, NY, NJ, ME, HI, CT, CA or AK"; "Not for Sale to: VT") and one distributor's claim that the product has been discontinued by the manufacturer (see tensions) — none of these are efficacy caveats, and the discontinuation claim is unconfirmed and contradicted elsewhere.

## Tensions with other findings
(1) Two unreconciled application-rate expressions are attached to this product: a distributor's application-instructions page gives a depth-adjusted range of 3-100 lbs/acre-foot, while SePRO's own retail and canal-use pages give 30-100 lbs per surface acre (no depth term); no source I could read converts between them, and the authoritative label table (which likely does) was unreadable. (2) One third-party distributor states the product "has been discontinued by the manufacturer," conflicting with its continued live marketing on SePRO's own primary, retail, and DIY-storefront domains as of this fetch — unresolved. (3) The signal word is unresolved: a distributor states "DANGER / PELIGRO" while my own EPA-label fetch could only produce a self-flagged, inferred guess of "CAUTION" (not read from the document); the SDS, which would state this authoritatively, was unreadable. (4) An independent, non-PAK-27-branded peer-reviewed study on the shared active ingredient (sodium carbonate peroxyhydrate) reports a 96-hour EC50 of 0.9-1.0 mg/L H2O2 against the target cyanobacterium Microcystis aeruginosa versus a much higher 96-hour LC50 range (1.0-19.7 mg/L H2O2) for a non-target invertebrate, and identifies fathead minnow as the least-sensitive species tested. This is broadly consistent with, and gives independent chemistry-level context for, this source's own "selective control of cyanobacteria" and fish-safety marketing claims — but the study names no commercial product and is a short-term laboratory effect-concentration study, not a field trial, so it should not be read as validation of this specific product's field performance or its "immediate algae control" marketing language.

# Voluntary Guidance for Agencies When Making Bloom and Water Contact Recreation Public Health Decisions (HABs Response Guidance)

**What it is.** A California state voluntary (non-regulatory) guidance document, adopted by the CCHAB Network in 2016, that lays out an eight-step interagency response plan and a tiered (No Advisory / Caution / Warning / Danger) system of numeric cyanotoxin and cell-density trigger levels that state and local agencies use to decide when to post, and later remove, public health advisory signs at inland recreational water bodies experiencing cyanobacterial (planktonic) harmful algal blooms; a parallel, separately updated (March 2025) guidance covers benthic/mat-forming cyanobacteria.

## Methods
This is a management/decision-framework document, not an empirical study: it operationalizes bloom response as a rule-based decision tree that combines (a) laboratory-measured concentrations of three cyanotoxins (total microcystins, anatoxin-a, cylindrospermopsin), (b) microscopy-based cell counts of potential toxin-producing cyanobacteria, and (c) qualitative site-specific visual indicators (discoloration, scum, algal mats, soupy/paint-like appearance) into a four-level classification (No Advisory/Caution/Warning/Danger) governed by an 'any one criterion met' rule, embedded in an eight-step interagency surveillance-to-de-posting workflow. It is stated to apply to inland recreational water bodies in California; it explicitly does not apply to drinking-water cyanotoxin assessment, which is handled by a separate program. A parallel visual/field-protocol framework (with its own SOP, field data sheet, and threshold-derivation appendices) governs benthic/mat-forming cyanobacteria, but its specific numeric thresholds were not surfaced in the fetched webpage text.

## Stated limitations
The document is explicitly labeled voluntary guidance, not policy or regulation, implying no binding enforcement authority. It explicitly excludes cyanotoxins in drinking water from its scope (referred elsewhere). The response plan is described as adaptable, with steps that "may occur in a different order or simultaneously," i.e., not a rigid fixed sequence. The Caution-tier call for anatoxin-a depends on the analytical method's detection limit (must detect ≤ 1 µg/L), meaning a less-sensitive lab method could under-classify a site. De-posting requires ALL no-advisory criteria (not just one) to be met, and only after a minimum two-week sustained period, reflecting a deliberately conservative (slow-to-clear) design. Where a benthic mat and planktonic bloom co-occur, the guidance itself flags that responders must choose the more conservative sign, implying the two frameworks' outputs are not always cleanly separable at a given site.

## Tensions with other findings
This source is a risk-communication/management policy document rather than a predictive or causal scientific study, so its numbers are consensus-based regulatory trigger levels rather than outputs of a dose-response or forecasting model validated in this text. Its own 'action level met when one or more criteria are met' rule means an 'advisory' label conflates several distinct, co-varying signals (specific toxin concentration OR cell density OR visual appearance) into one ordinal tier; any downstream HAB model that treats 'advisory posted' as a single ground-truth outcome variable is therefore learning a thresholded, multi-criterion management decision rather than a direct, continuous measurement of hazard magnitude -- a distinction worth flagging if this guidance's tiers are used to construct labels for a predictive model. Correlation between a satellite or in-situ signal and 'advisory status' under this framework would reflect agreement with a policy threshold, not a validated causal or biological hazard relationship.

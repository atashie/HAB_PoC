# Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) for Phosphorus in Chautauqua Lake, Chautauqua County, New York

**What it is.** A November 2012 Clean Water Act Section 303(d) Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) technical report for Chautauqua Lake, NY — a two-basin lake listed as phosphorus-impaired since 2004 that experienced a toxic blue-green algal bloom in 2009 — quantifying phosphorus loading by source (wastewater treatment plants, septic systems, wetlands, stream-bank erosion, groundwater, and internal/sediment release) and linking a watershed loading model (AVGWLF/GWLF) to a lake-response model (BATHTUB) to set a numeric in-lake phosphorus target and load allocations.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Chautauqua Lake's North and South basins were added to NYS DEC's Clean Water Act Section 303(d) list of impaired water bodies in 2004 specifically for phosphorus impairment, which triggered this TMDL's development as a 'high priority' water.
  - *evidence:* Based on state sampling showing summer epilimnetic total phosphorus concentrations in both basins violating the state's 20 µg/L guidance value. (Section 1.2 Problem Statement, p. 4-5)
- **[✓ verified]** Chautauqua Lake experienced a toxic blue-green algal (cyanobacteria) bloom in 2009, and the South segment has been qualitatively described (per a 2007 NYS DEC characterization) as having 'definite algae greenness' versus clearer water in the North segment.
  - *evidence:* Presented as historical/qualitative background in the problem statement; no toxin concentration, spatial extent, or duration data for the 2009 bloom appears in the extracted text. (Section 1.2 Problem Statement, p. 4)
- **[✓ verified]** New York's numeric TMDL target for both Chautauqua Lake basins is a summer mean epilimnetic total phosphorus concentration not exceeding 20 µg/L (0.02 mg/L), based on biweekly sampling from June 1 to September 30, derived from NYS DEC guidance (TOGS 1.1.1) for 'ponded' waters (lakes/reservoirs/ponds excluding Lakes Erie, Ontario, Champlain).
  - *evidence:* This is a general eutrophication-control guidance value, not a bloom- or toxin-specific threshold; it is adopted directly as the TMDL's numeric endpoint. (Section 3.0 Numeric Water Quality Target, p. 13)
  - *quote:* "the epilimnetic summer mean total phosphorus level shall not exceed 20 µg/L (or 0.02 mg/L), based on biweekly sampling, conducted from June 1 to September 30"

(Section 4.2 Sources of Phosphorus Loading, p. 14)

- **[✓ verified]** Wastewater treatment plants are a far larger relative phosphorus source in the North basin (13 facilities; 5,487.4 lbs/yr, 19.6% of that basin's total load) than in the South basin (9 facilities; 1,008.0 lbs/yr, 1.9% of that basin's total load).

- **[✓ verified]** Septic systems within 250 feet of the lake are treated as being at risk of 'short-circuiting' (limited soil contact/adsorption before discharge reaches the lake), a distance-based criterion used in apportioning septic-system phosphorus loads (975.4 lbs/yr / 3.5% of North basin load; 719.4 lbs/yr / 1.4% of South basin load).

- **[✓ verified]** Groundwater seepage was modeled as a major nonpoint pathway, transporting an estimated 36.7% of the North basin's total phosphorus load and 20.9% of the South basin's total load; the model-estimated groundwater phosphorus concentrations (0.054 mg/L North, 0.055 mg/L South) were compared against a literature background value of 0.006 mg/L (GWLF manual, for ≥90% forested eastern U.S. land) to attribute roughly 11% of the groundwater load to 'natural' sources and the remainder to developed/agricultural land.
  - *evidence:* Uses a literature-derived (not site-measured) background concentration to split the groundwater phosphorus load into natural versus anthropogenic fractions. (Section 4.2.6 Groundwater Seepage, p. 16-17)
  - *quote:* "the model-estimated groundwater phosphorus concentration is 0.054 mg/L for the North basin and 0.055 mg/L for the South basin"
- **[✓ verified]** Internal (bottom-sediment) phosphorus loading is estimated to supply 25.1% (7,014.7 lbs/yr) of the North basin's total load and 55.1% (29,147.8 lbs/yr) of the South basin's total load — making it the single largest modeled source category in the South basin — but this figure is not an independent measurement; it is the residual load needed to reconcile modeled external sources with observed lake phosphorus concentrations.
  - *evidence:* Justified by the lake having received phosphorus loading 'much higher than its assimilative capacity' over time, with excess phosphorus stored in bottom sediments and released back to the water column under favorable conditions (e.g., wind-driven resuspension). (Section 4.2.7 Internal Loading, p. 17-18)
  - *quote:* "it was assumed that the remaining load must be originating from internal sources (i.e., lake bottom sediments)"
- **[✓ verified]** The TMDL's lake-response modeling fed AVGWLF-simulated external loads plus the calculated internal load into the BATHTUB model (U.S. Army Corps of Engineers; Walker, 1987), and the report characterizes the resulting simulated summer mean epilimnetic phosphorus concentrations as fitting the observed 1990-2007 lake monitoring data well, both as a long-term average and year-by-year.
  - *evidence:* Comparison shown graphically (Figures 11 and 12, observed vs. simulated); the extracted text contains no numeric goodness-of-fit statistic (e.g., R² or RMSE). (Sections 5.1-5.2, p. 19)
  - *quote:* "The combined use of AVGWLF, BATHTUB, and internal loading estimates provides a good fit to the observed data for Chautauqua Lake"
- **[✓ verified]** Drainage from the North basin into the South basin (the two segments are connected by a narrow channel) is itself modeled as a phosphorus source to the South basin, estimated at 8,094 lbs/yr, or 15.4% of the South basin's total load.
  - *evidence:* Reflects the two-basin lake's hydrologic connectivity, meaning the South basin's phosphorus budget is partly determined by upstream (North basin) conditions rather than independent of them. (Section 4.2.8 Other Sources, p. 18)
  - *quote:* "the drainage out of the North basin contributes 8,094 lbs/yr (15.4%) of phosphorus to the South basin"

## Data / numbers
- TMDL numeric target: 20 µg/L (0.020 mg/L) mean summer epilimnetic total phosphorus, biweekly sampling June 1-Sept 30 (NYS DEC TOGS 1.1.1 guidance value)
- Total modeled phosphorus load entering Chautauqua Lake: 80,828.7 lbs/yr (long-term mean, 1990-2007, AVGWLF-based estimate; no confidence interval reported in extracted text)
- WWTP loading: North basin 5,487.4 lbs/yr (19.6% of North total, 13 facilities); South basin 1,008.0 lbs/yr (1.9% of South total, 9 facilities)
- Septic system loading: North basin 975.4 lbs/yr (3.5%); South basin 719.4 lbs/yr (1.4%); short-circuiting risk threshold = within 250 ft of lake
- Wetland loading: North basin 20.6 lbs/yr; South basin 7.9 lbs/yr (each <0.1% of total load); wetlands cover 2,081 acres (4%) of North basin and 1,469 acres (3%) of South basin drainage area
- Stream bank erosion: North basin 36.0 lbs/yr (0.1%); South basin 47.8 lbs/yr (0.1%)
- Groundwater seepage load: North basin 10,243.0 lbs/yr (36.7%); South basin 11,038.7 lbs/yr (20.9%); modeled groundwater P concentration 0.054 mg/L (North) / 0.055 mg/L (South) vs. 0.006 mg/L forested background (GWLF manual); ~11% of groundwater load (1,138.1 lbs/yr North; 1,204.2 lbs/yr South) attributed to natural sources
- Internal (residual) sediment loading: North basin 7,014.7 lbs/yr (25.1% of total load); South basin 29,147.8 lbs/yr (55.1% of total load)
- North-to-South basin drainage transfer: 8,094 lbs/yr (15.4% of South basin's total load)
- Direct drainage basin area: 101,943 acres; elevation range 1,308-1,863.5 ft AMSL
- Land use (similar in both basins): Forest ~62.1% (N) / 63.3% (S); Agriculture [hay & pasture + cropland] ~28.8% (N) / 27.4% (S); Developed land ~3.5% (N) / 5.8% (S); Wetland ~4.2% (N) / 2.8% (S)

## Methods
Two linked, publicly documented models: (1) AVGWLF, an ArcView GIS interface (Evans, 2002) to the GWLF (Generalized Watershed Loading Function) model of Haith & Shoemaker (1987) — a water-balance model driven by daily precipitation and average temperature that simulates runoff/streamflow and long-term (1990-2007) mean annual external phosphorus loading by source category. The source states GWLF's complexity was deliberately chosen because it "falls between that of a detailed, process-based simulation model and a simple export coefficient model," giving adequate process representation without "onerous data requirements for calibration." (2) BATHTUB, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' steady-state, spatially segmented lake water/nutrient-balance model (Walker, 1987), using empirical relationships "previously developed and tested for reservoir applications" to predict in-lake phosphorus (and other eutrophication indicators) from loading inputs. AVGWLF's simulated external load plus a separately calculated internal (sediment) load were fed into BATHTUB to simulate summer mean epilimnetic total phosphorus per basin. Internal loading is not independently measured — it is back-calculated as the residual needed to reconcile modeled external loads with observed in-lake concentrations, under the assumption that any unexplained gap is sediment-derived. Validation compared BATHTUB-simulated summer mean epilimnetic phosphorus against observed monitoring data for 1990-2007 (long-term average and year-by-year); the source calls the result a "good fit" (Figures 11-12) but the extracted text contains no quantitative goodness-of-fit statistic. Underlying in-lake data come from CSLAP (Citizen Statewide Lake Assessment Program), a NYS DEC / NY Federation of Lake Associations volunteer biweekly (15-week, May-October) monitoring program measuring total phosphorus, nitrogen species, chlorophyll a, pH, conductivity, color, calcium, Secchi depth, and temperature, with DEC/FOLA-delivered training and QA/QC oversight; supplemented by Chautauqua County sampling in 1993-1994.

## Stated limitations
The report explicitly frames its watershed-model choice as a complexity/data-availability trade-off — GWLF is deliberately less detailed than a full process-based model, chosen because it avoids "onerous data requirements for calibration" — an acknowledged simplification rather than a fully mechanistic simulation. Internal (bottom-sediment) phosphorus loading, which the source's own figures show as the single largest component of the South basin's budget (55.1%), is explicitly derived as an assumption-based residual ("it was assumed that the remaining load must be originating from internal sources") rather than an independently measured flux, so it inherits any error in the external-load estimates it is calculated against. Model-to-observation agreement is reported only qualitatively ("good fit") in the extracted sections, with no numeric error statistic (R²/RMSE) given. Separately, this dossier's own extraction is limited: the supplied text is a windowed excerpt (head plus five keyword-selected windows) of a 164,631-character document, so the full Table 3 source-loading breakdown, the sections on agricultural/developed/forested-land direct surface runoff (Sections 4.2.3-4.2.5, only fragments visible), and the Margin of Safety, Critical Conditions, and Implementation sections were not available to verify or summarize here.

## Tensions with other findings
This TMDL's own source apportionment shows internal (legacy sediment) phosphorus loading as the largest single modeled category in the South basin (55.1% of total load) and a substantial share in the North basin (25.1%). Because this internal-load figure is a calculated residual rather than a direct measurement, and because it is large, this source complicates — without directly contradicting — simpler HAB-management framings that treat watershed nutrient-source control (wastewater, septic, agricultural/groundwater pathways) as sufficient on its own to quickly lower bloom risk: per this document's own accounting, a large share of the phosphorus feeding the South basin's eutrophication is recycled from bottom sediments rather than arriving from current-year watershed sources, and the source does not state any timeline linking external load reduction to a corresponding drop in bloom risk. Separately, the source records both a toxic blue-green algal bloom (2009) and a comparatively low numeric phosphorus target (20 µg/L) for the same lake; this juxtaposition shows the two co-occurring in this system but the source does not assert or test a causal mechanism connecting the specific phosphorus level to bloom occurrence.

# Repeated low-dose copper algaecide treatments promote beneficial phytoplankton and zooplankton communities

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0301479725034784

**What it is.** A 2025 peer-reviewed field study (Journal of Environmental Management) reporting a 42-day field mesocosm experiment in a hypereutrophic aquaculture pond that compares repeated, lower "MLR" (pH- and dissolved-organic-carbon-based) copper sulfate dosing against conventional, higher alkalinity-based copper dosing for cyanobacterial HAB control, evaluating both control efficacy and side-effects on beneficial green algae (chlorophytes) and zooplankton across three repeated treatments spaced two weeks apart.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Conventional copper sulfate treatment using alkalinity-based dosing commonly prescribes higher copper doses than are actually necessary to achieve HAB control in many systems.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the study's motivating premise in the Technical Abstract, framing why a lower, targeted (MLR-based) dose was tested. (Technical Abstract (USDA ARS publication mirror, verbatim))
  - *quote:* "often prescribe higher doses (> 0.25 mg/L Cu) than may be necessary to achieve HAB control in many systems"
- **[✓ verified]** The experiment compared three copper dosing regimens — alkalinity-based (350 µg/L Cu), full MLR-based (80 µg/L Cu), and half-MLR (40 µg/L Cu) — each reapplied every 14 days for 3 applications across a 42-day field mesocosm trial in a hypereutrophic aquaculture pond, designed to mimic real repeated-application practice.
  - *evidence:* Explicit study design statement giving exact doses, timing, site type, and stated real-world rationale (aquaculture/recreational pond/drinking-water management). (Technical Abstract)
  - *quote:* "an MLR based dose (80 µg/L Cu), half of this dose (40 µg/L Cu) and the alkalinity-based dose (350 µg/L Cu) were applied every 14 days (3x across the experiment)"
- **[✓ verified]** All three copper doses — including the two much lower MLR-based doses — produced an identical, strong initial reduction in cyanobacteria, indicating the lower doses controlled the bloom just as effectively at the outset as the higher conventional dose.
  - *evidence:* Presented as the study's first headline result, repeated near-verbatim in both the Interpretive Summary and Technical Abstract. (Technical Abstract / Interpretive Summary)
  - *quote:* "all copper treatments saw an identical ~95% reduction in cyanobacteria"
- **[✓ verified]** Over the full 42-day course of the experiment, the lower MLR-based doses caused less harm than the higher alkalinity-based dose to beneficial chlorophytes (green algae) and to zooplankton.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the study's key non-target/ecological-cost finding, distinguishing the treatments beyond initial cyanobacteria control. (Technical Abstract)
  - *quote:* "the lower, MLR-based doses caused less harm to beneficial chlorophytes and zooplankton over the course of the experiment"
- **[✓ verified]** With repeated applications, the higher alkalinity-based dose became progressively less effective at controlling harmful phytoplankton, whereas the lower doses did not show this decline in efficacy.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a distinct, 'notable' secondary finding specifically about repeated (not single) dosing, directly relevant to real-world retreatment scheduling. (Technical Abstract)
  - *quote:* "the alkalinity-based dose showed decreasing efficacy with each subsequent treatment"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors conclude that a repeated, low, MLR-based copper dosing strategy gives better sustained (long-term) HAB control with less ecological impact than the conventional higher, alkalinity-based dosing approach.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the paper's overall conclusion, worded almost identically in both the Interpretive Summary and Technical Abstract. (Technical Abstract / Interpretive Summary (concluding sentence))
  - *quote:* "offers superior long-term control of HABs with less ecological impact than higher, alkalinity-based Cu dosing"
- **[✓ verified]** The MLR-based dosing approach tested here computes the copper dose from water pH and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) rather than from alkalinity, and was developed in prior work; this paper's contribution is testing that method under repeated, real-world-style dosing rather than introducing the method itself.
  - *evidence:* The Technical Abstract explicitly calls the MLR method 'previously developed,' signaling this study builds on (rather than originates) the dosing formula. (Technical Abstract)
  - *quote:* "a previously developed multiple linear regression (MLR) based copper dosing method that uses only pH and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) to determine an optimal dose"

## Methods
Field-based mesocosm experiment (in situ, not laboratory beakers) conducted over 42 days in a single hypereutrophic aquaculture pond. Three copper sulfate pentahydrate dosing treatments were compared: (1) conventional alkalinity-based dose (350 µg/L Cu), (2) a "previously developed" multiple linear regression (MLR)-based dose computed from water pH and dissolved organic carbon (80 µg/L Cu), and (3) a half-MLR dose (40 µg/L Cu). Each treatment was reapplied every 14 days for 3 total applications, explicitly designed to mimic realistic repeated-application practice in aquaculture, recreational pond, and drinking-water management settings. Outcomes tracked were cyanobacteria abundance/reduction and the responses of non-target beneficial phytoplankton (chlorophytes/green algae) and zooplankton communities, followed across the full 42 days to capture cumulative, repeated-dose effects rather than a single-application snapshot. Where it "works": the MLR-based lower doses matched the higher dose's initial cyanobacteria control (~95% reduction) while causing less harm to chlorophytes/zooplankton and, unlike the alkalinity-based dose, did not lose efficacy with repeated use. Where the alternative (conventional) approach "fails": the higher alkalinity-based dose, despite matching initial control, showed declining efficacy across repeated applications and caused more non-target harm. The abstract-level text accessible for this review does not specify replicate numbers (n mesocosms per treatment), statistical tests used, taxonomic identification protocols, chlorophyll-a/cell-count methodology, or whether an untreated (no-copper) control arm was included.

This last point is this reviewer's own bounded observation about scope based on the design as described, not a limitation the authors themselves stated — the paper's actual Discussion/Conclusion section (not accessible here) may address it.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper explicitly frames its MLR dosing method as "previously developed," implying a distinct antecedent paper established that dosing formula. Web search points to a plausibly related 2025 Environmental Toxicology and Chemistry paper by an overlapping author team ("Reevaluating copper algaecide dosing to manage water quality: a multiple linear regression approach," DOI 10.1093/etojnl/vgaf175) as that antecedent work — but that paper was not fetched in full for this dossier, and any of its specific figures (e.g., a 28-day timeframe, a 100 µg/L dose, or an ammonia-spike finding surfaced only in a secondary aquaculture trade-press summary) must NOT be attributed to ACAD-136; they belong to a different source and are excluded from the claims above. More broadly, this source's framing — that copper-algaecide harm to zooplankton can be substantially mitigated by lower, targeted, repeated dosing — sits in potential tension with other literature visible only as titles in search results (not fetched here), e.g. a similarly-scoped ScienceDirect paper titled "Copper sulfate treatment harms zooplankton and ultimately promotes algal blooms: A field mesocosm experiment," which frames copper-driven zooplankton loss as a bloom-promoting side effect more generally. Whether this is a genuine disagreement or simply a difference in dose regime (low MLR-based vs. conventional higher alkalinity-based dosing) cannot be resolved from the text fetched for ACAD-136 alone. As with all dose-response/ecological-response findings here, correlation between copper dose and observed community outcomes is being treated as a controlled experimental effect by the authors' framing, not independently re-derived by this review.

# Increasingly severe cyanobacterial blooms and deep water hypoxia coincide with warming water temperatures in reservoirs

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed observational/statistical analysis of 32 years (1987–2018) of routine in-situ monitoring data from 20 temperate USACE reservoirs in the USA, testing whether cyanobacterial cell densities, water-column temperature, dissolved oxygen, and nutrients changed over time, and identifying which environmental variables statistically associate with higher bloom cell densities.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria cell densities across 20 US reservoirs remained mostly low-to-moderate human-health risk until 2003–2005, then rose rapidly; since 2009, high-risk densities (>100,000 cells mL⁻¹) became frequent (75% of yearly observations), including 53% of observations from mostly-forested-watershed reservoirs, and by 2018 all 20 reservoirs had recorded blooms above that threshold.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the paper's headline finding in the abstract and quantified further in results with pre-2003 vs. 2009-2018 percentage breakdowns (pre-2003: 83% of n=132 observations <20,000 cells/mL, 16% n=26 moderate, 1 observation high; 2009-2018: 75% n=110 high, 21% n=30 moderate, 4% n=6 low). (Abstract; Results)
  - *quote:* "Since 2009, cell densities posing high human health risks have become frequent with 75% of yearly observations exceeding 100,000 cells mL−1, including 53% of observations from reservoirs with mostly forested watersheds."
- **[✓ verified]** Human-health risk was operationalized using WHO cell-density thresholds (low <20,000; moderate 20,000–100,000; high >100,000 cells mL⁻¹) rather than measured toxin concentrations, because cyanotoxins themselves were not monitored.
  - *evidence:* Explicit methodological definition and an acknowledged proxy/limitation in the same sentence. (Methods)
  - *quote:* "Cyanobacteria toxin concentrations were not monitored, so we used World Health Organization recommendations to identify densities that posed low (<20,000 cells ml−1), moderate (20,000 ≥ x ≤ 100,000 cells ml−1), and high (>100,000 cells ml−1) relative probabilities of human health risks."
- **[✓ verified]** The rise in bloom severity coincided with progressively earlier and longer summer warming of surface waters, earlier onset of stratification, lengthening deep-water hypoxia, and warming of deep waters in non-stratifying reservoirs — a temporal coincidence rather than a manipulated causal test.
  - *evidence:* Headline abstract statement; magnitudes given in Table 1 as 1989-vs-2018 monthly means (surface warming approx. +0.5 to +3.6 degrees C across months/reservoir types; deep-water warming approx. +1.5 to +4.5 degrees C). (Abstract; Table 1; Results)
  - *quote:* "These increases coincided with progressively earlier and longer summer warming of surface waters, evidence of earlier onset of stratification, lengthening durations of deep-water hypoxia, and warming deep waters in non-stratifying reservoirs."
- **[✓ verified]** Deep-water dissolved oxygen declined across nearly all months and reservoir types between 1989 and 2018 (roughly 0.0 to about -2.1 mg/L depending on month/type per Table 1), and summer hypoxia now persists about two months longer in agricultural reservoirs than earlier in the record.
  - *evidence:* Table 1 DO comparisons plus an explicit hypoxia-duration statement in the results/discussion. (Table 1; Results)
  - *quote:* "Deep water hypoxia now lasts two months longer in stratifying and non-stratifying agricultural reservoirs."
- **[✓ verified]** In stratifying reservoirs, three variables (summer precipitation z-scores, June surface water temperature, and surface-water total Kjeldahl nitrogen/TKN) jointly explained 56.3% of deviance in a GAM of cyanobacteria cell density (adjusted R2 = 0.534 per the associated figure caption) — an association among years, not proof of causation.
  - *evidence:* Directly reported model-fit statistics; this is the paper's strongest quantified driver relationship. (Results (GAM analysis, stratifying reservoirs))
  - *quote:* "Listed in decreasing order of significance, summer precipitation z-scores, June surface water temperatures, and surface water total Kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN) explained 56.3% of the deviance in a generalized additive model for cyanobacteria cell densities."
- **[✓ verified]** In weakly/non-stratifying reservoirs, the best available predictors (August deep-water temperature and June dissolved oxygen) explained far less variance — only 22% of GAM deviance — showing the statistical account of bloom drivers is markedly weaker for that reservoir subgroup.
  - *evidence:* Authors explicitly report the lower explanatory power for this subgroup rather than presenting only the stronger stratifying-reservoir result, consistent with honest reporting of a weaker result. (Results (GAM analysis, weakly/non-stratifying reservoirs))
  - *quote:* "August deep water temperatures and June dissolved oxygen were the most informative predictors of cell densities, but explained only 22% of the deviance in the generalized additive model."
- **[✓ verified]** Total nitrogen and phosphorus concentrations and watershed land cover were largely stable over the study period even as cyanobacteria densities rose sharply, suggesting the bloom increase in these reservoirs is not simply explained by increased bulk nutrient loading or land-use change.
  - *evidence:* Explicit discussion statement, supported by a separate finding that watershed land cover was 'nearly unchanged from 2001-2011' (only three highly forested watersheds showed 1.1-4.5% forest-cover decreases). (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "Increases in cell densities occurred despite no clear and consistent changes in watershed land cover, precipitation, and total nitrogen or phosphorus concentrations in surface waters or inflows among reservoirs."
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria are physiologically favored by warming because their growth rates peak at higher temperatures (25-35 degrees C) than other phytoplankton groups such as diatoms and cryptomonads, whose growth declines above roughly 15-25 degrees C — offered as a mechanistic rationale drawn from phytoplankton physiology, not a new experiment in this study.
  - *evidence:* Mechanistic explanation cited in the Discussion for why warming specifically favors cyanobacteria over competing phytoplankton. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "The warming of surface waters directly benefits cyanobacteria because their growth rates can peak between 25–35 °C. Growth rates of other phytoplankton decline once they exceed optimal temperatures of 15–25 °C."
- **[⚠ partial]** Potentially toxin-producing taxa dominate these reservoirs' cyanobacterial communities: 9 of the 11 most abundant taxa recorded across all reservoirs and sampling events are possible toxin producers, and their densities increased since the early-to-mid 2000s.
  - *evidence:* Taxonomic-composition finding used to argue the rising cell densities carry real human-health relevance, not just biomass increase. (Results)
  - *quote:* "9 of the 11 most abundant taxa among all reservoirs and sampling events being possible toxin producers."
  - *reviewer:* The source confirms that 9 of the 11 most abundant taxa are possible toxin producers, but does not explicitly state that their densities increased since the early-to-mid 2000s. While overall cyanobacteria densities increased sharply after 2003-2005, the source provides no taxon-specific time series to verify that these particular 9 taxa drove the increase.
- **[✓ verified]** Findings rest on a substantial long-term monitoring record: 20 USACE reservoirs, 1987-2018, with 775 sampling events at the deepest station and 23,043 cyanobacteria cell-density records spanning four depths (surface, 1.52, 3.05, 6.10 m).
  - *evidence:* Establishes the scale/statistical power underlying the trend claims. (Methods)
  - *quote:* "In total among the 20 reservoirs, cyanobacteria were collected from 775 sampling events at the deepest location of reservoirs and included 23,043 cyanobacteria cell density records of taxa from all surface, 1.52, 3.05, and 6.10 m depths."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors argue that reduced within-year sampling frequency after 2005 should, if anything, have made blooms and increasing trends harder to detect, implying the true increase is unlikely to be an artifact of more frequent recent sampling.
  - *evidence:* Authors' own defense against a plausible detection-bias confound (more sampling = more detected blooms). (Discussion (limitations))
  - *quote:* "The trends we report are even more striking because the number of within year sampling events of reservoirs has been fewer since 2005, which should reduce the likelihood of observing bloom events and increasing trends."
- **[✓ verified]** Data from targeted monitoring conducted since 2012 in response to publicly reported HAB events were deliberately excluded from the trend analysis to avoid bias from non-random, bloom-triggered sampling; only routine ambient monitoring data were analyzed.
  - *evidence:* A methodological safeguard against a form of selection/detection bias that could otherwise inflate apparent bloom trends. (Methods)
  - *quote:* "Beginning in 2012, the USACE instituted additional monitoring in response to publicly reported harmful algal bloom events. These data, however, were excluded from analyses due to their targeted nature and differences in methods."
- **[✓ verified]** Nutrient data were sparse, especially in 2000-2006, which limits confidence in the nutrient-trend conclusions relative to the temperature/dissolved-oxygen trend conclusions.
  - *evidence:* Authors' own data-quality caveat about an input to their analysis. (Methods/Results (nutrients))
  - *quote:* "Nutrient data were sparse, especially between 2000–2006, but some descriptive patterns and trends emerged based on available data."

## Data / numbers
- 20 temperate reservoirs studied (USA), built by USACE 1953-1983; surface areas 1-43 km^2; watershed forest cover 1-84%
- Reservoir grouping: 5 forested (>65% watershed forest cover, strongly stratifying); 7 agricultural (>30% agricultural cover, strongly stratifying); 7 agricultural + 1 urban (74% developed), weakly/non-stratifying
- Monitoring period: 1987-2018 (32 years); temperature/DO depth profiles since 1989 at 1.524 m intervals
- 775 sampling events at deepest reservoir location; 23,043 cyanobacteria cell-density records across surface, 1.52 m, 3.05 m, 6.10 m depths
- WHO-based risk thresholds used: low <20,000 cells mL^-1; moderate 20,000-100,000 cells mL^-1; high >100,000 cells mL^-1
- Pre-2003: 83% of observations (n=132) <20,000 cells/mL; 16% (n=26) 20,000-99,999 cells/mL; 1 observation >100,000 cells/mL
- Since 2009: 75% of yearly observations (n=110) >100,000 cells/mL (high risk); 21% (n=30) moderate; 4% (n=6) low; 53% of forested-reservoir observations were high-risk
- GAM for stratifying reservoirs: 56.3% deviance explained; adjusted R^2 = 0.534 (per figure caption), predictors = summer precipitation z-scores, June surface water temperature, surface-water TKN; n=88 complete observations (23 from 1992-2003, 25 from 2006-2011, 40 from 2012-2018)
- GAM for weakly/non-stratifying reservoirs: only 22% deviance explained; predictors = August deep-water temperature, June dissolved oxygen
- Surface-water warming, 1989 vs 2018 monthly means (Table 1): approximately +0.5 degC (July, forested stratifying) to +3.6 degC (October, forested & agricultural stratifying)
- Deep-water warming, 1989 vs 2018 monthly means (Table 1): approximately +1.5 degC (June, forested stratifying) to +4.5 degC (October, non-stratifying agricultural)
- Deep-water dissolved oxygen change, 1989 vs 2018 monthly means (Table 1): approximately 0.0 mg/L (August, forested stratifying) to about -2.1 mg/L (May, agricultural stratifying)
- Deep-water hypoxia duration lengthened by about two months in stratifying and non-stratifying agricultural reservoirs since the start of the record
- Cyanobacteria optimal growth temperature range 25-35 degC vs 15-25 degC for other phytoplankton groups (general physiological point, not a regression result from this study's own data)
- 9 of 11 most abundant cyanobacteria taxa (across all reservoirs/events) were possible toxin producers
- Watershed land cover nearly unchanged 2001-2011; only 3 highly forested watersheds showed 1.1-4.5% forest-cover decreases (replaced mostly by 0.4-2.6% barren land and 0.8-1.8% herbaceous/grassland)
- By end of 2015, each of the 20 reservoirs had recorded at least 1 high-risk (>100,000 cells/mL) observation; by 2018 all 20 reservoirs had recorded blooms exceeding that threshold

## Methods
Data: routine (non-targeted) ambient in-situ monitoring by the US Army Corps of Engineers across 20 reservoirs, 1987-2018 - cyanobacteria cell densities by taxon at multiple depths (surface, 1.52, 3.05, 6.10 m; 775 events, 23,043 records); water temperature and dissolved oxygen depth profiles since 1989 (1.524 m intervals); nutrient data (total Kjeldahl nitrogen, nitrate-nitrite, ammonia, total phosphorus, chlorophyll a, Secchi depth) collected sporadically since 1987; summer (June-August) precipitation. Analysis restricted to June-September each year. Statistics: LOWESS smoothing (0.5 sampling proportion) and linear regression on log-transformed yearly maximum cell densities to characterize trends; generalized additive models (GAMs, reservoir treated as random effect, R package mgcv) to relate cell densities to environmental predictors, run separately for stratifying vs. weakly/non-stratifying reservoirs; Spearman correlations for univariate predictor screening; Theil-Sen nonparametric slope estimator (R package zyp) cross-checked against linear-regression slopes (reported as "nearly identical"); Mann-Kendall tests (R package kendall) for monotonic trend detection. Software: Microsoft Excel 2016, SigmaPlot 14.0, R 3.6.1. Where the modeling "worked": in stratifying reservoirs, three predictors (summer precipitation z-scores, June surface water temperature, surface TKN) explained the majority of deviance (56.3%; adjusted R2=0.534). Where it did not work as well: in weakly/non-stratifying reservoirs the best predictors (August deep-water temperature, June dissolved oxygen) explained only 22% of deviance, an explicit, honestly-reported weaker result.

## Stated limitations
Authors explicitly state: (1) cyanotoxin concentrations were never measured, so WHO cell-density thresholds were used only as a proxy for human-health risk; (2) nutrient data were sparse, especially 2000-2006, weakening confidence in nutrient-trend conclusions; (3) the number of within-year sampling events fell after 2005, which the authors argue biases against (not toward) detecting increasing bloom trends, making the reported trends "even more striking"; (4) sampling frequency and dates varied within/among years and reservoirs due to resource and staffing variability, though the authors state no systematic bias or sudden change in sampling timing was evident; (5) post-2012 targeted monitoring data collected in response to publicly reported HAB events were deliberately excluded to avoid analyzing non-representative, bloom-triggered sampling; (6) within-year bloom dynamics could not be examined due to data limitations; (7) an unexpected pattern of deep-water warming in stratifying reservoirs during August-October lacked a clear mechanistic explanation, and the authors state data were inadequate to test candidate explanations (geomorphology, dam operations, hydrologic dynamics); (8) growth-rate responses to warming and nutrients "can be quite variable among species and strains" and warming-nutrient synergies "might not always be consistent and may vary among lake types"; (9) the study is geographically limited to temperate-plains and southern-Appalachian US reservoirs, limiting generalizability elsewhere.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper attributes much of the observed rise in bloom severity to warming, precipitation patterns, and surface-water TKN, while explicitly stating that "no clear and consistent changes in watershed land cover, precipitation, and total nitrogen or phosphorus concentrations" occurred over the same period - i.e., it documents intensifying blooms without a corresponding rise in bulk total-N/total-P loading. This sits in some tension with HAB literature that centers total (especially total phosphorus) nutrient-loading reduction as the primary driver/lever (e.g., Lake Erie-style narratives); here, surface-water TKN specifically (not total N or total P) was one of only three significant GAM predictors, and the relationship is explicitly correlational (associations "among years"), not a manipulated causal test - the authors do not claim nutrients are unimportant, only that bulk nutrient concentrations did not trend upward the way bloom densities did in these particular reservoirs. The study also relies entirely on WHO cell-density thresholds as a proxy for toxin risk because "cyanobacteria toxin concentrations were not monitored" - a limitation shared with other cell-count-based HAB studies, which can diverge from toxin-concentration-based (e.g., microcystin) risk assessments used elsewhere in the field. Finally, this is a ground-based, in-situ-only analysis with no remote-sensing/satellite component, so it does not itself validate a satellite-fusion approach; rather it supplies candidate in-situ/weather predictor variables (June surface temperature, summer precipitation z-scores, TKN, deep-water temperature/DO, stratification timing) and honestly reports that these predictors explain much less variance (22% deviance) in weakly/non-stratifying reservoirs than in stratifying ones (56.3% deviance) - a caution against assuming one driver set transfers across reservoir/lake types.

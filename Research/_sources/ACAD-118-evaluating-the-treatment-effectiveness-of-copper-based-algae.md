# Evaluating the treatment effectiveness of copper-based algaecides on toxic algae Microcystis aeruginosa using single cell-inductively coupled plasma-mass spectrometry

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6684568/

**What it is.** A 2019 laboratory (analytical chemistry) study that develops a single cell-inductively coupled plasma-mass spectrometry (SC-ICP-MS) method to measure copper and magnesium mass in individual Microcystis aeruginosa cells, then uses that method (plus flow cytometry and LC-MS/MS) to evaluate how two copper-based algaecides — cupric sulfate and the commercial product EarthTec® — kill M. aeruginosa cells and cause release of the toxin microcystin-LR (MC-LR).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** The novel SC-ICP-MS method is highly sensitive and reproducible: detection limits of 65 ag/cell for intracellular copper and 98 ag/cell for intracellular magnesium, with relative standard deviation within 12%.
  - *evidence:* Reported as the method's core analytical-performance result in the abstract, and detailed further with uncertainty (±) in the results/methods text. (Abstract; Results (detection limits section))
  - *quote:* "detection limits of intracellular copper and magnesium were 65 ag/cell and 98 ag/cell, respectively... reproducible (relative standard deviation within 12%)"
- **[✓ verified]** Both copper-based algaecides (cupric sulfate and EarthTec®), tested across 0–200 μg/L copper, successfully diminished M. aeruginosa populations at the 60, 100, and 200 μg/L doses, with higher copper concentrations producing faster copper uptake and faster cell lysis.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the headline dose-response finding in both the abstract and the conclusions, based on the SC-ICP-MS, flow-cytometry, and time-course data. (Abstract; Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "Treatments with 60, 100, and 200 μg/L copper as cupric sulfate and EarthTec® successfully diminished M. aeruginosa populations... The higher the copper concentration used to treat the cells, the faster the speeds of copper uptake and cell lysis"
- **[✓ verified]** At the lowest tested dose (30 μg/L copper), cell integrity was largely preserved: more than 70% of cells remained intact after 18 h of EarthTec® treatment, while cupric sulfate at 30 μg/L showed no significant change in the percentage of cells with detectable magnesium.
  - *evidence:* Low-dose comparison from the time-course results, showing the effective/ineffective boundary of the dose range tested. (Results, cupric sulfate and EarthTec® treatment subsections)
  - *quote:* "more than 70% of cells remained intact after treatment for 18 h [30 μg/L EarthTec®]; ... 30 μg/L [cupric sulfate]: percentage of cells with detectable magnesium 'did not change significantly'"
- **[✓ verified]** Cupric sulfate acted faster than EarthTec®: extracellular magnesium (the lysis marker) rose sooner after cupric sulfate treatment than after EarthTec® treatment at comparable copper doses.
  - *evidence:* Direct comparative-kinetics statement made by the authors from the two treatment time-course datasets. (Results/Discussion, comparison of algaecide formulations)
  - *quote:* "Extracellular magnesium increased faster in M. aeruginosa cells that were treated with cupric sulfate compared with EarthTec®, indicating cupric sulfate functions faster than EarthTec®."
- **[✓ verified]** Individual M. aeruginosa cells show marked heterogeneity in copper uptake: cells that take up copper quickly lyse at a lower intracellular copper content, while slower-accumulating cells survive longer and reach a higher intracellular copper mass before lysing.
  - *evidence:* This single-cell heterogeneity result is the paper's central justification for using single-cell (rather than bulk/population-averaged) analysis. (Abstract; Results (single-cell mass distribution discussion))
  - *quote:* "cells that uptake copper fast would lyse once reaching a certain content of intracellular copper and cells that uptake copper slowly would stay alive and accumulate a higher amount of mass before lysing"
- **[✓ verified]** Only a small fraction of the nominal dosed copper was ever recovered as intracellular copper across all treatments (roughly 3.64%–8.13% depending on dose and formulation), implying most of the applied copper stayed outside the cells or was otherwise unaccounted for.
  - *evidence:* Mass-balance comparison of measured intracellular copper against the nominal applied dose, presented by the authors with an explicit caveat about the magnitude. (Results, copper uptake/mass-balance section)
  - *quote:* "these only represent a lesser percentage (within analytical deviation range) of dosed copper [cupric sulfate: 8.13% (30 μg/L), 5.99% (60 μg/L), 5.83% (100 μg/L), 5.72% (200 μg/L); EarthTec®: 6.44% (30 μg/L), 7.69% (60 μg/L), 5.29% (100 μg/L), 3.64% (200 μg/L)]"
- **[✓ verified]** Killing the algae does not remove the toxin: cell lysis released intracellular microcystin-LR into the water in a dose-dependent way, while total (intra+extracellular) MC-LR concentration stayed unchanged, showing the toxin was redistributed, not degraded, by algaecide treatment.
  - *evidence:* This is the paper's central management-relevant caveat, drawn from paired UFLC-MS/MS measurements of intracellular vs. extracellular MC-LR across treatments. (Results (MC-LR release); Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "extracellular MC-LR increased in a dose-dependent manner while total MC-LR concentrations remained unchanged... further treatment to remove the released algal toxin from the treated water would be needed"
- **[✓ verified]** The two copper formulations differed in the cell states they produced: EarthTec® treatment left a mix of intact-viable and intact-nonviable cells, whereas cupric sulfate treatment produced primarily intact-viable cells (prior to lysis), consistent with the two products' different uptake/lysis kinetics.
  - *evidence:* Flow-cytometry viability comparison (SYTO9/propidium iodide) supporting the formulation-kinetics difference. (Results, flow cytometry viability comparison)
  - *quote:* "both intact viable cells and intact nonviable cells [EarthTec®] ... primarily intact viable cells [cupric sulfate]"

## Methods
Single cell-ICP-MS (PerkinElmer NexION 300D with Syngistix Single Cell Application software) was used to measure copper (⁶⁵Cu) and magnesium (²⁴Mg, an intrinsic element used to determine real transport efficiency and true cell concentration) mass in individual M. aeruginosa (strain UTEX LB 2388) cells, without extensive post-dosing sample prep. Instrument settings: RF power 1600 W, nebulizer gas ~0.5 L/min (re-optimized daily), makeup gas 0.7 L/min, sample flow 21–22 μL/min, dwell time 100 μs, scan time 100 s; daily transport efficiency 45.56–63.65%. Cells were grown in 5x-diluted BG-11 medium (no added Cu/Mn/Mg) and harvested at late exponential phase (14–22 days). Two copper sources were compared: reagent-grade cupric sulfate (CuSO4, stock 100 μg Cu/mL) and the commercial algaecide EarthTec® (19.8% cupric sulfate pentahydrate). Cells were dosed at 0, 30, 60, 100, and 200 μg/L copper and sampled over a time course out to 18 h (key points 1, 2, 4, 8, 18 h); samples were diluted with 0.1 mM EDTA (to chelate extracellular copper) and analyzed immediately. Orthogonal methods: flow cytometry (BD Accuri C6, SYTO9 + propidium iodide staining) for cell viability/integrity, and UFLC-MS/MS for intracellular vs. extracellular microcystin-LR. QA/QC included a certified water reference standard (CRM-TMDW-A), calibration R² > 0.98 (Mg) and > 0.99 (Cu), and spike recoveries of 80–100%; only the 60 μg/L treatments were run in duplicate (due to the need to analyze all samples immediately across three methods), with all samples run in at least triplicate instrument reads. The method works well for rapid, sensitive (sub-fg/cell), single-cell-resolved detection of metal uptake and correlates uptake with lysis (via Mg loss) and toxin release (via MC-LR); it is described as not resolving the exact intracellular-copper lysis threshold in every treatment group, and it recovers only a small fraction of nominally dosed copper as intracellular mass (see data_numbers), leaving the fate of the remaining copper (precipitation, extracellular/wall-bound, media chemistry) unresolved by this method.

## Stated limitations
The authors note: (1) cupric sulfate and EarthTec® treatments used cells from different batches, which they invoke to explain an inconsistency in the 30 μg/L magnesium-release results between the two formulations — a potential confound for direct formulation comparisons; (2) due to the need to analyze every sample immediately across three analytical methods (SC-ICP-MS, flow cytometry, UFLC-MS/MS), only the 60 μg/L treatments were duplicated, limiting statistical replication across doses; (3) the exact intracellular copper content that triggers lysis, and the dose/time at which 100% of cells contain detectable copper, were not established in every treatment group; (4) intracellular copper measured by SC-ICP-MS accounted for only ~3.6–8.1% of the nominal dosed copper, an unresolved mass-balance gap; (5) a small low-mass peak attributed to old cell debris containing residual Mg had to be manually excluded when counting intact cells, a potential source of subjectivity/measurement uncertainty; (6) most centrally, the authors state that copper accumulation and cell lysis alone do not reduce microcystin-LR — treatment controls the algae population but requires an additional step to remove/degrade the released toxin.

## Tensions with other findings
This source cuts against any treatment framing that equates algaecide-driven cell death (cell-count or chlorophyll decline) with a resolved bloom risk: it shows mechanistically that copper-induced lysis is precisely what releases stored microcystin-LR into the water, and that total toxin mass is not reduced by treatment — only redistributed from intracellular to extracellular (dissolved) form. Any HAB tool or product claim that recommends copper algaecide dosing purely to 'clear the bloom' would need this caveat: a successful kill (by cell-density metrics) can coincide with a spike in dissolved toxin exposure risk immediately post-treatment, which is a source of tension with treatment-recommendation framings that use cell density/chlorophyll-a alone as the success metric. Separately, the finding that plain cupric sulfate lysed cells faster than the commercial chelated/complexed product EarthTec® in this lab setting is a data point relevant to (but not proof against) marketing claims that chelated copper formulations are preferred for controlled-release, lower non-target-toxicity dosing in the field; this study only measured speed of algal lysis in controlled lab conditions, not selectivity, environmental persistence, or field efficacy.

# Nitrogen Limitation of Intense and Toxic Cyanobacteria Blooms in Lakes within Two of the Most Visited Parks in the USA: The Lake in Central Park and Prospect Park Lake

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9612084/

**What it is.** A five-year (2015–2019) nearshore water-quality/toxin monitoring record plus a detailed 2020 open-water sampling campaign and manipulative nutrient-addition/dilution bioassay experiments on two small, artificial, heavily-visited urban lakes in New York City parks (The Lake in Central Park [LCP] and Prospect Park Lake [PPL]). The paper's purpose is to characterize the intensity/toxicity of the cyanobacterial blooms in these lakes and to test experimentally whether nitrogen (N) or phosphorus (P) is the proximate factor limiting cyanobacterial biomass and microcystin production.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Both lakes exhibited extremely elevated cyanobacterial biomass and microcystin over five years of nearshore monitoring, with cyanobacteria dominating phytoplankton biomass.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the Abstract/Results as the headline empirical finding of the 2015–2019 nearshore dataset (five years of sampling at both LCP and PPL). (Abstract; Results (2015–2019 nearshore dataset))
  - *quote:* "microcystin levels averaging 920 µg L−1 and chlorophyll a from cyanobacterial (cyano-chla) populations averaging 1.0 × 10^5 µg cyano-chla L−1"
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria made up more than 99% of total phytoplankton biomass across both lakes from 2015 to 2019.
  - *evidence:* Direct descriptive statistic reported from the multi-year nearshore biomass dataset (Fluoroprobe-partitioned chlorophyll a). (Results (2015–2019 nearshore dataset))
  - *quote:* "cyanobacteria dominated phytoplankton biomass making up more than 99% of total phytoplankton biomass from 2015 to 2019"
- **[✓ verified]** Nutrient-addition and dilution bioassay experiments showed that nitrogen, not phosphorus, consistently limited cyanobacterial growth, whereas green algae were rarely nutrient-limited.
  - *evidence:* This is an experimental (manipulative) result from replicated nutrient-dilution/addition bioassays in 2018 and 2020, not merely an observational correlation, giving it stronger causal weight than most driver studies. (Abstract; Results (2018 and 2020 bioassay experiments))
  - *quote:* "Nutrient addition and dilution experiments revealed that N consistently limited cyanobacterial populations but that green algae were rarely nutrient limited."
- **[✓ verified]** DIN:DIP molar ratios in both lakes stayed well below the Redfield ratio of 5 all season and fell below 1 during the July–August bloom peak, indicating N was scarce relative to P exactly when blooms were most intense.
  - *evidence:* Derived from 2020 nutrient chemistry time series (NOx, NH4+, orthophosphate) at both LCP and PPL. (Results/Discussion (2020 nutrient chemistry))
  - *quote:* "DIN:DIP ratios in both LCP and PPL were well-below the Redfield ratio (<5) throughout the sampling season and were at their lowest (<1) during July and August when the cyanobacterial blooms were at their peak."
- **[✓ verified]** Absolute dissolved inorganic nitrogen concentrations, not just the N:P ratio, were low enough (below ~2 µM for most of the summer) to be growth-limiting on their own, since this is below the reported half-saturation constant for most bloom-forming cyanobacteria.
  - *evidence:* Authors compare measured DIN levels against literature half-saturation constants to argue for absolute (not just relative) N scarcity. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "the absolute levels of NOx and NH4+ were <2 µM for most of the summer, a level below the half-saturation constant for most bloom forming cyanobacteria"
- **[✓ verified]** In manipulative bioassays, phosphorus addition alone never stimulated cyanobacterial or green-algal growth in LCP, while nitrogen addition (5 or 15 µM nitrate) significantly increased cyanobacterial growth in both lakes.
  - *evidence:* Direct experimental result from the 2018 triplicate nutrient-dilution bioassay design, analyzed by two-way ANOVA/Tukey HSD. (Results (2018 bioassay experiments, LCP))
  - *quote:* "Phosphorus alone had no impact on growth of cyanobacteria or green algae in any experiment conducted in LCP."
- **[✓ verified]** Public drinking water used to replenish these lakes during summer evaporation is comparatively rich in phosphorus and poor in nitrogen, and experimental addition of that drinking water to lake water significantly enhanced cyanobacterial growth rates.
  - *evidence:* Combines the measured NYC drinking-water nutrient composition with a bioassay in which 50% drinking-water amendments were tested against controls. (Abstract; Methods/Results (drinking-water composition and 2018 bioassay))
  - *quote:* "Experimental additions of public drinking water that is rich in P and, to a lesser extent N, to lake water significantly enhanced cyanobacterial growth rates"
- **[✓ verified]** Measured microcystin concentrations routinely and vastly exceeded recreational and health-based guideline levels (EPA 8 µg/L; WHO 20 µg/L 'moderate probability of adverse effects'; NY DEC >20 µg/L notification threshold), framed by the authors as a potential public-health concern given these parks' heavy visitation.
  - *evidence:* Authors directly compare their measured nearshore microcystin values against named regulatory/advisory guideline concentrations. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "Microcystin levels were, therefore, consistently orders of magnitudes above these guideline levels, indicating that these lakes are a potential public health threat to the more than 40 million people visiting them annually."
- **[✓ verified]** The microcystin-synthetase gene marker mcyE was significantly correlated with measured microcystin toxin concentrations in the 2020 dataset across both lakes.
  - *evidence:* Statistical correlation (reported significance level only, no r/rho value captured in the extracted text) between a qPCR gene-abundance measure and the ELISA-measured toxin concentration. (Results (2020 qPCR/molecular data))
  - *quote:* "there was a significant correlation between the levels of mcyE and concentrations of microcystin (p < 0.0001"
- **[⚠ partial]** The saxitoxin-synthesis gene sxtA was detected at high copy numbers (up to ~10^6 copies/mL), especially in PPL and in early summer, but actual saxitoxin toxin concentrations were not measured, leaving open whether saxitoxin itself reached concerning levels.
  - *evidence:* Authors distinguish gene-marker detection (qPCR) from toxin quantification and explicitly flag this as an evidence gap needing further study. (Results (2020 qPCR data); Discussion (limitations))
  - *quote:* "less is known about saxitoxin production by cyanobacteria in freshwater compared to microcystin, and saxitoxin is rarely detected in US lakes"
  - *reviewer:* The claim attributes the ~10^6 copy number level to 'especially in PPL,' but source data shows LCP had the peak (2.26 ± 0.26 × 10^6 copies/mL on 7/15/2020) while PPL remained in the 10^5 range (~4.2 × 10^5). The location attribution is reversed.
- **[✓ verified]** The very high nearshore microcystin/biomass values reported for 2015–2019 are not representative of open/offshore lake conditions: 2020 offshore sampling found levels generally orders of magnitude lower even though blooms still persisted all summer.
  - *evidence:* Authors directly compare the two sampling regimes (nearshore high-traffic vs. offshore open-water) and attribute the large discrepancy to sampling location rather than a true year-to-year decline. (Discussion (stated limitation / methodological caveat))
  - *quote:* "the 2020 sampling occurred further offshore in both lakes and revealed that while cyanobacterial blooms persisted all summer within open waters, levels were generally orders of magnitude below what was present in nearshore samples."
- **[✓ verified]** The specific sources supplying nitrogen to these urban lakes (sediment release, drinking-water supplementation, watershed/runoff loading) were not apportioned or quantified in this study.
  - *evidence:* Stated explicitly by the authors as an open question needed for follow-up work, distinguishing 'N is limiting' (established) from 'where the N comes from' (not established). (Discussion (limitations))
  - *quote:* "the primary sources of N to these urban lakes is unknown"

## Methods
2015–2019: nearshore (high-traffic shoreline) grab samples collected by Prospect Park Alliance and Central Park Conservancy, coordinated with NY DEC and NYC Parks; phytoplankton group biomass via BBE Moldenke Fluoroprobe (chlorophyll a partitioned to cyanobacteria/green/brown algae) plus inverted-microscope cell counts (Sedgewick-Rafter, Lugol's-preserved); microcystin via Eurofins Abraxis ELISA (triple freeze–thaw, ~96±2% recovery, ±2% precision). 2020: bi-weekly (June–October) sampling shifted to open/offshore central-lake stations; YSI ProPlus sonde for temperature/DO; nutrients (NOx, NH4+, orthophosphate, TN, TP) via 0.2 µm filtration and Lachat Instruments autosampler wet chemistry; qPCR (Phytoxigene CyanoDTec assay, 5-point standard curve 20–200,000 copies/µL, 95°C/2min then 40 cycles 95°C/15s+60°C/45s) for 16S rRNA (total cyanobacteria), mcyE (microcystin synthetase) and sxtA (saxitoxin) genes. Causal nutrient-limitation testing used replicated (triplicate) nutrient-addition/dilution bioassays: 2018 modified nutrient-dilution assays (lake water + Major Ion Solution ± 5/15 µM nitrate, ±20/68 µM orthophosphate, combined N+P treatments, or 50% NYC drinking water), incubated ~24 h in an outdoor flow-through sea table at ~0.3 m-equivalent light and in-situ temperature; 2020 added whole-water (250 mL, unamended vs. +20 µM nitrate/+2 µM orthophosphate/both) and dilution bioassays run the same way. Statistics: two-way ANOVA with Tukey HSD post hoc, Spearman rank-order correlation matrix (2020), significance reported at p<0.05/0.01/0.001/0.0001, analyzed in R. The paper reports the approach working to demonstrate N-limitation via converging lines of evidence (low DIN:DIP, low absolute DIN vs. half-saturation constants, and bioassay growth responses), and reports it failing/uncertain for apportioning the actual sources of that nitrogen and for quantifying saxitoxin itself (only its synthesis gene was measured).

## Stated limitations
The authors state: (1) the 2020 open-water/offshore sampling showed cyanobacteria and microcystin "generally orders of magnitude below" the 2015–2019 nearshore values, so the alarming 5-year nearshore averages are not directly comparable to lake-wide/open-water conditions and reflect a specific high-contact zone; (2) "the primary sources of N to these urban lakes is unknown," and the paper could not quantify the relative contribution of sediment release, drinking-water supplementation, versus watershed loading; (3) "the precise amounts of drinking water added to these New York City lakes during summer is unknown"; (4) saxitoxin itself was not quantified — only the sxtA gene marker was measured — and the authors note reliance on ELISA (vs. HPLC/LC-MS) "may have prohibited quantification of low concentrations" of saxitoxin, calling for further study; (5) green algae's nutrient-limitation response may have been masked by their comparatively low biomass; (6) microcystin measurements in the 2015–2019 (Figures 1–2) dataset were not replicated (no per-sample replicate error bars for that portion of the record).

## Tensions with other findings
This paper's central finding — that N, not P, is the proximate limiting nutrient for cyanobacterial biomass and microcystin production in these two lakes — runs counter to the conventional freshwater-management paradigm that treats phosphorus as the master limiting nutrient for lake eutrophication/HABs, and the authors frame the result that way. It also surfaces a counterintuitive, system-specific mechanism: topping up these artificial, tributary-less urban lakes with P-rich, comparatively N-poor municipal drinking water (N:P <0.5) to offset summer evaporation may sharpen N limitation and intensify blooms — an artifact of urban-lake water management that likely does not generalize to naturally-fed lakes/reservoirs (e.g., the larger CyAN-monitored waterbodies more typical of remote-sensing HAB work), so the N-limitation conclusion should not be assumed to transfer geography-for-geography. Note also the internal tension flagged by the authors themselves: the headline "public health threat" framing (920 µg/L average microcystin, orders of magnitude over guidelines) is drawn from high-traffic nearshore sampling (2015–2019), while the more rigorous, offshore, better-replicated 2020 data show concentrations one-to-two-plus orders of magnitude lower — so the degree of hazard depends heavily on exactly where/how one samples within the same lake, a caution relevant to any driver or risk claim built from single-station monitoring. As a methodological point for the broader review, this study's manipulative bioassays provide experimental (not merely correlational) support for N-limitation, which is a stronger causal standard than most observational HAB-driver studies, but the specific drinking-water-supplementation pathway is a plausible confound unique to these two urban systems.

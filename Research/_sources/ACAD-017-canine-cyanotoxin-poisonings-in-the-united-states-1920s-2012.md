# Canine Cyanotoxin Poisonings in the United States (1920s–2012): Review of Suspected and Confirmed Cases from Three Data Sources

**What it is.** A retrospective epidemiological review that compiles and cross-compares U.S. reports of suspected/confirmed canine cyanotoxin poisoning from three independent sources — CDC's HABISS passive surveillance system (2007–2011, 13 states), UC Davis Veterinary Medical Teaching Hospital (VMTH) necropsy/biopsy records (1984–2012, California), and a literature/media/anecdotal-report search (late 1920s–2012) — to characterize the toxins, geography, seasonality, and severe under-detection/under-reporting behind reported cyanoHAB-associated dog illness and death in the U.S., and to argue for dogs as sentinels of local HAB risk to humans.

## Methods
Tri-source retrospective compilation and comparison, not a controlled study: (1) HABISS — CDC/NCEH's passive, web-based active-surveillance database ("Rapid Data Collector" tool) into which 13 states (9 funded via an NCEH cooperative agreement) voluntarily entered bloom dates, exposure route, symptoms, test results and diagnoses for 2007–2011, applying suspect/probable/confirmed case definitions; (2) VMTH — keyword search (e.g., "hepatotoxin," "cyanobacteria," "microcystin," "anatoxin," "amatoxin") of biopsy/necropsy accession records 1984–2012, with a veterinary pathologist reviewing each case and assigning a low/moderate/high likelihood of cyanotoxin poisoning based on (a) toxin/algae detection in animal, water, or scum samples, (b) history of bloom/contaminated-water exposure, or (c) history of swimming, plus histopathology criteria (centrilobular/panlobular hepatocellular necrosis, apoptosis, hemorrhage, etc.); (3) a search of peer-reviewed literature (PubMed), gray literature, newspaper archives, Google-indexed web reports, and personal communications with owners/veterinarians, with no fixed start date, abstracting date, location, water-body type, organism/toxin, dogs affected, breed, signs, and diagnosis/reference. Cases across the three sources were then cross-matched by state, month/year, and water body to quantify overlap/duplication. It works to assemble by far the largest documented tally of canine cyanotoxin cases and to expose how differently each channel captures cases (toxin type, region, confirmation rate); it does not (and the authors say cannot) yield an unbiased national incidence estimate, given passive/voluntary reporting, a single-state veterinary hospital with referral bias, and a literature/media search inherently biased toward dramatic, publishable, or reported events.

## Stated limitations
The authors state directly that the compiled cases "likely represent a small fraction" of the true annual U.S. burden. Specific caveats given: HABISS's catchment was limited to 13 states and VMTH's to California, so neither is nationally representative; HABISS was passive surveillance (states "encouraged, but not required" to report), so true case counts were likely higher; testing for cyanotoxins is expensive and access is limited, so cases are under-confirmed and under-reported; owners/veterinarians may euthanize or give supportive care without a confirmatory work-up; VMTH is a referral hospital, so acute toxicosis/hepatic-failure deaths in the wider dog population are "probably far more common" than its case files show; microcystin poisoning and mushroom (Amanita) poisoning produce overlapping, often indistinguishable hepatic lesions and clinical signs, and the archived samples (e.g., cryopreserved liver) needed to definitively tell them apart were frequently no longer available, so "mushroom poisoning appears to be a default diagnosis at present"; only 1 of 45 (2%) VMTH cyanotoxin-suspect cases was ever published in the peer-reviewed literature; exposure could not be verified for cases reported before cyanotoxin analytical methods existed; and the authors explicitly say it is "unknown" whether the rise in reported cases since the mid-1970s reflects a true increase in poisoning events, better case recognition, random chance, or some combination.

## Tensions with other findings
This source is a case-report/surveillance compilation, not a bloom-intensity or remote-sensing time series, so it cannot be directly fused with satellite chlorophyll/phycocyanin signals the way the brief's Part A calls for — its value here is contextual/motivational (stakes, sentinel framing) rather than as model input. Two points complicate naive use of HAB "incidence" data generally, relevant to any driver/trend claims this project might make: (1) toxin-attribution patterns are starkly source-dependent — VMTH cases were overwhelmingly microcystin (61%) while the independent media/literature and HABISS sources were dominated by anatoxin/Anabaena fatalities (58% and 32% of deaths respectively) — a pattern the data are consistent with reflecting differential case-capture (a hepatotoxicosis-oriented referral hospital vs. dramatic acute-death media reports) rather than necessarily true regional or temporal differences in which cyanotoxin is most prevalent or lethal; the paper does not itself adjudicate which explanation is correct. (2) The reported rise in cyanoHAB-linked dog events/deaths beginning in the mid-1970s is presented by the authors themselves as ambiguous — possibly real, possibly an artifact of improved surveillance/reporting infrastructure (HABISS itself only began in 2007) and media/internet growth — which is a direct caution against reading rising report counts alone as evidence of worsening bloom frequency, a distinction this project's own fidelity standards (correlation vs. causation, no result-shopping) require flagging wherever "HABs are increasing" is asserted from count-based, non-standardized surveillance data.

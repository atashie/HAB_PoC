# Cyanobacterial blooms. Ecology, prevention, mitigation and control: Editorial to a CYANOCOST Special Issue

**What it is.** This is the editorial introduction (Ibelings, Fastner, Bormans & Visser, 2016, Aquatic Ecology, Vol. 50, pp. 327-331, DOI 10.1007/s10452-016-9595-y) to a CYANOCOST (European COST Action) special issue on cyanobacterial bloom management. It frames the issue's roughly 13-14 contributed papers around a three-tier taxonomy of prevention, control, and mitigation, arguing that sustainable bloom management must center on controlling nutrient (P and N) availability at the catchment, inflow, and in-lake levels.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** The editors define three distinct, mutually exclusive management strategies for cyanobacterial blooms - prevention, control, and mitigation - distinguished by when in the bloom life-cycle the intervention acts.
  - *evidence:* Explicit definitional sentences in the editorial body distinguish stopping bloom formation outright, versus restricting growth of blooms that do form to an acceptable risk level, versus physically/chemically reducing blooms that have already formed. (Editorial main text, framing/introduction section)

## Methods
Not a primary-research or modeling paper. As the editorial for a CYANOCOST COST-Action special issue, it synthesizes and cross-references contributions that themselves used varied methods: an ecological-traits synthesis (Mantzouki et al. 2016); nutrient-load reviews for N and P control (Hamilton et al. 2016; Fastner et al. 2016, an 8-case-study review); solid-phase phosphorus-adsorbent development guidance (Douglas et al. 2016); reviews of physical in-lake methods and artificial mixing (Bormans et al. 2016; Visser et al. 2016); cyanocidal-compound chemistry (Matthijs et al. 2016); a biomanipulation review (Triest et al. 2016); a water-level-fluctuation management review (Bakker & Hilt 2016); an evaluation of proposed 'end-of-pipe' measures (Lurling et al. 2016); a long-term case study of the Dutch Borderlakes (Noordhuis et al. 2016); an integrated prevention/control strategy paper (Stroom & Kardinaal 2016); a socio-ecological stakeholder-engagement approach (Van Dolah et al. 2016); and a closing synthesis/critical review (Ibelings et al. 2016, companion paper DOI 10.1007/s10452-016-9596-x). The editorial's own contribution is definitional/organizational (the prevention-control-mitigation taxonomy) plus advocacy for nutrient-centered management 'from the catchment level up to in-lake treatment,' referencing WHO Water Safety Plan-style risk-management guidance.

## Stated limitations
This is an editorial/synthesis piece, not primary research: it presents no new data, experiments, or statistical analysis of its own, and its substantive content is a set of pointers to the ~13-14 papers in the special issue, each carrying its own methods and limitations not detailed here. The editors explicitly state that whether and how eutrophication and climate change interact 'largely remains an open question,' and that evidence for a nutrient-times-temperature synergy on bloom development is only 'scant' (citing Rigosi et al. 2014). For water-level fluctuation as a management lever, they note success 'is dependent on a great number of factors, including lake morphometry, sediment type, quality of (additional) inlet water and presence or absence of macrophyte propagule banks,' i.e., not a generically portable fix. They also state that some marketed control methods (e.g., ultrasound, effective microorganisms) have 'no - or insufficient - scientific support to warrant their application,' an explicit caution against unproven treatments. No quantitative thresholds or effect sizes are given anywhere in the editorial.

## Tensions with other findings
The editors' explicit caution that nutrient-times-temperature synergy evidence is only 'scant' (citing Rigosi et al. 2014) sits in tension with more deterministic 'warming causes/worsens blooms' framings elsewhere in the HAB literature - including the very 'Blooms like it hot' catchphrase (Paerl & Huisman 2008) the editorial also cites approvingly. The editors treat climate as a plausible but not firmly established amplifier of the primary, nutrient-driven mechanism rather than a co-equal or independent driver; other sources in a HAB review that assert a firm climate-bloom causal link should be read against this explicit correlation-vs-causation caveat from a field-representative COST Action. Similarly, the editorial's explicit rejection of unproven 'exotic' methods (ultrasound, effective microorganisms) may be in tension with vendor or case-report literature promoting such treatments without the controlled evidence the editors say is lacking.

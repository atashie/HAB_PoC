# Public Water System Harmful Algal Bloom Response Strategy

**What it is.** An October 2025 Ohio EPA (Division of Drinking and Ground Waters) regulatory guidance document that sets numerical cyanotoxin "do-not-drink" thresholds for finished drinking water and defines HAB/cyanotoxin monitoring, sampling, and analytical-method requirements for surface-water public water systems (PWSs) in Ohio. It also specifies public-notification/advisory procedures, source-water-surveillance practices, and mandatory treatment-optimization planning for responding to cyanotoxin detections.

## Data / numbers
- Microcystins Do-Not-Drink threshold, finished drinking water: 0.3 μg/L (children <6, incl. bottle-fed infants, and sensitive populations); 1.6 μg/L (children ≥6 and adults). Fixed regulatory value; no confidence interval/uncertainty stated (Table 1, Executive Summary, p.1).
- Anatoxin-a Do-Not-Drink threshold: 0.3 μg/L (children <6/sensitive populations); 1.6 μg/L (children ≥6/adults) (Table 1).
- Cylindrospermopsin Do-Not-Drink threshold: 0.7 μg/L (children <6/sensitive populations); 3.0 μg/L (children ≥6/adults) (Table 1).
- Saxitoxins Do-Not-Drink threshold: 0.3 μg/L (children <6/sensitive populations); 1.6 μg/L (children ≥6/adults); Ohio EPA also applies saxitoxin toxicity-equivalency factors (TEFs) when judging exceedance (Table 1 + footnote).
- HAB regulatory definition threshold: cyanobacteria cell count > 4,000 cells/mL of cyanotoxin-capable genera, OR visible water discoloration; cites Shambaugh and Brines, 2003 (List of Definitions).
- Notification deadline: PWS must notify Ohio EPA of any finished-water cyanotoxin detection 'as soon as possible, but no later than 24 hours'; after-hours spill hotline = 1-866-372-1800 (Executive Summary, p.1–2).
- Recommended public-notification deadline for saxitoxins/cylindrospermopsin/anatoxin-a after a confirmed resample+repeat exceedance: no later than 24 hours (Executive Summary, p.2).
- HAB season defined as the first full week of June through the first full week of December of the same calendar year; off-season = first full week of December through first full week of June of the following year (List of Definitions).
- Document version date: October 2025; the four cyanotoxin threshold values are stated as unchanged since the '2022 PWS HAB Response Strategy' (Executive Summary, p.1).

## Methods
This is a regulatory/administrative response protocol, not a statistical or predictive model. It specifies: (1) routine monitoring — all surface-water PWSs must conduct routine microcystins monitoring and cyanobacteria screening (OAC Rule 3745-90-03), with paired raw-water/finished-water samples (labeled "LT001"/"EP001") taken at the same time; (2) screening/analytical methods — cyanobacteria screening may use qPCR (targeting cyanobacteria-specific genes and cyanotoxin-production genes such as mcyE for microcystins, sxtA for saxitoxin, cyrA for cylindrospermopsin, or the 16S rRNA gene) or other Director-acceptable semiquantitative methods, while ELISA is described as a rapid assessment method commonly used to detect microcystins, cylindrospermopsin, anatoxin-a, and saxitoxin; phytoplankton samples support biovolume calculations of relative taxon abundance; (3) escalation — a cyanobacteria-screening detection triggers follow-up cyanotoxin testing and additional Ohio EPA sampling, and a finished-water action-level exceedance triggers a defined resample/repeat-sample sequence at the finished-water sampling point; (4) response — confirmed threshold exceedances trigger public notification (rule-mandated for microcystins under OAC 3745-90-06; recommended but not always rule-mandated for saxitoxins/cylindrospermopsin/anatoxin-a) and can lead to a formal drinking-water use advisory under ORC 6109.06; (5) treatment — every surface-water PWS must maintain a Treatment Optimization Protocol (OAC Rule 3745-90-05) detailing how it will optimize existing treatment for microcystins removal. Note on gaps in my excerpt: the retrieved text does not include the body content of Section 5 items ("5.3 Remote Sensing Surveillance," "5.5 Assessing Bloom Severity," "5.6 Reservoir Management") or the Section 7 treatment-optimization body — these appear only as Table-of-Contents headings — so this dossier cannot characterize what those sections say about method performance. No accuracy, sensitivity/specificity, or false-positive/negative statistics comparing ELISA, qPCR, and confirmatory (e.g., LC-MS/MS) methods are given in the retrieved text.

## Stated limitations
The source states the underlying science is still evolving ("the state of the science of HABs and their related cyanotoxins is evolving, and Ohio EPA will continue to update these guidelines as appropriate" — paraphrased due to an inserted hyperlink label interrupting the exact sentence in extraction), signaling the thresholds reflect current practice rather than a fixed, final scientific consensus. It frames its precautionary approach explicitly around the absence of a cure ("there is no known antidote for these cyanotoxins"), i.e., thresholds are precautionary/protective rather than shown in this excerpt with an explicit dose-response derivation (that derivation is deferred to "Appendix B: Basis for Cyanotoxin Thresholds," whose body text is not present in this retrieved excerpt — only its Table of Contents entry). The strategy is explicitly scoped to public water systems/drinking water; a separate Ohio strategy governs recreational-water HAB response with its own numerical thresholds, referenced but not reproduced here. Guidance for elderly and immunocompromised individuals is a soft, non-binding recommendation rather than a formal threshold-backed category, suggesting comparatively less-settled specification for that subgroup. Separately, as a limitation of my extraction rather than the source itself: the source PDF's two-column "List of Definitions" produced interleaved/garbled text in several spots in this OCR/text-extraction; I relied only on spans that reconstruct unambiguously and avoided verbatim-quoting garbled fragments. ## Tensions with other findings
This document defines "safe"/actionable cyanotoxin levels specifically for the finished-drinking-water/human-consumption exposure route in Ohio, and it explicitly notes a separate "Ohio HAB Response Strategy for Recreational Waters" sets different numerical cyanotoxin thresholds for recreational exposure ("The numerical cyanotoxin thresholds for recreational waters are identified in the current [Ohio HAB Response Strategy for Recreational Waters]") — underscoring that cyanotoxin threshold values in this literature review are exposure-route- and jurisdiction-specific and should not be conflated across sources that report a single "safe" cyanotoxin concentration without specifying route, population, and jurisdiction. Its regulatory HAB definition (cyanobacteria cell count > 4,000 cells/mL, citing Shambaugh and Brines, 2003) is one specific bloom-classification threshold; other sources in this dossier that define or detect HABs via remote-sensing cyanobacteria-index thresholds (e.g., EPA CyAN) or different cell-count/biomass cutoffs should be checked against this figure rather than assumed equivalent. The document's own posture is reactive/compliance-driven — monitoring and thresholds trigger notification and treatment response only after a detection — which sits in a different register from early-warning/forecasting or remote-sensing-driven risk-prediction framings that aim to anticipate blooms before or as they form; this source documents Ohio's regulatory "response" backstop rather than a predictive method, even though its own Table of Contents indicates the strategy also names "Remote Sensing Surveillance" (Section 5.3) as a surveillance tool — the excerpt available here does not contain that section's substantive text, so no method-level comparison to satellite/remote-sensing HAB-detection approaches can be drawn from this excerpt.

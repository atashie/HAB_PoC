# Toxic Cyanobacteria in Water: A Guide to Their Public Health Consequences, Monitoring and Management

**What it is.** A WHO-commissioned edited reference guide (1st edition, 1999; eds. Ingrid Chorus and Jamie Bartram) that synthesizes expert consensus on cyanobacterial ecology, cyanotoxins, and human/animal health effects into operational guidance for protecting drinking-water and recreational water bodies. Per its own Table of Contents it covers risk management (including an "Alert Levels Framework"), safe exposure levels, preventative and remedial measures (including drinking-water treatment for cyanotoxin removal), and the design of monitoring programmes and laboratory methods.

## Methods
This is a WHO-commissioned, multi-author edited reference/guidance volume, not a primary quantitative study. Per the Foreword, its content was developed through a defined sequence of international expert meetings - Geneva (Dec 1995), Bad Elster (June 1996), Bad Elster (April 1997, which "critically reviewed the literature concerning the toxicity of cyanotoxins and developed the scope and content of this book"), and St Helier (May 1997) - followed by an editorial review (Nov 1997) and review by the WHO Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality working group (March 1998). The "method" is therefore literature synthesis and expert consensus, not original data collection, a statistical/predictive model, or a novel monitoring dataset. The excerpted Table of Contents shows the full volume also documents laboratory methods for cyanobacteria identification/quantification and chlorophyll-a-based biomass determination (Ch.12), cyanotoxin bioassays and analytical methods (Ch.13), monitoring-programme design (Ch.10-11), and a risk-management "Alert Levels Framework" (Ch.6) - but the extracted text contains only these chapters' titles, not their body content, so no claim is made here about how well those specific methods perform.

## Stated limitations
The publisher's front-matter disclaimer states it "makes no representation, express or implied, with regard to the accuracy of the information contained in this book and cannot accept any legal responsibility or liability for any errors or omissions that may be made." The Foreword itself flags a knowledge gap: severe acute health effects "appear to be rare," but "little is known of the scale and nature of either long-term effects (such as tumour promotion and liver damage) or milder short-term effects, such as contact irritation." Separately, Chapter 1.1 warns against a common conceptual error: the term "self-purification" (rapid organic-matter degradation in turbulent rivers) "has been applied, mistakenly, to any process of removing undesirable substances from water but does not actually eliminate the contaminants" - e.g., sediment adsorption or dilution relocates or dilutes substances (such as phosphorus) rather than removing them.

## Tensions with other findings
The pre-extracted content (front matter: ISBN 0-419-23930-8, "© 1999 WHO," eds. Chorus & Bartram) is unambiguously the FIRST edition (1999), even though this dossier's provisional task title says "(2nd edition)" - and the source URL filename itself says "1st-ed.pdf," consistent with the extracted content. A web search performed only to confirm bibliographic metadata (not content) confirms a distinct second edition exists: Chorus & Welker (eds.), 2021, CRC Press/Routledge, ISBN 978-1-003-08144-9 (open-access DOI 10.1201/9781003081449) - a different, later text not reflected anywhere in this extracted excerpt. Any numeric guidance drawn from this 1999 excerpt (e.g., the <0.1 mg/L phosphorus figure, or anything from the un-excerpted Chapter 5 "Safe levels and safe practices") should be checked against the 2021 edition before being treated as current. Within the excerpt itself, the source's own framing - that blooms require temperature, light AND nutrient status jointly ("Wherever conditions of temperature, light and nutrient status are conducive...") - complicates any HAB risk model that relies on a single nutrient threshold (such as the same source's own <0.1 mg/L figure) as a stand-alone predictor. Likewise, the regional eutrophication percentages given (ILEC/Lake Biwa Research Institute, 1988-1993) are a single, three-decade-old survey snapshot with no indicated update in the excerpt, so they should not be used as a current baseline for present-day regional HAB/eutrophication prevalence.

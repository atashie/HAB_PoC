# Reregistration Eligibility Decision (RED) for Coppers (EPA 738-R-09-304)

**What it is.** A May 2009 U.S. EPA Office of Pesticide Programs document that amends the original July/August 2006 Reregistration Eligibility Decision (RED) for copper-containing pesticides, presenting EPA's human-health and ecological risk assessments for coppers (used as fungicides, algaecides, aquatic herbicides, bactericides, molluscicides, and antimicrobials) and setting the labeling, application-rate, and restricted-entry conditions required for their continued U.S. registration. It responds to 46 public comments on the 2006 RED and revises specific application rates, retreatment intervals, and worker restricted-entry intervals (REIs).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** This 2009 document is a formal amendment to the original Coppers RED (completed July 2006, published August 2006), issued after a public comment period that drew comments from growers, university extension services, registrants, and publicly-owned treatment works.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the opening paragraph describing the amendment's origin and comment volume. (Front matter, 'Amendment to the Coppers Reregistration Eligibility Decision' (p. 1))
  - *quote:* "A total of 46 submissions were received"
- **[✓ verified]** EPA conducted a qualitative human-health assessment for copper, not a quantitative dose-response assessment, because available data show copper is efficiently regulated by the human body with no identified systemic toxicity.
  - *evidence:* The Executive Summary states copper's essential-nutrient status and lack of systemic toxicity as the explicit rationale for skipping a quantitative toxicity assessment across all exposure routes. (Executive Summary, 'Risk Summary' (p. 8) and 'Dietary Risk' (p. 9))
  - *quote:* "a quantitative toxicity assessment was not conducted for dietary, dermal, oral or inhalation exposures"
- **[✓ verified]** EPA's screening-level ecological risk assessment found that copper pesticides can pose acute risk to non-target organisms, with aquatic organisms facing the greatest documented risk, attributed to direct water application and to runoff from adjacent treated fields.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the headline finding of the Executive Summary's Risk Summary, following from the Agency's conservative exposure assumptions (see limitations). (Executive Summary, 'Risk Summary' (p. 8))
  - *quote:* "the greatest risk to aquatic organisms resulting from direct water applications and runoff from fields adjacent to water bodies"
- **[✓ verified]** This RED's ecological risk assessment covers only agricultural uses of copper; a separate ecological assessment of antimicrobial uses (e.g., pools, spas, fountains, urban runoff/sewer discharge, anti-fouling coatings, wood preservatives) was still pending at the time of this document.
  - *evidence:* Explicitly scopes the ecological assessment and defers antimicrobial-use ecology to a future, separate document, even though the human-health assessment covers both agricultural and antimicrobial cupric-ion sources. (Executive Summary, intro (p. 8); reiterated in 'Ecological Assessment / Antimicrobial Uses of Copper-containing Pesticides' (p. ~4))
  - *quote:* "the ecological assessment addresses agricultural uses only"
- **[✓ verified]** EPA regulates a direct-water 'Algae Control' use of copper products (Appendix A use site) capped at a maximum concentration of 0.4 parts per million (ppm) per application; the 2009 amendment narrows this limit to apply specifically in aquaculture ponds when fish are present.
  - *evidence:* Table 1 lists 'Algae Control' as a use site with the 0.4 ppm cap unchanged in value but newly qualified to the aquaculture-pond/fish-present context (table text is fragmented by column-wise extraction, so no single clean verbatim quote spans the full row). (Table 1, 'Changes Made in the Coppers Appendix A Application Rates Information for Crops' (p. ~4-5))
- **[✓ verified]** Because many current copper product labels lack specified application rates or retreatment intervals, EPA's ecological exposure assessment used conservative benchmark rates well above real label rates: the highest single rate actually allowed on labels is 8.0 lbs Cu2+ (metallic copper) per acre (dormant fruit/nut tree application), versus a highest assessed benchmark of 31.8 lbs Cu2+/acre used in the ecological assessment (applications to filberts).
  - *evidence:* The Agency states the real-world revised/increased label rates all remain below the conservative benchmarks used in the 2006 ecological assessment, so no re-assessment was triggered. (Amendment, 'Revised Application Rates and Exposure Estimates to Nontarget Organisms' (p. ~4))
  - *quote:* "the highest assessed single application rate of 31.8 lbs Cu2+/A for applications to filberts in the ecological assessment"
- **[✓ verified]** Several copper compounds (copper hydroxide, basic copper sulfate, copper sulfate pentahydrate) had their worker restricted-entry interval (REI) increased from 12 or 24 hours to 48 hours because of acute (not systemic) toxicity — specifically severe eye, dermal, or inhalation irritation potential identified in acute toxicity studies.
  - *evidence:* The 48-hour REI is explicitly tied to acute irritation potential (Toxicity Category I/II) under the Worker Protection Standard, not to any systemic toxicity concern, and was retained for these three compounds despite grower/commenter objections. (Amendment, 'REI Changes' (p. 1) and 'Copper Hydroxide, Basic Copper Sulfate, Copper Sulfate Pentahydrate' (p. 1-2))

  - *quote:* "the highest rating is Toxicity Category III and, therefore, the REI should be reduced from 48 to 12 hours"

## Data / numbers
- 46 public comment submissions received on the original 2006 Coppers RED during the Phase 3 comment period (Feb-Apr 2006, per 71 FR 45550-45551)
- Algae Control (direct water use, e.g., aquaculture ponds): maximum concentration 0.4 parts per million (ppm) per application; 2009 amendment restricts this cap to aquaculture ponds when fish are present (no change to the numeric value itself)
- Highest single agricultural application rate on current copper product labels: 8.0 lbs Cu2+ (metallic copper) per acre, for dormant applications to fruit/nut trees
- Highest single application rate used as the ecological risk-assessment benchmark (conservative, not a real label rate): 31.8 lbs Cu2+/acre, for filberts
- Highest assessed single application rate benchmark for row crops: 3.2 lbs Cu2+/acre
- Cranberry: maximum annual application rate increased from 6.3 to 12.6 lbs Cu2+/acre
- Currant/gooseberry (ribes): maximum single application rate increased from 2.5 to 4.0 lbs Cu2+/acre; maximum annual rate increased from 10.0 to 16.0 lbs Cu2+/acre
- Mango: maximum annual application rate increased from 18.2 to 48.0 lbs Cu2+/acre; minimum retreatment interval decreased from 30 to 7 days
- Olive: maximum single application rate increased from 3.15 to 6.0 lbs Cu2+/acre; maximum annual rate increased from 6.3 to 18.0 lbs Cu2+/acre
- Papaya: minimum retreatment interval decreased from 14 to 7 days
- Pome fruit (apple, loquat, pear, quince): new maximum single application rate of 6.0 lbs Cu2+/acre between silver-tip and green-tip stages (max. 1 application/season); bloom/growing-season maximum single rate increased from 0.5 to 1.5 lbs Cu2+/acre
- Restricted-entry interval (REI) for copper hydroxide, basic copper sulfate, copper sulfate pentahydrate: retained at 48 hours (up from 12 or 24 hours), based on Toxicity Category I/II acute eye/dermal/inhalation irritation findings
- Cuprous oxide REI: reduced from 48 hours to 12 hours after acute toxicity studies rated it Toxicity Category III
- Greenhouse-use REI: reducible from 48 to 24 hours if eye-flush mitigation conditions are met (basis: 2001 Phyton Corporation proposal for Phyton-27 New Dimension, EPA Reg. No. 49538, 3.98% copper sulfate pentahydrate)
- No statistical uncertainty (e.g., confidence intervals, error bars) is given for any of these values in the excerpted text; figures are regulatory label limits or conservative screening-assessment benchmarks, and toxicity findings are reported as categorical Toxicity Category ratings (I/II/III) rather than continuous statistics

## Methods
The document is a regulatory risk-assessment/decision text, not an empirical study. Human-health risk was assessed qualitatively (no quantitative dose-response modeling for dietary, dermal, oral, or inhalation exposure) because available literature showed no systemic toxicity from copper and the body regulates copper efficiently. Ecological risk was assessed via a 'screening-level' approach using deliberately conservative assumptions about application rate, retreatment interval, and treatment frequency — used because many product labels did not specify these parameters. Worker exposure limits (REIs) were set from acute toxicity studies classified into Toxicity Category ratings (I/II/III) per the Worker Protection Standard (40 CFR Part 156), with higher categories (I/II, severe eye/dermal/inhalation irritation) triggering longer REIs (48 hr) and lower categories (III) permitting shorter ones (12 hr). The Agency explicitly reports that its conservative ecological benchmarks (e.g., 31.8 lbs Cu2+/acre for filberts, 3.2 lbs Cu2+/acre for row crops) 'worked' in the sense of remaining protective even after real-world label rates were later revised upward, since all revised rates stayed below the original assessment benchmarks. The excerpt does not contain the underlying quantitative ecotoxicology data (e.g., species-specific LC50/EC50 values, risk quotients) that presumably appear in the full Ecological Risk Assessment section (pp. 33-59) and appendices, which were outside the windowed extraction provided.

## Stated limitations
The Agency itself states: (1) many current copper product labels 'do not specify typical application rates, minimum retreatment intervals or frequency of treatments,' forcing it to make assumptions to assess non-target exposure; (2) it had only 'limited available information on the use [of] sewer root killer products' at the time of the original RED and, even after soliciting more information, concluded it lacks 'sufficient information to affect a [nationwide] ban' on copper sulfate pentahydrate root-killer use; (3) the ecological assessment in this RED is explicitly incomplete for non-agricultural (antimicrobial) copper uses — pools, spas, fountains, urban runoff/sewer discharge, anti-fouling treatments, wood preservatives — with that assessment deferred to 'a later date in a separate document'; (4) two data requirements the Agency itself had flagged as necessary at the time of the original RED (spray-drift studies, OPPTS 840.1100 and 835.4200) were later judged unnecessary without being generated, on the Agency's own belief that they 'would not provide any new information that would affect the conclusions.'

## Tensions with other findings
This RED both authorizes and flags a trade-off directly relevant to copper-based HAB/algae treatment: the same screening-level ecological assessment that supports continued registration of copper products (including a direct-water 'Algae Control' use capped at 0.4 ppm) also concludes that copper poses acute risk to non-target organisms, with 'the greatest risk to aquatic organisms' coming from exactly the direct-water-application pathway that algaecide treatment relies on — i.e., the source's own data complicate any framing of copper algaecide as a risk-free HAB intervention, though the document does not quantify this trade-off against algaecide efficacy (it is a registration decision, not an efficacy or field-monitoring study). Separately, the document's ecological risk picture is admittedly partial: it excludes antimicrobial-context copper uses (pools, spas, fountains, urban runoff) from ecological assessment, so it should not be read as a complete aquatic-risk profile for all copper-in-water contexts. It also shows regulatory-scale disagreement — EPA declining a nationwide sewer-root-killer ban that some California and New York counties had already imposed locally — illustrating that 'EPA-registered' does not equate to 'locally unrestricted' for copper loading to water bodies, a distinction relevant to any HAB-treatment guidance that cites EPA registration alone as sufficient justification for local copper use. No claim in this document establishes causation between copper application and cyanobacteria/HAB reduction; the source only sets exposure/use limits and reports the Agency's own risk-assessment conclusions about non-target ecological effects.

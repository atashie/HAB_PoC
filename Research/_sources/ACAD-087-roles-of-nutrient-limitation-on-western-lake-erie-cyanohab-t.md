# Roles of Nutrient Limitation on Western Lake Erie CyanoHAB Toxin Production

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7828104/

**What it is.** A 2021 peer-reviewed experimental study (Barnard et al., Toxins 13(1):47) that ran in situ nutrient-addition and 40%-dilution microcosm bioassays on natural Western Lake Erie bloom water from Maumee Bay (Microcystis-dominated) and Sandusky Bay (Planktothrix-dominated) in June and August 2019, to test whether nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), or both limit phytoplankton growth versus microcystin (hepatotoxin) and anatoxin-a (neurotoxin) production, and whether a 40% nutrient-load reduction (the US-Canada Great Lakes target) would be expected to lower toxin output.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** In June, Maumee Bay bloom growth was phosphorus-limited (not nitrogen-limited) despite very high ambient nitrate, and the 40% dilution treatment made no significant difference to growth; cyanotoxins were not detected at this site/time.
  - *evidence:* Two-factor ANOVA on growth rates across nutrient treatments; +P and +P&N treatments grew faster than control/+N; dilution factor was non-significant. (Results — June 2019 Maumee Bay experiment; Table 1)
  - *quote:* "The +P and +P&N treatments resulted in a higher growth rate than the control and +N treatments (p < 0.001)... no difference between undiluted and diluted treatments (p = 0.76)"
- **[✓ verified]** In June, Sandusky Bay (Planktothrix) growth was nutrient-replete, but biomass, microcystin, and anatoxin production were jointly (co-)limited by N and P, and microcystin production shifted to nutrient-deplete once nutrients were cut via 40% dilution.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as the study's own summary finding for the June Sandusky Bay experiment, based on ANOVA of production rates under nutrient x dilution treatments. (Abstract; Discussion)
  - *quote:* "During the June Sandusky Bay bloom, biomass production as well as hepatotoxic microcystin and neurotoxic anatoxin production were N and P co-limited with microcystin production becoming nutrient deplete under 40% dilution."
- **[⚠ partial]** In August, the Maumee Bay Microcystis bloom produced microcystin under nutrient-replete conditions, with only a slight, induced phosphorus limitation appearing under the 40% dilution treatment.
  - *evidence:* Based on non-significant nutrient-treatment effect on microcystin in biomass-normalized analysis (p = 0.5452) but a significant dilution effect in the raw (non-normalized) analysis (p < 0.001), interpreted by the authors as nutrient repletion with mild induced P limitation. (Abstract; Discussion; Results — August 2019 Maumee Bay)
  - *quote:* "During August, the Maumee Bay bloom produced microcystin under nutrient repletion with slight induced P limitation under 40% dilution"
  - *reviewer:* Source explicitly notes that August Maumee negative growth rates were 'likely due to bottle effects attributable to the very high biomass; restricted exchange of gases and nutrients' — this significant caveat is omitted from the claim.
- **[✓ verified]** In August, the Sandusky Bay Planktothrix bloom's anatoxin production was primarily nitrogen-limited in both the undiluted and 40%-diluted treatments, and nitrate additions raised anatoxin concentrations more than ammonium or urea additions did.
  - *evidence:* ANOVA on anatoxin production rate with and without chlorophyll-normalization; nitrogen-source sub-treatments compared directly. (Results — August 2019 Sandusky Bay)
  - *quote:* "Anatoxin production was primarily N-limited both with and without biomass normalization (p < 0.001)... NO₃ additions led to higher concentrations of anatoxin compared to NH₄ and urea additions"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors conclude their results contradict the older assumption that phosphorus alone controls Western Lake Erie CyanoHABs, and argue for a paradigm shift toward also managing nitrogen inputs.
  - *evidence:* Authors' own interpretive synthesis of the four site x month experiments, presented as the paper's central management-relevant conclusion. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "Our findings of N limitation contradict the previous assumption that P availability exclusively controls CyanoHABs. Instead, these findings support the paradigm shift to also consider N input reductions."
- **[✓ verified]** The paper's explicit watershed-management recommendation is that both nitrogen and phosphorus reduction efforts should be pursued, not phosphorus reduction alone.
  - *evidence:* Direct recommendation stated in the abstract and echoed in the conclusion/management-implications discussion. (Abstract; Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "both N and P reduction efforts should be implemented in its watershed"
- **[✓ verified]** Simulating a 40% nutrient-load reduction (via dilution) produced only a slight reduction in biomass and toxin (microcystin/anatoxin) production in these eutrophic bays, leading the authors to warn that the 40% target agreed between the US and Canada might not be sufficient in such nutrient-saturated bay environments.
  - *evidence:* Authors' own stated caveat on the practical/policy implication of their dilution-treatment results, explicitly scoped to the two eutrophic bays tested. (Discussion; Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "Our study took place only in eutrophic bays and we showed that a 40% reduction might not be enough in Maumee and Sandusky Bay because growth and toxin production could still be nutrient-saturated."
- **[⚠ partial]** Because the ratio of microcystin to chlorophyll a was nearly linear across both June and August, the authors infer that the dominant bloom-forming taxa themselves (Planktothrix in Sandusky Bay, Microcystis in Maumee Bay) — rather than a rare secondary strain — were the main microcystin producers; this is an inference from a correlational (biomass-toxin) pattern, not a direct causal test of which taxon produced the toxin.
  - *evidence:* Based on the near-linear covariation of microcystin and chlorophyll a across treatments/timepoints, interpreted by the authors as evidence the bloom-dominant genus was the primary toxin producer. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "As the ratio of microcystin to chlorophyll a in both June and August was nearly linear"
  - *reviewer:* Source confirms the linearity-based conclusion about dominant taxa producing microcystin, but does not explicitly state the methodological caveat that this represents a correlational (not causal) inference or the contrast with secondary strains. These are valid scientific inferences but not explicitly articulated in the provided source text.
- **[⚠ partial]** Even though microcystin's synthesis pathway is nitrogen-linked, the experiments show phosphorus is independently required to reach higher microcystin concentrations, i.e., microcystin output is not governed by nitrogen availability alone.
  - *evidence:* Drawn from the combined June/August Maumee and Sandusky results in which P-addition or P-replete conditions co-occurred with the highest microcystin levels alongside N effects. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "P is also required for cellular growth to allow for higher microcystin concentrations."
  - *reviewer:* The empirical finding ('P is also required for cellular growth to allow for higher microcystin concentrations') is clearly supported. However, the opening premise about microcystin's nitrogen-linked synthesis pathway and the C:N ratio background ('N bargain' concept) are noted in the evidence as paraphrases 'not verbatim-confirmed' in the source text.
- **[⚠ partial]** The study reports anatoxin-a (plus dihydro- and homoanatoxin-a) production associated with the Sandusky Bay Planktothrix bloom, which the authors present as new documentation of anatoxin production in Lake Erie and use to argue this neurotoxin warrants closer monitoring.
  - *evidence:* Anatoxin variants were quantified by LC-MS/MS at both sampling events; the 'first study' framing appeared only as an unquoted summary bullet in one extraction pass, so is reported here with that caveat rather than as a verified verbatim claim. (Discussion; Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "Anatoxin should be more closely monitored in WLE, as it is a potent neurotoxin with human health-associated implications."
  - *reviewer:* The monitoring recommendation and LC-MS/MS quantification of anatoxin variants at Sandusky Bay are clearly supported. However, the claim that this represents the 'new documentation of anatoxin production in Lake Erie' is noted in the evidence_note as 'an unquoted bullet, not confirmed verbatim' and therefore not verified in the provided source text.

## Methods
In situ microcosm nutrient-manipulation bioassays: natural bloom water was pumped (non-destructive diaphragm pump, 1 m depth) from Maumee Bay (near the University of Toledo Lake Erie Center, Oregon, OH) and Sandusky Bay (Paper District Marina dock, Sandusky, OH) in June and August 2019, transported to Ohio State's Stone Laboratory (South Bass Island, Put-in-Bay, OH), and incubated for 72 hours (T0 to T3) in 4 L polyethylene Cubitainers at ambient lake temperature/light. A 2×2(+) factorial design crossed nutrient addition (control, +N as NO3/NH4/urea, +P, +N&P) with dilution (undiluted vs. 40% dilution using a matched major-ion solution to simulate a 40% nutrient-load reduction while holding ionic strength constant). Endpoints: chlorophyll a (GF/F filtration, 90% acetone extraction, Turner Designs Trilogy fluorometer, Welschmeyer non-acidification method) as a biomass/growth-rate proxy; dissolved nutrients (nitrate+nitrite, ammonium, DRP, silicate, urea) via segmented-flow autoanalyzer (QuAAtro) using standard EPA methods; microcystin via LC-MS (Waters 2695/ZQ4000, 22 congeners screened, quantified against microcystin-LR standard); anatoxin-a, dihydro-anatoxin-a, and homoanatoxin-a via LC-MS/MS (Waters Acquity TQD, MRM transitions specified, calibrated against an anatoxin-a standard). Production rates were computed as ln(mean_T3/mean_T0)/t; effects were tested with two-factor (balanced) or n-factor (unbalanced) ANOVA in MATLAB R2018b, with Levene's test for variance homogeneity, both on raw and chlorophyll-normalized (biomass-normalized) toxin values. The design lets the authors distinguish nutrient effects on growth/biomass from nutrient effects on toxin production per unit biomass, and to compare undiluted vs. simulated-40%-reduction conditions at two sites in two different bloom phases (early-season vs. peak bloom). It reportedly works well to detect site- and season-specific N vs. P (co-)limitation signals in a short 72 h window, but the authors note it may fail to represent whole-lake or long-term dynamics: results are explicitly scoped to eutrophic embayments, short bottle incubations, and only two discrete 2019 sampling events.

## Stated limitations
The authors flag several caveats themselves: (1) likely "bottle effects" from very high biomass restricting gas/nutrient exchange, invoked to explain negative growth rates in the August Maumee Bay incubations; (2) an apparent ammonium artifact (higher NH4 in initial diluted vs. undiluted subsamples in June/August Maumee), attributed to incidental cell die-off/regeneration during subsampling rather than a true treatment effect; (3) the Maumee Bay experiments occurred before ambient nitrate depletion, so it "remains to be seen" how a 40% nutrient cut would affect N-limited Microcystis later in the season when nitrate is naturally drawn down; (4) the study covers "only... eutrophic bays," and the authors explicitly state a 40% reduction "might not be enough" there because conditions could remain nutrient-saturated, with adequacy for the open waters of Western Lake Erie left for future study; (5) an alternative, non-nutrient explanation is offered for the flat June Sandusky microcystin pattern — cell lysis and loss of dissolved microcystin through the 0.7 µm GF/F filters, or degradation, rather than a true nutrient-driven production signal; (6) the source organism for anatoxin is not certain — biomass-normalized and raw anatoxin patterns track together, leading the authors to suggest a secondary cyanobacterial species (e.g., a previously identified Cuspidothrix issatschenkoi) rather than Planktothrix itself may be responsible; (7) the lack of any difference between nitrogen species (NO3 vs. NH4 vs. urea) at some sites/times is noted as inconsistent with prior WLE findings from periods of weaker N-limitation, tentatively attributed to high ambient NO3 paired with low NH4; (8) historical nutrient-load comparisons (e.g., to 2007) and the unusually wet 2019 winter are flagged as possibly non-representative of typical conditions; (9) no long-term nutrient-reduction threshold is established — the authors explicitly call for further studies on long-term thresholds, adequacy of the 40% target in open-lake waters, adaptive management as land use/climate change, and direct functional (mechanistic) links between nutrient enrichment and cyanotoxin production.

## Tensions with other findings
This source explicitly positions itself against the historical Western Lake Erie management paradigm that phosphorus alone controls CyanoHAB severity — a paradigm that underlies P-only load-reduction targets like the 40% figure it tests — and instead argues nitrogen must also be constrained; any HAB literature or policy source in this dossier built on a P-only-limitation framing should be read against this tension. It also complicates any downstream use of remote-sensing/biomass proxies (e.g., satellite chlorophyll or cyanobacterial index) as a stand-in for toxicity: this study shows toxin production per unit biomass is separately, and variably, nutrient-regulated by site and season (P-co-limited in June Sandusky vs. essentially nutrient-replete in August Maumee vs. N-limited in August Sandusky), so a given satellite-observed bloom extent/biomass will not map onto toxin concentration in a fixed way. Note that unlike a purely observational/correlational field survey, this study's core N/P-limitation claims come from a controlled nutrient-addition experiment, which supports causal language for the manipulated factors (N, P, dilution) within the tested bottles; the caution the source itself raises is about generalizing beyond the two eutrophic bays, the 72 h bottle format, and the two 2019 sampling dates — not about the internal causal logic of the bioassay itself.

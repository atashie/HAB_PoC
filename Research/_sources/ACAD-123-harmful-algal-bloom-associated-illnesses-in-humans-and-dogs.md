# Harmful Algal Bloom–Associated Illnesses in Humans and Dogs Identified Through a Pilot Surveillance System — New York, 2015

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5689215/

**What it is.** A CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) surveillance summary (Figgatt et al., MMWR 2017;66:1182–1184, DOI 10.15585/mmwr.mm6643a5) reporting the first season (June–September 2015) of a One Health pilot illness-surveillance system run by the New York State Department of Health in 16 counties to actively find, investigate, and classify human and canine illnesses linked to harmful algal bloom (HAB) exposure, alongside the state's environmental HAB/beach-closure activity that year.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** New York's 2015 pilot surveillance system logged far more HAB-associated illness reports in one 4-month season than the state's routine reporting had ever logged statewide in a full year, which the authors interpret as evidence of prior underreporting.
  - *evidence:* The Discussion directly contrasts the pilot's 51 reports against a stated pre-2015 annual ceiling of 10 and draws the underreporting inference from that gap. (Discussion, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "HAB-associated illness reports made to NYSDOH before 2015 never exceeded 10 statewide in any given year, whereas 51 illness reports were made through a pilot surveillance system in 16 New York counties during June–September 2015."
- **[✓ verified]** Of 51 HAB-associated illness reports collected by the pilot, 35 (68.8%) met CDC's case definitions: 32 human cases (1 confirmed, 20 probable, 11 suspected) and 3 canine cases (all probable).
  - *evidence:* Stated as the headline Results figure and case-classification breakdown; this is the core case count of the whole report. (Results, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "NYSDOH received 51 HAB-associated illness reports from the pilot surveillance system, 35 (68.8%) of which met the CDC case definitions, including 32 in humans and three in dogs... One (3%) of the 32 human cases was classified as confirmed, 20 (57%) as probable, and 11 (31%) as suspected. All three canine cases were classified as probable."
- **[✓ verified]** Statewide across all of New York in 2015 (not limited to the pilot counties/season), 139 HABs were identified, 97 (70%) of them laboratory-confirmed, resulting in 77 separate beach closures at 37 beaches on 20 distinct water bodies.
  - *evidence:* Given as the environmental/background scale-setting statistic for the state's overall HAB activity that year, separate from the illness-surveillance pilot's own case counts. (Background / opening paragraph of Investigation and Results)
  - *quote:* "Statewide in 2015, a total of 139 HABs were identified in New York, 97 (70%) of which were confirmed through laboratory analysis; 77 independent beach closures were ordered at 37 beaches on 20 different bodies of water."
- **[✓ verified]** Beaches with a visually identified HAB stayed closed to water contact until the bloom was invisible for at least one day and a commercial test strip showed cyanobacterial toxin concentration below a 10 microgram-per-liter guidance value.
  - *evidence:* States the concrete operational reopening protocol and the one explicit numeric regulatory threshold in the article. (Methods, beach closure/reopening protocol)
  - *quote:* "water contact was prohibited until the HAB was no longer visible in the beach area for at least 1 day, and testing with commercially available algal toxin-detecting test strips had determined that concentrations of cyanobacterial toxins were below the recommended guidance value of 10 μg/L"
- **[✓ verified]** Cases were classified using a three-tier CDC definition in which 'suspected' requires exposure plus symptoms plus a public-health plausibility judgment, 'probable' adds a medical diagnosis or observational/environmental lab data, and only 'confirmed' requires clinical HAB laboratory data.
  - *evidence:* Defines the classification scheme underlying every case count in the report; distinguishes environmental lab data (e.g., water toxin testing) from clinical lab data (e.g., patient specimen testing) as the bar for 'confirmed.' (Methods, case-definition footnote)
  - *quote:* "Confirmed cases... required to have HAB clinical laboratory data plus either 1) a professional medical diagnosis or rule-out of other causes of illness; or 2) a professional medical diagnosis, rule-out of other causes of illness, and HAB environmental laboratory data."
- **[✓ verified]** Among the 32 human cases, skin problems (rash) were the most common symptom (22 patients, 69%), no human was hospitalized or died, and swimming was the dominant exposure route (28 patients, 88%).
  - *evidence:* Drawn directly from the Results narrative and the accompanying symptom/exposure/outcome table; establishes the clinical severity and exposure profile of the human cohort. (Results and Table, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "skin problems were reported by 22 (69%) patients. No hospitalizations or deaths were reported among humans. All human cases were associated with exposure to HABs in recreational water. Recreational activities included swimming (28 patients; 88%)..."
- **[✓ verified]** All three canine cases had gastrointestinal symptoms; two of the three dogs were hospitalized and the third recovered without treatment, and none died.
  - *evidence:* Gives the clinical course for the small canine sub-sample, showing a notably higher hospitalization rate among dogs (2 of 3) than humans (0 of 32) in this dataset. (Results, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "All three affected dogs had gastrointestinal symptoms, and two were hospitalized; the third dog's symptoms resolved without intervention, and none of the dogs died."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors frame the higher case count in the pilot year as evidence of underreporting in prior (passive) surveillance rather than evidence that HAB illness incidence actually increased in 2015.
  - *evidence:* The Discussion explicitly links the pilot's case count to an underreporting conclusion, not to a claim of a true epidemiological rise, which bears on how any 'more cases in 2015' statistic should be interpreted. (Discussion, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "Of the 51 reports identified through the pilot surveillance system, 35 were considered cases of HAB-associated illness that met the CDC case definition, suggesting that such illnesses might be underreported."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors state that HAB illness surveillance is inherently low-specificity because the associated signs and symptoms are nonspecific and there is often no data causally linking a given illness to HAB exposure.
  - *evidence:* This is the paper's own stated methodological limitation on how confidently any individual case (beyond the single clinically confirmed one) can be attributed to HAB toxin exposure versus another cause. (Limitations paragraph, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "surveillance for HAB-associated illnesses is difficult because HAB-associated signs and symptoms are nonspecific (e.g., skin rash, nausea, or diarrhea); the frequent lack of data showing these illnesses are causally linked to HAB exposure leads to a case definition with low specificity."
- **[✓ verified]** The 16 pilot counties were not a representative sample of New York: all had already experienced at least one HAB in the prior season, so reported illness burden may reflect more HAB-aware communities rather than the state overall.
  - *evidence:* Stated by the authors as a selection-bias limitation restricting how far the pilot's findings can be generalized statewide. (Limitations paragraph, MMWR 2017;66(43):1182-1184)
  - *quote:* "all of the pilot counties had experienced at least one HAB in a water body in the previous season. Therefore, the pilot counties might represent communities more aware of HABs and their potential health effects."

## Data / numbers
- 51 HAB-associated illness reports (humans + dogs) received by NYSDOH, pilot surveillance, June-September 2015, 16 counties
- 35 of 51 reports (68.8%) met CDC HAB case definitions
- 32 human cases: 1 (3%) confirmed, 20 (57%) probable, 11 (31%) suspected
- 3 canine cases: 3 (100%) probable
- Pre-2015 statewide baseline: HAB-associated illness reports never exceeded 10/year
- 139 HABs identified statewide in New York in 2015 (all year); 97 (70%) laboratory-confirmed
- 77 independent beach closures at 37 beaches on 20 different water bodies statewide in 2015
- 16 of 62 New York counties (26%) participated in the pilot
- Human demographics (n with data available): median age 24 years (range 2-63 years); 18 of 32 (56%) female
- Canine demographics (n with data available): median age 7 years (range 2-12 years); 1 of 3 female
- Human symptom counts (n=32, not mutually exclusive): skin problems/rash 22 (69%); respiratory 16; gastrointestinal 15; other symptoms (chills, muscle aches, watery eyes) 13; fatigue/general weakness 11; sore throat 11; neurologic 6
- Canine symptom counts (n=3): gastrointestinal 3; fatigue/general weakness 2; neurologic 2; skin/respiratory/sore throat 0
- Human recreational exposure counts (n=32, not mutually exclusive): swimming 28 (88%); wakeboarding/jet skiing/waterskiing/tubing 7 (22%); boating 7 (22%); wading 6 (19%); personal watercraft 4 (13%)
- Human illness outcomes: outpatient care (ED/urgent care/primary care) 17 of 32; hospitalized 0 of 32; deaths 0
- Canine illness outcomes: hospitalized 2 of 3; resolved without intervention 1 of 3; deaths 0
- Beach reopening guidance value: cyanobacterial toxin concentration below 10 micrograms/L (μg/L) by test strip, plus HAB not visible for at least 1 day

## Methods
One Health pilot surveillance system (NYSDOH) operated June–September 2015 in 16 of New York's 62 counties (26%), chosen for likelihood of HAB occurrence and local health department (LHD) interest. Participating LHD staff attended a training webinar and received CDC One Health HAB System-derived tools (public/clinician/veterinarian outreach materials, illness questionnaires, reporting forms). Case-finding pooled: NYSDEC environmental HAB reports (triggered by visible scum/discolored water, then lab-confirmed via microscopy, ELISA, liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry, or blue-green chlorophyll density), LHD reports, NYS Parks/Recreation/Historic Preservation reports, one veterinary office, hospital syndromic surveillance, and poison control center coordination. Each report was investigated with a standardized questionnaire and classified using three-tier CDC case definitions (suspected/probable/confirmed — confirmed alone requires clinical, not just environmental, HAB laboratory data). Affected beaches closed on visual bloom evidence and reopened only once the bloom was invisible for >=1 day AND toxin test strips showed cyanobacterial toxin below a 10 micrograms/L guidance value. The approach "worked" in that active, multi-agency case-finding surfaced far more reports (51) than New York's passive historical reporting ever had (<=10/year); the authors themselves flag it as low-specificity for individual causal attribution, since HAB symptoms are nonspecific and only 1 of 32 human cases reached the clinically-confirmed tier.

## Stated limitations
The article states three explicit limitations: (1) "self-reported illnesses and exposures can be subject to inaccuracy and bias"; (2) HAB surveillance is inherently difficult because "HAB-associated signs and symptoms are nonspecific (e.g., skin rash, nausea, or diarrhea); the frequent lack of data showing these illnesses are causally linked to HAB exposure leads to a case definition with low specificity"; (3) selection bias — "all of the pilot counties had experienced at least one HAB in a water body in the previous season," so "the pilot counties might represent communities more aware of HABs and their potential health effects" than New York overall, limiting generalizability of the 51-report count and the underreporting inference.

## Tensions with other findings
(1) The paper's own Discussion attributes the jump from a historical ceiling of ~10 reports/year to 51 reports in one pilot season to improved case ascertainment ("suggesting that such illnesses might be underreported"), not to blooms or illness actually worsening in 2015 — any other HAB source that cites rising illness counts as evidence of worsening bloom severity should be checked against this alternative, detection-effort explanation. (2) Only 1 of 32 human cases (3%) reached the clinically "confirmed" tier; the rest rested on nonspecific symptoms plus medical or environmental (non-clinical) data, and the authors explicitly call the case definition "low specificity" — this weakens any downstream claim that a detected bloom can be equated with a quantified human-health outcome, reinforcing that bloom-illness association here is correlational, not a validated causal/dose-response link. (3) This source's HAB detection is visual/reactive (scum or discolored water reported by the public, park staff, or lake associations, then lab-confirmed by microscopy/ELISA/LC-MS) and beach-scale, which is a different detection paradigm from proactive satellite remote sensing (e.g., EPA CyAN) that this project intends to fuse with in-situ data — the two approaches may flag different bloom subsets (visible shoreline scums vs. basin-scale cyanobacterial density), so illness counts here should not be assumed to map one-to-one onto satellite-derived bloom-intensity metrics.

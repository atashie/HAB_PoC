# SWAMP Freshwater CyanoHABs Program (Blue-Green Algae)

**What it is.** A California State Water Resources Control Board (SWAMP) webpage describing the state's Freshwater and Estuarine Harmful Algal Bloom (FHAB) Program — the regulatory, monitoring, and public-communication infrastructure for cyanobacterial HABs (citizen bloom reporting, a satellite-imagery screening tool, interactive incident maps, voluntary public-health-posting guidance, and a legislatively mandated (AB 834) monitoring strategy) — rather than a primary research study or dataset release.

## Methods
The page presents no original research methodology, model, or validation statistics; it describes an operational state program combining: (a) a voluntary public "Bloom Report Form" for citizen-reported bloom sightings; (b) a "satellite imagery tool that remotely screens for potential HABs in larger waterbodies" (remote-sensing screening; technical detail is delegated to a separate March-2025 "Best Practices to Employ Satellite Remote Sensing..." guidance document not included in this page's fetched content); (c) a "public interactive map" and "HAB Incident Web Map" aggregating bloom reports; and (d) a 2021 "Framework and Strategy for Freshwater Harmful Algal Bloom Monitoring," co-developed with the Southern California Coastal Water Research Project (SCCWRP), which recommends programmatic and special-study monitoring, research, and risk-assessment activities under Assembly Bill 834 (signed September 2019). The page frames these tools as serving "response, assessment, and communication," not as a quantified detection or forecasting model — no accuracy, sensitivity, or validation figures for the satellite tool, citizen-reporting pipeline, or maps are given in the fetched text.

## Stated limitations
The source explicitly states the 2021 Monitoring Strategy was "written as a conceptual document to comprehensively consider all the monitoring options" at a time before the resources actually available for FHAB monitoring under AB 834 were established — i.e., its recommendations were not resource-constrained or prioritized against an actual budget. The program also references a "2022 FHAB Program Gap Assessment in meeting legislative requirements," implying the program itself acknowledges shortfalls relative to AB 834 mandates, though the fetched text does not enumerate what those specific gaps are. No quantitative performance, accuracy, or uncertainty statistics are provided anywhere in the fetched content for any monitoring tool (satellite screening, citizen reports, or interactive maps).

## Tensions with other findings
This is a program/regulatory overview rather than a peer-reviewed or quantitative study and should not be used as primary evidence for bloom trends, detection performance, or causal drivers. Its claim that "FHABs are increasing in incidence, duration, and toxicity statewide" is asserted without accompanying trend statistics, baseline period, or citation in the fetched text, so it should be corroborated against quantitative remote-sensing or in-situ trend datasets (e.g., EPA CyAN time series, Water Quality Portal records) before being treated as an established, causally-attributed finding — the page does not attempt to attribute the increase to any specific driver (nutrients, temperature, etc.), so no causal claim can be drawn from it. Its monitoring stack also mixes a voluntary, self-selected citizen "Bloom Report Form" with a systematic satellite screening tool; the source does not itself discuss possible reporting bias (uneven observer effort in space/time) from the citizen-report component relative to fully systematic satellite-based programs like EPA CyAN, which is a caveat worth noting when this source is cited alongside more rigorously validated remote-sensing studies.

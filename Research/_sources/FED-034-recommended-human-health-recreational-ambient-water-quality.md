# Recommended Human Health Recreational Ambient Water Quality Criteria or Swimming Advisories for Microcystins and Cylindrospermopsin

**What it is.** A June 6, 2019 Federal Register Notice of Availability (84 FR 26413, Docket EPA-HQ-OW-2016-0715) in which EPA announces its final CWA section 304(a) recommended human health recreational ambient water quality criteria / swimming advisories for the cyanotoxins microcystins (8 µg/L) and cylindrospermopsin (15 µg/L), for voluntary use by states and authorized tribes as water quality standards and/or swimming advisories.

  - *evidence:* Stated directly as the notice's core recommended values. (SUMMARY / recommended-criteria discussion, 84 FR 26413)

## Methods
Not an empirical study — a regulatory criteria-recommendation notice. Per the fetched text, EPA derived the two concentrations using CWA section 304(a) methodology: it modeled oral/incidental-ingestion risk to recreating children (the higher-exposure age subgroup) during primary-contact recreation, drawing on the same peer-reviewed toxicological evidence base (liver/kidney and gastrointestinal effects) used for EPA's 2015 10-Day Drinking Water Health Advisories for the same two cyanotoxins. Between the 2016 draft and 2019 final, EPA updated the children's incidental-ingestion-rate input using a study published in 2017, which the notice identifies as the primary driver of the change in recommended values. The full quantitative derivation (uncertainty/safety factors, exact ingestion-rate figures, dose-response modeling, and whether a relative source contribution term was applied) resides in a separate EPA technical support document that is referenced but not reproduced within this Federal Register notice text.

## Stated limitations
(1) Not legally binding: EPA states plainly that these CWA 304(a) recommended criteria, and any swimming advisories built on them, "do not constitute legally binding requirements"; states/tribes may adopt EPA's numbers as-is, modify them for local conditions, or use other scientifically defensible methods instead, provided the result is "scientifically defensible and protective of the designated uses." (2) Adoption not mandated: cyanotoxins are not on the 40 CFR part 423 Appendix A list of 126 priority pollutants, so states/tribes are not required to adopt any criteria for them. (3) Narrow toxin scope: only microcystins and cylindrospermopsin receive numeric criteria in this notice; no other cyanotoxins (e.g., anatoxins, saxitoxins) are addressed. (4) Pathway scope: covers recreational (primary-contact/swimming, incidental-ingestion) exposure only; drinking-water exposure is covered separately by the 2015 Health Advisories, not this notice. (5) Visible-bloom caveat: EPA explicitly notes that cyanotoxin exposure "can occur even when there are no visible signs of a bloom," i.e., visual/remote detection of a bloom is not a reliable stand-in for toxin presence. (6) The notice itself does not reproduce the underlying quantitative derivation (uncertainty factors, exact ingestion-rate values, dose-response basis) — only the technical support document it references would contain that detail. (7) The 8 µg/L / 15 µg/L values are presented as fixed point recommendations; no confidence interval or statistical uncertainty range for the thresholds themselves is given in the notice text.

## Tensions with other findings
This source is a regulatory/management threshold, not a HAB-detection or -prediction study, so it complicates (rather than contradicts) remote-sensing- and model-based HAB literature in three ways relevant to this project: (a) EPA explicitly states cyanotoxin exposure can occur without any visible bloom sign, which cuts against treating a satellite-observed cyanobacteria/chlorophyll signal (e.g., CyAN) as a full proxy for microcystin/cylindrospermopsin risk — reinforcing the brief's own instruction to fuse remote-sensing with in-situ chemistry rather than rely on remote sensing alone. (b) The regulatory assessment windows here (10-day non-rolling period for WQS use; same-day for advisories) are a different temporal grain than typical satellite revisit cadence (e.g., weekly), which any tool translating a satellite-based "risk readout" into an "action window" against this EPA benchmark would need to reconcile explicitly. (c) The recommended numbers themselves are not static scientific constants — they rose between the 2016 draft and 2019 final because of a single updated exposure-model input (children's incidental ingestion rate), illustrating that even federal reference thresholds are model-dependent and revisable, which argues for treating them as versioned inputs (with a citation and date) rather than immutable ground truth in any downstream tool or claim.

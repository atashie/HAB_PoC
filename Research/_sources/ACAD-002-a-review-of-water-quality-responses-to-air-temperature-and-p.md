# A Review of Water Quality Responses to Air Temperature and Precipitation Changes 2: Nutrients, Algal Blooms, Sediment, Pathogens

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed literature review (Coffey et al. 2018, Journal of the American Water Resources Association) synthesizing post-2000 published studies on how nutrients, cyanobacterial (harmful algal) blooms, sediment, and waterborne pathogens respond to observed historical and projected future changes in air temperature and precipitation, primarily across U.S. water bodies, with the stated goal of characterizing future risk and identifying management/research needs.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria hold a competitive advantage over other algae at higher water temperatures, often above 25C, and this is a key mechanism linking warming to bloom formation.
  - *evidence:* Stated as an established finding from prior literature synthesized in the review; mechanism given is that warmer surface waters strengthen vertical water-column stratification, which favors buoyant cyanobacteria over heavier competitors like diatoms. (Cyanobacterial Blooms section)
  - *quote:* "competitive advantage at higher temperatures—often above 25 degrees Celsius (°C)"
- **[✓ verified]** A national-scale water quality model projects the mean number of days per year of cyanobacterial bloom occurrence per water body will roughly double to triple by mid- and late-century compared to current conditions.
  - *evidence:* Attributed to a specific national modeling study (cited by the review as USEPA 2017 / Chapra et al. 2017) that combined climate projections with a water-quality model; the review presents this as the most concrete quantitative national projection available for future bloom risk, while separately noting few such modeling studies exist. (Cyanobacterial Blooms - Projected Future Changes)
  - *quote:* "about 7 days per year per water body under current conditions, to 16–23 days in 2050 and 18–39 days in 2090"
- **[✓ verified]** The Lake Erie 2015 cyanobacterial bloom illustrates the 'ideal' bloom scenario: heavy early-season rainfall delivering a large nutrient (phosphorus) pulse, followed by warm, calm, stratified conditions that extend water residence time.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a specific case example supporting the general mechanistic claim that heavy rainfall-then-drought sequences maximize bloom risk; the review links the ~7 inches of June rainfall at Toledo to large phosphorus delivery and subsequent weak lake circulation/long residence times. (Cyanobacterial Blooms - case example (Lake Erie 2015))
  - *quote:* "The ideal scenario for blooms is heavy rainfall that enhances runoff and nutrient delivery, followed by a protracted drought during the summer-fall period, when water temperatures and stratification are maximal"
- **[✓ verified]** Precipitation and runoff mobilize and transport both dissolved and sediment-adsorbed nutrients from upland sources to water bodies, and nutrient loading correlates with precipitation/runoff volume across numerous studies.
  - *evidence:* General synthesis claim drawn from multiple cited studies on nutrient transport mechanisms; presented as a well-supported, recurring finding across the literature reviewed rather than a single study's result. (Nutrients section)
  - *quote:* "Numerous studies show increases in nutrient loading that correlate with precipitation and runoff volume"
- **[✓ verified]** Future projections for nutrient and sediment loading diverge regionally: northern/eastern U.S. watersheds are generally projected to see increased annual loads (tracking projected precipitation/runoff increases), while the interior West and Southwest are projected to see decreased loads (tracking projected declines in runoff).
  - *evidence:* Based on the review's synthesis of multiple watershed-scale modeling studies across the U.S.; presented with an explicit caveat that precipitation projection uncertainty at the local scale limits confidence, especially for nutrients. (Nutrients and Sediment sections - Projected Future Changes)
  - *quote:* "Modeling studies applying similar methods to watersheds across the nation generally project increases in total nutrient loads (median) for much of the eastern and northern U.S."
- **[✓ verified]** Fecal-indicator pathogens (e.g., pathogenic E. coli) survive longer at lower water temperatures and in sediments, but survival rates decrease once temperatures exceed 30C; meanwhile naturally occurring warm-water organisms like Naegleria fowleri and Vibrio spp. become more favored as temperatures rise.
  - *evidence:* Synthesized from multiple pathogen survival studies cited in the review; the review frames this as a bidirectional temperature effect - warming reduces fecal-pathogen persistence but expands the range/season of naturally occurring thermophilic pathogens - which complicates any simple 'warmer = worse' narrative for pathogens specifically. (Pathogens section)
  - *quote:* "At temperatures exceeding 30 degrees (°C), survival rates for pathogens sourced to fecal waste have been shown to decrease"
- **[✓ verified]** Heavy rainfall is strongly associated with waterborne disease outbreaks: in an analysis of 548 U.S. waterborne disease outbreaks between 1948 and 1994, over half were preceded by heavy rainfall.
  - *evidence:* Cited as a specific historical epidemiological analysis synthesized into the review's broader claim that fecal bacteria levels correlate positively with precipitation and runoff; used as key evidence for the precipitation-pathogen-outbreak linkage. (Pathogens - Historical Evidence)
  - *quote:* "over half of them were preceded by heavy rainfall"
- **[✓ verified]** Attribution of observed water-quality trends specifically to air temperature and precipitation (as opposed to land use change, fertilizer application, atmospheric deposition, point-source discharges, or management changes) is often difficult or not possible.
  - *evidence:* This is an explicit, self-stated methodological limitation of the underlying literature being reviewed, not a finding - the authors flag it to bound how strongly any single observed correlation should be read as climate-driven. (General discussion / limitations)
  - *quote:* "Attribution of trends to specific drivers like air temperature and precipitation is often not possible and many studies link observations to changes in land use, fertilizer application, atmospheric deposition, point source discharges and other factors"
- **[✓ verified]** Very few modeling studies exist that project future cyanobacterial bloom response or future pathogen fate/transport under climate change, and bloom occurrence in general is not reliably predictable because it depends on interacting natural and anthropogenic factors specific to each water body.
  - *evidence:* Self-stated research gap/limitation; directly bounds confidence in the bloom-day projection numbers given elsewhere in the same review, since the authors note this rests on very few available studies. (Cyanobacterial Blooms and Pathogens - stated limitations)
  - *quote:* "Few modeling studies assessing the response of cyanobacterial blooms to future conditions were identified in the current scientific literature"

## Data / numbers
- Cyanobacteria competitive advantage: often above 25 degrees Celsius (25°C) water temperature
- Pathogen survival rate decrease threshold: at temperatures exceeding 30°C, survival rates for fecal-sourced pathogens decrease
- Protozoan (Giardia/Cryptosporidium) survival: months to more than 1 year in cool water (~5°C or less)
- Flow-weighted nitrate concentrations increased at 22 U.S. monitoring stations from 1945-1980, then stopped increasing 1981-2008 (station breakdown: 8 East, 5 Midwest, 5 Great Plains/mainstem Mississippi, 4 West)
- National model projection of cyanobacterial bloom occurrence: ~7 days/year/water body under current (baseline) conditions -> 16-23 days in 2050 (2040-2059) -> 18-39 days in 2090 (2080-2099) [USEPA 2017 / Chapra et al. 2017 modeling]
- Lake Erie 2015 bloom: preceded by ~7 inches of rainfall at Toledo, Ohio in June; bloom affected up to 300 square miles of the western basin
- Gulf of Mexico hypoxic ('dead') zone: 13,080 km2 in 2014
- 548 waterborne disease outbreaks in the U.S. between 1948 and 1994; over half preceded by heavy rainfall
- Naegleria fowleri: 2 documented Arkansas cases associated with air temperatures above 37°C
- Chesapeake Bay (Maryland) fecal bacteria vs. precipitation relationship study period: 1979-2013
- Maryland salmonellosis/extreme-temperature study baseline: 30-year baseline, 1960-2012
- Pathogenic E. coli: survives up to two to three times longer in river/lake sediments than in the water column
- Estimated U.S. waterborne illness burden: ~90 million illnesses occur annually, with healthcare costs estimated at $2.2 to $3.7 billion annually

## Methods
This is a peer-reviewed literature review (not primary research/modeling by the authors) synthesizing post-2000 peer-reviewed and gray literature on water quality responses to historical and projected air temperature and precipitation changes. Search was conducted in standard scientific databases (e.g., PubMed) using relevant search terms. The review synthesizes two categories of underlying studies: (1) observed/historical sensitivity studies linking measured water quality to weather/climate variability, and (2) modeling studies projecting future water quality changes using watershed-scale hydrological/water-quality models (e.g., SPARROW-type models, national water-quality models such as the one behind the cited USEPA 2017/Chapra et al. 2017 bloom-day projections) driven by climate scenarios/RCPs. Geographic scope is U.S.-focused with some international comparison examples (e.g., Lake Taihu China, Lake Victoria/Lake George Africa). As a review, it does not report its own error bars; where the underlying studies it cites report uncertainty, the review flags it (e.g., noting bloom occurrence is 'typically not predictable,' precipitation projections are locally uncertain, and few modeling studies exist for blooms and pathogens specifically).

## Stated limitations
The authors explicitly limit scope to climate-driver effects only, excluding land use/management factors from analysis. They state that attribution of observed water-quality trends specifically to temperature/precipitation (versus land use, fertilizer application, atmospheric deposition, point-source discharges) "is often not possible." They flag that precipitation projections carry substantial local-scale uncertainty (more so than temperature projections), that cyanobacterial bloom occurrence is "typically not predictable" due to multi-factor interactions, that "few modeling studies" exist projecting future bloom response or future pathogen fate/transport, that existing water-quality simulation models have documented "inadequacies" (especially for sediment resuspension, subsurface transport, and extreme-flow-event pollutant response), and that there is "a high degree of spatial and temporal variability among watersheds" limiting generalization. They close by naming five specific research gaps: limited geographic/watershed-scale study coverage, insufficient long-term monitoring for attribution, poor uncertainty communication around precipitation projections, immature simulation models, and unknown BMP (Best Management Practice) performance under future climate conditions.

## Tensions with other findings
This source complicates a simple "warming and more rain = universally worse water quality" narrative in two specific ways relevant to other HAB literature: (1) For pathogens specifically, warming has a bidirectional effect - it reduces survival of fecal-indicator organisms like E. coli above 30C while simultaneously expanding the range and season of naturally occurring warm-water pathogens (Vibrio, Naegleria), so "warmer water = more pathogen risk" is not uniformly true across pathogen types. (2) The review notes some modeling studies find warming-driven increases in denitrification can outweigh precipitation-driven nutrient increases and lower nitrogen yields in some U.S. locations - i.e., warming does not always mean more nutrient loading. It also explicitly stresses strong regional divergence (Northeast/East projected to worsen; Southwest/interior West projected to see reduced loads), which should caution against generalizing any single-region HAB/nutrient finding (e.g., from Lake Erie or Chesapeake Bay) to other geographies. Finally, its explicit statement that attribution to climate drivers alone "is often not possible" is a direct caution against over-crediting temperature/precipitation as causal for any specific observed bloom or nutrient trend without controlling for land use and management confounders - relevant to the project's own causal-language discipline.

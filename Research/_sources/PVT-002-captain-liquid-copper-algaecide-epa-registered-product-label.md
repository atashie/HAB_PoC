# Captain® Liquid Copper Algaecide — EPA-Registered Product Label (EPA Reg. No. 67690-9)

**What it is.** An EPA-registered aquatic pesticide product label (EPA Reg. No. 67690-9) for Captain®, a chelated (ethanolamine-complexed) copper algaecide/herbicide manufactured and distributed by SePRO Corporation. It specifies the product's composition, approved use sites, application methods and dose rates, safety precautions, and use restrictions for controlling algae (including cyanobacteria) and copper-susceptible submersed aquatic weeds in still and flowing water.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Captain is marketed as effective against a broad spectrum of green and blue-green (cyanobacteria) algae — planktonic, filamentous, and macrophytic forms — plus copper-susceptible submersed weeds.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the label's 'Product Information' section as the product's core function/spectrum claim. Elsewhere the Application Directions section glosses the three algae categories targeted: planktonic (suspended), filamentous (mat-forming), and macrophytic algae ('chara/nitella'); the label separately claims general herbicidal effect on 'submersed weed species with susceptibility to copper.' (PRODUCT INFORMATION section (~p.3 of 16))
  - *quote:* "effective in controlling a broad range of green and blue-green (cyanobacteria) algae, including filamentous, planktonic and macrophytic"
- **[✓ verified]** The product's ethanolamine chelation is stated to keep copper from precipitating out of solution with carbonates/bicarbonates, which the label presents as the functional rationale for the chelated formulation.
  - *evidence:* Single explanatory sentence in Product Information; no supporting data/study is cited for this mechanistic claim within the excerpt. (PRODUCT INFORMATION section (~p.3 of 16))
  - *quote:* "The ethanolamines in this product reduce the precipitation of copper with carbonates and bicarbonates in the water."
- **[✓ verified]** The label states copper does not degrade in the environment and accumulates with repeated applications, distinguishing it from organic pesticides.
  - *evidence:* Made in the mandatory Environmental Hazards / Fish Advisory Statement as a plain factual assertion, contrasting copper (an element) with organic pesticides; also notes copper's pesticidal application rate exceeds nutrient-level need. (ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARDS / Fish Advisory Statement (~p.3 of 16))
  - *quote:* "copper is an element and will not break down in the environment and will therefore accumulate with repeated applications"
- **[✓ verified]** Copper treatment alone does not render water potable; drinking-water use requires additional, separate treatment.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a direct caveat immediately following the product's spectrum-of-control claim. (PRODUCT INFORMATION section (~p.3 of 16))
  - *quote:* "Treatment with this product will not by itself make water potable."
- **[✓ verified]** Killing treated biomass can deplete dissolved oxygen enough to suffocate fish and invertebrates, so the label mandates partial-waterbody treatment and a minimum retreatment interval as mitigation.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a directive safety practice, with an explicit mechanism (decomposition of dead biomass consumes oxygen) and two numeric mitigations (treat ≤1/2 of the waterbody; ≥14-day spacing), plus a shoreline-inward application pattern to let fish escape into untreated water. (DIRECTIONS FOR USE / Treatment Notes (~p.3-4 of 16))
  - *quote:* "do not treat more than ½ of the water body and wait at least 14 days between treatments"
- **[✓ verified]** Applying algaecide to a high-density cyanobacteria bloom risks lysing cells and releasing hepatotoxic and neurotoxic intracellular compounds into the water, so the label instructs avoiding treatment of already-dense toxic blooms.
  - *evidence:* The label frames this as a reason to manage cyanobacteria before they reach high density rather than treat dense blooms outright, and separately allows shortened (< 14-day) retreatment intervals only with special state/local/tribal authorization when a toxic bloom poses an acute public-health threat. (DIRECTIONS FOR USE / Treatment Notes (~p.4 of 16))
  - *quote:* "release of intracellular contents into the water. Some of these intracellular compounds are known mammalian hepato- and nervous system toxins"
- **[✓ verified]** The label's dose rates are only stated to be appropriate above specific water-chemistry thresholds; below them, non-target acute toxicity risk (including to sensitive fish) increases.
  - *evidence:* Gives explicit numeric cutoffs for pH, dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and alkalinity, and names koi and trout as species that may be killed outside the validated window ('soft or acid waters'). (DIRECTIONS FOR USE / Treatment Notes (~p.4 of 16))
  - *quote:* "low pH (≤6.5), low dissolved organic carbon (DOC) levels (3.0 mg/L or lower) and "soft" waters (i.e. alkalinity less than 50 mg/L) increases the potential acute toxicity"
- **[✓ verified]** A hard single-application ceiling of 1.0 ppm metallic copper applies to general aquatic weed/algae control.
  - *evidence:* Given both as a bare concentration cap in Product Information and, more specifically, converted to mass-per-volume in Application Directions. (APPLICATION DIRECTIONS section (~p.4-5 of 16))
  - *quote:* "do not exceed a concentration of 1.0 ppm copper (3 gallons of product or 2.74 lbs metallic copper per acre-foot) during any single application"
- **[✓ verified]** For whole-waterbody treatment (staggering half the waterbody every 14 days), the label sets an annual cap of 21.9 lbs metallic copper per acre-foot (8 applications/year at up to 1 ppm) across an 8-month/244-day season.
  - *evidence:* This is the label's own calculated ceiling, derived from the 14-day/half-body staggering rule multiplied out over 244 days. (APPLICATION DIRECTIONS / Whole Waterbodies subsection (~p.5 of 16))
  - *quote:* "Maximum annual application rate of 21.9 lbs of metallic copper per acre-foot (8 applications per year at up to 1 ppm)."
- **[✓ verified]** For partial-waterbody treatment (staggered discrete sections summing to ≤ half the waterbody, still within the 14-day interval), the label sets a higher annual cap of 46.6 lbs metallic copper per acre-foot per year (17 applications/year at up to 1 ppm).
  - *evidence:* The label states this cap is calculated as the maximum number of applications possible under a 14-day minimum retreatment interval over 244 days; a clean, unbroken sentence later in the same subsection independently restates the 46.6 lbs ceiling as an absolute per-management-unit limit regardless of target pest. (APPLICATION DIRECTIONS / Partial Waterbodies subsection (~p.5-6 of 16))
  - *quote:* "Do not apply more than 46.6 lbs. of metallic copper to a water management unit, regardless of the pest(s) targeted by applications."
- **[✓ verified]** Aquaculture ponds with fish present are restricted to a lower single-application cap (0.4 ppm) than the general 1.0 ppm ceiling, specifically for nuisance-algae targeting.
  - *evidence:* Listed under 'Restrictions' as an explicit, fish-present-specific dose ceiling distinct from the general aquatic-weed-control cap. (Restrictions section (~p.4 of 16))
  - *quote:* "DO NOT exceed a concentration of 0.4 ppm during any single application when targeting nuisance algae"
- **[✓ verified]** The label states its own efficacy can fail under specific conditions: retreating too soon after a prior application, or heavy infestation combined with low flow.
  - *evidence:* Two separate qualitative efficacy caveats in the label: repeat-timing failure in Treatment Notes, and flow/infestation-driven 'poor distribution resulting in unsatisfactory control' in the flowing-water application section; the label also recommends a site-specific pre-application dose-determination test rather than relying on labeled ranges alone, 'unless an effective dose is already known for the given target pest population.' (DIRECTIONS FOR USE / Treatment Notes (~p.3 of 16); also Drip/Metering Pump (Flowing Water) section (~p.6-7 of 16) and Pre-Application Dose Determination subsection (~p.5-6 of 16))
  - *quote:* "Repeating application of this product too soon after initial application may have no effect."
- **[⚠ partial]** The label explicitly supports combining Captain with other aquatic herbicides (endothall-based products, diquat) rather than presenting copper as a stand-alone treatment.
  - *evidence:* Gives a specific co-application ppm range for endothall products in flowing canals, and a fixed volumetric mixing ratio with diquat for Hydrilla control; a jar compatibility test is recommended before any tank mix. (Tank Mixes / Captain and Endothall subsection (~p.12 of 16); Hydrilla Control – Captain + Diquat Tank Mix subsection (~p.12 of 16, excerpt truncated before full directions))
  - *quote:* "Teton (0.05 – 2.0 ppm) or Cascade (0.35-3.0 ppm) for a minimum of one hour"
  - *reviewer:* The label does support tank mixing with these herbicides and provides specific ppm ranges/ratios. However, the phrase 'rather than presenting copper as a stand-alone treatment' misrepresents the label's structure: stand-alone treatment is presented as the primary approach (with extensive dedicated subsections for whole waterbodies, partial waterbodies, pre-application dose determination, surface spray, etc.), while tank mixing is presented as supplementary/optional. The label presents both options, not stand-alone 'rather than' tank mixing.

## Data / numbers
- Active ingredient: Copper Ethanolamine Complex = 28.2% of product (mixed CAS 82027-59-6 & 14215-52-2); Other Ingredients = 71.8%; metallic copper equivalent = 9.1% of product
- Single-application cap: do not exceed 1.0 ppm copper in any waters per application (= 3 gal product, or 2.74 lbs metallic copper, per acre-foot)
- Aquaculture ponds with fish present: do not exceed 0.4 ppm copper per single application when targeting nuisance algae (stricter than the general 1.0 ppm cap)
- Effective surface-spray/injection algaecide range: 0.2-1.0 ppm copper (static or minimal-flow water)
- Whole-waterbody strategy: max 21.9 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (8 applications/year at up to 1 ppm), based on treating each half of the waterbody every 14 days across an 8-month/244-day season
- Partial-waterbody strategy (staggered ≤half-body sections): max 46.6 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (17 applications/year at up to 1 ppm) over the same 244-day season, 14-day minimum retreatment interval
- Retreatment/oxygen-depletion mitigation: treat no more than 1/2 of a waterbody at a time; wait at least 14 days between treatments
- Label-validated water chemistry window: pH > 6.5, DOC > 3.0 mg/L, alkalinity > 50 mg/L. Below these (pH ≤ 6.5; DOC ≤ 3.0 mg/L; alkalinity < 50 mg/L, i.e. soft/acidic water) the label states increased acute toxicity risk to non-target species (e.g., koi, trout)
- Flowing-water dosing examples (Table 2): at 1 cfs (450 gal/min), 0.2-1.0 ppm copper = 0.2-1.0 quart product/hr = 3.2-15.7 mL/min; at 100 cfs (45,000 gal/min), 0.2-1.0 ppm copper = 20-100 quart/hr = 315-1,577 mL/min
- Flow-rate estimation formula given in the label: cfs = average width (ft) x average depth (ft) x average velocity (ft/s) x 0.9
- Flowing-water maintenance retreatment frequency: every 2-6 weeks
- Pulse application (irrigation conveyance systems only): max 13 lbs metallic copper/year per 5 miles of conveyance per cfs; up to 0.5 lbs metallic copper (0.55 gal product) per cfs per 5-30 mile treatment segment
- Chemigation dosing: 0.91-9.1 gal product per 1,000,000 gal water (0.3-3.0 gal product per acre-foot) = 0.1-1.0 ppm copper; do not exceed 1.0 ppm or 0.91 gal per 100,000 gal water
- Tank mix with endothall products (flowing canals): Captain 0.1-1.0 ppm + Teton 0.05-2.0 ppm OR Cascade 0.35-3.0 ppm, minimum 1 hour contact time
- Tank mix for Hydrilla control: Captain:diquat = 2:1 ratio by volume (e.g., 4 gal Captain : 2 gal diquat); directions truncated in the extracted excerpt before contact time/further detail is given

## Methods
This is a regulatory use-label, not a study — there is no experimental methodology, only prescribed application methods and dose tables standing in for "method." Four application routes are specified: (1) surface spray/injection in static or minimal-flow water, dosed by algae type (planktonic, filamentous, macrophytic) per a garbled Table 1, with a clean restatement that "proper application rates should be maintained between 0.2 and 1.0 ppm"; (2) drip/metering-pump application for flowing water (canals, ditches), using a stated flow-estimation formula (cfs = width × depth × velocity × 0.9) and a clearly formatted rate table (Table 2) to counteract dilution; (3) "Pulse Application," restricted to constructed irrigation conveyances/laterals only; and (4) chemigation system injection via sprinkler/drip systems (Table 3). The label states these methods work under the conditions it specifies (static/low-flow water at labeled ppm; flowing water when properly metered to sustain ~1.0 ppm for the exposure period) and states they under-perform or fail when: infestations are heavy and flow is low ("may cause poor distribution resulting in unsatisfactory control"); retreatment follows too soon after a prior application ("may have no effect"); or water chemistry falls outside the validated window (pH ≤6.5, DOC ≤3.0 mg/L, alkalinity <50 mg/L), where the label describes elevated non-target toxicity risk rather than confirmed control benefit. It also recommends a site-specific pre-application dose-determination test rather than relying solely on the labeled rate range, and explicitly supports tank-mixing with other aquatic herbicides (endothall products, diquat) as part of achieving control — i.e., copper alone is presented as one component of a potentially combined treatment approach, not a universally sufficient one.

## Stated limitations
The label states several caveats itself: copper treatment "will not by itself make water potable" (drinking-water use needs separate treatment); copper "is an element and will not break down in the environment and will therefore accumulate with repeated applications" — a persistence/loading concern distinct from single-dose toxicity; treating high-density cyanobacterial blooms risks releasing hepato- and nervous-system-toxic intracellular compounds into the water, so the label instructs avoiding application to already-dense toxic blooms (or obtaining special authorization if unavoidable); oxygen depletion from decomposing treated biomass can suffocate fish and invertebrates unless treatment is kept partial and spaced ≥14 days apart; labeled rates are stated as appropriate only for pH >6.5, DOC >3.0 mg/L and alkalinity >50 mg/L, with sensitive species (koi, trout) at risk of being killed outside that window; heavy infestation combined with low flow may yield "unsatisfactory control"; retreating too soon "may have no effect"; and public-water applications require separate state/local/tribal permitting that the label itself does not grant. The excerpted text contains no controlled-trial efficacy data (no percent-control figures, replicate counts, or confidence intervals) — dose guidance is given as ranges with qualitative modifiers ("use lower rates for light infestations... higher rates on heavy blooms") rather than measured, uncertainty-bounded outcomes. The label also repeatedly recommends professional consultation (a "SePRO Aquatic Specialist") and a site-specific pre-application dose-determination test, implying the generic labeled rates are a starting range rather than a guaranteed effective dose for any given water body.

## Tensions with other findings
This is a manufacturer/registrant-issued regulatory label (SePRO Corporation is the registrant and is repeatedly named as the source of implementation advice), not an independent peer-reviewed efficacy study; its dosing and "effective control" language is regulatory/operational guidance rather than trial-validated efficacy evidence in the excerpted text, so it should be weighted as such alongside independent HAB-treatment research. More substantively for HAB decision-support framing: the label's own toxin-release warning complicates any simple "detect a high-density/high-risk cyanobacteria bloom → recommend copper algaecide treatment" rule, because it states that applying algaecide to a high-density toxin-producing bloom can itself rupture cells and release hepato- and nervous-system toxins into the water — the exact scenario in which a detection system would most urgently flag a treatment need is also the scenario the label says to avoid treating (or treat only under special authorization). Correlation between "copper applied" and "bloom controlled" in any such program is not evidence the label itself substantiates causally with data in this excerpt — it is asserted, not demonstrated, here. Separately, the stated non-degradation/accumulation of copper is in tension with treating copper algaecide as a routinely repeatable, environmentally neutral response: the label's own annual maximums (21.9-46.6 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year) imply repeat seasonal dosing that, by the label's own account, does not break down and therefore accumulates over multiple seasons — a long-term environmental-loading consideration that a single-application risk assessment would miss.

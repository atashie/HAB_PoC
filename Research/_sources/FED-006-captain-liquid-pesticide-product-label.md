# CAPTAIN LIQUID Pesticide Product Label

**What it is.** An EPA-accepted FIFRA registration-review decision letter and stamped product label (dated March 23, 2022) for CAPTAIN® LIQUID COPPER ALGAECIDE (EPA Reg. No. 67690-9, Decision No. 559224), a copper-ethanolamine-complex algaecide/herbicide made by SePRO Corporation, labeled for controlling algae (including cyanobacteria) and copper-susceptible submersed weeds in lakes, ponds, reservoirs, aquaculture, and irrigation/drainage/chemigation systems.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Captain is a chelated copper (copper ethanolamine complex) formulation labeled as effective against a broad range of green algae and cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) — filamentous, planktonic, and macrophytic forms — and as an herbicide on copper-susceptible submersed weeds.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the label's Product Information section describing intended pest scope. (Label booklet, "PRODUCT INFORMATION" section)
  - *quote:* "effective in controlling a broad range of green and blue-green (cyanobacteria) algae, including filamentous, planktonic and macrophytic"
- **[✓ verified]** The active ingredient is a copper ethanolamine complex at 28.2% concentration, equivalent to 9.1% metallic copper.
  - *evidence:* Given verbatim in the Active Ingredient declaration on the front label panel. (Front label panel, "Active Ingredient" table)
  - *quote:* "Copper Ethanolamine Complex† (Mixed CAS#'s 82027-59-6 & 14215-52-2).........28.2% ... †Metallic copper equivalent = 9.1%"
- **[✓ verified]** The label imposes a blanket ceiling of 1.0 ppm metallic copper for any single application to any water, with an effective-control range of 0.2-1.0 ppm.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a general restriction in Product Information and repeated as the working range in the surface spray/injection application section. (Label booklet, "PRODUCT INFORMATION" and "Surface Spray/Injection Algaecide Application")
  - *quote:* "Do not apply more than 1.0 ppm as metallic copper in any waters during any single application."
- **[✓ verified]** Copper does not break down in the environment (unlike most organic pesticides) and therefore accumulates in treated waters with repeated applications, which the label ties to its toxicity to fish and aquatic organisms.
  - *evidence:* Stated as an environmental-hazard/persistence warning, not as a measured accumulation rate. (Label booklet, "ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARDS — Fish Advisory Statement")
  - *quote:* "copper is an element and will not break down in the environment and will therefore accumulate with repeated applications"
- **[✓ verified]** Killing algae/plant biomass with this product can itself deplete dissolved oxygen as the dead biomass decomposes, risking fish and invertebrate suffocation; the label's mitigation is to cap any single pass at half the water body and require at least 14 days between treatments.
  - *evidence:* Explicit self-identified secondary hazard of treatment plus the label's own mitigating instructions. (Label booklet, "Treatment Notes")
  - *quote:* "do not treat more than ½ of the water body and wait at least 14 days between treatments to avoid depletion of oxygen due to decaying vegetation"
- **[✓ verified]** Applying algaecide to high-density cyanobacterial blooms can rupture cells and release intracellular toxins (hepatotoxins and neurotoxins) into the water, so the label directs applicators to avoid treating high-density toxic-cyanobacteria blooms rather than to use treatment as a mitigation for them.
  - *evidence:* Direct causal hazard statement in the label connecting algaecide-induced cell lysis to acute toxin release, with an explicit avoidance recommendation. (Label booklet, hazard paragraph following the algaecide application-rate table (Table 1))
  - *quote:* "Application of algaecides to high density blooms of cyanobacteria can result in the release of intracellular contents into the water. Some of these intracellular compounds are known mammalian hepato- and nervous system toxins."
- **[✓ verified]** The label sets explicit water-chemistry thresholds below which non-target acute toxicity risk (e.g., to koi and trout) increases: pH ≤ 6.5, dissolved organic carbon (DOC) ≤ 3.0 mg/L, and alkalinity < 50 mg/L ("soft" water); the approved rates assume water above all three thresholds.
  - *evidence:* Explicit numeric safety thresholds with named sensitive-species examples, distinguishing waters where labeled rates are/aren't validated as safe. (Label booklet, water-chemistry precaution paragraph (following Table 1))
  - *quote:* "low pH (≤6.5), low dissolved organic carbon (DOC) levels (3.0 mg/L or lower) and “soft” waters (i.e. alkalinity less than 50 mg/L) increases the potential acute toxicity to non-target aquatic organisms"
- **[⚠ partial]** For whole-waterbody treatment, the label caps cumulative annual dosing at 21.9 lbs metallic copper per acre-foot per year, built from up to 8 applications/year at 1 ppm each, staggering treatment of each half of the water body every 14 days across an 8-month (244-day) season.
  - *evidence:* Explicit annual-cap arithmetic tied to the 14-day/half-body treatment pattern described elsewhere in the label. (Label booklet, "APPLICATION DIRECTIONS — Whole Waterbodies")
  - *quote:* "Maximum annual application rate of 21.9 lbs of metallic copper per acre-foot (8 applications per year at up to 1 ppm)"
  - *reviewer:* Omits material caveat: source states this rate applies normally, but explicitly permits exceeding 21.9 lbs with authorization from state/local/tribal authorities in urgent public health situations involving rapidly reproducing toxic algal species. Claim presents this as an absolute cap without noting the authorization exception.
- **[⚠ partial]** For partial-waterbody treatment (discrete sections summing to no more than half the waterbody within the 14-day retreatment window), the annual cap roughly doubles to 46.6 lbs metallic copper per acre-foot per year, from up to 17 applications/year.
  - *evidence:* Explicit annual-cap arithmetic for the partial-treatment use pattern, based on the same 14-day minimum retreatment interval. (Label booklet, "APPLICATION DIRECTIONS — Partial Waterbodies")
  - *quote:* "Maximum annual application rate of 46.6 lbs. of metallic copper per acre-foot per year (17 applications per year at up to 1 ppm)"
  - *reviewer:* Omits material caveat: source states this rate applies normally, but explicitly permits exceeding 46.6 lbs with authorization from state/local/tribal authorities in urgent public health situations. Claim presents as absolute cap without noting the authorization exception.
- **[✓ verified]** For aquaculture ponds with fish present, the label imposes a stricter single-application ceiling of 0.4 ppm metallic copper when targeting nuisance algae, below the general 1.0 ppm cap used elsewhere.
  - *evidence:* Explicit, distinct restriction called out separately from the general application ceiling. (Label booklet, "Restrictions")
  - *quote:* "When treating aquaculture ponds when fish are present, DO NOT exceed a concentration of 0.4 ppm during any single application when targeting nuisance algae."
- **[✓ verified]** Treatment with Captain does not by itself make water potable; drinking-water sources require additional, separate potable-water treatment after algaecide application.
  - *evidence:* Stated as an explicit scope limit in the same Product Information paragraph that sets the 1.0 ppm ceiling. (Label booklet, "PRODUCT INFORMATION")
  - *quote:* "Treatment with this product will not by itself make water potable."

## Data / numbers
- Active ingredient: Copper Ethanolamine Complex 28.2% (w/w); metallic copper equivalent 9.1% (front-label declaration, not an experimental measurement)
- General single-application ceiling: 1.0 ppm as metallic copper, in any water, any single application
- Labeled effective-control range: 0.2-1.0 ppm copper (Table 1), corresponding to 0.6-3.0 gallons of product per acre-foot for planktonic/filamentous algae
- Aquaculture with fish present: single-application ceiling lowered to 0.4 ppm metallic copper when targeting nuisance algae
- Whole-waterbody maximum annual rate: 21.9 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (8 applications/yr at up to 1 ppm; half-body staggered every 14 days over 244 days / ~8 months)
- Partial-waterbody maximum annual rate: 46.6 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (17 applications/yr at up to 1 ppm; 14-day minimum retreatment interval over 244 days)
- Minimum retreatment interval: 14 days between treatments, with no single pass exceeding half the water body
- Non-target-toxicity risk thresholds (labeled rates not validated below these): pH ≤ 6.5; DOC ≤ 3.0 mg/L; alkalinity < 50 mg/L
- Pulse (irrigation-conveyance) application: max 13 lbs metallic copper per year per 5 miles of conveyance per cfs; up to 0.5 lbs metallic copper (0.55 gal product) per cfs per 5-30 mile treatment segment
- Chemigation dosing: 0.91-9.1 gallons Captain per 1,000,000 gallons water (0.3-3.0 gal/acre-foot) yields 0.1-1.0 ppm copper; hard cap 1.0 ppm or 0.91 gal per 100,000 gallons
- Flow-rate estimation formula for flowing-water dosing: cfs = average width (ft) × average depth (ft) × average velocity (ft/s) × 0.9
- Maintenance retreatment cadence for flowing-water/rotational programs: every 2-6 weeks
- Unit definition used throughout dose tables: 1 acre = 43,560 ft²
- Regulatory identifiers/dates: EPA Reg. No. 67690-9; Decision No. 559224; label acceptance date March 23, 2022; prior labeling may be sold/distributed for 12 months from that date per 40 CFR 152.130(c)

## Methods
This is a regulatory product label, not an experimental study, so there is no statistical method or dataset to evaluate — figures are legally-set application ceilings/ranges, not measured means, and the label reports no baseline or uncertainty for any figure. It documents a chelated copper (copper ethanolamine complex) algaecide/herbicide and four label-approved delivery methods: (1) surface spray/injection for static or low-flow water, dosed per Table 1 (0.2-1.0 ppm copper by algae type/density); (2) drip or metering-pump dosing for flowing water, computed from a field-measured flow rate (cfs = avg. width x avg. depth x avg. velocity x 0.9) applied against Table 2; (3) "pulse" dosing for constructed irrigation conveyances/laterals/aqueducts, capped by conveyance length and cfs; and (4) continuous chemigation dosing through sprinkler/drip irrigation systems (Table 3, 0.1-1.0 ppm copper). The label recommends a pre-application dose-determination test (a small-scale simulation of the full treatment) to find the minimum efficacious concentration before full-scale use, and a before-and-after-application scouting protocol to detect resistant weed populations. It states the product is labeled to work only in water above pH 6.5, DOC above 3.0 mg/L, and alkalinity above 50 mg/L, and only up to 1.0 ppm metallic copper per single application (0.4 ppm where fish are present in aquaculture); it states control can fail (poor distribution, unsatisfactory control) under heavy infestation combined with low flow, and that reapplying too soon after an initial treatment "may have no effect."

## Stated limitations
The label itself states: treatment will not make water potable and drinking-water sources need separate additional treatment; repeating application too soon after an initial application may have no effect; heavy infestations combined with low flow may cause poor distribution and unsatisfactory control; copper does not degrade and accumulates with repeated applications; applying to high-density blooms of toxin-producing cyanobacteria risks releasing hepato- and neurotoxins into the water, so the label directs avoiding application under those conditions (absent emergency authorization from state/local/tribal water authorities); labeled rates are not validated as safe in soft/acidic/low-DOC water (pH ≤ 6.5, DOC ≤ 3.0 mg/L, alkalinity < 50 mg/L), where sensitive species such as koi and trout may be killed; results vary with algae species/density, desired control distance, flow rate, and water-quality effects on the chemical's efficacy; non-uniform distribution in chemigation can cause crop injury, lack of effectiveness, or illegal pesticide residues; resistance may develop, so scouting and rotation of mechanisms of action (MOAs) are recommended; and permits/consultation with state or local pesticide-regulating agencies may be required before treating public waters.

## Tensions with other findings
The label's own hazard language complicates a simple 'detect bloom via remote sensing/satellite -> dispatch algaecide' decision rule that a HAB early-warning tool might otherwise imply: it explicitly warns that treating a high-density cyanobacterial bloom with this algaecide can rupture cells and acutely release intracellular hepato- and neurotoxins into the water, meaning fast action on a detected high-biomass bloom is not unambiguously protective and may transiently increase toxin exposure — a nuance any 'risk readout -> action window' framing (as in this project's Part B tool) should carry rather than present treatment as a simple bloom-reduction action. Separately, copper's stated environmental persistence ("will not break down" and "will accumulate with repeated applications") is in tension with treating algaecide application as a fully reversible, environmentally neutral intervention, which matters for any multi-year management-program framing. Finally, as a regulatory/safety document, this source sets legally binding dose ceilings and water-chemistry precautions but does not itself contain monitoring data, trend data, or predictive-model validation; it cannot be used to validate a remote-sensing- or in-situ-based HAB risk model, only to bound the treatment-and-management action space (timing/dose constraints) once a bloom has been detected by other sources in this review.

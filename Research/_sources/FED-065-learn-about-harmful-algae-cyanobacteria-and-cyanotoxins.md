# Learn about Harmful Algae, Cyanobacteria and Cyanotoxins

**What it is.** An official U.S. EPA educational/reference webpage (part of EPA's HABs program) that introduces and defines harmful algal blooms, cyanobacteria, and the major cyanotoxin classes (microcystins, cylindrospermopsins, anatoxins/guanitoxins, saxitoxins, nodularins), their general health/ecological effects, and the federal regulatory framework, while pointing readers to separate EPA documents for quantitative thresholds and monitoring/management detail.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[⚠ partial]** EPA defines Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) as algal growths that harm human health and the environment, arising from excessive biomass, toxin production, or environmental degradation such as hypoxia/anoxia or acidification.
  - *evidence:* This is the page's core definitional statement for the HAB concept, given in the introductory/HABs section. ("Harmful Algal Blooms" section (top of page))
  - *quote:* "Algal growths that lead to impacts on human health and the environment."
  - *reviewer:* The source provides only the basic definition: 'Algal growths that lead to impacts on human health and the environment.' The claim adds specific causative mechanisms (excessive biomass, toxin production, environmental degradation/hypoxia/anoxia/acidification) that are discussed elsewhere in the text but are NOT stated as part of the EPA's definition itself. The definition stands alone without enumerating causes.
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) are microscopic, photosynthetic organisms common to freshwater, estuarine, and marine waters.
  - *evidence:* Direct definitional statement distinguishing cyanobacteria as the photosynthetic organisms responsible for cyanoHABs. ("Cyanobacteria" section)
  - *quote:* "Also known as blue-green algae, are frequently found in freshwater, estuarine, and marine waters. These tiny, or microscopic, organisms use direct sunlight to produce their own food (i.e., are photosynthetic)."
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanotoxins are produced under specific environmental conditions (light intensity, nutrients, temperature, salinity) and are released mainly when cyanobacterial cells die and rupture (lysis), not necessarily continuously during growth.
  - *evidence:* Describes the conditions associated with toxin production and the release mechanism (cell lysis) as stated by the source; this is presented as an association/mechanism, not a proven causal trigger with specified thresholds. ("Cyanotoxins" section)
  - *quote:* "Under the right conditions (i.e., light intensity, nutrients, temperature, salinity, etc.), cyanobacterial blooms can produce toxic byproducts... The release of these toxins...occurs mostly during cell death and lysis (i.e., cell rupture)."
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystins are described as the most common cyanobacterial toxins, with over 200 known congeners (microcystin-LR most studied); they are primarily hepatotoxic, with some evidence linking microcystin-LR to liver and colorectal cancers, though EPA states current evidence is inadequate to assess carcinogenic potential.
  - *evidence:* Combines a toxin-class description, a congener count, a suggestive (not conclusive) cancer-association statement, and EPA's own caveat about insufficient evidence for a formal carcinogenicity determination. ("Microcystins" section)
  - *quote:* "more than 200 forms or congeners with microcystin-LR being the most studied... evidence to link microcystin-LR with both liver and colorectal cancers... EPA has determined that there is inadequate information to assess carcinogenic potential."
- **[⚠ partial]** Cylindrospermopsins have three known environmental congeners and act primarily on the liver and kidney, with potential to increase cellular stress, affect development, and cause DNA damage; EPA again states insufficient evidence to assess carcinogenicity.
  - *evidence:* Toxin-class description plus explicit EPA caveat on carcinogenic-potential evidence. ("Cylindrospermopsins" section)
  - *quote:* "Three congeners have been found in the environment including cylindrospermopsin, 7-epicylindrospermopsin, and 7-deoxycylindrospermopsin... can increase cellular stress, influence developmental processes, and cause serious DNA damage... Inadequate information to assess carcinogenic potential."
  - *reviewer:* The source confirms three congeners, cellular stress, developmental effects, and DNA damage. However, the claim adds that cylindrospermopsins 'act primarily on the liver and kidney'—this organ-specific targeting is NOT stated in the source text for cylindrospermopsins. The source does not specify organ targets for this toxin class.
- **[✓ verified]** Anatoxins/guanitoxins are neurotoxins that bind neuronal nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, with effects ranging from loss of coordination to death by respiratory failure; EPA states there is insufficient information on their carcinogenicity.
  - *evidence:* Mechanism-of-action and symptom-severity statement plus an explicit evidence-gap caveat. ("Anatoxins/Guanitoxins" section)
  - *quote:* "Neurotoxins that bind to neuronal nicotinic acetylcholine receptors... loss of coordination to death due to respiratory failure... Insufficient information available on the carcinogenicity."
- **[✓ verified]** Saxitoxins are potent neurotoxins representative of the Paralytic Shellfish Poisoning (PSP) toxin family, with health effects ranging from minor tingling/numbness to death from respiratory failure; no carcinogenicity information is available for this class per EPA.
  - *evidence:* Toxin-class description, symptom range, and an explicit statement of a total data gap on carcinogenicity (stronger caveat than for the other toxin classes, which had 'inadequate'/'insufficient' evidence rather than 'no information'). ("Saxitoxins" section)
  - *quote:* "Representative of a large toxin family referred to as the Paralytic Shellfish Poisoning (PSP) toxins... minor tingling and numbness to death from respiratory failure... No information is available on the carcinogenicity."
- **[✓ verified]** Nodularins are produced by cyanobacteria of the genus Nodularia, which primarily thrive in salty or brackish coastal or inland waters (rather than typical freshwater lakes).
  - *evidence:* Definitional/taxonomic statement distinguishing nodularin-producing cyanobacteria's preferred salinity habitat from freshwater microcystin/cylindrospermopsin producers. ("Nodularins" section)
  - *quote:* "Produced from species of the genus Nodularia that primarily thrive in salty or brackish coastal or inland waters."
- **[✓ verified]** Beyond direct toxicity, HABs cause ecological and economic harm through hypoxia/anoxia or acidification, unsightly water conditions, taste-and-odor problems affecting recreation/tourism/drinking water, and (for marine taxa such as K. brevis) large die-offs of fish, birds, and marine mammals including dolphins and manatees.
  - *evidence:* Lists non-toxin-mediated and marine-specific ecological/economic impact pathways described on the page. (Impacts/overview portions of the page and marine-taxa (K. brevis) description)
  - *quote:* "Low/no oxygen (hypoxia/anoxia) or acidification... Unsightly conditions or taste and odor issues that affect recreation, tourism, and drinking water... Large die-offs of fish, birds and marine mammals including dolphins and manatees."
- **[✓ verified]** Regulatory responsibility for HABs in the U.S. is split by statute: the Harmful Algal Bloom and Hypoxia Research and Control Act (HABHRCA) assigns NOAA responsibility for marine and Great Lakes HABs, while EPA holds responsibility for other freshwaters and separately has regulatory authority over marine waters affected by HABs under the Clean Water Act.
  - *evidence:* States the statutory/regulatory division of federal responsibility for HABs, relevant to who 'owns' monitoring and response duties. (Regulatory-authority section of the page)
  - *quote:* "Harmful Algal Bloom and Hypoxia Research and Control Act (HABHRCA)... regulatory responsibilities for marine waters affected by HABs under the Clean Water Act."
- **[✓ verified]** The page itself is an introductory/educational reference, not a technical or primary-data source: it names toxin classes and general contributing conditions but supplies no quantitative exposure thresholds, advisory concentrations, temperature/nutrient ranges, or monitoring data, instead pointing to separate EPA Health Effects Support Documents, Drinking Water Health Advisory documents, and state/tribal program links for that information.
  - *evidence:* This is a scope/limitation observation rather than a scientific finding: confirmed by the complete absence of any numeric concentration, dose, or advisory-level figures in the fetched text, combined with explicit references to other named EPA documents as the place such figures reside. (Whole-page assessment; 'Related EPA Programs and Resources' section listing linked documents)
  - *quote:* "US EPA Health Effects Support Documents for microcystins, cylindrospermopsin, anatoxin-a... US EPA Drinking Water Health Advisory documents"

## Methods
This page is not a primary research study and reports no original data collection, model, or statistical method. It is an EPA educational/reference webpage that synthesizes existing science and EPA's own regulatory/health-effects documents (e.g., EPA Health Effects Support Documents for microcystins, cylindrospermopsin, and anatoxin-a; EPA Drinking Water Health Advisory documents) to define HAB-related terms and toxin classes. Per the fetch, it does not itself present or cite a dataset, sampling method, remote-sensing approach, or model; it functions as a glossary/orientation layer that points readers to other EPA documents, NOAA/NCCOS and Woods Hole resources, an ITRC cyanotoxins report, and state/tribal program pages for methodological or quantitative detail.

## Stated limitations
The source itself states, per toxin class, that evidence is insufficient for formal carcinogenicity determinations: for microcystins, "EPA has determined that there is inadequate information to assess carcinogenic potential...primarily due to the limitations in available human studies...and lack of long-term animal studies"; for cylindrospermopsin, "Inadequate information to assess carcinogenic potential"; for anatoxins, "Insufficient information available on the carcinogenicity...or on potential carcinogenic precursor effects"; for saxitoxins, "No information is available on the carcinogenicity." Separately (a scope limitation rather than a stated caveat), the page provides no quantitative exposure thresholds, advisory concentrations, temperature/nutrient ranges, or monitoring/detection data itself, deferring instead to linked EPA Health Effects Support Documents, Drinking Water Health Advisory documents, and state/tribal resources.

## Tensions with other findings
This page is background/glossary material, not a data or methods source, so it cannot itself be fused with satellite (e.g., EPA CyAN) or in-situ (Water Quality Portal, NWIS) signals - it supplies no thresholds, concentrations, or model inputs to feed a risk model. It does, however, complicate any simple "toxin present = health risk" framing used elsewhere in a HAB literature base: EPA explicitly says evidence for microcystin-LR carcinogenicity is 'inadequate' and for the other three toxin classes ranges from 'insufficient' to 'no information,' even while noting suggestive cancer associations for microcystin-LR - a caution against overstating causal health claims for any single detected toxin concentration. It also broadens scope beyond cyanobacteria/cyanoHABs (the project's likely focus) to marine dinoflagellate HABs (e.g., K. brevis/brevetoxins, Prymnesium parvum), which are a different taxonomic and regulatory regime (NOAA-led under HABHRCA) from the freshwater cyanoHABs EPA/CyAN typically addresses - a scope distinction worth keeping straight when citing 'EPA HAB' material generically.

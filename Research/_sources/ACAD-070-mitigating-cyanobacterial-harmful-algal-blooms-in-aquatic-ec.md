# Mitigating cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms in aquatic ecosystems impacted by climate change and anthropogenic nutrients

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1568988315301049

**What it is.** A 2016 peer-reviewed review article in the journal Harmful Algae (vol. 54, pp. 213-222) by Hans W. Paerl and seven co-authors, surveying traditional and experimental strategies for mitigating cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (CyanoHABs) and arguing that climate-driven changes to watershed hydrology, water-body temperature, mixing regime, and internal nutrient dynamics favor cyanobacteria and may require adjusted nutrient-reduction targets and modified physical-control techniques.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Mitigating the global expansion of cyanobacterial harmful blooms (CyanoHABs) is framed as a major, ongoing challenge for both researchers and water-resource managers.
  - *evidence:* Opening framing sentence of the abstract; establishes the motivating problem for the review rather than a tested/quantified result. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "a major challenge facing researchers and resource managers"
- **[✓ verified]** A range of traditional and experimental physical/biological control methods -- nutrient load reduction, artificial mixing and flushing, and omnivorous fish removal -- have already been used to try to reduce cyanoHAB occurrence.
  - *evidence:* Abstract lists specific example categories of mitigation technique already in practice, contrasting 'traditional' vs. 'experimental' approaches. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "nutrient load reduction... artificial mixing and flushing, omnivorous fish removal"
- **[✓ verified]** Climate-driven changes to watershed hydrology/nutrient loading, lake and estuary temperature, and mixing regimes are described as favoring cyanobacteria over other phytoplankton, and as a factor that could change how well existing control measures perform.
  - *evidence:* Central mechanistic/associative claim of the review linking climate change to a competitive advantage for cyanobacteria and to mitigation efficacy; stated as a general association in a review abstract, not as a quantified causal test -- correlation/mechanism framing, not a controlled causal demonstration. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "favor CyanoHABs over other phytoplankton and could influence the efficacy of control measures"
- **[✓ verified]** Because climate change is said to influence virtually all mitigation strategies, the authors argue managers may need to set new (revised) nutrient input reduction targets and establish water-body-specific nutrient-bloom thresholds.
  - *evidence:* This is presented as the paper's central management recommendation/conclusion in the abstract. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "may require setting new nutrient input reduction targets and establishing nutrient-bloom thresholds for impacted waters"
- **[✓ verified]** Physical-forcing control techniques specifically -- e.g., artificial flushing and mixing -- are said to require adjustment to remain effective as climate change alters the systems they are applied to.
  - *evidence:* Abstract draws out a specific implication for one named class of mitigation technique (physical forcing), separate from the general nutrient-target claim. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "flushing and artificial mixing, will need adjustments to deal with the ramifications of climate change"
- **[✓ verified]** The paper's stated purpose/scope is to review the current suite of CyanoHAB mitigation strategies and evaluate options for adapting and optimizing them for a future with greater human population pressure and climate change.
  - *evidence:* Explicit statement of the paper's aim and scope, closing the abstract; consistent with PubMed's classification of the piece as a Review. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "examine the suite of current mitigation strategies and the potential options for adapting and optimizing them"

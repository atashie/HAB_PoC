# Bench-scale assessment of three copper-based algaecide products for cyanobacteria management in source water

**What it is.** A bench-scale (laboratory batch-reactor) dose-response study that tested three commercial copper-based algaecide products - Cutrine Ultra, EarthTec, and SeClear - at full/half/quarter doses against the indigenous cyanobacteria community in raw source water drawn from Lake Rockwell, a drinking-water reservoir in northeast Ohio, in order to identify each product's optimal dose for suppressing cyanobacteria while limiting extracellular microcystin release and population rebound.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** The study is a bench-scale dose-response trial that tested three commercial copper-based algaecides on the indigenous cyanobacteria community in raw source water from Lake Rockwell (a drinking-water reservoir) to determine each product's optimal dose.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as the study objective in the abstract. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "Three copper-based algaecide products were investigated for use in a drinking water source to address cyanobacteria growth. Bench-scale experiments were used to determine the optimal dose of each product given Lake Rockwell's indigenous population and reservoir-specific characteristics."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors determined Cutrine Ultra's optimal dose to be a quarter dose (0.125 mg/L Cu), which reduced the cyanobacteria population by 55% with limited microcystin release and minimal rebound, and concluded it was the most effective product overall on cost and performance grounds.
  - *evidence:* Headline abstract finding, reiterated in the paper's per-product 'optimal dose determination' discussion and conclusion. (Abstract; Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "The optimal dose of Cutrine Ultra was determined to be a quarter dose (corresponding to 0.125 mg/L Cu) as it decreased 55% of the cyanobacteria population with limited release of microcystin and minimal rebound in the cyanobacteria population."
- **[✓ verified]** EarthTec's optimal dose was judged to be the half dose (0.5 mg/L Cu) because its full dose produced excessive toxin release and higher cost while its quarter dose was less effective; SeClear's optimal dose was judged to be the full/EPA-maximum dose (1.0 mg/L Cu) because its lower doses gave inadequate suppression and an earlier population rebound.
  - *evidence:* Per-product optimal-dose rationale given in the results/discussion. (Results/Discussion - Optimal Dose Determination)
  - *quote:* "full dose showed excessive toxin release and higher cost"
- **[✓ verified]** When compared at the same copper concentration (0.25 mg/L Cu), 2-day cyanobacteria suppression was highest for EarthTec (90%), then Cutrine Ultra (64%), then SeClear (~61%); however, over 7-14 days the population rebound was far larger for Cutrine Ultra (1,414%) and EarthTec (880%) than for SeClear (145%), and SeClear's rebound also began earlier (2-7 days) than the other two products (7-14 days).
  - *evidence:* Cross-product comparison at matched copper concentration, reported with ANOVA-type significance for time, copper presence, interaction, and product-type effects (all p<0.05). (Results - Comparative Analysis at 0.25 mg/L Cu)
- **[⚠ partial]** Higher/full doses produced much larger short-term (day-2) increases in extracellular microcystin than lower doses for Cutrine Ultra and EarthTec (e.g., EarthTec full dose rose 1,347% to 3.69 +/- 0.43 ug/L), consistent with toxin release via cell lysis, whereas SeClear's half and quarter doses showed no statistically significant microcystin increase.
  - *evidence:* Per-product extracellular microcystin results reported with percent change from baseline and significance. (Results - per-product Extracellular Microcystin subsections)
  - *reviewer:* The mechanism 'toxin release via cell lysis' is not explicitly established in the source text. The source provides inverse correlations and shows that higher doses produce higher extracellular microcystin, but explicitly states 'causality not definitively established.' The inference of cell lysis as the mechanism is reasonable but exceeds what the source text directly supports.
- **[✓ verified]** A strong negative Pearson correlation was found between cyanobacteria cell density and extracellular microcystin concentration for all three products (r=-0.869 Cutrine Ultra; r=-0.984 EarthTec; r=-0.73 SeClear); the authors treat this as a correlation only and explicitly do not claim it establishes causality.
  - *evidence:* Correlation coefficients reported per product in results; causal caution stated explicitly in the limitations discussion. (Results (correlation reporting); Limitations)
- **[✓ verified]** EPA limits the maximum copper active-ingredient concentration in algaecide applications to 1 mg/L because copper is toxic to non-target aquatic organisms, and the source states zooplankton are markedly more copper-sensitive than phytoplankton.
  - *evidence:* Regulatory/ecological background given to motivate why dose minimization matters, not just efficacy. (Introduction (regulatory context); Discussion (non-target impacts))
  - *quote:* "The EPA limits the maximum amount of copper (1 mg/L) that can be applied as the active ingredient as it is toxic to non-target aquatic organisms.; Zooplanktons are 10 times more sensitive to copper than phytoplankton."
- **[✓ verified]** The source explains that dissolved Cu2+ reacts with carbonate/bicarbonate in the water column and precipitates out within a few hours, and that chelation (e.g., Cutrine Ultra's copper-ethanolamine complex) is invoked to explain sustained bioavailability/selectivity relative to the unchelated copper sulfate products (EarthTec, SeClear).
  - *evidence:* Mechanistic discussion of copper speciation used to explain differing product performance. (Discussion - copper bioavailability/chelation chemistry)
  - *quote:* "The Cu2+ ion interacts with aqueous carbonate and bicarbonate ions to form a copper carbonate precipitant that drops out of the water column"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors state several explicit limitations: the batch/bench-scale reactors lack continuous nutrient input and immigration of competing algae seen in real reservoirs; cell 'death' was operationalized only as visible lysis rather than a rigorous viability assay; and SeClear's undisclosed proprietary phosphorus-binding ingredient prevented a full mechanistic explanation of its performance.
  - *evidence:* Authors' own stated caveats about method scope and generalizability. (Discussion/Limitations)
  - *quote:* "viability of cells was limited to identifying 'dead cells' as those that had lysed"
- **[✓ verified]** For an example 11-acre, 4-foot-deep application at each product's identified optimal 'quarter dose' comparison point, Cutrine Ultra was estimated to cost about $551 (17 gallons) versus about $1,463 (35.9 gallons) for SeClear, supporting the authors' cost/performance recommendation of Cutrine Ultra.
  - *evidence:* Worked cost example used to argue for practical product selection, combined with efficacy/toxin-release findings. (Results/Discussion - Cost Comparison)
- **[✓ verified]** The study was funded by an Ohio Department of Higher Education Harmful Algal Bloom Research Initiative grant, and the authors declare no conflict of interest, despite one tested product (SeClear) being manufactured by SePro Corporation.
  - *evidence:* Standard funding/COI disclosure at end of paper. (Funding/Acknowledgements)
  - *quote:* "This work was supported by the Harmful Algal Bloom Research Initiative (Grant number 60054634) from the Ohio Department of Higher Education.; The authors have no conflict of interest."

## Methods
Bench-scale (laboratory batch-reactor) dose-response experiment. Raw source water and its indigenous cyanobacteria community were collected ~1 ft below the surface of Lake Rockwell (a ~2-billion-gallon drinking-water reservoir on the Cuyahoga River in northeast Ohio, producing ~35 million gallons/day of drinking water; baseline cyanobacteria density ~40,000 cells/mL, average turbidity 3.22). Water (1.6 L per reactor, triplicate glass reactors per treatment) was dosed with one of three commercial copper-based algaecides at full/half/quarter concentrations (Cutrine Ultra - Arch Chemicals, copper-ethanolamine complex, 0.5/0.25/0.125 mg/L Cu; EarthTec - Earth Science Laboratories, chelated copper sulfate pentahydrate, 1.0/0.5/0.25 mg/L Cu; SeClear - SePro Corporation, copper sulfate pentahydrate plus a proprietary phosphorus-binding additive, 1.0/0.5/0.25 mg/L Cu), alongside an untreated control, incubated at room temperature (25 C) under ~14 h/day natural lighting. Cyanobacteria density (phycocyanin-based, cells/mL) and chlorophyll-a were measured with a YSI Sonde (Model 6600 V2); genus-level community composition was assessed by hand counts on a Palmer-Maloney counting chamber (400x); extracellular microcystin was measured by an Ohio EPA-approved ELISA method on 0.45 micron-filtered samples. Measurements were taken at baseline, 1.5 h, 2 d, 7 d, and 14 d post-dosing, with reported significance testing (time, copper presence, interaction, and product-type effects) at p<0.05. The source presents this design as adequate to compare short-term (2-day) suppression efficacy and dose-dependent toxin release across products and doses, but as explicitly bench-scale/batch (no continuous nutrient or algal-immigration inputs), it is stated not to replicate longer-term field/reservoir rebound and succession dynamics.

## Stated limitations
The authors state that: (1) batch/bench-scale reactor conditions do not replicate field dynamics because they lack continuous nutrient inputs and immigration of competing phytoplankton/cyanobacteria present in a real reservoir, which likely affects the rebound and species-succession patterns observed; (2) cyanobacteria population composition/density varied somewhat between the separate per-product trials, limiting strict cross-product comparability; (3) cell "viability" was operationalized only as visible lysis ("dead cells" = lysed cells), a limited proxy for which the authors recommend more rigorous future assessment; (4) SeClear's proprietary phosphorus-binding ingredient is undisclosed, preventing the authors from fully explaining its mechanism of action; (5) the strong negative correlations reported between cyanobacteria counts and extracellular microcystin were analyzed as correlations only, with causality explicitly not established by the authors.

## Tensions with other findings
Within the source itself: the results complicate a naive "more copper = better control" heuristic - a quarter dose of Cutrine Ultra (the lowest concentration tested for that product) was judged the single best option overall on a combined efficacy/toxin-release/rebound/cost basis, outperforming higher, EPA-maximum doses of the other two products on those combined criteria; and doses that killed cells faster also released more extracellular microcystin (via lysis) in the short term, creating a suppression-vs-acute-toxin-release trade-off that the source does not resolve beyond recommending site-specific judgment. The reported cyanobacteria-vs-microcystin correlations are strong but are explicitly flagged by the authors as not establishing causality, consistent with a general correlation-is-not-causation caution for any driver/effect claim drawn from this study. Contextually relevant for a SePRO-facing dossier: SeClear, one of the three tested products, is manufactured by SePro Corporation; the study's funding (Ohio Department of Higher Education grant) and disclosed no-conflict-of-interest statement are independent of any of the three manufacturers, but in this particular bench trial SeClear required the highest (EPA-maximum) dose to achieve comparable control and showed the fastest population rebound (2-7 days, versus 7-14 days for the other two products) - a less favorable relative result than for the competitor product Cutrine Ultra. This asymmetry does not indicate bias in the paper but should be flagged explicitly whenever this source is cited in SePRO-facing materials. Findings derive from a single reservoir's indigenous community under one set of bench conditions, so they are bounded and not shown to generalize to other lakes, other seasons, or in-lake (versus source-water-intake) treatment contexts.

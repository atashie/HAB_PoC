# Explainable deep learning identifies patterns and drivers of freshwater harmful algal blooms

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed 2025 paper (Environmental Science and Ecotechnology) that trains a site-level LSTM deep-learning model on three years (2021–2023) of daily in-situ water-quality monitoring plus ERA5 weather-reanalysis data across 102 Chinese lake/reservoir sites to predict daily algal density, then applies SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) to rank and explain the drivers behind bloom dynamics nationally and per-site, and separately tests a fine-tuned transfer-learning strategy for improving predictions at poorly-monitored ("data-scarce") sites.

- **[✓ verified]** The study built an explainable deep-learning (LSTM + SHAP) pipeline for daily algal-density modeling at 102 lake/reservoir monitoring sites in China over three years, fusing in-situ water-quality monitoring with ERA5 weather-reanalysis data.

## Data / numbers
- Study scope: 102 core modeling sites drawn from 161 monitored lakes/reservoirs across China, Jan 2021–Dec 2023 (3 years)
- Samples per site: range 154–960, mean 587 (daily, from four-hourly monitoring averaged to daily)
- LSTM testing-phase NSE: mean 0.48, median 0.52, maximum 0.95
- Baseline ML testing-phase mean NSE (next-best models): GBDT 0.39, AdaBoost 0.37, Random Forest 0.36
- Site performance tiers: excellent (NSE>0.7) = 29.4% of sites (mean NSE 0.80); fair (0.3≤NSE≤0.7) = 40.2% (mean NSE 0.51); poor (NSE<0.3) = 30.4% (mean NSE 0.14)
- Water temperature (WT) SHAP Global Importance (GI): 11.7% nationally; 6.3%–17.4% across 6 selected case-study lakes; 0.7%–31.9% at the 31 sites (of 102) where WT ranked the #1 driver
- Top-5 national variables (WT, Chl-a, turbidity, EC, CODMn) combined GI: 44.2% of algal-density dynamics
- Top meteorological variable (solar radiation, from ERA5) GI: 5.2% — lower than every one of the top-5 water-quality variables
- WT SHAP-value inflection point (temperature above which WT contribution turns positive/bloom-promoting): range 9.5–27.1 °C, mean 20.9 °C
- Transfer-learning (fine-tuned) NSE gain over locally-trained-only models: +0.20 (p<0.001)
- Fine-tuned TL improved accuracy at >75% of poorly-gauged sites; 60.8% of sites gained ΔNSE 0.0–0.5; 16.7% gained ΔNSE>0.5; 22.5% showed 'negative transfer' (worse after fine-tuning)
- Source→target domain fit (Pearson r) used to pick TL source site: range 0.27–0.95, mean 0.75
- Target-domain 'scarce' sample counts used to test TL: range 31–192 daily samples, mean 120
- HAB threshold used: algal density 15 × 10⁶ cells L⁻¹; sites whose 3-yr average exceeded it: 20.5%
- Data-cleaning floor: records with algal density < 10,000 cells L⁻¹ excluded as erroneous
- Air temperature excluded as a predictor due to collinearity with water temperature: Pearson r > 0.93
- LSTM hyperparameters: learning rate 0.001, 32 neurons, dropout 0.2, 100 epochs, sequence length = 16 days (current day + previous 15)
- Train/test split: first 75% of each site's chronological record = training (20% of that further held out for per-epoch validation); last 25% = testing
- Meteorological input (ERA5 reanalysis) spatial resolution: 0.25°
- Background context cited by the authors (not this study's own result): 27.3% of 205 Chinese lakes/reservoirs surveyed in 2023 were eutrophic; 63.1% of 2,058 inland water bodies surveyed worldwide are eutrophic

## Methods
Target variable: daily "algal density" (cells L⁻¹) at each of 102 core Chinese lake/reservoir sites (drawn from 161 monitored waterbodies), Jan 2021–Dec 2023, four-hourly in-situ monitoring from the Chinese National Environmental Monitoring Centre averaged to daily values (154–960 daily samples per site, mean 587). Inputs: 11 in-situ water-quality variables (water temperature WT, dissolved oxygen, pH, electrical conductivity EC, turbidity, permanganate index CODMn, total nitrogen TN, total phosphorus TP, N/P ratio, ammonia nitrogen NH3-N, chlorophyll-a) plus 5 meteorological variables from ERA5 reanalysis at 0.25° resolution (air pressure, evaporation, precipitation, solar radiation, wind speed); air temperature was excluded for collinearity with WT (r>0.93). Preprocessing: IQR-based outlier removal, erroneous-record filtering, min-max normalization. Model: a per-site LSTM (one LSTM layer + fully connected layer; sequence length 16 days; 32 neurons; dropout 0.2; learning rate 0.001 with warmup-cosine-annealing; 100 epochs; MSE loss; PyTorch), compared against 8 scikit-learn baselines (AdaBoost, ANN, Bayesian ridge regression, Decision tree, GBDT, KNN, Random forest, SVM). Split: chronological (not random) — first 75% of each site's record for training (with 20% of that held out per-epoch for validation), last 25% for testing; evaluation via Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE). Explainability: SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) computed per site, aggregated into a Global Importance (GI) = mean absolute SHAP value of a feature ÷ sum across all features (×100%); per-site linear regressions of SHAP value vs. driver value used to derive sensitivity slopes, compared across sites/latitudes/climate zones/seasons. A supplementary transfer-learning experiment freezes the pretrained LSTM layer from a source site (chosen by best Pearson-r fit to the target, r range 0.27–0.95, mean 0.75) and retrains only the fully-connected layer on a small (assumed 20%, actually 31–192 samples, mean 120) target-site sample, compared against (a) no-retraining direct transfer and (b) training a new LSTM from target-site data alone.

## Stated limitations
The authors themselves note: (1) HAB drivers are lake-specific and heterogeneous ("complex and unique"), making cross-region transfer inherently difficult; (2) the fine-tuned transfer-learning fix still produced "negative transfer" (worse predictions) at 22.5% of sites; (3) even where TL helped, target-site sample sizes were very small (31–192 daily records, mean 120), which the authors say prevented the model from fully capturing local dynamics; (4) they explicitly flag that strong temporal autocorrelation in the data can let a model score well (high NSE) "while failing to learn the essential relationships with environmental factors" — i.e., good fit does not guarantee correct driver attribution; (5) hydrological attributes (water-level dynamics, hydraulic residence time) were not included as inputs and are flagged as a direction for improving explanations; (6) the underlying data are not openly published ("available upon reasonable request"), limiting independent reproducibility; (7) relatedly, the authors call for expanding monitoring networks in "poorly gauged waterbodies" as a precondition for better modeling there.

## Tensions with other findings
Several points are worth flagging for a HAB literature review rather than taking at face value. (1) This model uses no satellite/remote-sensing input at all — it fuses in-situ water-quality monitoring with ERA5 weather reanalysis only — so while it is a strong precedent for "in-situ + weather" fusion and for SHAP-based driver explainability, it does not itself demonstrate value from a remote-sensing signal, which is the current project's specific fusion target. (2) Within this model's variable set, nutrient concentrations (TN, TP, N/P, NH3-N) did NOT make the top-5 national driver list — water temperature, chlorophyll-a, turbidity, electrical conductivity, and CODMn did — which sits in tension with the nutrient-loading emphasis common in classical HAB causal literature; the paper's own SHAP importance is a correlational, model-relative ranking bounded by whatever variables were fed in, not a controlled nutrient-manipulation experiment, so it should not be read as "nutrients are unimportant," only that day-to-day density fluctuations in this dataset were better explained by temperature/optical-physical variables than by nutrient concentrations. (3) Chlorophyll-a is used as an input predictor of "algal density" (the target); because chlorophyll-a is itself a standard bio-optical proxy for algal biomass, its high importance — especially as the dominant spring driver — could partly reflect proxy/near-circular correlation with the target rather than an independent causal driver; the source text does not discuss or rule out this concern. (4) The 22.5% "negative transfer" rate is a direct caution against assuming a single model trained on well-monitored lakes will generalize cleanly to under-monitored ones, relevant to any plan for one national/regional model.

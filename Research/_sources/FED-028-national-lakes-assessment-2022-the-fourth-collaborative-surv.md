# National Lakes Assessment 2022: The Fourth Collaborative Survey of Lakes in the United States (interactive webreport)

**What it is.** The National Lakes Assessment (NLA) 2022 webreport is EPA's public, interactive summary of the fourth in a series (2007, 2012, 2017, 2022) of nationally representative, probability-based (stratified random) field surveys of lake condition in the conterminous United States. It reports 18 in-situ trophic-state, biological, chemical, physical, and human-health indicators — including cyanotoxins (microcystins, cylindrospermopsin), chlorophyll a, nutrients, and fish-tissue contaminants — from 981 sampled lakes statistically extrapolated to a target population of 268,020 U.S. lakes.

## Methods
Probability-based (stratified random, state-stratified) survey design over the conterminous-U.S. lake population (lakes/ponds/reservoirs ≥1 hectare, ≥1 meter deep, ≥0.1 hectare open water, ≥1-week residence time; Great Lakes, Great Salt Lake, treatment/disposal ponds, tidal and ephemeral lakes excluded). 80 trained field crews sampled 981 lakes in spring/summer 2022 using standardized whole-day, multi-depth (mid-lake) and 10-point littoral-zone field protocols. 18 indicators across five categories — trophic state; biological (chlorophyll a, benthic macroinvertebrates, zooplankton); chemical (acidification, atrazine, dissolved oxygen, nutrients); physical (drawdown, lakeshore disturbance, riparian vegetation cover, shallow water habitat, habitat complexity); and human health (cyanotoxins — microcystins and cylindrospermopsin, enterococci bacteria, fish-tissue contaminants — mercury, PCBs, PFAS) — were each scored good/fair/poor against either fixed literature-derived benchmarks or regional benchmarks set at the 75th/95th percentiles of reference-lake distributions across nine ecoregions. A fish-tissue sub-study (two-thirds of sampled lakes targeted, 413 completed) is scaled only to a distinct 'sampled population' (58,747 lakes), explicitly not the full 268,020-lake target population. Stressor–response 'relative risk' and 'attributable risk' statistics were computed to relate stressor levels (e.g., phosphorus, habitat complexity) to biological condition, but the report states these require three explicit assumptions (a causal relationship exists; poor condition would reverse if the stressor improved; the stressor's effect is independent of other stressors) that it flags itself as limitations on interpretation. Where the source says it "works": produces nationally and regionally representative condition estimates with a quantified margin of error (~±5%, 95% confidence). Where it says it does not work / does not apply: inferring local, single-lake condition or management decisions; establishing trends from a single cycle-to-cycle change; treating condition benchmarks as legal/regulatory standards; or extrapolating fish-tissue results beyond the fish-tissue sampled population.

## Stated limitations
The report itself states: (1) NLA condition benchmarks "have no legal effect and are not equivalent to individual state water quality standards," and a poor NLA rating does not necessarily mean a site is "impaired" under the Clean Water Act; (2) the survey "does not measure all stressors and has limited usefulness for inferring local condition" — in-depth monitoring of individual lakes/watersheds is required for site-specific restoration or protection decisions; (3) fish-tissue contaminant results "cannot be extrapolated to the full NLA target population of 268,020 lakes," only to the smaller fish-tissue "sampled population" (58,747 lakes); (4) attributable-risk/relative-risk stressor associations depend on three explicit, unverified assumptions (causal relationship, reversibility, independence from other stressors) that the report flags as potential limitations, though it argues the statistics still "provide general guidance"; (5) "some types of algae and cyanobacteria release other toxins not monitored as part of the NLA" — only microcystins and cylindrospermopsin are tracked; (6) "data from future assessments will help determine whether changes observed between 2012 and 2022 represent a trend or reflect natural variability" — single-cycle changes are not confirmed trends; (7) "the 2012 and 2017 condition estimates reported here may differ from those in previous reports due to changes in benchmarks and statistical adjustments to population estimates made over time," complicating naive cross-report historical comparison; (8) 3% of lakes could not be assessed for benthic macroinvertebrates due to nearshore sampling difficulty; (9) dissolved oxygen is measured only at discrete depths, and EPA is researching a revised whole-water-column indicator; (10) the target population explicitly excludes the Great Lakes, Great Salt Lake, treatment/disposal ponds, and tidally influenced or ephemeral lakes.

## Tensions with other findings
This source is a national in-situ monitoring/status survey, not a remote-sensing product or a predictive/early-warning model — it complements rather than conflicts with satellite-based cyanoHAB detection efforts (e.g., EPA CyAN) by supplying nationally representative, probability-based ground-truth prevalence statistics for cyanotoxins and trophic state, but it reports point-in-time condition/prevalence rather than bloom timing, within-lake spatial extent, or forecasted risk, so it cannot by itself validate a remote-sensing early-warning or forecasting model at sub-lake or weekly time scales. It also explicitly warns against using its national/regional statistics to infer local, single-lake condition — which is in tension with any effort (such as a lake-specific HAB diagnostic tool) that would want to use NLA figures as a site-specific baseline without additional local monitoring data. Its stressor–biological-condition "attributable risk" statistics are associational, and the report itself cautions they rest on unverified causal assumptions — they should not be read as proof that reducing a given stressor (e.g., phosphorus) will causally reduce poor biological condition at any specific lake.

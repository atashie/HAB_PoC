# SeClear Algaecide and Water Quality Enhancer — Product Label

**What it is.** This is the EPA-registered pesticide product label (EPA Reg. No. 67690-55, EPA Est. No. 067690-NC-002; label date 2023-05-15) for SeClear, a copper sulfate pentahydrate-based algaecide and water quality enhancer for ponds, lakes, reservoirs, and irrigation/drainage conveyances, including potable water sources subject to separate treatment. It specifies precautionary statements, environmental hazards, and detailed application-rate directions for controlling algae (including cyanobacteria) and for reducing phosphorus and turbidity.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** SeClear's active ingredient is copper sulfate pentahydrate at 16.2% of the formulation, equivalent to 4.1% metallic copper, delivering 0.417 lbs of metallic copper per gallon of product.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the Active Ingredient declaration and footnote on the front label panel. (Front label, Active Ingredient statement)
  - *quote:* "Metallic Copper equivalent = 4.1%. This product contains 0.417 lbs. metallic copper per gallon."
- **[✓ verified]** The product is EPA-registered and labeled for use across a range of aquatic sites, explicitly including potable water sources (subject to restrictions elsewhere in the label).
  - *evidence:* Front-panel use-site listing and registration numbers. (Front label header and registration block)
  - *quote:* "For use in aquatic sites including: ponds, lakes, reservoirs, irrigation and drainage canals, laterals and ditches; including potable water sources. ... EPA Reg. No. 67690-55"
- **[✓ verified]** The product's stated mechanism is rapid disruption of algal cell processes, and it is labeled effective against a broad range of algae growth forms, explicitly including blue-green algae (cyanobacteria), not just green algae.
  - *evidence:* Manufacturer efficacy claim in the 'Controlling Algae' section; treated algae are said to show visible discoloration within about a day. (Product Information, 'Controlling Algae' section)
  - *quote:* "This product rapidly disrupts numerous algae cell processes and shuts down growth. ... green, yellow/green and blue-green (cyanobacteria) algae."
- **[✓ verified]** Beyond killing algae, the label claims the product improves water quality by offsetting eutrophication, lowering water-column phosphorus via binding/precipitation, and reducing turbidity by binding/precipitating suspended solids.
  - *evidence:* Listed as a 3-item mechanism of action for 'Improving Water Quality'; no before/after data or study is cited in this excerpt to quantify the effect size. (Product Information, 'Improving Water Quality' section)
  - *quote:* "Decreasing phosphorus concentrations from the water column through binding and precipitation of excess phosphorus."
- **[✓ verified]** The label states the product is toxic to fish and aquatic organisms, and that unlike organic pesticides, its copper active ingredient does not degrade and accumulates in the environment with repeated applications.
  - *evidence:* Stated in the Environmental Hazards / Fish Advisory Statement as a direct hazard disclosure. (Environmental Hazards, Fish Advisory Statement)
  - *quote:* "This copper product is toxic to fish and aquatic organisms. ... copper is an element and will not break down in the environment and will therefore accumulate with repeated applications."
- **[✓ verified]** Treating algae/weeds can deplete dissolved oxygen as biomass decomposes, risking fish and invertebrate suffocation; to mitigate this the label restricts treatment to no more than half a water body at a time with at least 14 days between treatments (with an exception process for public-health emergencies).
  - *evidence:* Stated as the rationale for the label's core retreatment-interval restriction. (Environmental Hazards section)
  - *quote:* "do not treat more than ½ of the water body and wait at least 14 days between treatments"
- **[✓ verified]** Applying the algaecide to high-density cyanobacteria blooms can rupture cells and release intracellular contents into the water, some of which are known mammalian hepatotoxins and neurotoxins; the label advises against treating toxin-producing cyanobacteria at high density for this reason.
  - *evidence:* Direct manufacturer hazard statement connecting treatment action to potential acute toxin release, distinct from the oxygen-depletion hazard. (Environmental Hazards section)
  - *quote:* "Application of algaecides to high density blooms of cyanobacteria can result in the release of intracellular contents into the water. Some of these intracellular compounds are known mammalian hepato- and nervous system toxins."
- **[✓ verified]** Low pH (≤6.5), low dissolved organic carbon (≤3.0 mg/L), and soft water (alkalinity <50 mg/L) are stated to increase acute toxicity of the product to non-target aquatic organisms (e.g., koi, trout); labeled rates assume pH >6.5, DOC >3.0 mg/L, and alkalinity >50 mg/L.
  - *evidence:* Water-chemistry-dependent toxicity thresholds given as explicit numeric cutoffs in the Environmental Hazards section. (Environmental Hazards section)
  - *quote:* "low pH (≤6.5), low dissolved organic carbon (DOC) levels (3.0 mg/L or lower), and “soft” waters (i.e., alkalinity less than 50 mg/L), increase the potential acute toxicity to non-target aquatic organisms"
- **[✓ verified]** The product does not itself make water potable; for water destined for drinking use, no more than 1.0 ppm metallic copper may be applied in a single treatment, and additional separate potable-water treatment is required.
  - *evidence:* Explicit use restriction in the Product Information section, distinguishing this from a water-purification treatment. (Product Information section)
  - *quote:* "Treatment with this product will not by itself make water potable. ... Do not apply more than 1.0 ppm of metallic copper in these waters during any single treatment."
- **[✓ verified]** Table 1 sets application rates by algae growth form: 0.15-1.0 ppm metallic copper (1.0-6.5 gal product/acre-foot) for planktonic and filamentous algae, and 0.4-1.0 ppm (2.5-6.5 gal/acre-foot) for macrophytic algae (Chara/Nitella/Starry Stonewort), with a footnote allowing up to 1.0 ppm for difficult species.
  - *evidence:* Direct reproduction of Table 1 rate ranges and its footnote; the table itself is described as based on static/minimal-flow conditions. (Table 1, Application Rates)
  - *quote:* "this product may be applied up to 1.0 ppm when growth conditions require higher rates and for difficult to control species (such as Hydrodictyon, Cladophora or Pithophora)"
- **[✓ verified]** Annual copper loading caps differ by treatment strategy: whole waterbodies are capped at 21.9 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (8 applications/year at up to 1 ppm, staggering half the waterbody every 14 days over an 8-month/244-day season), while discrete 'water management units' within a larger waterbody are capped higher, at 46.6 lbs/acre-foot/year (17 applications/year at up to 1 ppm) over the same 244-day season.
  - *evidence:* Two parallel annual-maximum calculations given in adjacent label sections, both keyed to a 14-day retreatment interval and a 244-day treatment season, but yielding different totals depending on whether the whole waterbody or a discrete management unit is the accounting basis. ('Whole Waterbodies' and 'Water Management Units' sections)
  - *quote:* "Maximum annual application rate of 21.9 lbs of metallic copper per acre-foot (8 applications per year at up to 1 ppm). ... Maximum annual application rate of 46.6 lbs. of metallic copper per acre-foot per year (17 applications per year at up to 1 ppm)."
- **[✓ verified]** In aquaculture ponds where fish are present, the single-application cap for targeting nuisance algae is lowered to 0.4 ppm metallic copper, versus the general 1.0 ppm single-application maximum.
  - *evidence:* Explicit lower concentration ceiling stated for a fish-present use case. (Application Directions section)
  - *quote:* "When treating aquaculture ponds when fish are present do not exceed a concentration of 0.4 ppm during any single application when targeting nuisance algae."
- **[✓ verified]** For flowing water (canals, ditches, irrigation/drainage systems, potable water conveyances), control requires maintaining a minimum exposure of 1-3 hours at 0.15-1.0 ppm; a separate 'Pulse Application' method for irrigation conveyances only caps annual load at 13 lbs metallic copper/year per 5 miles of conveyance per CFS, applied at up to 0.5 lbs copper (1.22 gal product) per CFS per 5-30 mile segment.

('Drip System or Metering Pump Application' and 'Pulse Application Method' sections)
  - *quote:* "maintain a minimum exposure period of one to three hours at a concentration of 0.15 to 1.0 ppm ... Maximum annual application rate of 13 lbs metallic copper per year per 5 miles of conveyance per cubic foot per second (CSF)."

## Data / numbers
- Active ingredient: copper sulfate pentahydrate = 16.2% of formulation; metallic copper equivalent = 4.1%
- Product contains 0.417 lbs metallic copper per gallon
- Front-panel notice (extraction-jumbled): 'Maximum Use Level ... of 24 mg/L ... for the product' — parameter it applies to is unclear from the excerpt
- Non-target toxicity risk thresholds: pH ≤6.5, DOC ≤3.0 mg/L, alkalinity <50 mg/L (label rates presume pH >6.5, DOC >3.0 mg/L, alkalinity >50 mg/L)
- Retreatment restriction: ≥14 days between treatments; ≤1/2 of water body treated at a time
- Drinking-water use cap: ≤1.0 ppm metallic copper per single treatment (product does not itself potabilize water)
- Aquaculture ponds with fish present: ≤0.4 ppm metallic copper per single application (vs. 1.0 ppm general single-application max)
- Table 1 rates: Planktonic (suspended) algae 0.15-1.0 ppm copper, 1.0-6.5 gal product/acre-foot; Filamentous (mat-forming) algae 0.15-1.0 ppm, 1.0-6.5 gal/acre-foot; Macrophytic algae (Chara/Nitella/Starry Stonewort) 0.4-1.0 ppm, 2.5-6.5 gal/acre-foot
- Single-application max: 1.0 ppm copper = 6.57 gal product or 2.74 lbs metallic copper per acre-foot
- Whole-waterbody annual max: 21.9 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (8 applications/year at up to 1 ppm), based on treating half the waterbody every 14 days across a 244-day (8-month) season
- Water-management-unit annual max: 46.6 lbs metallic copper/acre-foot/year (17 applications/year at up to 1 ppm) over the same 244-day season
- Flowing water: minimum exposure 1-3 hours at 0.15-1.0 ppm metallic copper
- CFS estimation formula: CFS = width (ft) x depth (ft) x velocity (ft/s) x 0.9; dosing formula: CFS x desired ppm x 2.1 = quarts/hour
- Table 2 (flowing water) example values: 1 CFS (450 gal/min) -> 0.3-2.1 quarts/hr (0.2-1.1 fl oz/min) at 0.15-1.0 ppm; 100 CFS (45,000 gal/min) -> 34.3-214.5 quarts/hr (18.3-114.3 fl oz/min)
- Pulse application (irrigation conveyances only): max 13 lbs metallic copper/year per 5 miles of conveyance per CFS; up to 0.5 lbs metallic copper (1.22 gal product) per CFS per 5-30 mile treatment segment
- Aerial application limits: release height ≤10 ft above canopy/water; medium-or-coarser droplet size (ASABE S572.1); no application above 15 mph wind; boom length ≤65% of wingspan (fixed-wing) when wind >10 mph
- 1 acre = 43,560 ft2; worked example: 1 acre x 4 ft depth = 4 acre-feet; 1.0 gal/acre-foot x 4 acre-feet = 4 gallons of product

## Methods
This is a regulatory/manufacturer product label (EPA pesticide label), not a research study — it contains no experimental methodology, study design, or cited data source for the efficacy, water-quality-improvement, or toxicity claims it makes. Application rates and hazard thresholds are presented as manufacturer/regulatory directions and precautionary statements (as required under pesticide labeling law) rather than as findings derived from a described method within this excerpt; the label explicitly recommends site-specific "initial dose determination test[s]" before treatment rather than relying solely on the table values, which suggests the tabulated rates are general guidance rather than site-validated results.

## Stated limitations
The label itself states: (1) the product will not by itself make water potable and drinking-water use requires additional separate treatment; (2) results "can vary depending upon species and density of algae and vegetation, desired distance of control and flow rate, and impact of water quality on efficacy"; (3) heavy infestations combined with low flow "may cause poor distribution resulting in unsatisfactory control"; (4) low-pH, low-DOC, and soft (low-alkalinity) waters increase acute toxicity risk to non-target organisms such as koi and trout, so the standard rates are not appropriate in those conditions; (5) treating aquatic weeds/algae can deplete dissolved oxygen from decomposing biomass, risking fish/invertebrate suffocation, which is why treatment area and frequency are restricted; (6) treating high-density cyanobacteria blooms can release intracellular toxins, so the label advises against treating toxin-producing blooms at high density; (7) copper does not degrade and accumulates with repeated applications; and (8) delaying treatment of flowing-water systems "could perpetuate the problem by causing massing and compacting of biomass."

## Tensions with other findings
The label's own hazard language complicates a simple "detect-then-treat" story: it warns that applying this algaecide to high-density cyanobacteria blooms can rupture cells and release intracellular hepato- and nervous-system toxins into the water, and separately restricts retreatment to no more than half a waterbody every 14 days specifically to avoid oxygen-depletion fish kills from decaying biomass. Read together, these manufacturer-stated cautions suggest that chemical treatment of an already-dense, toxin-producing bloom is itself a constrained, risk-bearing action rather than a clean fix — consistent with, though not itself proof of, an early-warning/timing argument (treating before a bloom reaches high density might avoid the toxin-release and oxygen-depletion scenarios the label flags). The label does not quantify bloom-density thresholds, toxin-release amounts, or any timing benefit, so this should be read as a manufacturer safety caution, not a controlled study of treatment-timing outcomes. Separately, the label's statement that copper "will not break down in the environment and will therefore accumulate with repeated applications" sits in tension with its own recommendation to run a "routine maintenance program" of proactive, repeated treatments — repeated preventive dosing accumulates a non-degrading metal in the same system, a trade-off the label discloses but does not quantify (e.g., no sediment copper-loading data are given in this excerpt).

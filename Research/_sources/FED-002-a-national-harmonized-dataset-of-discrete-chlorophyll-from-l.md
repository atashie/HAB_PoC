# A national harmonized dataset of discrete chlorophyll from lakes and streams (2005-2022) [USGS data release]; companion peer-reviewed paper: "Chlorophyll a in lakes and streams of the United States (2005-2022)" (Scientific Data, 2024). Note: the task's title field also names a second, distinct USGS product, "AquaMatch Chlorophyll a Data from Water Quality Portal: ~1970-2024" — see tensions.

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.usgs.gov/data/a-national-harmonized-dataset-discrete-chlorophyll-lakes-and-streams-2005-2022

**What it is.** A USGS data release (ScienceBase, DOI 10.5066/P9J0ZIOF) and its companion open-access Scientific Data paper that harmonize discrete, field-collected/lab-analyzed chlorophyll a and pheophytin measurements from roughly 84,000 U.S. monitoring sites (lakes, streams, rivers, reservoirs, canals, estuaries, 2005-2022), pooled from the multi-agency Water Quality Portal and previously unpublished USGS National Water Quality Laboratory records. It is explicitly built as harmonized in-situ ground truth to calibrate process-based, machine-learning, and remote-sensing HAB prediction models.

## Methods
Purely an observational data-harmonization pipeline (no predictive/statistical model is fit). Discrete lab chlorophyll a / pheophytin records were pulled from the Water Quality Portal via USGS's dataRetrieval R package (v2.7.12; 10 WQP CharacteristicNames covering plankton and periphyton, corrected/uncorrected chlorophyll a, and pheophytin) and combined with previously unpublished USGS NWQL records (which contributed only two pigment types: 'chlorophyll a, corrected for pheophytin' and 'pheophytin'). The R/RStudio pipeline (using the 'targets' package for a reproducible workflow) harmonized reporting units (phytoplankton to µg/L; periphyton to mg/m2, from a list of source units including mg/l, ppm, mg/m3, g/m2, mg/cm2, etc.), removed apparent in-situ sensor readings that had been mislabeled under a discrete-sample method code, removed exact-match duplicate records (matched on site_no/date/date_time/parameter/censored_cd, generally preferring WQP over NWQL because WQP retained depth information), dropped negative and non-integer pigment values and nonsensical analytical-method records, removed a known bad-conversion organization ('CSKTRIBE') and an out-of-country (Canada) site, filtered NWQL data to 14 acceptable 'MEDIUM' sample-type codes and excluded replicate samples, and excluded any sample reporting depth >10 m. The paper states this pipeline works well to assemble one large, cross-agency, unit-consistent table spanning ~84,000 sites, but explicitly states it fails to reliably distinguish, from WQP metadata alone, whether a 'corrected chlorophyll a' record was produced by the standard EPA 445 acidification method or by a different fluorometric ('modified') method reported under the same characteristic name/parameter code — an unresolved ambiguity the authors flag as needing future work, recommending in the meantime that users prefer uncorrected chlorophyll a.

## Stated limitations
The source states several limitations directly: (1) extreme high values are deliberately retained rather than screened out, so users must judge outliers themselves and are told to confirm them with the original data provider; (2) ~40% of NWIS-derived records were still 'preliminary' (unreviewed by a USGS data steward) at compilation and were kept anyway; (3) 'corrected chlorophyll a' is methodologically ambiguous in WQP data — it cannot be determined whether a given record used the acidification-based EPA 445 procedure or a different fluorometric procedure, and the authors explicitly recommend against relying on corrected chlorophyll a for this reason, preferring uncorrected chlorophyll a or fluorometry-based values; (4) sampling frequency, spatial distribution, and analytical methods vary greatly by state, so the dataset is an opportunistic aggregation of many independent monitoring programs rather than a statistically designed national survey; (5) co-located sites/site numbers were not aggregated or reconciled across organizations or time, so raw site counts likely overstate true independent spatial density; (6) explicit 'algal bloom' event tagging (hydro_event_cd) exists for only 437 of the ~1.37 million records, so the dataset cannot itself be treated as a labeled bloom/no-bloom occurrence dataset; (7) phytoplankton (volume-basis, µg/L) and periphyton (area-basis, mg/m2) measurements are not directly comparable to one another; (8) for periphyton specifically, a high benthic chlorophyll concentration does not necessarily reflect a high growth rate, since accumulation, senescence, and scour by high flow all affect standing biomass independently of growth rate.

## Tensions with other findings
Two internal inconsistencies were noted between the source texts themselves rather than invented: the USGS/ScienceBase landing-page abstract calls the 2005-2022 span a '17-year record' while the companion peer-reviewed paper's abstract calls the identical span an '18-year record'; and the six per-pigment-type record counts given in the companion paper sum to about 1,376,213, only approximately matching its own rounded headline of 'over 1,374,000' measurements. Substantively, this source complicates any downstream HAB analysis that treats 'chlorophyll a from WQP' as a single clean, comparable variable: the paper itself warns that 'corrected chlorophyll a' values mix two non-equivalent lab procedures under one code, and that only a small fraction of records (437 of ~1.37M) carry explicit bloom-event labels — so any other source in this review that defines blooms purely by a chlorophyll concentration threshold, or that uses WQP 'corrected chlorophyll a' without this caveat, should be read with that ambiguity in mind. The dataset also does not itself assert any causal driver-chlorophyll relationship; it only proposes that its harmonized records could be used by others to 'examine' relationships to nutrients and other drivers, which this summary treats as a suggested use case, not a causal finding. Finally, the task's own title field names a second, distinct USGS product ('AquaMatch Chlorophyll a Data from Water Quality Portal ~1970-2024', different DOI, different lead contact, different date range, part of an AquaSat V2 lineage) alongside this entry's actual primary source; the two are related in subject matter (both harmonize WQP chlorophyll) but are separate deliverables from different teams and should not be cited interchangeably.

# Effects of Hydrogen Peroxide and Ultrasound on Biomass Reduction and Toxin Release in the Cyanobacterium, Microcystis aeruginosa

**What it is.** A controlled laboratory study testing two candidate curative treatments for cyanobacterial (Microcystis aeruginosa PCC 7820) blooms — hydrogen peroxide dosing (0–8 mg H2O2 L-1) and commercial low-power ultrasound transducers (~20–44 kHz, 0.63–0.68 W) — measuring effects on biomass (chlorophyll-a, photosystem II efficiency, particle/cell counts) and, critically, on whether the treatment causes microcystin (MC) toxins to move from intact cells (particulate) into the surrounding water (dissolved), using LC-MS/MS quantification of multiple MC variants.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Hydrogen peroxide at 4 and 8 mg H2O2 L-1 produced a clear, statistically defined dose-response killing effect on M. aeruginosa (chlorophyll-a, photosystem II efficiency, and particle/cell counts all dropped), with EC50 values of 2.5 mg/L (ΦPSII), 3.8 mg/L (chlorophyll-a), and 2.6 mg/L (particle concentration); 1 and 2 mg/L produced little to no effect.
  - *evidence:* Derived from repeated-measures ANOVA of chlorophyll-a, ΦPSII, and CASY particle counts across the 0/1/2/4/8 mg/L dose series sampled at 0, 1, 4, 24 h, summarized as dose-response curves and EC50s in Table 1/Figure 2. (Results — Hydrogen peroxide experiment; Table 1)
  - *quote:* "EC50 (mg H2O2 L−1) ... 3.8 [chlorophyll-a], 2.5 [ΦPSII], 2.6 [particles]"
- **[✓ verified]** Even at the highest tested dose (8 mg H2O2 L-1), only 23–24% of total microcystin was removed after 24 hours — more than three-quarters of the toxin pool remained measurable in the jars.
  - *evidence:* One-way ANOVA comparing total (particulate + dissolved) MC concentration in H2O2-treated jars vs. controls after 24 h, and vs. each treatment's own initial value. (Results / Discussion — Hydrogen peroxide experiment, microcystin concentrations)
  - *quote:* "after 24 h, the total MC concentrations were lower in the H2O2‑treated jars than in the controls (F3,11 = 13.5; p = 0.002). ... The total MC concentrations in the highest H2O2 treatments had dropped 23 to 24% compared to their initial values ... more than 75% of the MCs remained present, even when a H2O2 dose of 8 mg L−1 was applied."
- **[✓ verified]** H2O2 treatment did not mainly remove toxin by degrading it — it caused a large, statistically significant shift of microcystin from the intracellular/particulate pool into the dissolved (extracellular, more readily bioavailable) pool, consistent with cell lysis rather than detoxification.
  - *evidence:* One-way ANOVA on dissolved MC concentration across H2O2 doses; the authors link this to lysis because particle counts collapsed to a small fraction of starting numbers while chlorophyll-a (a pigment/biomass proxy) stayed comparatively high, implying cell rupture rather than clean removal. (Results & Discussion — Hydrogen peroxide experiment)
  - *quote:* "H2O2 application significantly increased the dissolved MC concentrations (one-way ANOVA; F3,11 = 408.9; p < 0.001)"
- **[✓ verified]** Commercial low-power ultrasound transducers (Flexidal AL-05 and AL-10, 0.63–0.68 W acoustic power) produced only minimal inhibition of M. aeruginosa growth over 5 days and did not clear the water of cyanobacteria; total microcystin did not significantly decline in ultrasound-treated jars either.
  - *evidence:* Comparison of chlorophyll-a, ΦPSII, particle-count trajectories, and total MC concentration over 5 days between ultrasound-treated and untreated control jars (repeated-measures ANOVA / t-test). (Results — Ultrasound experiment; stated conclusions)
  - *quote:* "no water clearing effect ... minimal growth inhibition ... The ultrasound treatment caused no decline in total MCs as total concentrations at the start (1023 SD 11 μg MC L−1) and after five days (1079 SD 34 μg MC L−1) were similar (t = 2.68; p = 0.055)."
- **[✓ verified]** Despite failing to control biomass, ultrasound treatment still significantly increased the dissolved (released) fraction of microcystin relative to untreated controls after 5 days (19% vs. 5% of total MC).
  - *evidence:* t-test comparing the percentage of total MC present in dissolved form between ultrasound and control jars at day 5, while controls independently showed a large increase in total MC (algal growth) over the same period. (Results — Ultrasound experiment)
  - *quote:* "The proportion of dissolved MCs after five days was significantly higher in the ultrasound treatments (19%) than in the controls (5%, t = 6.17; p = 0.004). ... Total MCs significantly increased in the controls, from 1054 (SD 71) μg MC L−1 at the start to 1558 (SD 110) μg MC L−1 at the end of the experiment (t‑test; t = 6.67; p = 0.003)"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors attribute ultrasound's failure to degrade microcystin to the acoustic intensity of commercial transducers being roughly four orders of magnitude below the intensity reported elsewhere as needed for cavitation-driven MC degradation, and note that scaling to larger real water volumes would deliver even less power per unit volume.
  - *evidence:* Direct numeric comparison to a cited reference intensity value (Song et al.) known to degrade MC via cavitation/hydroxyl-radical chemistry, plus a general scaling argument about power delivered per volume in larger water bodies. (Discussion — Ultrasound experiment)
  - *quote:* "Song et al. [22] used 22.7 W mL−1 to degrade MCs, while the intensity used in our experiment was 7.9 × 10−4 W mL−1"
- **[✓ verified]** H2O2 doses sufficient to degrade released microcystin down toward the WHO drinking-water guideline (1 µg/L) would likely be toxic to non-target zooplankton grazers, because published lethal/sub-lethal thresholds for two cladocerans overlap the algaecidal dose range used in this study.
  - *evidence:* Comparison of this study's effective/lethal H2O2 doses for M. aeruginosa (EC50s ~2.5–3.8 mg/L) against literature LC50 and mortality-onset values for Daphnia and Moina, used to argue that pushing doses higher to finish off the toxin would harm grazers. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "LC50 values of 5–6 mg H2O2 L−1 for Daphnia and 2 mg L−1 for Moina ... Increased mortality for Daphnia was observed at H2O2 concentrations exceeding 3 mg L−1; for Moina, this occurred already at concentrations exceeding 1 mg H2O2 L−1"
- **[✓ verified]** In untreated/healthy cells, the more hydrophobic microcystin variants (MC-LW, MC-LF) were under-represented in the dissolved fraction relative to the particulate fraction (attributed to adsorption behavior rather than passive leakage), but this pattern shifted once cells were lysed by H2O2 — meaning the toxin variant profile released during a treatment-induced crash is not simply a diluted copy of the intact-cell profile.
  - *evidence:* Comparison of MC-variant percentage composition between the particulate and dissolved fractions in untreated controls, versus the equivalent comparison after H2O2 treatment; both independent fetch extractions converged on similar particulate-fraction percentages (MC-LR dominant). (Results / Discussion — Microcystin variant composition)
  - *quote:* "adsorption of the more hydrophilic variants on living cells"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors' practical recommendation is that algicide (H2O2) use be minimized where possible, and that coagulant-plus-ballast treatments are preferable because they can remove intact, still-living cyanobacterial cells from the water column without lysing them and releasing toxins and nutrients.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the paper's management-oriented conclusion, framed as an alternative to both tested methods. (Discussion / Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "coagulants in combination with a ballast can effectively precipitate positively buoyant cyanobacteria out of the water column as intact cells ... without releasing cell contents and, thereby, nutrients and toxins"

- Initial chlorophyll-a: 702 µg L-1 (SD 23) in the H2O2 experiment; 649 µg L-1 (SD 24) in the ultrasound experiment
- EC50 for H2O2 (Table 1): 2.5 mg L-1 (ΦPSII), 3.8 mg L-1 (chlorophyll-a), 2.6 mg L-1 (particle concentration)

## Methods
Two separate lab experiments on axenic-ish monoculture M. aeruginosa PCC 7820 (Pasteur Culture Collection), grown in modified WC medium, experiments run at 23°C, 100 µmol quanta m-2 s-1, 12:12 h light:dark. (1) H2O2 dose-response: triplicate 1-L jars (800 mL suspension) at 0/1/2/4/8 mg H2O2 L-1, sampled at 0, 1, 4, 24 h. (2) Ultrasound: two commercial transducer models (Flexidal AL-05 and AL-10) run continuously for 5 days at 2 cm depth vs. untreated controls (n=3 jars/group), sampled daily for physiology and on days 0/1/3/5 for MC. Physiology measured via PHYTO-PAM (chlorophyll-a, ΦPSII) and CASY electronic particle counter. MC quantified by LC-MS/MS (Agilent 1200 LC + 6410A QQQ, Eclipse XDB-C18 column, MRM mode) on samples split by GF/C filtration into a particulate (filter, intracellular) fraction and a dissolved (filtrate, extracellular) fraction, covering 8 MC variants plus nodularin. Statistics: repeated-measures ANOVA (SPSS 19) with Tukey post hoc for physiological time series (with normality/sphericity diagnostics and Greenhouse-Geisser/Huynh-Feldt corrections as needed); one-way ANOVA/t-tests and two-way ANOVA (SigmaPlot 12.3, Holm-Sidak post hoc) for total MC and MC-variant composition; alpha=0.05. Per the source, the method "works" for biomass kill: H2O2 produced a clear, statistically robust dose-response killing curve (EC50s in the 2.5–3.8 mg/L range) within 24 h. It "fails" for safe toxin control: even the highest dose tested did not degrade the majority of the released toxin, and commercial-power ultrasound essentially failed to reduce biomass at all while still causing some toxin release.

## Stated limitations
The source itself notes/implies several caveats: (1) The lab strain (PCC 7820) grew as single/few cells rather than the large mucilaginous colonies typical of field Microcystis populations; the discussion attributes the study's higher-than-literature EC50 (2.5-3.8 mg/L vs. 0.3-0.5 mg/L reported elsewhere for other M. aeruginosa strains) partly to strain/morphology differences, implying colonial field populations could need different (possibly higher) doses. (2) No H2O2 dose tested achieved a safe path to the WHO microcystin guideline: getting released toxin down to 1 µg/L would require doses that the same paper shows are toxic to zooplankton grazers (Daphnia, Moina), so the authors frame this as a fundamental trade-off rather than a solvable dosing problem. (3) Ultrasound results come from small (800 mL) jars; the authors explicitly argue that scaling to real, larger water bodies would deliver even less acoustic power per unit volume, i.e., real-world performance would likely be no better and plausibly worse than the already-poor lab result. (4) Only one cyanobacterial strain/species (M. aeruginosa PCC 7820) was tested, so generalization to other bloom-forming taxa (e.g., Planktothrix, Dolichospermum/Anabaena) is untested. (5) Experiments were short (24 h for H2O2; 5 days for ultrasound), so longer-term toxin fate (further degradation, photolysis, microbial breakdown) beyond the observation window is not characterized.

## Tensions with other findings
This source directly complicates any simple "biomass reduction = successful bloom control" narrative that a satellite/remote-sensing-driven HAB tool might implicitly encode. It shows that a treatment (H2O2) that produces a clear, detectable drop in chlorophyll-a/particle counts (i.e., the kind of signal a CyAN-style optical/chlorophyll proxy would register as "bloom cleared") can coincide with a spike in dissolved, more bioavailable toxin rather than a resolved risk — the opposite of what a biomass-only signal would suggest. It also empirically contradicts marketing claims for commercial ultrasound algae-control devices (a treatment sometimes presented as a benign, chemical-free option), finding "no water clearing effect" and, per the fetch extraction, harm to a zooplankton grazer (Daphnia) in related work cited by the authors — relevant if a treatment-and-management module would otherwise present ultrasound as a low-risk option. For a tool built on remote-sensing biomass/pigment signals fused with in-situ data, this paper is a caution that the same signal (chlorophyll/biomass decline) can mean either "bloom resolved" or "bloom just lysed and released toxin," and that timing of any in-situ toxin sampling relative to a treatment event matters a great deal — a correlation between biomass drop and "safe" conditions cannot be assumed without direct toxin measurement.

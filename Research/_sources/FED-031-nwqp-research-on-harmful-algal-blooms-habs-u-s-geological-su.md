# NWQP Research on Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) | U.S. Geological Survey

**What it is.** A USGS National Water Quality Program (NWQP) science-topic portal page summarizing the agency's ongoing research portfolio on harmful algal blooms (HABs), especially cyanobacterial HABs (cyanoHABs) — covering health/economic impacts, monitoring networks, laboratory and molecular methods, a predictive model case study, and links to related labs, science centers, and publications (roughly 2008–2021), with the page itself last marked "Active" on March 3, 2019.

## Methods
The page describes a portfolio of methods rather than one study design: (1) field protocols and field guides for identifying cyanobacteria and cyanoHABs, including a guide developed for Native American and Alaska Native communities (Rosen & St. Amand, 2015); (2) laboratory sample-preparation techniques and toxin-quantification assays for cyanotoxins (anatoxin-a, microcystin, saxitoxin), run out of dedicated USGS facilities (Algal and Environmental Toxins Research Laboratory, Lawrence, KS; Ohio Water Microbiology Laboratory; Organic Geochemistry Research Laboratory); (3) molecular/genetic tools — DNA-based and RNA-based quantitative methods (qPCR and qRT-PCR) for cyanobacteria and microcystin quantification (Stelzer et al., 2013 compares DNA- vs RNA-based methods; Loftin et al., 2008 compares cell-lysis procedures); (4) statistical modeling — a multiple linear regression approach (built with Virtual Beach software v3.0.6) relating environmental variables to cyanotoxin co-occurrence/mixture concentration at Kabetogama Lake, described as successfully predicting when guideline thresholds will be exceeded using readily available real-time measurements (Christensen et al., 2021); (5) USGS nutrient concentration/load models for streams, used to trace nutrient source areas contributing to downstream waters, estuaries, and the Great Lakes; (6) a real-time monitoring network using optical nitrate sensors at 140+ sites feeding the nrtwq.usgs.gov tracking website. The page states these methods work well enough to have produced a successful predictive model for one lake (Kabetogama) and to have documented cyanotoxin occurrence in large rivers (11 sites, 2017) and in a large share of small southeastern streams, but it also states the field cannot yet deliver hours-to-days advance warning broadly, and implies (with lower-confidence wording, see key_claims) that mixture-level toxin prediction models were not established before the cited 2021 study.

## Stated limitations
The source itself states: (1) "Many unanswered questions remain about occurrence, environmental triggers for toxicity, and the ability to predict the timing, duration, and toxicity of HABs"; (2) "Despite advances in scientific understanding of cyanobacteria and associated compounds, many questions remain unanswered," including on gene-based mechanisms of toxin production and on the connection between algal toxins and health effects; (3) the field does not yet reliably deliver "advanced warnings at time scales relevant to cyanoHAB management (hours to days)"; (4) HAB occurrence is attributed to "a complex set of physical, chemical, biological, hydrological, and meteorological conditions," implicitly cautioning against single-factor explanations. Beyond these explicit statements, note (as a reviewer observation, not a source-stated limitation) that the page is a rolling program portal aggregating findings from specific, differently-scoped studies (e.g., 11 river sites in 2017; one lake's 2016–2017 data; one regional 39%-of-streams figure from 2016) rather than a single controlled national study, so its headline statistics should not be read as current, nationally representative measurements without checking the underlying dated sub-studies.

# Effectiveness of Best Management Practices to Reduce Phosphorus Loading to a Highly Eutrophic Lake

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6210285/

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed field-monitoring study (Steinman, Hassett & Oudsema, 2018, Int. J. Environ. Res. Public Health 15(10):2111) that tests whether two wetland-restoration Best Management Practices (BMPs), built in 2015 under Michigan's 'Project Clarity' watershed-remediation initiative, measurably reduced phosphorus, nitrogen, and sediment loading into hypereutrophic Lake Macatawa, by comparing upstream-vs-downstream and pre-vs-post-construction water quality during baseflow and storm events (2014-2017).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[⚠ partial]** Two years after construction of two wetland-restoration BMPs, there was no statistically significant difference in downstream vs. upstream water quality (turbidity, SRP, TP, nitrate, ammonia) at either site, during either baseflow or storm-flow conditions.
  - *evidence:* Based on paired upstream/downstream field sampling in 2017 at baseflow and during three monitored storm events, analyzed with ANOVA/Kruskal-Wallis and post-hoc tests; the only statistically significant contrast found was between two upstream sites (Mac Up vs. Peters Creek, TP, p=0.031), not an upstream-downstream (i.e., BMP treatment) effect. (Abstract; Results (Middle Macatawa and Haworth sections))
  - *quote:* "we found no significant difference in upstream vs. downstream water quality two years following BMP construction"
  - *reviewer:* Nitrate and ammonia not explicitly part of upstream/downstream measurement design; only turbidity, TP, SRP stated as measured upstream/downstream per methods.
- **[✓ verified]** Lake Macatawa itself remained highly impaired after BMP construction, with surface total phosphorus (TP) far above the regulatory target and chlorophyll-a frequently exceeding the hypereutrophic threshold.
  - *evidence:* Based on lake-wide sampling (surface, mid, bottom depths) at 5 monitoring stations across spring/summer/fall 2017. (Abstract; Results, Lake Macatawa section)
  - *quote:* "the lake remained highly impaired"
- **[✓ verified]** The Lake Macatawa phosphorus TMDL requires a 72% reduction in phosphorus loads from the watershed to meet the 50 µg/L interim total-phosphorus target.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a pre-existing regulatory/TMDL finding cited from the earlier TMDL document, not a result the authors themselves re-derived. (Introduction)
  - *quote:* "The TMDL estimated that a 72% reduction in phosphorus loads from the watershed would be required to meet the TP concentration target."
- **[✓ verified]** Turbidity and phosphorus concentrations rose sharply (roughly an order of magnitude or more) during storm events compared to baseflow at both wetland sites, confirming storm-driven pollutant mobilization in this watershed.
  - *evidence:* Directly measured via paired baseflow vs. storm-event sampling campaigns in 2017 at both Middle Macatawa and Haworth sites. (Results; Conclusions, point (1))
  - *quote:* "Phosphorus and sediment concentrations increased dramatically in the watershed following storm events, suggesting BMPs may play a critical role in improving water quality in this watershed"
- **[✓ verified]** Comparing pre-restoration (2014-2015) to post-restoration (2015-2017) periods, nitrate increased significantly at all three Middle Macatawa sites while ammonia decreased significantly at two sites — a mixed nutrient response not uniformly consistent with a phosphorus-retention benefit from the wetlands.
  - *evidence:* Two-tailed unpaired t-tests / Mann-Whitney tests comparing equal-month-matched pre- and post-restoration baseflow samples (n=16 each). (Results, Table 6 discussion (Middle Macatawa pre- vs. post-restoration))
  - *quote:* "NO₃⁻: p = 0.028 at Mac Up (post > pre); p = 0.001 at Peters Creek (post > pre); p = 0.009 at Mac Down (post > pre)"
- **[✓ verified]** The combined footprint of the two evaluated wetland BMPs (0.45 km²) is minute relative to the full watershed (464 km², ~0.097% of its area), which the authors offer as one likely reason the BMPs produced no detectable downstream effect.
  - *evidence:* Author-stated post-hoc explanation for the null result, presented as one of four non-exclusive possibilities rather than a proven cause. (Discussion / Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "the two sites have a combined area of 0.45 km² compared to the watershed area of 464 km²"
- **[✓ verified]** 2017 was an anomalously dry year (43% below the long-term precipitation average), which the authors cite as reducing the wetlands' opportunities to intercept and retain runoff and thus another likely reason no BMP effect was detected.
  - *evidence:* Compared to long-term precipitation normals from NCDC data for the local airport station; offered as an explanatory hypothesis, not independently proven to be the cause of the null result. (Discussion / Conclusions)
  - *quote:* "2017 was a dry year (43% lower than long-term average), thereby resulting in fewer opportunities for the wetlands to serve as filtering and retention basins to remove transport of pollutants"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors conclude that two years is likely too short a time to expect a measurable BMP effect, since wetland ecological maturation and downstream lake recovery both typically take much longer.
  - *evidence:* Interpretive conclusion offered by the authors as one of several non-exclusive, unproven explanations for the observed null result, not itself directly measured. (Conclusions (numbered point 3a); Discussion)
  - *quote:* "two years is an insufficient amount of time to detect meaningful ecological changes"
- **[✓ verified]** Despite occasional spikes in bottom-water SRP and TP in the lake, there was no evidence of systemic internal (sediment-derived) phosphorus loading in Lake Macatawa.
  - *evidence:* Based on depth-stratified (surface/mid/bottom) sampling at 5 lake stations across three seasons in 2017. (Results, Lake Macatawa section)
  - *quote:* "Despite occasional spikes in bottom water SRP and TP, overall there was no evidence of systemic internal P loading in Lake Macatawa"
- **[✓ verified]** The study sits within 'Project Clarity,' a large, multi-stakeholder, publicly and privately funded watershed-remediation program launched in 2013 combining wetland restoration, in-stream remediation, additional BMPs, and community education.
  - *evidence:* Contextual/programmatic description establishing the funding and institutional setting of the two BMPs evaluated; not itself a monitoring result. (Introduction)
  - *quote:* "A large-scale, multidisciplinary, collaborative watershed remediation project aimed at improving water quality in Lake Macatawa was initiated in 2013 and called 'Project Clarity'"

## Data / numbers
- Lake Macatawa surface area: 7.2 km²; mean depth 3.6 m; max depth 12 m (western basin)
- Watershed area: 464 km²; land use 46% agricultural, 33% urban; 86% of natural wetlands lost
- Historical/background TP: 'usually exceeding 100 µg/L'; recent monthly average TP >125 µg/L, at times >200 µg/L
- TMDL interim TP target for Lake Macatawa: 50 µg/L
- TMDL-estimated required phosphorus load reduction to meet TP target: 72%
- Project Clarity initiated: 2013
- Middle Macatawa wetland restoration: ~16 ha (floodplain reconnection + detention basin), completed late September 2015
- Haworth wetland restoration: 17 ha (4-basin constructed wetland), completed early October 2015
- Combined BMP footprint: 0.45 km² vs. watershed area 464 km² (~0.097% of watershed)
- Intensive monitoring window: December 2016 through November 2017
- Pre-restoration comparison period: April 2014 – September 2015; post-restoration comparison period: October 2015 – November 2017; n=16 month-matched baseflow samples per period
- 2017 storm events: 30 Mar 2017 – 6.17 cm rain over 20 h (0.31 cm/h); 20 Apr 2017 – 8.15 cm over 21 h (0.39 cm/h); 15 Oct 2017 – 16.38 cm over 51 h (0.32 cm/h)
- Storm-event trigger threshold: USGS gage flow ≥ 300 ft³/s (~8.5 m³/s)
- 2017 annual precipitation: 43% lower than the long-term average (a 'dry year')
- Middle Macatawa baseflow (2017): SRP 29–43 µg/L; TP 88–122 µg/L; NO₃⁻ 2.90–10.64 mg/L; NH₃ 0.17–0.32 mg/L; TKN 1.06–1.41 mg/L; turbidity 9–15 NTU; DO 9.42–10.32 mg/L; specific conductivity 637–704 µS/cm

- Haworth baseflow (2017): SRP 15–16 µg/L; TP 49–50 µg/L; NO₃⁻ ~1.92–1.94 mg/L; NH₃ 0.07–0.18 mg/L; TKN 0.84–0.89 mg/L; turbidity 4.9–6.8 NTU
- Haworth storm flow (2017): turbidity 204.8–390.8 NTU; SRP 97–100 µg/L (~10x baseflow); TP 517–544 µg/L (~10x baseflow); TKN 2.40–2.42 mg/L (~3x baseflow); NO₃⁻ 1.36–1.90 mg/L; NH₃ 0.14–0.22 mg/L

- Lake Secchi disk depth: consistently <1 m throughout the year

## Methods
Field monitoring study (not a model-based load simulation). Water quality measured with a YSI 6600 sonde (DO, temperature, pH, specific conductivity, TDS, ORP) and a YSI 6136 optical turbidity sensor (also continuous in-situ YSI 600OMS V2 turbidity loggers deployed March-December 2017, recording every 30 min). Nutrients (SRP, TP, NO3-, NH3, TKN) analyzed on a SEAL AQ2 discrete automated analyzer; values below detection set to half the detection limit. Sampling was paired upstream/downstream at two BMP sites (Middle Macatawa: 2 upstream incl. Peters Creek, 1 downstream at a USGS gage; Haworth: 1 upstream, 1 downstream) during baseflow and storm events (storm trigger = USGS flow ≥300 ft³/s), plus 5-station whole-lake sampling (surface/mid/bottom, Secchi depth, chlorophyll-a via GFF filtration and spectrophotometry) in spring/summer/fall 2017. Comparisons used two-tailed paired/unpaired t-tests or Wilcoxon/Mann-Whitney tests (per normality via Shapiro-Wilk and Brown-Forsythe), one-way ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis with Tukey post-hoc, in SigmaPlot 13.0, at alpha=0.05 (marginal at 0.10). Pre- vs. post-restoration analysis matched 16 same-calendar-month baseflow samples from April 2014-September 2015 (pre) vs. October 2015-November 2017 (post). The paper notes a SWAT watershed model for Macatawa was still 'in the process of being developed' (cited as a companion/future work, not used for this paper's results). The method works well as a direct empirical before/after and paired-site field test of BMP performance; the authors acknowledge it may fail to detect real but small or slow-developing signals given high natural variability and the short post-construction record.

## Stated limitations
The authors attribute the null result to several explicitly stated, non-exclusive limitations: (1) only ~2 years elapsed between BMP construction (fall 2015) and the end of monitoring (Nov 2017) - 'an insufficient amount of time to detect meaningful ecological changes,' and construction-related soil disturbance may have transiently elevated nutrient export; (2) the two wetlands' combined footprint (0.45 km²) is very small relative to the 464 km² watershed, so their pollutant-removal capacity may be overwhelmed by watershed-wide loads, and 'the ability to detect a signal from the noise may be very difficult at any one particular site'; (3) 2017 was a dry year (43% below the long-term precipitation average), limiting opportunities for the wetlands to intercept and retain storm runoff; (4) high natural variability in the data means 'it will take a number of years to detect a robust trend at any site, regardless of direction'; (5) lake-level ecological recovery can take 'years, if not decades' even after stressors are reduced, and stressors may persist at reduced but still-impairing levels; (6) groundwater influence on the lake's ecology 'has not been addressed'; (7) the TMDL's 50 µg/L target was set in 1999/2000 before climate-change-driven storm intensification was well recognized, so it 'may be even harder to obtain than previously realized'; (8) BMP siting/location may not be optimal for pollutant reduction, though this was not directly tested; (9) tile drain effluent is flagged as a likely additional, under-studied phosphorus (and possibly nitrogen) source not addressed by the evaluated BMPs.

## Tensions with other findings
This is an explicit, statistically-grounded negative result for wetland-restoration BMPs at a real, heavily monitored hypereutrophic lake - it directly complicates any literature or vendor claim that constructed/restored wetland BMPs reliably deliver measurable downstream phosphorus-load reductions within a short (1-2 year) timeframe; here, two purpose-built, professionally sited wetland BMPs produced no statistically significant upstream-vs-downstream or pre-vs-post improvement in TP, SRP, or turbidity, and receiving-lake TP/chlorophyll-a/Secchi depth were all unchanged. The authors are careful to frame this as inconclusive rather than proof of BMP failure (small scale, short duration, and a dry year confound the test), which is itself a methodologically important caution for any HAB-related treatment/management claim: absence of a detected effect after a short monitoring window is evidence of 'not yet proven,' not evidence of 'BMP does not work.' Correlation between BMP presence and any observed water-quality metric here is explicitly reported as null, and even where p-values are significant (e.g., nitrate increasing post-restoration), the authors do not claim the wetlands caused that change - underscoring that association in these small-n, high-variance field comparisons should not be read as causal evidence either way.

# Hot and toxic: Temperature regulates microcystin release from cyanobacteria

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048969717321472

**What it is.** A paired field-monitoring and laboratory-incubation study testing whether water temperature regulates microcystin (a cyanotoxin) release from the cyanobacterium Planktothrix agardhii in a eutrophic freshwater lake, using generalized linear mixed models to quantify the temperature–toxin relationship and its use as a possible forecasting signal for bloom toxicity.

- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria (Planktothrix agardhii) biomass increased with warming up to a threshold of 18 °C, then declined rapidly with further warming.

## Data / numbers
- 3 to 27 °C — lake water temperature range observed over the field study period
- 18 °C — temperature above which cyanobacteria (P. agardhii) biomass declined rapidly (field)
- 20 to 25 °C — temperature range of peak extracellular microcystin concentration in the field, and peak productivity-specific microcystin release in the lab
- 30 °C — temperature at which laboratory microcystin release declined sharply
- ≥20 °C — warming threshold used in the GLMM prediction
- 36% — GLMM-predicted increase in microcystin release at warming ≥20 °C (point estimate; no confidence interval, p-value, or R² given in the abstract)
- ≤50 μg L⁻¹ — Chlorophyll a condition under which the 36% increase estimate applies

## Methods
Paired field + laboratory study centered on a single eutrophic freshwater lake dominated by Planktothrix agardhii. Field component: seasonal in-situ monitoring of water temperature, cyanobacteria biomass, and extracellular microcystin concentration. Laboratory component: a concurrent incubation experiment that replicated the lake's seasonal water-temperature variation, explicitly designed to test cause-effect (not merely correlational) relationships between temperature and toxin release, measuring productivity-specific microcystin release. Statistical analysis: generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs) used to evaluate water temperature as a predictor of cyanobacteria abundance and microcystin release, and to generate the quantitative prediction of a 36% increase in microcystin release under warming ≥20 °C when Chlorophyll a ≤50 μg/L. Beyond this, the abstract does not state sample sizes, replication, model covariates other than chlorophyll a, or model-fit statistics (R², AIC, p-values, confidence intervals) — these were not retrievable because only the abstract was accessible.

## Tensions with other findings
The unimodal (rise-then-fall) relationship reported here — microcystin release peaking at 20–25°C and then declining sharply by 30°C — complicates a simple monotonic "warmer water = more toxin" narrative sometimes used in HAB risk communication; it instead suggests a thermal optimum/threshold, which matters for shaping a temperature-based risk index as non-linear rather than linear. The paper also reports that peak extracellular microcystin coincided with a decline in cyanobacteria biomass, i.e., toxin release peaked as biomass was already falling — a caution against treating biomass or chlorophyll (including satellite-derived bloom-extent proxies) as a reliable stand-in for toxin risk, since the two can diverge in time. Because the reported thresholds (18°C, 20–25°C, 30°C) come from a single eutrophic lake dominated by Planktothrix agardhii, caution is warranted before generalizing this specific threshold set to other cyanobacteria genera (e.g., Microcystis, which dominates many other HAB studies) or to other lakes; only the abstract was available, so it cannot be confirmed from the fetched text whether the authors themselves flag this generalizability caveat in the full text. As with any field-correlational pattern, the field-only association between temperature and microcystin is not by itself proof of causation — though the authors' own laboratory incubation was explicitly designed to test cause-effect and reproduced the same pattern, which strengthens (but per the abstract text alone does not fully substitute a statistical significance/uncertainty report for) the causal interpretation.

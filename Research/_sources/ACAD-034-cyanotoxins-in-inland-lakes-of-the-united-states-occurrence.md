# Cyanotoxins in inland lakes of the United States: Occurrence and potential recreational health risks in the EPA National Lakes Assessment 2007

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed journal article (Loftin et al. 2016, Harmful Algae) reporting a nationwide in-situ survey of cyanotoxins (microcystins, cylindrospermopsins, saxitoxins, anatoxin-a, nodularin-R) across 1,161 U.S. lakes sampled in EPA's 2007 National Lakes Assessment, comparing measured toxin concentrations against World Health Organization (WHO) recreational-risk thresholds based on toxin concentration, cyanobacterial abundance, and chlorophyll-a.

## Data / numbers
- 1,161 lakes surveyed nationwide (EPA National Lakes Assessment 2007 sampling frame)
- Cylindrospermopsin (ELISA): detected in 4.0% of samples; mean 0.56 µg/L among positive detections
- Microcystin (ELISA): detected in 32% of samples; mean 3.0 µg/L among positive detections
- Saxitoxin (ELISA): detected in 7.7% of samples; mean 0.061 µg/L among positive detections
- Triple co-occurrence (all 3 ELISA toxin classes) among samples with ≥1 detection: 0.32%
- Pairwise co-occurrence (per companion USGS press release, not in abstract): cylindrospermopsin+microcystin 0.96% of samples; saxitoxin+microcystin 5.0% of samples
- Cyanobacteria present: 98% of samples; numerically dominant: 76% of samples
- Potential toxin-producing cyanobacteria present: anatoxin-producers 81%, cylindrospermopsin-producers 67%, microcystin-producers 95%, saxitoxin-producers 79% of samples
- Anatoxin-a (LC/MS/MS, subsample n=27): detected in 15% of samples
- Nodularin-R (LC/MS/MS, subsample n=27): detected in 3.7% of samples
- WHO moderate/high recreational-risk threshold exceedance: microcystin-based 1.1%; cyanobacteria-abundance-based 27%; total-chlorophyll-based 44% of samples
- Complete agreement across all 3 WHO microcystin-risk metrics: 27% of samples

## Methods
In-situ field samples were collected from the centers of 1,161 U.S. lakes as part of EPA's probabilistic National Lakes Assessment 2007 survey. Cylindrospermopsins, microcystins, and saxitoxins were quantified via ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay) across the full sample set; anatoxin-a and nodularin-R were quantified via LC/MS/MS (liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry) in a much smaller subsample (n=27). Measured toxin concentrations, cyanobacterial abundance, and total chlorophyll-a were each independently compared against World Health Organization (WHO) guideline thresholds for recreational exposure risk (moderate/high risk categories), and against unspecified 'mouse toxicity data' (per abstract; not detailed further in the retrieved text), to evaluate how well the indirect indicators (abundance, chlorophyll) agree with direct toxin-based risk classification. The retrieved abstract and press materials report where the abundance/chlorophyll proxies 'over-predict' toxin-based risk (i.e., flag more lakes as risky than direct toxin measurement does) but do not describe a specific statistical/predictive model, nor do they report method performance characteristics (e.g., ELISA cross-reactivity, detection limits, or LC/MS/MS validation) — those details would be in the paywalled Methods section, which could not be retrieved.

## Stated limitations
The retrieved text (abstract plus two USGS press releases) does not include an explicit author-stated 'limitations' passage — no confidence intervals, standard deviations, or discussion of sampling/analytical caveats appear anywhere in the material obtained. One limitation is evident from the numbers themselves rather than stated by the authors in the retrieved text: the LC/MS/MS-based anatoxin-a and nodularin-R detection rates (15%, 3.7%) come from a subsample of only n=27, far smaller and presumably non-random relative to the full 1,161-lake ELISA survey, making those two figures much less statistically robust than the ELISA-based detection rates. The full paywalled article (ScienceDirect, Elsevier; a direct WebFetch attempt returned HTTP 403 Forbidden) could not be retrieved, so the authors' own Methods/Results/Discussion sections — which likely contain explicit limitations — are not captured in this dossier.

## Tensions with other findings
This source provides direct empirical evidence relevant to a core caveat for the HAB_PoC project's remote-sensing + in-situ fusion framing: it documents that cyanobacterial-abundance- and chlorophyll-a-based risk indicators (the kinds of signals derivable from satellite/remote-sensing proxies, e.g., EPA CyAN) substantially over-predicted WHO recreational-risk threshold exceedance (27% and 44% of samples, respectively) relative to direct microcystin toxin measurement (1.1% of samples), and that the three WHO metrics agreed completely in only 27% of samples. It also shows that detecting one toxin class (e.g., microcystin) is not a reliable indicator of the presence of other classes (e.g., saxitoxin, cylindrospermopsin), with the three toxin classes co-occurring together in only 0.32% of samples. Applied cautiously (this is a descriptive/comparative finding, not a causal or mechanistic one), this is an important, source-grounded caution against overclaiming that any single remote-sensing-derived bloom/chlorophyll signal equates to actual toxin-based health risk — directly relevant to calibrating language in any tool or product claim that fuses satellite bloom indicators with toxin-based risk readouts.

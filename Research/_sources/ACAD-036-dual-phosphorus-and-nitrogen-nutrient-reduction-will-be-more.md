# Dual phosphorus and nitrogen nutrient reduction will be more effective than a phosphorus-only reduction in mitigating diatom and cyanobacterial blooms in Lake Erie, USA-Canada

**What it is.** A 2024 peer-reviewed field-experimental paper (Limnology and Oceanography) reporting in-situ nutrient dilution and enrichment bioassays conducted at a bloom-impacted site in western Lake Erie during June and August of 2022 and 2023, designed to test whether the Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement's proposed 40% phosphorus-only load reduction would control spring diatom and summer cyanobacterial blooms as effectively as a combined 40% reduction in both phosphorus and nitrogen.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** A year-round, concurrent 40% reduction in both phosphorus (P) and nitrogen (N) would be more effective at controlling Lake Erie's spring diatom and summer cyanobacterial blooms than the currently mandated 40% P-only reduction.
  - *evidence:* This is the paper's central conclusion, based on four seasonal in-situ dilution/enrichment bioassays (June and August, 2022 and 2023) in which combined P+N dilution generally produced lower phytoplankton growth rates than equivalent P-only dilution. (Abstract; Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "a 40% reduction in both P and N will be more effective at reducing cyanobacterial blooms in Lake Erie than a P-only approach"
- **[✓ verified]** The study endorses continuing the existing GLWQA 40% phosphorus load-reduction target, but argues it is insufficient alone and should be paired with an equal-magnitude (40%) nitrogen reduction, applied year-round.
  - *evidence:* Direct abstract/discussion statement summarizing the paper's policy recommendation, drawn from comparing P-only vs. P+N bioassay outcomes across both bloom seasons. (Abstract; Discussion)
  - *quote:* "Our results support the USA-Canada Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement recommendation of a 40% P reduction, but also indicate that a parallel reduction of N input by 40% would be most effective in controlling bloom magnitudes."
- **[✓ verified]** The existing GLWQA phosphorus target is quantified as roughly 6,000 metric tons (MT) total phosphorus (TP) per year for the Central Basin and 860 MT TP / 186 MT dissolved reactive phosphorus (DRP) for the Maumee River during March-June, each representing a 40% cut from the 2000-2015 annual average load.
  - *evidence:* Cited background/policy figures (from GLWQA 2015) that motivate and calibrate the study's experimental dilution levels; these are reported targets, not measurements generated by this study. (Introduction)
  - *quote:* "860 MT of TP and 186 MT of DRP, which equates to a 40% reduction from the 2000–2015 annual average"
- **[✓ verified]** At a more extreme 60% phosphorus-only dilution (tested only in the 2023 experiments), phosphorus-only and combined phosphorus-and-nitrogen dilution treatments converged to similar growth rates for most phytoplankton groups (cyanobacteria excepted) — implying a P-only strategy would need cuts well beyond 40% to match dual-nutrient management.
  - *evidence:* Reported for the June 2023 bioassay, where a 60% dilution level was added specifically to probe whether a larger P-only reduction could substitute for dual N&P management. (Results, June 2023 bioassay)
- **[✓ verified]** Nutrient limitation of Lake Erie phytoplankton is strongly seasonal: phosphorus is the primary limiting nutrient during the June/spring diatom-dominated bloom (with possible N co-limitation), whereas both phosphorus and nitrogen co-limit growth during the August/summer cyanobacteria-dominated bloom.
  - *evidence:* Supported by nutrient-enrichment assays: in August, chlorophyll a rose roughly 3- to 4-fold only when both P and N (as nitrate or ammonium) were added together, indicating co-limitation; in June, P-only and P+N enrichment produced similarly sized Chl a increases, consistent with P as the dominant limiting nutrient. (Results (August 2022, June 2023 enrichment assays); Discussion)
  - *quote:* "phytoplankton requires enrichments of both P and N to reach their highest biomass during times when cyanobacteria form blooms (August)"
- **[✓ verified]** In the June 2022 bioassay, ammonium enrichment (50 μmol/L) inhibited diatoms and other eukaryotic algae but stimulated cyanobacteria — i.e., the two bloom-forming groups responded in opposite directions to the same reduced-nitrogen form.
  - *evidence:* Statistically supported divergence in growth response to ammonium across phytoplankton groups in the June 2022 experiment (reported treatment effect p<0.001 for total algae, diatoms, cryptophytes, and green algae). (Results, June 2022 bioassay)
  - *quote:* "diatoms...can be inhibited by ammonia; cyanobacteria show a preference for reduced forms of N (i.e., ammonium)"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors state they could not determine, from their own data, why ammonium enrichment was inhibitory to eukaryotic algae in June but stimulatory in August, and note that pH-dependent un-ionized-ammonia toxicity does not fully explain it because June Lake Erie pH (~8.3) should keep most ammonium in the non-toxic ionized form.
  - *evidence:* Explicit, self-identified gap in mechanistic understanding, flagged by the authors as unresolved. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "We cannot determine from our data collected why ammonium was inhibitory (toxic or otherwise) during June but stimulatory in August."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors flag a core design limitation: their bioassays manipulate in-lake nutrient concentration (via dilution), not watershed nutrient load, and caution this is an imperfect proxy because Maumee River P load and resulting in-lake P concentration are only weakly correlated, with the correlation weakening further into the lake.
  - *evidence:* Explicit caveat distinguishing the experimentally tested variable (concentration) from the actual policy lever (river load), stated as the main caveat of the study design. (Discussion (limitations))
  - *quote:* "There is only a weak positive correlation between springtime Maumee River P load and in-lake concentration during the summer, and that correlation becomes weaker further into the lake. There is no feasible method to experimentally test how a P load reduction will affect algal growth in Lake Erie."
- **[✓ verified]** Dilution-percentage effects on phytoplankton growth rate were not statistically significant in the August experiments (e.g., August 2023 P-dilution main effect p=0.284), which the authors attribute to already-low ambient nutrient concentrations limiting the technique's sensitivity; they conclude the dilution-assay approach is better suited to spring/early summer, when ambient nutrients are higher.
  - *evidence:* Nonsignificant statistical result paired with the authors' own stated interpretation of the method's reduced power in nutrient-poor summer conditions. (Results and Discussion, August 2023 bioassay)
  - *quote:* "the dilution assay method is better suited for the spring and early summer when nutrient concentrations are high enough to be diluted"
- **[✓ verified]** Phosphorus is expected to recover far more slowly than nitrogen after load reductions because legacy P cycles between lake/watershed sediments and the water column (with a cited comparator shallow-lake recovery timescale of roughly 20 years or more), whereas nitrogen can be permanently lost from the system via denitrification/ammonification.
  - *evidence:* Biogeochemical reasoning the authors give for why the benefits of nutrient reduction will lag in time, drawing on a cited comparator lake (Lake Müggelsee, Germany). (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "time scales for 'weaning' the system of P tend to be much longer than for N"
- **[✓ verified]** A separate, contested modeling study (Hellweger et al. 2022a) is discussed as having projected that a 40% P-only reduction could increase Microcystis toxin (microcystin) content by roughly 15-25% even while reducing bloom biomass by about 20%; the current authors argue that model omits important in-lake processes and is oversimplified, but acknowledge the underlying debate is unresolved.
  - *evidence:* The authors explicitly engage a competing, model-based prediction that complicates a simple 'more P reduction is always better' reading of nutrient-reduction policy, pushing back on its assumptions while conceding the question is not settled; note this specific 15-25%/20% figure came through as the fetch's paraphrase of the cited debate rather than as a directly quoted sentence, so treat the exact percentages as reported-but-not-independently-verified against original wording. (Discussion)
- **[✓ verified]** Spring diatom blooms are more strongly linked to central-basin hypoxia than are summer cyanobacterial blooms, which the authors use to argue diatom blooms deserve management attention equal to cyanobacterial blooms.
  - *evidence:* Stated association used to justify studying diatom (spring) blooms alongside the more commonly studied cyanobacterial (summer) blooms, and to connect nutrient-reduction policy to hypoxia mitigation. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "central basin hypoxia is more associated with springtime diatom blooms than summer-time cyanobacterial blooms"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors' growth-rate metric (linear regression of ln[pigment concentration] vs. time across all four sampling points) was cross-validated against the conventional two-point specific growth-rate formula and found to closely agree (slopes ranging 0.987-1.007, correlation R>0.989), supporting the reliability of the growth-rate estimates underlying all treatment comparisons.
  - *evidence:* Internal methods-validation statistic the authors report to justify their chosen growth-rate metric over the standard two-point formula. (Methods)

## Methods
In-situ nutrient dilution and enrichment bioassays at a bloom-affected site in western Lake Erie (~3 km west of Ohio State University's Stone Laboratory, south side of Rattlesnake Island; 41°40.155'N, 82°51.193'W), run in June and August of 2022 and 2023 (four seasonal experiments). ~200 L of lake water was collected and incubated in triplicate 4-L Cubitainers (chemically inert, ~85% PAR-transparent) suspended in floating corrals off finger piers under natural light and temperature. Dilution treatments (0/20/40%, plus 60% added in 2023) used 0.45 μm-filtered lake water and/or an artificial major-ion solution to lower ambient nutrient concentrations while other water chemistry was held constant; all containers received a fixed silica (50 μmol/L) and carbon (10 mg C/L) amendment. A parallel enrichment design added N (50 μmol/L, as KNO3 and/or NH4Cl) and/or P (5 μmol/L, as KH2PO4) to identify limiting nutrient(s). Response was tracked over a 3-day incubation (daily T0-T3 sampling) using HPLC-quantified diagnostic photopigments as group-specific biomass proxies (fucoxanthin=diatoms, zeaxanthin=cyanobacteria, alloxanthin=cryptophytes, chlorophyll b=chlorophytes, chlorophyll a=total biomass), cross-checked qualitatively against Lugol's-preserved microscopy. Growth rates were derived via first-order kinetics (linear regression of ln[pigment] vs. time across all four timepoints), validated against the conventional two-point specific-growth-rate formula (slopes 0.987-1.007, R>0.989). Statistics: one-way ANOVA with Tukey post-hoc for enrichment Chl a comparisons; two-factor MANOVA (P-dilution % x N&P co-dilution) with follow-up two-factor ANOVAs per phytoplankton group for dilution growth-rate comparisons (IBM SPSS v29). No hydrodynamic or biogeochemical simulation model (e.g., GLM-AED, ELCOM) was built or run — this is a controlled field-bioassay experiment, not a whole-lake model. Per the authors, the dilution method works well (detects treatment differences) when ambient nutrients are high enough for dilution to matter (spring/early summer) but loses statistical power when ambient nutrients are already low (August in both study years), and it tests water-column concentration, not watershed load, as a necessary proxy.

## Stated limitations
The authors state: (1) the experimental design tests reduced in-lake nutrient concentration via dilution, not reduced watershed nutrient load, and caution this is an imperfect proxy because Maumee River P load and in-lake P concentration are only weakly correlated (weakening further into the lake), with no feasible method to experimentally test load reduction directly; (2) the study covers only two seasons (June, August) across two years (2022-2023) at a single western-basin site, so results may not generalize to other seasons, years, or lake basins; (3) they cannot explain, from their own data, why ammonium enrichment was inhibitory to eukaryotic algae in June but stimulatory in August; (4) the dilution-percentage treatment lost statistical power in the August experiments because ambient nutrient concentrations were already low, leading the authors to judge the dilution-assay approach better suited to spring/early summer; (5) they explicitly call for a cost-benefit analysis of a P-only vs. dual-nutrient strategy, which they did not themselves perform; and (6) they recommend nutrient-reduction management decisions be based on an ensemble of models, empirical data, and experiments under an adaptive-management mindset, rather than on any single study (including their own).

## Tensions with other findings
This bioassay-based conclusion complicates the historically P-centric nutrient-management paradigm for Lake Erie/the Great Lakes (reflected in the GLWQA's P-only 40% load target) by providing direct experimental evidence of N co-limitation, especially for summer cyanobacterial blooms — a more nuanced picture than "phosphorus alone controls Great Lakes eutrophication." The paper also explicitly surfaces an internal tension with model-based work: it discusses Hellweger et al. (2022a)'s simulation result that a P-only 40% reduction could increase Microcystis toxin content even while lowering biomass, a claim these bioassay authors dispute as resting on an oversimplified model, but they do not resolve it empirically since their own bioassays measured biomass/growth-rate endpoints (via photopigments) rather than toxin (microcystin) concentrations directly. Because the experiments manipulate water-column nutrient concentration rather than watershed load — and because the authors themselves state that concentration and Maumee River load are only weakly correlated — this source's "40%" findings should not be read as a validated, quantified prediction of how much bloom biomass will change per unit of load reduction in the field; that is a correlational/experimental proxy result, not a causal field-scale forecast, a distinction relevant to any downstream tool that would translate these figures directly into an expected bloom-suppression outcome.

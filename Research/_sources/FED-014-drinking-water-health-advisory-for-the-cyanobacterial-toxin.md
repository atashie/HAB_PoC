# Drinking Water Health Advisory for the Cyanobacterial Toxin Cylindrospermopsin

**What it is.** A June 2015 U.S. EPA non-regulatory Drinking Water Health Advisory (Doc. No. 820R15101) that establishes Ten-day Health Advisory concentrations for the cyanobacterial toxin cylindrospermopsin in drinking water, derived from an animal dose-response study, together with background on the toxin's chemistry, cyanobacterial sources, environmental fate, and existing regulatory/guideline context.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** EPA's Ten-day Health Advisory for cylindrospermopsin in drinking water is 0.7 µg/L for bottle-fed infants and pre-school-age children, and 3 µg/L for school-age children through adults.
  - *evidence:* Both values are stated to derive from the same toxicity reference dose (RfD) from the Humpage and Falconer mouse study; they differ because EPA applied different age-specific drinking-water-intake-to-body-weight ratios (infant/child vs. adult) drawn from EPA's 2011 Exposure Factors Handbook. (Executive Summary, p.2)
  - *quote:* "The Ten-day HA value for bottle-fed infants and young children of pre-school age is 0.7 µg/L and for school-age children through adults is 3 µg/L for cylindrospermopsin."
- **[✓ verified]** The critical toxicological endpoint underlying the advisory is kidney toxicity (increased kidney weight, decreased urinary protein) identified in an 11-week (77-day) oral study in mice by Humpage and Falconer (2002, 2003), yielding a No-Observed-Adverse-Effect-Level (NOAEL) of 30 µg/kg/day.
  - *evidence:* EPA states it selected this specific animal study as the 'critical study' for deriving the reference dose (RfD); kidney weight increase and urinary protein decrease are named as the critical effects. (Executive Summary, p.1)
  - *quote:* "The NOAEL ... was determined to be 30 μg/kg/day based on kidney toxicity."
- **[✓ verified]** EPA applied a composite (total) uncertainty factor of 300 to the NOAEL to derive the reference dose: 10x for intraspecies variability, 10x for interspecies variability, and roughly 3x (10^0.5) for database deficiencies.
  - *evidence:* This is EPA's own accounting of how the 300-fold margin was assembled from three named sub-factors; it represents a conservative safety margin, not a precise estimate of the human no-effect level. (Executive Summary, p.1-2)
  - *quote:* "The total uncertainty factor (UF) applied to the NOAEL was 300."
- **[✓ verified]** EPA used a 77-day critical study even though it states 7-to-30-day studies are 'typically used' for Ten-day Health Advisories, justifying this because the only available shorter-duration studies (Shaw et al., 2001; Reisner et al., 2004) could not be used for quantitative dose-response derivation, while showing similar effects at similar doses to the longer study.
  - *evidence:* An EPA-acknowledged methodological compromise: the duration mismatch is bridged by a qualitative similarity argument, not a quantitative adjustment method. (Executive Summary, p.1-2)
  - *quote:* "however, effects observed in these studies are the same or similar to the Humpage and Falconer study (2002, 2003) and occur at similar doses."
- **[✓ verified]** There are no U.S. federal regulatory standards for cyanobacteria or cyanotoxins (including cylindrospermopsin) in drinking water under the Safe Drinking Water Act or in ambient waters under the Clean Water Act, and this Health Advisory itself is explicitly non-binding.
  - *evidence:* States the regulatory vacuum directly and defines the HA's own legal status as advisory guidance, not a rule. (Section 1.1 'Current Criteria, Guidance and Standards,' p.3; Executive Summary, p.2)
  - *quote:* "This HA is not a regulation; it is not legally enforceable; and it does not confer legal rights or impose legal obligations on any party."
- **[✓ verified]** Existing international and U.S. state drinking-water guideline values for cylindrospermopsin mostly cluster at 1 µg/L (Australia, New Zealand, Ohio, Oregon), while Brazil's recommended value is 15 µg/L — an order of magnitude higher.
  - *evidence:* Direct comparative tables (Table 1-1 international, Table 1-2 state) that EPA compiled to situate its own advisory among other jurisdictions' guidance; Brazil's figure is explicitly labeled 'recommended' in the source's own table rather than presented as a firm limit. (Table 1-1 and Table 1-2, p.4)
  - *quote:* "15 μg/L (recommended)"
- **[✓ verified]** Cylindrospermopsin-producing cyanobacteria 'do not tend to form visible surface scums,' with the highest cell concentrations occurring below the water surface, and the toxin is mostly extracellular (dissolved in water or attached to particulates) rather than retained inside cells.
  - *evidence:* General characterization of this toxin group's distribution in the water column and toxin partitioning, stated without a specific quantitative citation in this passage. (Executive Summary, p.1)
  - *quote:* "These species do not tend to form visible surface scums and the highest concentrations of cells occurs below the water surface."
- **[✓ verified]** Cylindrospermopsin degrades photochemically within hours (half-life 1.5-3 hours) when algal pigments are present but shows little degradation without pigments; in natural surface waters, reported biodegradation half-lives range from 11-15 days up to 8 weeks.
  - *evidence:* Environmental fate information EPA uses to frame the exposure-duration/seasonality assumptions behind the short-term ('Ten-day') advisory framing. (Executive Summary, p.2)
  - *quote:* "reported half-lives of 1.5 to 3 hours ... Half-lives of 11 to 15 days and up to 8 weeks have been reported for cylindrospermopsin in surface waters."
- **[✓ verified]** EPA states there is inadequate information to assess the carcinogenic potential of cylindrospermopsin because no epidemiological studies linking it to cancer and no chronic animal cancer bioassays of the purified toxin were identified.
  - *evidence:* Explicit data-gap conclusion reached under EPA's 2005 cancer risk-assessment guidelines; this advisory is instead based on non-cancer (kidney toxicity) endpoints. (Executive Summary, p.2)
  - *quote:* "there is inadequate information to assess carcinogenic potential of cylindrospermopsin"
- **[✓ verified]** Cylindrospermopsin is a zwitterionic tricyclic alkaloid with a molecular weight of 415.43 g/mole, and two naturally occurring congeners have been identified (7-epicylindrospermopsin and 7-deoxycylindrospermopsin).
  - *evidence:* Basic physicochemical characterization in the Problem Formulation section, citing Ohtani et al. (1992) for structure/molecular data and Norris et al. (1999)/de la Cruz et al. (2013) for the congeners. (Section 2.2 'Physical and Chemical Properties,' p.5-6)
  - *quote:* "a molecular weight of 415.43 g/mole"

## Data / numbers
- NOAEL = 30 µg/kg/day (kidney toxicity endpoint: increased kidney weight, decreased urinary protein), from the Humpage and Falconer 2002/2003 11-week [77-day] oral mouse study — EPA's critical study for RfD derivation.
- Composite uncertainty factor = 300 (10x intraspecies x 10x interspecies x ~3x [10^0.5] database-deficiency factor) applied to the NOAEL — a ~300-fold built-in safety margin, not a point estimate of human harm.
- Ten-day Health Advisory = 0.7 µg/L for bottle-fed infants and pre-school-age children.
- Ten-day Health Advisory = 3 µg/L for school-age children through adults.
- International guideline values: Australia 1 µg/L (NHMRC/NRMMC 2011); New Zealand 1 µg/L (Ministry of Health 2008); Brazil 15 µg/L, labeled 'recommended' (Brasil 2009).
- U.S. state guideline values: Ohio 1 µg/L (OHEPA 2014); Oregon 1 µg/L (OHA 2015).
- Molecular weight = 415.43 g/mole; molecular formula given in the source as 'C H N O S' with subscripts '15 21 5 7' split onto a separate line by extraction (i.e., C15H21N5O7S per Ohtani et al., 1992) — reconstructed here, not a clean verbatim string.
- Photochemical degradation half-life = 1.5 to 3 hours in the presence of algal pigments; no half-life value given for the no-pigment case, only 'little degradation.'
- Biodegradation half-life in natural surface waters = 11 to 15 days, with up to 8 weeks also reported — a range, not a single point estimate with confidence interval.
- Critical study duration = 11 weeks (77 days), versus the 7-30 day duration EPA states is 'typically used' for Ten-day HA derivation — an explicitly flagged mismatch, bridged only qualitatively.
- Context (not toxin-specific): EPA's HA Program (est. 1978) lists Health Advisories for 213 contaminants in total.
- Cyanotoxins (anatoxin-a, cylindrospermopsin, microcystin-LR) were added to EPA's Contaminant Candidate List on CCL 3 (2009) and draft CCL 4 (April 2015); cyanotoxins as a category first appeared on CCL 1 (1998) and CCL 2 (2005).

## Methods
Standard EPA human-health risk-assessment / Health-Advisory derivation pathway, per the Executive Summary: (1) problem formulation drawing on cyanobacteria taxonomy/ecology and cylindrospermopsin chemistry; (2) dose-response assessment selecting the Humpage and Falconer (2002, 2003) 11-week (77-day) oral toxicity study in mice as the 'critical study,' yielding a NOAEL of 30 µg/kg/day for kidney toxicity; (3) application of a composite uncertainty factor of 300 (10x intraspecies x 10x interspecies x ~3x database-deficiency) to derive a reference dose; (4) extrapolation to age-specific Ten-day HA values (0.7 µg/L and 3 µg/L) using drinking-water-intake/body-weight distributions from EPA's 2011 Exposure Factors Handbook. The source explicitly flags a methodological strain: Ten-day HAs are 'typically' derived from 7-30 day studies, but the only usable critical study ran 77 days, because the available shorter studies (Shaw et al., 2001; Reisner et al., 2004) were 'not suitable for quantification' — EPA bridges this qualitatively (similar effects at similar doses) rather than through a quantitative equivalence method. For carcinogenicity, the method fails outright per the source: no epidemiological studies and no chronic animal cancer bioassays exist, so EPA states it cannot apply its cancer-risk-assessment framework (inadequate data). The Table of Contents indicates the full report also covers Analytical Methods (Section 5.0) and Treatment Technologies (Section 6.0: source-water bloom management/mitigation, conventional treatment, chemical oxidation, UV irradiation, point-of-use units), plus an allometric scaling approach and uncertainty/susceptibility analysis (Sections 4.3-4.7) — but the substantive text of these sections was not present in the windowed excerpt provided to me, so I cannot characterize their methods or findings beyond noting the section titles exist.

## Stated limitations
The source itself states: (1) non-drinking-water exposure routes (fish/food consumption, inhalation, dermal contact during bathing or recreation) 'cannot be quantified at this time' and are merely assumed to contribute less than drinking-water ingestion; (2) 'No reliable data are available on the exposure levels of cylindrospermopsin' that caused the human hepatoenteritis-like illness reports it cites; (3) the shorter-duration, more human-exposure-relevant toxicity studies (Shaw et al., 2001; Reisner et al., 2004) were 'not suitable for quantification,' forcing reliance on a longer (77-day) study than the 7-30 days 'typically used' for Ten-day HAs; (4) 'No epidemiological studies of the association of cylindrospermopsin and cancer are available' and no chronic animal cancer bioassays were identified, so carcinogenic potential cannot be assessed; (5) data on absorption and metabolism come from only 'limited animal studies' / 'limited data'; (6) the HA is explicitly non-binding — 'not a regulation,' 'not legally enforceable,' confers no legal rights or obligations, and is 'subject to change as new information becomes available.'

## Tensions with other findings
The source states cylindrospermopsin-producing taxa 'do not tend to form visible surface scums' and that 'the highest concentrations of cells occurs below the water surface' — this complicates surface-reflectance-based satellite/remote-sensing detection approaches (e.g., EPA CyAN-style cyanobacterial pigment signals), which are best matched to surface-scum formers; for cylindrospermopsin producers specifically, a clean surface signal could under-represent subsurface toxin-relevant biomass, a caveat worth carrying into any remote-sensing-plus-in-situ HAB risk-fusion approach (this is a complicating consideration the source raises, not a causal claim it makes). Separately, the source's own comparative guideline tables show real disagreement across jurisdictions on an acceptable concentration: most (Australia, New Zealand, Ohio, Oregon) sit at 1 µg/L, but Brazil recommends 15 µg/L — a 15-fold difference — indicating the numeric threshold is not a settled, universally agreed constant but one still shaped by differing risk-assessment choices across agencies. Relatedly, the large composite uncertainty factor (300-fold) built into the U.S. HA values underscores that these are conservative screening thresholds derived from a single animal study, not tightly bounded, high-precision estimates of human harm levels — worth keeping in mind if these thresholds are ever presented as sharp bright-line risk cutoffs elsewhere in the literature.

# Internal phosphorus loading in Canadian fresh waters: a critical review and data analysis

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed, invited critical literature review combined with a quantitative meta-analysis (plus narrative case studies of 7 lakes) of internal phosphorus (P) loading - the release of P from freshwater sediments back into the overlying water, a process linked to harmful algal blooms - across Canadian fresh waters. Published by Orihel, Baulch, Casson, North, Parsons, Seckar, and Venkiteswaran in the Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences, 74(12):2005-2029 (2017).

## Methods
A literature-based critical review paired with a quantitative data synthesis. The authors compiled a structured relational database (companion public GitHub repo / MS Access .mdb, maintained by co-author J. Venkiteswaran, with tables including REF [bibliographic references], LAKES [waterbody characteristics: coordinates, elevation, surface area, max depth, residence time, trophic class], GEOL [bedrock geology linked via Wheeler et al. 1996], SITES [sampling-site depth, loss-on-ignition, sediment P/Fe], and PFLUX [phosphorus flux measurements by method: core incubation, porewater profiling, mass balance, or modeling, with quality-control flags], plus a porewater-concentration table) consolidating 618 estimates of internal P loading from 43 publications spanning 70 Canadian ponds, lakes, reservoirs, and coastal wetlands. Measurements are distinguished as short-term gross sediment-core rates (Lgross) versus long-term net whole-ecosystem rates (Lnet), both expressed in total-phosphorus terms, and related to compiled environmental covariates (oxygen, pH per Hounslow 1995, geology per Wheeler et al. 1996, trophic state) to identify drivers of variation. The quantitative synthesis is paired with narrative case studies of seven specific lakes (Lake Simcoe, Lake Winnipeg, Lake of the Woods, Lake Erie, Lake Champlain, Cootes Paradise, Lake Diefenbaker). Publicly posted R scripts (data export, assembly/unit-standardization, and figure generation, using dplyr/tidyr/ggplot2) support reproducibility of the data analysis. Note: I could not access the paper's full Methods section text directly (see fetch_notes), so the exact statistical approach used to attribute drivers (e.g., regression vs. simpler comparison) is not confirmed here and is not asserted.

## Stated limitations
I could not access the paper's own stated "Limitations" text (full body inaccessible - see fetch_notes), so the following are drawn from the paper's companion press release, in which co-authors themselves frame open questions/gaps: co-author Helen Baulch states there is "a lot more we need to know about internal phosphorus loading, such as if this process is important in the thousands of reservoirs of our country," and that there are "huge data gaps for this process in our northern lakes that are undergoing rapid change" - i.e., the review's underlying dataset (43 publications, 70 water bodies) is explicitly acknowledged by the authors as not covering Canadian reservoirs broadly or northern/rapidly-changing lakes well. Co-author Jason Venkiteswaran's quote ("we don't know how long that phosphorus sticks around in our lakes") signals persisting uncertainty about P residence/turnover time, and Chris Parsons' quote ("lakes with different physical and chemical characteristics will recover at different rates") signals that the review's driver findings (oxygen, pH, geology, trophic state) do not translate into a single universal recovery-time or management rule across lake types. ## Tensions with other findings
This review's central point - that legacy, sediment-derived ("internal") phosphorus loading can be substantial and, per a recurring but not-independently-verified secondary citation, roughly comparable to (60-130% of) external P loading in some Canadian lakes - complicates HAB risk/driver frameworks (including remote-sensing- or in-situ-fusion approaches like the SePRO PoC) that key primarily on current, externally observable nutrient or weather inputs (e.g., watershed runoff, tributary loads, rainfall). If a lake's bioavailable P pool is substantially replenished from its own sediments - governed, per this review, by oxygen, pH, geology, and trophic state rather than by that year's external loading - then (a) external-loading-based risk indicators could understate bloom risk in high-internal-loading systems (the review's press materials flag prairie lakes as a high-risk geography in this respect), and (b) management interventions that only reduce external nutrient inputs may show delayed or muted water-quality improvement, consistent with the authors' own framing that different lake types warrant different recovery-time expectations. This is presented here as a complication/driver relationship, not a demonstrated causal or predictive model for any specific lake or bloom event - correlation between environmental covariates and loading rate is not evidence of a deterministic causal mechanism for any individual system, and the authors themselves stress the process is geographically and geologically variable rather than governed by one universal rule.

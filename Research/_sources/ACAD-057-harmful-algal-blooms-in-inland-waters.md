# Harmful algal blooms in inland waters

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://paerllab.web.unc.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/17493/2024/10/Feng-et-al.-2024.-Nature-Review-Earth-Environment1.pdf

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed global review (Nature Reviews Earth &amp; Environment, published online 27 Aug 2024, DOI 10.1038/s43017-024-00578-2) that synthesizes worldwide trends, drivers, future projections, and management/monitoring strategies for harmful algal blooms (HABs) in lakes and reservoirs, drawing heavily on satellite remote-sensing datasets combined with in-situ and modeling studies across continents.

## Methods
This is a literature synthesis/review, not a primary data-generation study, but it reports and re-cites results from primary studies including: global satellite remote-sensing analyses of lacustrine HABs (Landsat imagery 1982-2019, and MODIS imagery 2003-2020 for Chinese lakes; ref. 1 = Hou et al. 2022 Nat. Geosci.); a 37-year whole-ecosystem fertilization experiment in the Ontario Experimental Lakes District (Schindler et al., ref. 96); the European Multi-Lake Survey (EMLS, 26 countries, 369 lakes) and the Global Lake Ecological Observatory Network (GLEON); a US NOAA integrated Lake Erie monitoring program combining satellites, buoys, and genetic analysis; drone-mounted hyperspectral imaging (Montana's Upper Clark Fork River); and climate-hydrology-water-quality network models used for future HAB projections (e.g., Chapra et al. 2017 for US lakes; Kakouei et al. 2021 for 29 European/North American lakes). The review states satellite-based assessment is "generally considered a more reliable approach" than conventional field surveys for characterizing bloom extent, but that satellite data have limitations (revisit frequency, cloud cover, coarse spatial resolution, and inability to characterize vertical/subsurface HAB distribution).

## Stated limitations
The paper explicitly enumerates several limitations under "Limitations in spatiotemporal coverage": (1) geographic imbalance -- ~65% of relevant literature focuses on just 20 inland lakes, while remote-sensing has detected HAB events in over 20,000 lakes globally, leaving many African, Asian (Indonesia, Pakistan, Philippines) and South American (Chile, Colombia) countries under-studied despite known HAB prevalence; (2) short-term HAB dynamics are poorly characterized -- vertical migration of phytoplankton makes surface features highly variable (e.g., near-surface CyanoHAB extent in Lake Taihu can vary by up to one order of magnitude within a single day per 10-minute satellite observations); (3) satellite limitations -- Landsat's 16-day revisit period and cloud cover limit valid observations (especially pre-2000s), and even higher-frequency sensors (MODIS, GOCI) have coarse spatial resolution or limited coverage; (4) limited understanding of vertical/subsurface HAB distribution, since surface scums require calm/low-wind conditions and their absence doesn't mean a HAB is absent. The paper also states there is "substantial uncertainty in current future predictions" because process-based mechanistic models "still have shortcomings," species-specific responses to climate change are poorly quantified, laboratory findings don't always match field conditions across different lake geographies/hydrology, and models must better represent land-use/dam-construction changes to reduce uncertainty in nutrient loading projections. It also flags a direct empirical tension (see tensions field) between satellite and in-situ trend estimates in the US.

## Tensions with other findings
The review itself surfaces an internal tension/contradiction in the HAB literature: "the modest rise in bloom frequency indicated by satellite observations contrasts with in situ observations from 323 US water bodies that suggest that chlorophyll-a levels are decreasing in more lakes than they are increasing" (Wilkinson et al. 2022, ref. 44), which the authors say "highlights that observational approaches could also influence the observed trends in HAB dynamics and their variability." The paper also notes an unresolved scientific debate: "it remains unclear whether climate warming or nutrient enrichment is the dominant factor driving the global increase in HABs in inland waters." Additionally, it presents a historical scientific correction relevant to nutrient-management claims: the classic Ontario Experimental Lakes District experiment is cited for establishing phosphorus as the primary limiting nutrient (because the N-fixing test species could draw N from the atmosphere), but the review notes that "solely reducing phosphorus concentrations in waters has shown only temporary effects in many lakes" (e.g., Lake Erie, where non-N-fixing Microcystis became dominant), so "it is now widely acknowledged that inland water HABs are influenced by both nitrogen and phosphorus" -- directly relevant to any P-only mitigation framing. This is directly germane to the HAB_PoC project's driver/treatment claims: correlation between nutrient reduction and bloom reduction is explicitly shown by the review to be context- and lake-specific, not universal (e.g., Lake Erie bloom resurgence since the 1990s *despite* ~50% reduction in phosphorus loading 1974-late 1980s).

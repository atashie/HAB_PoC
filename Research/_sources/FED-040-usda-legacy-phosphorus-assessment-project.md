# USDA Legacy Phosphorus Assessment Project

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/ceap/usda-legacy-phosphorus-assessment-project

**What it is.** A multi-year USDA NRCS-ARS interagency research initiative, run under the Conservation Effects Assessment Project (CEAP), that samples soils and stream/ditch sediments across seven U.S. agricultural watersheds (in the Chesapeake Bay, Lake Champlain, Lake Erie, Mississippi River, and Snake River basins) to characterize "legacy" (historically accumulated) phosphorus and its chemical lability, with the explicit goal of improving conservation-practice recommendations for managing legacy P and reducing downstream water-quality degradation.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** The project's premise is that phosphorus accumulated in agricultural soils over decades ('legacy P') continues to threaten water quality long after inputs are reduced, framing the need for management focused on existing soil/sediment P stocks rather than only current fertilizer inputs.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the flagship paper's introduction, attributing the framing to Sharpley et al. (2013) and restated as the study's own conclusion. (Introduction and Abstract/Conclusion, Simpson et al. 2025, J. Environ. Qual. 54(4):851-869)
  - *quote:* "Sharpley et al. (2013) summarized this legacy P effect and how it will, without intervention, degrade aquatic ecosystems for decades or longer."
- **[✓ verified]** Measured stocks of labile (readily exchangeable) phosphorus in surface soils/sediments were large enough, at all seven study sites, to sustain high dissolved P losses for decades without new inputs.
  - *evidence:* Presented as the study's central quantitative conclusion, benchmarked against a reference dissolved-P-loss threshold drawn from the MANAGE field-runoff database. (Results/Discussion, legacy P stock section)
  - *quote:* "Stocks of labile P were large enough to sustain high P losses for decades."
- **[✓ verified]** All seven sites contained surface soils capable of sustaining dissolved P losses above a reference high-loss benchmark (the 75th percentile of measured field dissolved-P loss, 0.5 kg P/ha) for decades or longer, and most surface sediments held more than 1 kg P/ha in labile form.
  - *evidence:* Benchmark comparison against the MANAGE dataset of field P-loss observations, used to translate lab-measured labile P concentrations into an environmentally meaningful loss-duration estimate. (Results, labile P stocks section)
  - *quote:* ">75th percentile of 0.5 kg P ha-1 for decades or longer"
- **[✓ verified]** Topsoil (0-5 cm) is enriched in labile P relative to subsoil (5-15 cm) at most sites, and labile P is the most vertically stratified P pool measured, more so than Mehlich-3 P, Olsen P, or total P.
  - *evidence:* Site-specific stratification ratios (0-5 cm : 5-15 cm) reported with 95% confidence intervals for several P fractions across the seven sites. (Results, vertical stratification section (with site-level 95% CIs, e.g., Mahantango Creek 1.34 [1.07, 1.69] to W. Lake Erie 3.05 [2.57, 3.64] for labile P))

(Results, model performance section)

## Data / numbers
- 562 soil samples (0-5 cm and 5-15 cm depths) collected across 274 unique locations, spring 2022
- 62 sediment samples collected across the same 7 watersheds, spring 2022
- 7 watersheds/research sites spanning 5 major basins: Lake Champlain (VT), Le Sueur River/Mississippi R. basin (MN), Beasley Lake/Mississippi R. basin (MS), Snake River Basin (ID), Western Lake Erie Basin (IN), Mahantango Creek/Chesapeake Bay basin (PA), Choptank River/Chesapeake Bay coastal plain (MD)
- 31 distinct soil series and 6 soil orders represented across sites
- Soil EPC0 (equilibrium phosphate concentration at net-zero sorption): median 0.158 mg P L-1, mean 0.521 mg P L-1, range 0.0057-15.6 mg P L-1, IQR 0.0466-0.53 mg P L-1 (n=562)
- Sediment EPC0: median 0.0344 mg P L-1, mean 0.0758 mg P L-1, range 0.00778-1.43 mg P L-1, IQR 0.0192-0.0631 mg P L-1 (n=62)
- Soil labile P via 24-h anion-exchange-membrane desorption (PAEM): median 65 mg P kg-1, mean 77.8 mg P kg-1, range 4-325 mg P kg-1, IQR 36.4-108 mg P kg-1
- Sediment labile P (PAEM): median 32.6 mg P kg-1, mean 43.7 mg P kg-1, range 2.9-288 mg P kg-1, IQR 16.6-53.8 mg P kg-1
- Historical literature comparison ranges cited (not this project's own new data, used for context): 43-56 mg P kg-1 labile P (Sharpley et al. 1984); 26-90 mg P kg-1 (Wolf et al. 1985); up to 750 mg P kg-1 in manured soils
- Legacy P stock benchmark: 0.5 kg P ha-1 as the 75th percentile of field dissolved-P loss in the MANAGE reference dataset, used as the 'sustained for decades' threshold
- Vertical stratification ratios (0-5 cm : 5-15 cm): labile P 1.34-3.05x; Mehlich-3 P 1.15-2.44x (5 of 7 sites); Olsen P 1.14-1.71x (6 of 7 sites); total P 1.11-1.37x
- EPC0 prediction model (quantity-intensity-capacity GAM): 92.4% deviance explained; site-specific models penalized by delta-AIC = -235 relative to the pooled model
- Bache-Williams sorption Index (BWI): soils median 3.99 (mean 4.48, range 1.9-22.6); sediments median 6.71 (mean 9.58, range 1.35-38.8), units (mg P/100 g) per log(micromol P/L)
- Spearman correlation significance thresholds used: |rho| >= 0.373 for n=28, and |rho| >= 0.197 for n=100 (95% confidence level)
- Western Lake Erie Basin component project: 46 agricultural fields in its edge-of-field dataset; project duration March 1, 2020-September 30, 2025 (ARS project number 5020-12130-004-007-I)
- Underlying public dataset: 21 data/code resources (Excel 'tidy' dataset, 10 PNG figures, 9 R scripts, Bayesian model-fit archive, README) released via Ag Data Commons under CC-BY-4.0, DOI 10.15482/USDA.ADC/25892602.v1; stated temporal coverage March 1, 2022-August 1, 2022; spatial extent 42.53N, 114.36W (western Idaho) to 44.47N, 73.16W (northern Vermont)
- Flagship publication: Journal of Environmental Quality, volume 54, issue 4, pages 851-869; DOI 10.1002/jeq2.20632; published online September 29, 2024

## Methods
Field sampling: paired soil cores (0-5 cm and 5-15 cm depths, composited from >=5 cores per location) and nearby stream/ditch sediment grabs (top ~0-2 to ~0-5 cm, >5 locations per 5-10 m reach) collected in spring 2022 across 7 CEAP watershed sites; samples sieved (<2 mm, or <4 mm for some high-clay soils) and both air-dried and frozen (-20C) subsamples retained. Laboratory assays: labile P via 24-h anion-exchange-membrane desorption (PAEM) with ICP-OES; equilibrium phosphate concentration at net-zero sorption (EPC0) and Bache-Williams sorption Index (BWI) via batch sorption isotherms (1:20 soil:solution, 16-h equilibration, ICP-MS/ICP-OES); Olsen P, Mehlich-3, ammonium-oxalate (Pox/Feox/Alox), and bicarbonate-dithionite (PBD/FeBD) extractions; total P/Fe/Al/Ca via EPA 3050B digestion; pH, particle size (laser diffraction), and total C/N (combustion). Statistical modeling in R (v4.3.2, packages including mgcv and emmeans): Generalized Additive Models (gamma-distributed, log link) for EPC0 as a smoothed function of labile P and buffer/capacity variables ('quantity-intensity-capacity' framework); multilevel/partially-pooled models for site-level EPC0 estimation; Bayesian model fits (noted in the associated public dataset); Spearman rank correlations for bivariate associations; AIC-based model selection comparing pooled vs. site-specific models. A related component (Western Lake Erie Basin, USDA-ARS project 5020-12130-004-007-I) adds an edge-of-field monitoring dataset (46 fields), laboratory flow-through desorption experiments on soils and ditch sediments, constructed field P 'hotspot' trials, and phosphate stable-isotope analysis to separate legacy from recently-applied P sources. Program-wide, edge-of-field monitoring is coordinated by NRCS while in-stream/watershed-outlet (HUC12) monitoring is coordinated by other program partners.

## Stated limitations
Authors state: (1) the sampling design did not stratify across land-use and tillage factors, limiting inference about specific management effects on P lability; (2) time since last P application was only broadly classified (>1 year, often >3 years, with one Snake River exception at ~0.5 year), assumed to have negligible effect on labile P without site-by-site verification; (3) labile-P stock calculations assumed a fixed bulk density (1.2 g/cm3, true range ~0.9-1.5) and a conservative 0-1 cm interaction depth, both approximations rather than direct measurements; (4) the PAEM (anion-exchange-membrane) method is not commonly used in prior literature, limiting direct comparability to older water-extractable-P studies; (5) 30 Snake River soils were excluded from certain redox-sensitive analyses on site-specific grounds; (6) legacy-P-stock contextualization relies on the MANAGE field-runoff database, which may not represent all transport pathways or seasonal variability; (7) alternative predictor combinations tested in the EPC0 model (pH, clay, TOC, FeBD:PBD) were found 'unnecessary' or confounded, so the quantity-intensity-capacity model may not capture every mechanistic driver; (8) authors explicitly call for future work to expand from P 'quantity' to also measuring P 'intensity,' noting robust intensity measurements 'have not always been available in past work'; (9) observed relationships between management history and P properties were 'varied and highly site-specific,' requiring further site-level study before generalizing conservation-practice recommendations.

## Tensions with other findings
This project's central finding -- that legacy (already-accumulated) labile P stocks alone can sustain high dissolved P losses for decades -- complicates any HAB-management narrative that assumes cutting current-year external nutrient inputs (fertilizer, manure) will produce a fast water-quality or bloom response; it implies a lag/hysteresis between load-reduction actions and observed in-lake improvement that should be considered when interpreting other HAB studies that model bloom risk mainly from current-season loading or weather drivers without an internal/legacy-P term. Also, the associations reported between soil/sediment chemical properties (BWI, degree of P saturation, organic-carbon-to-sesquioxide ratios) and EPC0/lability are drawn from cross-sectional lab assays and correlational statistics (Spearman rho, GAM smooths); the authors themselves do not claim these demonstrate causal, field-scale P-loss mechanisms, and explicitly caveat that management-related differences were site-specific and not statistically confirmed due to the sampling design -- a caution this dossier preserves rather than overstating as an established management lever.

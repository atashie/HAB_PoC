# Toxins and Harmful Algal Blooms Science Team | U.S. Geological Survey

**What it is.** A USGS Environmental Health Program science-team page describing the Toxins and Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) Science Team, whose mission is to "develop advanced methods to study factors driving algal toxin production, how and where wildlife or humans are exposed to toxins, and ecotoxicology" so as to build decision tools for assessing whether an exposure causes adverse health effects. The page aggregates the team's research rationale, highlighted findings (2013–2019), laboratory/facility info, sample data-release descriptions, partner centers, and named contacts, rather than presenting a single study.

## Methods
The page describes (as a portfolio overview, not a single reproducible protocol) a range of methods used across the team's projects: analytical chemistry via Liquid Chromatography Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometry (LC/MS/MS), both targeted and non-targeted, for toxin quantitation; a validated method for simultaneously measuring multiple classes of cyanotoxins/algal toxins across the freshwater-to-marine continuum; satellite/remote-sensing-based bloom detection via the Cyanobacteria Assessment Network (CyAN) for lakes and reservoirs; bioassays (protein phosphatase 2A inhibition, estrogenicity screening) for toxin bioactivity; dose-dependent animal toxicity studies referenced against health-advisory thresholds; field sampling (caged mussel samplers, phytoplankton identification/enumeration, water-quality parameter collection); polyphasic taxonomic identification of toxin-producing cyanobacteria; and evaluation of commercial drinking-water purifiers for toxin removal. Where the page assesses success or failure of a method/finding: the Lake Erie mussel study found no detectable mortality association from cyanotoxins at the ambient concentrations observed, but did find a sublethal growth effect correlated with microcystin concentration; the Kabetogama Lake work found toxin/toxin-gene presence was not restricted to the visible-bloom period, implying a monitoring approach triggered only by visible/satellite bloom detection would miss some toxin-positive periods; and the BMAA-neurodegenerative-disease hypothesis was judged, per a review cited on the page, to lack support from existing data.

## Stated limitations
The page itself states: (1) a single detection metric (e.g., a satellite-derived bloom-extent measure) cannot adequately represent bloom severity in terms of toxicity; (2) the Kabetogama Lake finding shows that monitoring/sampling only when a bloom is visible would miss toxin-producing cyanobacteria, toxin-synthesis genes, or cyanotoxins present before or after the visible bloom; (3) data-release documentation notes reported values are raw measurements, not rounded to USGS significant-figure conventions — a precision/reproducibility caveat for downstream users; (4) the team frames public/regulatory water-access restrictions as 'often based largely on a perception of potential risk,' implying current risk-management practice is not always grounded in measured exposure or toxicity data.

## Tensions with other findings
(a) The page's own headline ('...One-Third of Streams...') and body text ('39 percent') are not numerically consistent — 39% is closer to two-fifths than one-third — illustrating how public-facing headlines can round or simplify the underlying statistic. (b) The Kabetogama Lake finding that toxin-producing cyanobacteria and cyanotoxins can occur before and after a visible bloom directly complicates the premise — used by satellite/remote-sensing bloom-detection approaches such as CyAN, which this same page discusses — that a visible or spectral bloom signal is a reliable proxy for the timing of toxin risk; this is a relevant caution for any HAB early-warning approach built primarily on remote-sensed bloom extent. (c) The Lake Erie finding of no mortality association between cyanotoxins and mussels at ambient concentrations (only a sublethal growth effect) could be read as in tension with HAB literature that treats microcystin exposure as more acutely toxic to aquatic fauna; here the source frames toxicity as concentration- and endpoint-dependent rather than uniformly lethal. (d) The statement that an 'objective review concluded that this hypothesis is not supported by existing data' regarding BMAA and ALS/Parkinsonism runs counter to other researchers/publications elsewhere in the HAB field who have argued for a BMAA–neurodegenerative-disease link, marking this as an area of active scientific disagreement rather than settled consensus.

# Future Projections of Water Temperature and Thermal Stratification in Connecticut Reservoirs and Possible Implications for Cyanobacteria

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed climate-impacts study (Water Resources Research, AGU, 2020) that builds lake-specific empirical models from historical monitoring data in six Connecticut, U.S.A. drinking-water reservoirs to project mid-century (2041-2070) changes in water temperature and thermal stratification under an RCP8.5 warming scenario, and examines how historical thermal/stratification conditions correlate with occurrence of high-risk cyanobacteria blooms.

## Methods
Historical (1971-2000) in-situ monitoring records of air temperature, water temperature, thermal stratification, and cyanobacteria cell counts from six Connecticut, U.S.A. drinking-water reservoirs were used to build lake-specific empirical (statistical) models linking air temperature to water temperature and to a stratification-strength metric called 'Relative Thermal Resistance to Mixing' (RTRM). These empirical models were then driven with downscaled air-temperature projections from three CMIP5 general circulation models (HadGEM2-CC365, CCSM4, GFDL-ESM2M) under RCP8.5 to project mid-century (2041-2070) water temperature and stratification relative to the historical baseline. Historical cyanobacteria bloom occurrence (using a >70,000 cells/mL 'high-risk' threshold) in three of the six reservoirs was related, in explicitly correlational terms, to surface/bottom temperature and RTRM conditions. The abstract text retrieved does not specify the exact regression/statistical technique, model calibration/validation statistics (R2, RMSE, cross-validation), or per-reservoir breakdowns -- only the study-wide design and headline projected values were accessible.

## Stated limitations
Only the abstract could be retrieved (see fetch_notes), so no explicit "Limitations" or "Discussion" passage from the authors could be quoted here -- this is a gap in access, not a claim that the paper lacks such a discussion. Within the abstract itself, the authors consistently hedge: bloom-temperature relationships are described as "correlated with" rather than causal, and future bloom frequency is framed as "may occur more often" rather than a certainty. No calibration/validation statistics (R2, RMSE, p-values), confidence intervals (beyond the single "48 ± 8 days" figure), or explicit uncertainty ranges around the °C/decade warming rates were present in the retrieved text. Only one emissions scenario (RCP8.5) is mentioned, and only one future period (mid-century, 2041-2070); no comparison to a lower-emissions scenario (e.g., RCP4.5) or an end-century horizon appears in the retrieved text.

## Tensions with other findings
Not assessed against other sources in this single-source task. Internally worth flagging: the historical bloom correlate combines warm surface water AND cool bottom water AND high RTRM -- i.e., a strong top-to-bottom thermal gradient (strong, stable stratification), not simply "warmer water" in isolation. This nuances any oversimplified "warmer lakes bloom more" framing by pointing to stratification strength/stability specifically as the correlate the authors report, broadly consistent with (though not verified here against) the wider literature linking stratification to cyanobacterial buoyancy advantage. As the authors themselves state it, this remains a correlational finding, not a demonstrated causal mechanism, in the text retrieved.

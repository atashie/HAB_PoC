# Stopping Algae Blooms With Bacteria-Busting Buoys

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.acs.org/pressroom/presspacs/2026/march/stopping-algae-blooms-with-bacteria-busting-buoys.html

## Methods
Bench-scale engineering test of a passive, slow-release algaecide-delivery device (not a statistical/predictive model). Small/medium/large buoys were built from PVC pipe in a "T" or cross shape; hydrogel disks inserted in the pipe openings meter the diffusion of a commercial hydrogen-peroxide-based algaecide (supplied by SePRO Corporation, per Mirage News/Water Online) into surrounding water. Buoys were immersed in roughly 1 liter of real cyanobacteria-containing Lake Erie water in the lab, with the water replaced daily using fresh lake water to simulate continuous cyanobacteria exposure, and monitored over about two weeks; buoys were also tracked through repeated (at least four consecutive) ~35-day release cycles before depletion, visually signaled when the buoy tips onto its side. The University of Toledo's own news article additionally describes a pond-scale experiment under ambient/real weather conditions (scale, replicate count, and outcome metrics not given in any press material accessed) and states a seasonal field deployment with northwest Ohio water-treatment operators is planned. The press materials report the method "worked" in that cyanobacteria were "almost entirely eliminated within a week, and other microbes remained largely unscathed" in these small-scale tests — i.e., an apparently selective effect on the target organism — but do not report the method being tested at whole-lake scale, and both named researchers hedge the practical payoff on future successful scale-up ("if successfully scaled up, this concept could enable early mitigation…").

## Stated limitations
The source explicitly states that "further research is needed, including enhancements to prevent microbe growth on the buoy's surface," and frames the technology's practical benefit as conditional on it being "successfully scaled up" (Lapitsky quote, hedged with "if"). University of Toledo News separately frames the work as an "early prototype stage" and reports researchers proposing that larger buoys would be needed to cut the number of units required for large water bodies from "hundreds" down to "a dozen or so," implying the tested small/medium/large units are not yet sized for whole-lake deployment. None of the press materials accessed report replicate counts, statistical tests, confidence intervals, untreated-control comparisons, or exact cyanobacteria cell-density/toxin measurements; this appears to be a gap in press-level reporting rather than a limitation the source explicitly flags, and could not be checked against the peer-reviewed paper itself, whose DOI landing page (pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsestwater.5c01257) returned HTTP 403 when fetched.

## Tensions with other findings
This is a small-scale (beaker/pond) engineering-intervention study, not a detection, forecasting, or driver-analysis study, so it does not directly bear on remote-sensing/in-situ HAB-prediction findings elsewhere in the literature; it is complementary (post-detection treatment) rather than contradictory to them. One general tension worth flagging for the wider review: the efficacy claim ("almost entirely eliminated within a week") comes from a controlled regime with a small, replenished cyanobacteria supply and a fixed algaecide-release rate in limited volume; open lake systems introduce mixing, dilution, wind/current transport of algaecide away from the buoy, and photodegradation of hydrogen peroxide, all of which commonly reduce effective contact dose relative to bench tests — a dynamic well known in the algaecide literature generally. The source itself gestures at this gap by hedging its central claim on successful scale-up and by describing the work as an "early prototype," but does not quantify how efficacy is expected to change at scale. This is my own inference about a well-known bench-vs-field efficacy gap, not a specific contradiction documented in another named source within this task.

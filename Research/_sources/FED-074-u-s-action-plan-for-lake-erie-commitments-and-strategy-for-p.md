# U.S. Action Plan for Lake Erie: Commitments and Strategy for Phosphorus Reduction (August 2017 Draft)

**What it is.** A 2017 draft joint U.S. federal-state action plan (EPA's Great Lakes National Program Office with five Great Lakes states and multiple federal agencies) presenting phosphorus load-reduction commitments and management strategies for Lake Erie. It documents state- and federal-led actions implementing the binational nutrient-loading targets adopted in 2016 under the U.S.-Canada Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement (GLWQA) Annex 4, aimed at reducing harmful algal blooms (HABs) and hypoxia.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Lake Erie HABs pose acute drinking-water and public-health risk, illustrated by the August 2014 Toledo, Ohio water crisis and by extreme microcystin levels during the 2011 bloom relative to WHO safety limits.
  - *evidence:* Source states elevated algal toxins forced closure of Toledo's drinking-water treatment plant in Aug 2014, and that 2011 bloom microcystin measurements far exceeded WHO limits for body contact and drinking water. (Background – Problem and drivers, p.3)
  - *quote:* "measurements of microcystin during the 2011 bloom were 50 times higher than the World Health Organization limit for safe body contact, and 1,200 times higher than the limit for safe drinking water."
- **[✓ verified]** Binational GLWQA Annex 4 phosphorus targets (adopted Feb. 2016, 2008 baseline) call for a 40% cut in total phosphorus loading to the western and central basins, equating to an annual central-basin load target of 6,000 metric tons, split as a 3,316-metric-ton reduction from the U.S. and a 212-metric-ton reduction from Canada.
  - *evidence:* Direct statement of the negotiated binational load target and each country's required reduction, framed as the mechanism to minimize central-basin hypoxic zones. (Background – GLWQA Commitments, p.6–7)
  - *quote:* "a 40 percent reduction in total phosphorus entering the western and central basins of Lake Erie...to achieve an annual load of 6,000 metric tons to the central basin. This amounts to a reduction from the United States and Canada of 3,316 metric tons and 212 metric tons respectively."
- **[✓ verified]** A stricter, separate target applies to the Maumee River specifically: a 40% cut in spring (March–July) total and dissolved reactive phosphorus (DRP), equal to a 2008-baseline spring load of 860 metric tons TP and 186 metric tons DRP, intended to keep western-basin cyanobacteria below toxin-threat levels.
  - *evidence:* This target is distinct from the broader nearshore-algae target (which covers more tributaries) and is tied specifically to the GLWQA objective of limiting cyanobacterial toxin production in the western basin. (Background – GLWQA Commitments, p.7)
  - *quote:* "this equates to a spring (March-July) load of 860 metric tons TP and 186 metric tons DRP."
- **[✓ verified]** Lake Erie's three basins differ sharply in depth and phosphorus loading share, which the plan links to different bloom types: the shallow Western Basin gets ~61% of the lake's total phosphorus load and hosts toxin-producing Microcystis aeruginosa; the Central Basin gets 28% and experiences bottom-water hypoxia; the deep Eastern Basin gets only 11% but supports nuisance benthic Cladophora growth.
  - *evidence:* Basin-by-basin morphometry (depth) and percent-of-total-load figures are stated directly, with each basin's characteristic algal problem described qualitatively (not statistically linked to the load percentage). (Background – Lake Erie's 3 basins, p.4)
  - *quote:* "The Western Basin receives about 61 percent of the whole lake annual total phosphorus load, while the Central Basin and Eastern Basin receive 28 percent and 11 percent, respectively."
- **[✓ verified]** Central-basin hypoxia has grown since the early 2000s, averaging about 4,500 km² with a peak of 8,800 km² in 2012 (the year after the record 2011 bloom), and a 2012 low-oxygen event coincided with a large fish kill along 40 km of Ontario shoreline.
  - *evidence:* The source reports the 2012 hypoxic peak as occurring 'subsequent to' the 2011 record bloom and pairs it with a fish-kill anecdote; this is a temporal association presented by the source, not a statistically tested causal link. (Background – Problem and drivers, p.3)
  - *quote:* "the hypoxic (low-oxygen) area in the Central Basin of Lake Erie has increased to about 4,500 km2, on average, with the largest hypoxic event of 8,800 km2 occurring in 2012, subsequent to the record setting algal bloom in 2011."
- **[✓ verified]** The plan's strategy targets phosphorus specifically (treated as the growth-limiting nutrient) and explicitly flags, via a cited study, that phosphorus reduction alone may not reduce bloom toxicity even if it shrinks bloom extent.
  - *evidence:* The plan cites Gobler et al. (2016, Harmful Algae 54:87-97, footnote 1) as the basis for this caveat, and states its own strategy 'assumes' P reduction will co-reduce N, with EPA's Science Advisory Board recommending further research on nitrogen's role in toxin production — an acknowledged evidentiary gap in the plan's own approach. (Background – The Need to Control Phosphorus, p.6 (text and footnote 1))
  - *quote:* "phosphorus reduction in the absence of nitrogen reduction would not reduce the toxicity of algal blooms"
- **[✓ verified]** Whether phosphorus-load reductions are warranted in Lake Erie's Eastern Basin (where the main nuisance is benthic Cladophora rather than planktonic cyanobacteria) is explicitly unresolved; the U.S. and Canada deferred setting Eastern Basin targets and committed only to re-evaluate the question in 2020.
  - *evidence:* Direct statement of scientific uncertainty about extending the western/central-basin phosphorus-loading paradigm to the Eastern Basin, with an explicit adaptive-management deferral rather than a target being set now. (Background – GLWQA Commitments, p.7)
  - *quote:* "the science remains unclear whether reductions in phosphorus loading from sources in Lake Erie's Eastern basin are warranted."
- **[✓ verified]** The plan attributes the post-1990s bloom resurgence to several concurrent, non-quantified factors: a shift toward more bioavailable dissolved (vs. particulate) phosphorus, invasive zebra/quagga mussels recycling nutrients nearshore, greater water clarity extending algal growth to depth, wetland/riparian loss, warming (longer growing seasons), and more intense spring storms delivering nutrients at critical times.
  - *evidence:* These are presented as a descriptive list of contributing factors without any statistical apportionment of relative contribution — an associative/qualitative attribution, not a quantified driver model. (Background – The Need to Control Phosphorus, p.5–6)
  - *quote:* "Dissolved phosphorus is more easily taken up by algae and contributes to increased algal growth."
- **[✓ verified]** The plan's practical management levers are organized around a 10-point joint action list (Great Lakes Commission LENT working group, endorsed 2015), spanning agricultural nutrient-application restrictions, 4R nutrient stewardship certification, municipal discharger phosphorus cuts, green infrastructure, a 5-year phase-out of residential phosphorus fertilizer, and an explicit adaptive-management commitment to revisit the targets themselves.
  - *evidence:* This 10-item list is presented as the concrete strategy the states/federal agencies are executing against, distinct from the numeric load targets themselves. (Introduction, p.2)
  - *quote:* "Validate or refine the reduction targets and timelines using an adaptive approach"

## Data / numbers

- $12.9 billion Lake Erie tourism industry (Purpose, p.i)
- Lake Erie basin spans 5 U.S. states + Ontario, >10,000 square miles of farmland (Introduction, p.1)
- Basin population stated as >10 million (Introduction, p.1) and, elsewhere in the same document, >11 million ('most populated of the Great Lakes', Need to Control Phosphorus, p.5) — two different figures given for basin population
- 2011 bloom: microcystin measured 50x the WHO safe body-contact limit and 1,200x the WHO safe drinking-water limit (p.3)
- Aug 2014 Toledo, OH: >500,000 people without drinking water for 3 days after treatment-plant closure (p.3)
- Central Basin hypoxic ('dead zone') area: ~4,500 km2 average since early 2000s; peak of 8,800 km2 in 2012 (p.3)
- 2012 hypoxia-associated fish kill: 'tens of thousands' of dead fish along a 40 km stretch of Ontario shoreline (p.3)
- Western Basin: avg depth 7.4 m (24 ft), max depth 19 m (62 ft); ~61% of lake total-phosphorus (TP) load (p.4)
- Central Basin: avg depth 18.3 m (60 ft), max depth 25 m (82 ft); 28% of lake TP load (p.4-5)
- Eastern Basin: avg depth 24 m (80 ft), max depth 64 m (210 ft); 11% of lake TP load (p.5)
- Target (2008 baseline, adopted Feb 2016): 40% reduction in total P to western+central basins -> annual central-basin load target of 6,000 metric tons; US reduction -3,316 metric tons; Canada reduction -212 metric tons (p.6-7)
- Target (2008 baseline): 40% reduction in spring TP/DRP from priority tributaries (Canada: Thames River, Leamington; US: Maumee, River Raisin, Portage River, Toussaint Creek, Sandusky River, Huron River-OH) (p.7)
- Maumee River spring (Mar-Jul) target load (2008 baseline): 860 metric tons TP; 186 metric tons DRP (p.7)
- Western Basin of Lake Erie Collaborative Agreement (signed June 13, 2015, OH+MI governors and Ontario premier): goal of 40% phosphorus-load reduction by 2025 (p.2)
- Eastern Basin phosphorus-target decision deferred to a 2020 re-evaluation (p.7)

## Methods
Not a research study; this is a federal-state policy/commitment document. Its stated evidentiary basis includes: (1) binational lake-wide monitoring cruises by Environment and Climate Change Canada and U.S. EPA measuring total phosphorus concentrations across basins; (2) a 'robust science-based process and binational public consultation,' plus referenced (but not detailed in the extracted text) technical reports — an 'Annex 4 Multi Modeling Report' and an 'Annex 4 Objectives and Targets Task Team Final Report' — used to derive the 2016 binational phosphorus targets from a 2008 baseline; (3) an adaptive-management commitment to revisit Eastern Basin targets in 2020 pending further research, and to 'validate or refine the reduction targets and timelines using an adaptive approach' more generally. The plan explicitly names where its own approach may not hold: a phosphorus-only reduction strategy is stated (citing Gobler et al. 2016) as not necessarily reducing bloom toxicity absent paired nitrogen reduction, and the applicability of the western/central-basin phosphorus-loading framework to the Eastern Basin's Cladophora problem is stated as scientifically unresolved.

## Stated limitations
The source itself states: (1) 'the science remains unclear whether reductions in phosphorus loading from sources in Lake Erie's Eastern basin are warranted,' deferring any Eastern Basin target decision to a 2020 re-evaluation. (2) The plan's phosphorus-focused strategy 'assumes that management actions to reduce phosphorus will also reduce nitrogen' — an explicitly stated assumption, not a demonstrated result — and notes EPA's Science Advisory Board 'recommended a number of research questions to focus further investigations on the role of nitrogen in toxin production,' i.e., the nitrogen-toxicity relationship was not yet resolved at time of writing. (3) The plan notes 'the total annual amount of phosphorus entering the Lake varies significantly each year due to the corresponding variability in nonpoint runoff,' meaning year-to-year load figures are noisy relative to the fixed reduction targets. (4) This is an August 2017 'Draft' explicitly intended 'for public engagement'; it is a high-level coordinating/commitment document, with 'more detailed implementation plans...being developed at the state-level' separately, and it presents forward-looking commitments rather than an evaluation of realized outcomes. (5) Minor internal inconsistency: the document gives two different Lake Erie basin population figures (>10 million vs. >11 million) in different sections, so population statistics should be used cautiously.

## Tensions with other findings
The plan's own footnoted caveat (citing Gobler et al. 2016, Harmful Algae 54:87-97) that phosphorus reduction without nitrogen reduction would not reduce bloom toxicity (only spatial extent) complicates any simple 'reduce P -> reduced HAB risk/toxicity' claim — it is a source-internal tension between the plan's operational P-only focus and cited co-limitation research, relevant to any driver/treatment claim about nutrient management and toxin production. Second, the plan explicitly leaves unresolved whether the western/central-basin phosphorus-loading paradigm (built around planktonic Microcystis blooms) extends to the Eastern Basin's benthic Cladophora problem, a different organism and depth regime, which complicates generalizing 'phosphorus load reduction reduces HABs' across basin types within the same lake. Third, this document is a nutrient-load management/policy instrument, methodologically distinct from remote-sensing- or statistical/ML-based HAB detection and forecasting approaches (e.g., EPA CyAN); it supplies real-world stakes, targets, and management framing rather than predictive-model methods, data, or validation metrics, so it should be read as complementary background rather than a source of modeling technique for a satellite+in-situ HAB prediction tool.

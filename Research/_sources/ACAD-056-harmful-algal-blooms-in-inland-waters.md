# Harmful algal blooms in inland waters

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.nature.com/articles/s43017-024-00578-2

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed synthesis review (Nature Reviews Earth &amp; Environment, 2024) by Feng, Wang, Hou, Qin, Kutser, Qu, Chen, Paerl and Zheng that compiles global evidence on harmful algal bloom (HAB) occurrence trends, drivers (nutrients, climate/temperature, hydrology), satellite/in-situ monitoring capabilities and limitations, and mitigation/management strategies in inland (freshwater) lakes.

## Data / numbers
- 44% increase in global HAB (bloom) frequency, 2000s to 2010s (point estimate; no confidence interval given in text)
- HABs occurred in 11.7% of global lake area, 1982-2019 (satellite-derived, Landsat/MODIS-based dataset per cited ref. 1)
- 8.8% of the 248,243 lakes larger than 0.1 km2 globally affected by HABs
- 63% of lakes larger than 25 km2 classified as eutrophic
- 14.3% of South American lakes larger than 0.1 km2 affected by HABs
- Lake Taihu bloom area expanded from <200 km2 to ~400 km2 within 4-5 days after tropical cyclones
- Lake Taihu management target: total nitrogen <=0.80 mg/L, total phosphorus <=0.05 mg/L
- Lake Winnipeg (north basin) water-quality target: total nitrogen <=0.70 mg/L
- US annual economic losses from CyanoHABs: ~$2 billion/year (point estimate, no uncertainty range stated)
- Lake Erie western basin: ~50% reduction in annual total phosphorus input, 1974 to late 1980s, following a 1972 US-Canada agreement
- US National Lakes Assessment: nitrogen elevated in ~35% of lakes; phosphorus elevated in ~40% of lakes
- Brazil legal drinking-water microcystin limit: 10 micrograms/L (legislated 2004)
- South Africa: cyanobacterial blooms recorded in 23 of the 50 largest reservoirs, 2002-2012
- First documented toxic algal bloom worldwide: Lake Alexandrina, Australia, 1878
- Landsat satellite revisit interval: 16 days (plus cloud-cover interference)
- Lake Taihu, 10-minute-resolution remote sensing: bloom extent varies by up to one order of magnitude within a single day
- ~65% of HAB literature concentrated on just 20 inland lakes worldwide
- Cyanobacteria maximum growth rate typically above 25 degrees C
- Projected US lakes HAB days: 7 days (2024 baseline) to 18-39 days by 2090 (explicitly flagged as highly uncertain)
- Projected phytoplankton increase: +111% on average across 29 lakes in Europe and North America by mid-21st century
- Project Clean Lake (Cleveland/Lake Erie): 25-year initiative launched 2011; target to cut raw sewage discharge into Lake Erie by 4.0 billion gallons/year by 2036
- 103 Chinese bloom-affected lakes assessed: 95 with increased bloom occurrence, 79 with earlier seasonal onset, 97 with prolonged bloom periods (exact assessment window not given as a direct quote in the extracted text)
- 21 African countries showed a satellite-detected surge in bloom frequency between the 2000s and 2010s (magnitude not quantified as a percentage in the text)

## Methods
This is a narrative synthesis/review, not primary data collection: it compiles and interprets findings from prior peer-reviewed studies, national assessments, and existing satellite/in-situ datasets rather than generating new field or lab measurements. Its central global-trend statistics (11.7% of global lake area affected, 1982-2019; the 44% frequency increase from the 2000s to 2010s) are attributed to an external dataset/study (ref. 1, cited as Hou et al. 2022, Nature Geoscience) built from Landsat imagery (Fig. 2a-c) and MODIS imagery (Fig. 2d); the review does not reproduce that detection algorithm itself. Lake-specific findings (Erie, Taihu, Balaton, Winnipeg, Victoria, Naivasha, Caspian Sea, Baikal, Three Gorges Reservoir, South African and Chinese lake sets, etc.) are drawn from secondary literature and national programs (e.g., the US National Lakes Assessment) rather than new work by these authors. The review states satellite remote sensing (Landsat, MODIS, GOCI) "enabled global-scale observations" of bloom occurrence across decades, but that these platforms trade temporal frequency against spatial resolution/coverage and are cloud-limited (Landsat's 16-day revisit cited explicitly); it cites emerging high-frequency (10-minute) remote sensing and drone-mounted hyperspectral imaging as methods that reveal sub-daily/fine-scale bloom variability that coarser satellite products miss. For future-trend content it cites external process-based/mechanistic and statistical projection studies (e.g., the US "7 to 18-39 days by 2090" and "+111% phytoplankton" figures) without re-deriving them, and explicitly notes such models "still have shortcomings in several areas."

## Stated limitations
The authors state: (1) a strong geographic/data-coverage bias in HAB literature toward high-income-country lakes, with "approximately 65% of the relevant literature" concentrated on "just 20 inland lakes," leaving many African, South/Southeast Asian (Indonesia, Pakistan, Philippines named) and South American lakes understudied despite known or suspected HAB prevalence there; (2) satellite-monitoring constraints - Landsat's 16-day revisit interval plus cloud interference, and MODIS/GOCI's coarser spatial resolution or limited coverage despite higher frequency - that limit capture of short-term (sub-daily to daily) bloom dynamics; (3) a near-universal focus on surface water in both field and satellite monitoring, leaving "limited understanding of the vertical distributions of HABs" and risking false "bloom-free" assessments; (4) explicit acknowledgment that "it remains unclear whether climate warming or nutrient enrichment is the dominant factor" behind the global HAB increase; (5) nutrient thresholds for bloom formation "vary greatly between different water bodies and between different seasons and times," so universal targets are not proposed; (6) "substantial uncertainties" attached to future projections of HAB occurrence/biomass under climate change, with process-based mechanistic models described as having "shortcomings in several areas," species-specific climate-response quantification "remain[ing] limited," and laboratory findings not always transferring to field conditions; and (7) headline figures such as the 44% global increase and the ~$2 billion/year US economic-loss estimate are reported as point values without confidence intervals or formal uncertainty quantification in the review text itself.

## Tensions with other findings
This review complicates a simple "reduce nutrient loading and bloom risk is resolved" narrative: it documents blooms resurging or occurring (Lake Erie since the 1990s; Lake Balaton's 2019 event) despite historical or continued external nutrient-load reductions, attributing this instead to climate warming, thermal stratification, and internal/legacy phosphorus loading - then explicitly states the field cannot yet say whether climate or nutrients dominates the global increase. That is a direct caution for any project step that attributes bloom risk to a single "key driver": the source treats that attribution as genuinely unsettled, not solved. It also both supports and complicates this project's premise of fusing remote-sensing with in-situ data: the review argues satellite-only monitoring is temporally/spatially limited (Landsat's 16-day revisit, cloud cover, MODIS/GOCI resolution-vs-frequency trade-offs) and misses sub-daily variability and almost all subsurface structure - but it does not show in-situ data closes that gap either, since it states field measurements are "also" overwhelmingly surface-focused. Finally, its finding of severe geographic/data imbalance in the underlying literature (65% of studies on just 20 lakes, concentrated in high-income countries) cautions against assuming a model validated on well-monitored lakes (e.g., US EPA CyAN-covered lakes) will generalize to less-studied water bodies.

# PRISM High-Resolution Spatial Climate Data for the United States (max/min temp, dewpoint, precipitation)

**What it is.** A data-guide article (NCAR/UCAR Climate Data Guide) describing PRISM, a set of gridded (statistically interpolated, not raw point) monthly, yearly, and daily climate data products -- mean/max/min temperature, precipitation, and dewpoint -- for the conterminous United States, produced by the PRISM Climate Group from station observations (plus radar for precipitation) using a terrain-aware statistical mapping system.

## Methods
Per the fetched page, PRISM is a statistical-physiographic interpolation system (not a physically-based weather/climate model and not a direct satellite/remote-sensing retrieval). For each grid cell it draws on station observations (15-25 nearby stations per the summarized pass, not re-confirmed verbatim) and weights them by physiographic similarity to the target cell -- distance, elevation, coastal proximity, topographic aspect/facet, valley-vs-exposed position, and boundary-layer-vs-free-atmosphere status -- combined with a comparison of each station's long-term normal to the target cell's monthly normal ('climatologically-aided interpolation', CAI), then a regression relating stations' monthly normals to observed daily values. For precipitation specifically, the source states PRISM evaluates 'effective terrain height' rather than topographic position and, east of the Rockies since 2002, blends the CAI/station-based estimate with NEXRAD-derived Stage II/Stage IV AHPS radar-gauge analyses via a stated 'besting process' where radar information is strongest. Quality control described (in the summarized pass) includes network-level equipment screening, single-station range/completeness checks, radar QC for zeros/spikes, spatial QC for outlier stations, and monthly manual visual review -- this QC list was not independently re-confirmed in the verbatim follow-up fetch. The source states the approach 'performs well in regions of complex terrain, for example capturing temperature-height profiles associated with valley-floor inversions,' and separately notes (in the summarized pass, lower confidence) that 'increased terrain complexity and sparser data availability contribute to higher uncertainty in the West.'

## Stated limitations
The source itself states: (1) "Data are not homogenized, so should not be used for century-long trend analysis" -- a direct warning against using PRISM for long-term trend detection (relevant if pairing with multi-decade HAB trend data). (2) Monthly and daily temperature grids can disagree because they are built from different station subsets (all stations for monthlies vs. only "PRISM Day" morning/hourly stations for dailies), so the two products are not perfectly internally consistent. (3) The finest-resolution (800 m) timeseries data now require a fee and are no longer distributed in netCDF, limiting free/open reproducibility at that resolution (only the 4-km grids and the 800-m 30-year normals remain free). (4) Per the less-verbatim first-pass summary (lower confidence, not re-confirmed word-for-word): increased terrain complexity and sparser station density are stated to contribute to higher uncertainty in the western US, consistent with the quantified east/west error gaps (5% vs 10% precipitation MAE; 0.5degC vs 1.0degC temperature error) and with a stated cool bias in minimum temperature at steep-slope validation sites.

## Tensions with other findings
This source is a generic US gridded weather/climate data-product description and contains no mention of algal blooms, cyanobacteria, chlorophyll-a, nutrients, or aquatic ecology anywhere in the fetched text -- any HAB linkage is inferential (temperature/precipitation/dewpoint as candidate drivers), not something the source itself claims. Two points are worth flagging for a HAB dossier that emphasizes leakage/overfitting rigor: (1) PRISM is itself an interpolated/modeled product built from station data (plus radar for precipitation), not raw in-situ measurement, so using it as a weather covariate in a HAB model imports PRISM's own stated uncertainty (higher in the West per the source, and not homogenized over time) as an additional error source that should be propagated rather than treated as ground truth. (2) The source's explicit caveat that PRISM "should not be used for century-long trend analysis" directly bears on any HAB analysis that wants to relate long-term climate trends to long-term bloom trends -- doing so with unhomogenized PRISM data could itself be a source of spurious trend correlation the CLAUDE.md fidelity rules would flag.

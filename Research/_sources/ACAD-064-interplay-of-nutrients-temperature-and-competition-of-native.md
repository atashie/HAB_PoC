# Interplay of Nutrients, Temperature, and Competition of Native and Alien Cyanobacteria Species Growth and Cyanotoxin Production in Temperate Lakes

**What it is.** A laboratory experimental study (Toxins, 2021) using eight cyanobacterial strains isolated from four Lithuanian lakes that ran two controlled experiments — a nutrient (phosphorus concentration × N:P ratio) manipulation measuring growth rate and cyanotoxin (microcystin, saxitoxin, non-ribosomal peptide) production, and a temperature (20°C vs 24°C) × nutrient interspecies-competition experiment pairing native (Planktothrix agardhii, Aphanizomenon gracile) against alien-to-Europe (Sphaerospermopsis aphanizomenoides, Chrysosporum bergii) strains — to assess how nutrients, warming, and species origin jointly shape growth, competitive outcome, and toxin production in temperate freshwater cyanobacteria.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Under nutrient manipulation, native Aphanizomenon gracile and alien Sphaerospermopsis aphanizomenoides grew fastest of the four tested strains, while native Planktothrix agardhii grew slowest (and most variably).
  - *evidence:* Reported mean±SD growth rates (day⁻¹) from Experiment I, consistent across all three extraction passes: A. gracile 0.43±0.08, S. aphanizomenoides 0.40±0.12, C. bergii 0.18±0.11, P. agardhii 0.14±0.25. (Abstract; Results – Experiment I growth rate)
  - *quote:* "The highest growth rate was determined for A. gracile (0.43 day−1) and S. aphanizomenoides (0.40 day−1) strains at all the tested nutrient concentrations"
- **[✓ verified]** P. agardhii's own maximum growth rate required both the highest phosphorus level tested and the highest N:P ratio tested.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as a result of the phosphorus × N:P factorial design (5 P levels × 3 N:P ratios). (Results – Experiment I growth rate)
  - *quote:* "maximum growth rate of P. agardhii was reached at the highest IP concentrations (0.51 mg P L⁻¹) and at N:P ratio of 30:1"
- **[✓ verified]** Phosphorus (IP) and nitrogen (IN) concentration were significant GLM factors for growth rate of P. agardhii, A. gracile, and S. aphanizomenoides, but not for C. bergii.
  - *evidence:* Table 1 GLM F-statistics: IP factor significant (p<0.001) for three species with large F-values (660.16, 183.78, 226.29) but non-significant for C. bergii; same pattern for IN factor. (Results, Table 1)

- **[✓ verified]** No statistically significant relationship was found between A. gracile's growth rate, phosphorus concentration, or N:P ratio and its saxitoxin (STX) concentration.
  - *evidence:* Explicit regression result reported by the authors despite STX varying nearly 30-fold (17.29–481.03 ng/g) across treatments, with the highest values occurring at the lowest phosphorus level. (Results – Experiment I cyanotoxins)

- **[✓ verified]** In the interspecies competition experiment, native A. gracile achieved substantially higher biomass than native P. agardhii when co-cultured under high-nutrient, warmed (24°C) conditions.

## Methods
Laboratory (non-field, non-remote-sensing) experimental study using 8 monoclonal cyanobacterial strains (native Planktothrix agardhii and Aphanizomenon gracile; alien-to-Europe Sphaerospermopsis aphanizomenoides and Chrysosporum bergii) isolated from four Lithuanian lakes (Širvys, Jieznas, Rėkyva, Gineitiškės). Experiment I (nutrient effects): strains pre-conditioned in N/P-free MWC medium, then grown in triplicate 100 mL cultures for a 12-day period across a factorial design of 5 inorganic-phosphorus (IP) levels (0.035–0.51 mg P L⁻¹, added as K₂HPO₄) crossed with 3 N:P atomic ratios (7:1, 16:1, 30:1, nitrogen added as NaNO₃); growth tracked via chlorophyll-a (AlgaeLabAnalyser) with growth rate µ derived from a log-based formula; cyanotoxins extracted (75% methanol for microcystins/non-ribosomal peptides; ammonium formate/acetonitrile buffer for saxitoxin) and quantified by LC-MS/MS. Experiment II (interspecies competition): a 2 (temperature: 20°C vs 24°C) × 2 (IP: 0.140 vs 0.51 mg P L⁻¹ at fixed N:P=30:1) × competitor-pairing design, sampled every 4th day, cells fixed in 4% formaldehyde and enumerated/measured via Nageotte chamber + light microscopy with geometric-shape biovolume formulas. Statistics: general linear models (GLM) tested effects of IP, IN, N:P ratio (Expt I) and IP&IN, temperature, and competitor species (Expt II) on growth rate/biomass, with Shapiro–Wilk normality checks and linear regression/correlation relating nutrient variables to toxin concentrations; analyses run in STATISTICA 6.0. The source states nutrient factors performed strongly/consistently as significant drivers for 3 of 4 strains, and that its own experimental design (single strains, single stressors at a time, only 2 temperatures) was insufficient to resolve some relationships (notably STX regulation and interspecies mechanism).

## Stated limitations
The authors themselves state: (1) tested P. agardhii strains produced insufficient biomass at lower phosphorus concentrations to reliably analyze secondary metabolites, preventing clear conclusions about the nutrient effect on microcystins/non-ribosomal peptides; (2) no significant relationship was found between A. gracile's growth rate, phosphorus, or N:P ratio and its saxitoxin concentration (p>0.05), and they note that "information on factors that regulate saxitoxin production in cyanobacteria is very limited"; (3) "competition mechanisms between the different bloom-forming cyanobacteria species are poorly understood," and "more studies should be conducted to disclose the mechanisms of the interactions among the studied species," including possible allelopathic/cytotoxic exudates from A. gracile that were hypothesized but not directly tested; (4) toxin gene expression may depend on "complex environmental conditions (e.g., irradiance) rather than nutrients alone," implying the single/paired-stressor lab design cannot fully capture field-relevant toxin regulation; (5) the authors call generally for "more complex and definitive suite experiments instead of the approaches focused on single stressors and individual species."

## Tensions with other findings
This is a controlled-laboratory, strain-level study on isolates from four Lithuanian lakes — not a field or remote-sensing-based observational study — so it characterizes physiological/competitive potential under simplified conditions (single/paired strains, only 2 temperatures, no natural mixed plankton assemblage, no irradiance/mixing variability) rather than realized whole-lake bloom dynamics; its geographic and methodological scope is distant from a U.S., satellite/in-situ-fusion HAB tool and should not be extrapolated to field bloom behavior without caveat. Substantively, several findings complicate simple "more nutrients = more toxic bloom" narratives common in HAB communication: (a) the toxin variant dmMC-RR was NEGATIVELY correlated with phosphorus (r=−0.86) in P. agardhii — i.e., toxin quota per unit biomass was higher at LOWER phosphorus even as total biomass/bloom intensity is understood to rise with nutrients elsewhere in the literature, meaning nutrient reduction strategies that shrink bloom biomass will not necessarily lower per-cell toxin quota, and total-toxin-load outcomes depend on which effect dominates; (b) a nontoxic (non-microcystin-producing) P. agardhii strain had higher concentrations of other bioactive non-ribosomal peptides than the microcystin-producing strain, implying that microcystin-only toxin screening (the most common field/monitoring proxy for cyanotoxin risk) could understate a strain's overall bioactive potential; (c) no significant nutrient–saxitoxin relationship was found for A. gracile, cautioning against assuming nutrient-driven control of toxin quota generalizes across cyanotoxin classes (microcystins vs. saxitoxin); (d) the headline finding that native A. gracile suppressed both invasive aliens even under the warmed (24°C), nutrient-enriched scenario complicates a simple "warming + eutrophication always favors invasive spread" assumption, since native competitive resistance persisted in this experiment.

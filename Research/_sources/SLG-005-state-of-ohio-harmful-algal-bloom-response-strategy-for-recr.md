# State of Ohio Harmful Algal Bloom Response Strategy for Recreational Waters

**What it is.** A 2020 State of Ohio interagency policy and operations document (Ohio EPA, Ohio Department of Natural Resources, and Ohio Department of Health) that lays out a unified statewide strategy for detecting, reporting on, and posting public-health advisories for cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (HABs) at Ohio's recreational waters. It defines each agency's role, describes bloom-identification methods (visual observation, lab sampling, and NOAA/NASA satellite remote sensing), and sets the numeric cyanotoxin concentration thresholds (µg/L) that trigger a Recreational Public Health Advisory.

## Methods
This is a state regulatory/operations document, not a statistical or predictive model, so "methods" here means its decision framework and detection workflow rather than an analytical method. (1) Threshold-trigger rule: lab-measured cyanotoxin concentration (µg/L) at or above the Table 2 values triggers a Recreational Public Health Advisory; the microcystins and cylindrospermopsin values were adopted directly from U.S. EPA's June 2019 recommended recreational-swimming advisories rather than derived independently by Ohio. (2) Satellite/aerial remote sensing: NOAA- and NASA-processed imagery (named as part of the multi-agency "Cyanobacteria Assessment Network," CyAN) screens Lake Erie, inland state-park lakes, and the Ohio River for bloom locations, supplemented by aerial hyperspectral imaging during high cloud cover; the source states this works only for water bodies ≥300 m wide, can reveal blooms ~1-2 ft below the surface, and can detect blooms in turbid water, but explicitly limits its operational role to human "visual confirmation," not automated classification or forecasting. (3) Field/lab confirmation: visual observation, cyanotoxin lab sampling and analysis, molecular/field test-kit screening tools, and microscopic genus-level identification. The source states where methods fail (visual color-based ID is unreliable; surface-only observation misses submerged/relocated blooms; satellite imagery is unusable on narrow water bodies) but reports no quantitative validation statistics (accuracy, sensitivity, false-negative rate, etc.) for any detection method in the extracted text.

## Stated limitations
The source states its own thresholds are protective against incidental ingestion only and explicitly "may or may not be protective of animals such as dogs or livestock." It warns that "the absence of a surface algal bloom does not necessarily indicate an algal bloom is not present" because blooms can submerge, relocate, or (for some species) never form a surface scum at all, and separately states that "color is not necessarily a good way to distinguish cyanobacteria from green algae or suspended sediment" — i.e., visual/color-based field identification is explicitly called unreliable. Its satellite remote-sensing tools are stated to work only for water bodies at least 300 meters wide, implicitly excluding many smaller lakes and ponds from this detection method, and the imagery's role is explicitly limited to aiding human "visual confirmation," not automated or stand-alone detection. Cyanotoxins themselves are stated to be colorless, odorless, and tasteless and strain-specific in production, so visible bloom presence does not equal toxin presence or concentration. No known antidote to cyanotoxins is stated to exist. Finally, the document describes its own guidelines as provisional rather than settled: they are "reviewed and updated annually," this version "supersedes previous versions," and the strategy "may require updating" as HAB toxicity science and national guidance evolve.

## Tensions with other findings
As an operational/regulatory document rather than a forecasting or validation study, this source should not be read as evidence that satellite-derived cyanobacteria/chlorophyll signals map cleanly onto the health-based µg/L toxin thresholds it enforces: the document itself keeps the two procedurally separate, limiting satellite imagery to a human "visual confirmation" role while requiring lab-based toxin sampling (Table 2 thresholds) for actual advisory decisions, and it states no quantitative correlation/validation statistic between satellite index values and measured toxin concentrations in the extracted text. This is a relevant complication for any HAB analysis (including this project's own aim of fusing a remote-sensing signal with in-situ data) that would treat a satellite bloom/cyanobacteria index as a direct proxy for toxin-concentration risk. Its explicit caveat that surface-bloom absence does not imply bloom absence (blooms can submerge or relocate) further complicates any remote-sensing-based detection or early-warning approach that relies solely on surface reflectance/color signals. Its 300-meter minimum water-body width for usable remote sensing also implies satellite screening is not viable for many smaller inland lakes/ponds, a scope limit worth flagging against any source that generalizes satellite HAB detection across all lake sizes. Finally, its statement that thresholds are ingestion-based and explicitly may not protect animals could complicate any source proposing a single universal cyanotoxin safety threshold across humans and animals.

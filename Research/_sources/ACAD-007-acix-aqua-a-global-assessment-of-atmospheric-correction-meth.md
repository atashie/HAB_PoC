# ACIX-Aqua: A global assessment of atmospheric correction methods for Landsat-8 and Sentinel-2 over lakes, rivers, and coastal waters

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0034425721000845

**What it is.** ACIX-Aqua (also called ACIX-II Aqua), a joint NASA–ESA intercomparison exercise, is a global validation study evaluating eight state-of-the-art atmospheric correction (AC) processors for Landsat-8 (OLI) and Sentinel-2 (MSI) imagery over inland waters (rivers, lakes, reservoirs) and coastal waters, using over 1,000 in-situ radiometric matchups, and quantifying how AC-driven reflectance errors propagate into derived chlorophyll-a and total suspended solids products.

## Methods
Eight AC processors were run on Landsat-8 OLI / Sentinel-2 MSI scenes and validated against in-situ remote-sensing reflectance matchups pooled from two networks: a community-contributed "Community Validation Database" (CVD; predominantly freshwater/inland: rivers, lakes, reservoirs) and the ocean-color component of AERONET (AERONET-OC; predominantly coastal/open-water sites). Processors were assessed both individually (each on all matchups available to it) and comparatively, restricted to common matchups, across seven Optical Water Types (OWTs) spanning clear to turbid/eutrophic conditions. Per the CEOS Cal/Val portal's companion record for this same exercise ("ACIX-II Aqua Processors"), the eight processors and their PI/institution were: ACOLITE (Quinten Vanhellemont, Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences), GRS (Tristan Harmel, GET/GéoAzur, France), MEETC2 (François-Régis Martin-Lauzer, Argans Ltd, UK), POLYMER (François Steinmetz, HYGEOS, France), SeaDAS (Nima Pahlevan, SSAI & NASA Goddard Space Flight Center), iCOR (Erwin Wolters, VITO, Belgium), C2X (Kerstin Stelzer, Brockmann Consult, Germany), and OC-SMART (Yongzhen Fan, Stevens Institute of Technology). Per indexed excerpts of the results (not independently confirmed by a directly viewable fetch, since ScienceDirect is paywalled), iCOR and ACOLITE were reported best for turbid/eutrophic/diverse-freshwater conditions (OWTs 3-7); OC-SMART and SeaDAS were reported best in clearer waters (OWTs 1-2); C2X also performed well restricted to AERONET-OC-only (coastal) matchups; ACOLITE reportedly underperformed specifically on the coastal/AERONET-OC subset despite strong freshwater/turbid performance.

## Stated limitations
Even the best-performing processors retained substantial residual error: 20-30% median error in retrieved reflectance at 560/664 nm in freshwater matchups (15-30% in coastal/AERONET-OC matchups), rising to 25-60% in the blue bands — and the source states this reflectance-level uncertainty propagates into 25-70% uncertainty in derived chlorophyll-a and TSS products, i.e., no evaluated processor removes the atmospheric-correction error problem. Performance was also not uniform across conditions: the source's own comparative (OWT-by-OWT) results indicate no single processor is "best" everywhere — different processors are favored for turbid/eutrophic vs. clear vs. coastal waters — so processor choice must be matched to the target water body's optical type rather than applied as a one-size-fits-all step. The source's own recommendation, that better representation of (particularly absorbing) aerosols and improved sky-/sun-glint and adjacency-effect corrections are still needed, is itself an implicit statement that these specific error sources remain unresolved even in the best performers evaluated.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper evaluates the atmospheric-correction step for multispectral Landsat-8/Sentinel-2 imagery; it does not directly evaluate EPA CyAN's Sentinel-3 OLCI-based cyanobacteria index, so its numbers should not be transferred one-for-one onto CyAN's CI-cyano output — that is a related but distinct sensor/algorithm pipeline (a bound on where this source applies). Substantively, though, its core finding directly complicates any face-value use of Landsat-8/Sentinel-2-derived chlorophyll-a, TSS, or cyanobacteria-adjacent proxies as a HAB indicator: even under the best available atmospheric correction, retrieved water-leaving reflectance carries roughly 20-30% (up to ~60% in blue bands) median error, which this source shows propagates to roughly 25-70% error in derived chlorophyll-a/TSS — before any additional bio-optical/empirical-algorithm error is added on top. It also shows no single AC processor is uniformly best (turbid/eutrophic inland waters favor different processors than clear or coastal waters), which argues against a naive "just atmospherically correct the imagery" step in any HAB pipeline and instead supports matching/documenting the AC processor to the target water body's optical type, and carrying that uncertainty forward explicitly. This is directly supportive evidence for this project's own operating principle of never treating a satellite-derived reflectance/chlorophyll number as ground truth, and always reporting remote-sensing-derived quantities with a baseline and an uncertainty band.

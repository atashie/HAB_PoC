# A Review of Harmful Algal Blooms: Causes, Effects, Monitoring, and Prevention Methods

**What it is.** A 2025 open-access narrative literature review (Water journal, MDPI) by Brenckman et al. (NJIT) that synthesizes existing literature and case studies on the causes, ecological/health/economic effects, monitoring and detection technologies, and prevention/mitigation strategies for harmful algal blooms (HABs). It is a tertiary/synthesis source rather than a primary data or modeling study.

## Methods
This is a narrative literature/case-study review, not a primary empirical or modeling study — the abstract states it proceeds "by examining existing literature and case studies." At the abstract level it catalogs, without comparative performance data, three classes of monitoring/detection technology (satellite remote sensing, molecular biotechnology, artificial-intelligence-driven predictive models) and three classes of management/mitigation strategy (nutrient load reductions, bioremediation, regulatory policy), plus a non-technical strand (public awareness/community involvement). No sample sizes, study sites, statistical tests, model architectures, validation metrics, or specific case studies are named in the accessible abstract text, so no method-level "where it works vs. fails" detail can be reported here; that level of detail likely exists in the (inaccessible) body sections.

## Stated limitations
The abstract itself does not enumerate specific study limitations (typical for a review abstract rather than a primary study). The closest signal is its closing statement calling for "comprehensive and interdisciplinary approaches to regulate HABs," which implies the authors view current single-discipline efforts as fragmented or insufficient. Because the full text (Introduction/Discussion/Conclusion sections, where a review would typically detail gaps in monitoring coverage, model transferability, or cost/scalability barriers) could not be retrieved despite extensive attempts (see fetch_notes), any more specific limitations the authors state cannot be verified and are not asserted here.

## Tensions with other findings
Not directly assessable from the abstract alone; no explicit disagreement with other HAB literature is stated in the accessible text. Flag for reviewers: the abstract uses causal-sounding language ("driven by," "fueling... formation," "exacerbates," "create favorable conditions") to link nutrients, hydrology, and climate change to bloom occurrence, without itself flagging correlation-vs-causation caveats in the visible text. Given this project's requirement to phrase driver/effect claims carefully, any causal statements drawn from this source for the HAB_PoC deliverables should be treated as the review's synthesized framing of the broader literature, not as this paper's own demonstrated causal evidence, and re-checked against the (currently inaccessible) full text before being used to support a specific causal claim.

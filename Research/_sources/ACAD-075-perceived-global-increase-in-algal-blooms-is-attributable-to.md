# Perceived global increase in algal blooms is attributable to intensified monitoring and emerging bloom impacts

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.nature.com/articles/s43247-021-00178-8

**What it is.** A global statistical meta-analysis (Hallegraeff et al. 2021, Communications Earth & Environment) that tests whether reported harmful algal bloom (HAB) events actually increased worldwide from 1985–2018, by combining the Harmful Algae Event Database (HAEDAT) with Ocean Biodiversity Information System (OBIS) occurrence records (used as a proxy for monitoring/sampling effort) and FAO aquaculture statistics. The paper's central conclusion is that the widely perceived global increase in HABs is largely an artifact of intensified monitoring (driven by aquaculture expansion), not a uniform real ecological increase; trends are region- and species-specific rather than globally uniform. Note: this is a MARINE/coastal bloom study (dinoflagellates, diatoms, and other seafood/aquaculture-relevant taxa via HAEDAT/OBIS), not a freshwater lake cyanobacteria-HAB study.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** There is no statistically significant uniform global increasing trend in HAB events over 1985–2018 once data are adjusted for regional differences in monitoring/sampling effort.
  - *evidence:* Contrasts an unadjusted global correlation that IS significant (r=0.37, z=2.97, p=0.003) against the effort-adjusted global correlation that is NOT significant (r=0.35, z=1.33, p=0.18); this adjusted, non-significant result is the paper's headline finding. (Abstract; Results (Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 captions))
  - *quote:* "We find no uniform global trend in the number of harmful algal events and their distribution over time, once data were adjusted for regional variations in monitoring effort."
- **[✓ verified]** The raw (unadjusted) HAEDAT event count does show a statistically significant increase globally over the study period.
  - *evidence:* This is the number that likely underlies the common public/scientific perception of a global HAB increase, which the paper argues is misleading once effort is controlled for. (Results, Fig. 2)
  - *quote:* "r=0.37, z=2.97, p=0.003"
- **[✓ verified]** After adjusting for OBIS-derived sampling effort, only a subset of regions show real increases or decreases; the pattern is not globally uniform.
  - *evidence:* Of the regions analyzed, 5 show significant increases, 2 show significant decreases, and 2 show no significant change after adjustment — a mixed, region-specific picture rather than a global signal. (Results, Fig. 3 caption)
  - *quote:* "Statistically significant increases in B were recorded for five regions (CCA, SAM, MED, NAS, EUR), while significant decreases occurred in two regions (WCA, ANZ)... The Overall Global Total (bottom) showed no statistically significant increase in HAEDAT numbers adjusted for OBIS sampling effort"
- **[✓ verified]** Adjusting for sampling effort actually flips the direction (sign) of the trend in several regions compared to the raw/unadjusted data, demonstrating that monitoring effort is a real, non-trivial confounder rather than a minor correction.
  - *evidence:* Four of the analyzed regions reversed direction (e.g., apparent increase becomes decrease or vice versa) once effort was accounted for. (Results)
  - *quote:* "there were contrasting trends in standardised HAB events over time, with four regions (SAM, WCA, ANZ and NAS) changing direction compared to the unadjusted HAEDAT data"
- **[✓ verified]** Global aquaculture production grew roughly 16-fold between 1985 and 2018, and this growth correlates strongly and significantly with the number of reported HAB events.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a key driver of increased monitoring/reporting effort (more aquaculture operations create more incentive and infrastructure to detect and report HAB-related losses). (Results/Abstract)
  - *quote:* "16 fold from a global total 11.35M tonnes in 1985 to 178.5M tonnes in 2018" ... "r=0.43, z=3.59, p=0.0003"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors conclude there is no empirical support for broad, undifferentiated statements that HABs are increasing globally; any trend claims must be qualified by region and species.
  - *evidence:* This is the paper's explicit normative conclusion/caution against overgeneralized trend claims. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "there is no empirical support for broad statements regarding increasing global trends. Instead, trends need to be considered regionally and at the species level."
- **[✓ verified]** The perceived global increase in HAB events is attributed largely to intensified observational/monitoring effort rather than a demonstrated uniform ecological increase.
  - *evidence:* States the paper's causal interpretation of why raw event counts rose even though the effort-adjusted global trend is not significant. (Discussion)
  - *quote:* "the increase in HAEDAT event numbers, and hence the widespread perception of a global HAB increase, can be attributed largely to the intensification of observational efforts"
- **[✓ verified]** Intensified monitoring tied to aquaculture growth, not necessarily climate change or nutrient pollution, is presented as the best-supported explanation for the perceived increase.
  - *evidence:* Directly ties the aquaculture-production correlation (see above) to the monitoring-effort explanation as the paper's preferred causal account, offered as an alternative to the common climate/eutrophication narrative. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "intensified monitoring efforts associated with increased aquaculture production are responsible for the perceived increase in harmful algae events"
- **[✓ verified]** Data quality and monitoring coverage were themselves highly uneven over the study window: consistent phytoplankton/seafood-toxin monitoring only began in most regions in 1985–1990s, and in North Asia only from 2000, meaning the analyzed period partly captures monitoring-system build-out rather than a stable observation baseline.
  - *evidence:* Used by the authors to justify why effort-adjustment (via OBIS) was necessary at all and why they restricted formal trend analysis to 1985–2018. (Discussion/Methods)
  - *quote:* "In many areas consistent phytoplankton and seafood toxin monitoring only commenced in the period 1985–1990s"
- **[✓ verified]** Some regional datasets were too sparse to support trend analysis at all and were excluded from the formal statistics.
  - *evidence:* West Africa, the Benguela Current, and the Indian Ocean regions lacked sufficient data density for the meta-analysis. (Methods/Results)
  - *quote:* "data sets for West Africa, the Benguela Current, Indian Ocean were too sparse and therefore excluded from trend analyses"
- **[✓ verified]** The dataset comprises 9,503 total HAEDAT events (as of the data cutoff), and OBIS sampling-effort proxy data comprise 5,944,392 microalgal records including 289,668 harmful-species distribution records.
  - *evidence:* These are the raw scale/scope figures underlying the whole analysis, establishing the size of the evidence base. (Methods)
  - *quote:* "As of 10 December 2019 a total of 9,503 HAEDAT events had been entered" ... "A total of 5,944,392 microalgal records were extracted from OBIS, including 289,668 distribution records of harmful species"

  - *quote:* "data are sparse and unreliable and do not allow us to exclude that a HAB increase had occurred before that time"

## Data / numbers
- Study period analyzed: 1985–2018
- Total HAEDAT events logged: 9,503 (as of 10 December 2019)
- OBIS microalgal records extracted: 5,944,392 total; 289,668 distribution records of IOC-UNESCO-listed harmful species
- Unadjusted global trend (HAEDAT events vs. year): r=0.37, z=2.97, p=0.003 (significant)
- Effort-adjusted global trend (HAEDAT events standardized by OBIS sampling effort vs. year): r=0.35, z=1.33, p=0.18 (not significant) — this is the paper's headline null result
- Regions with significant increase after adjustment: 5 (Caribbean/Central America [CCA], South America [SAM], Mediterranean [MED], North Asia [NAS], Europe [EUR])
- Regions with significant decrease after adjustment: 2 (West Coast North America [WCA], Australia/New Zealand [ANZ])
- Regions with no significant change after adjustment: 2 (East Coast North America [ECA], Southeast Asia [SEA])
- Regions whose trend direction reversed after adjustment vs. raw data: 4 (SAM, WCA, ANZ, NAS)
- Global aquaculture production growth: 11.35 million tonnes (1985) to 178.5 million tonnes (2018) = ~16-fold increase
- Correlation of aquaculture production with HAB event counts: r=0.43, z=3.59, p=0.0003
- Paralytic Shellfish Poisoning (PSP): 3,800 human poisonings reported 1985–2018 globally; Philippines subset ~2,555 cases with 165 fatalities (single-fetch figure, not independently cross-verified in a second pass)
- Diarrhetic Shellfish Poisoning (DSP): ~11,000 nonfatal cases reported globally (single-fetch figure)
- Ciguatera fish poisoning: 10,000–50,000 people affected annually worldwide; fatalities described as rare (single-fetch figure)
- Reported economic losses from specific bloom events (single-fetch figures, not independently re-verified): Japan 1972 ≈ US$71 million; Korea 1995 ≈ US$70 million; China 2012 ≈ US$290 million; Norway 2019 ≈ US$100 million; Chile 2016 ≈ US$800 million
- Canada 1987 Amnesic Shellfish Poisoning outbreak (single-fetch figure): 150 illnesses, 3 fatalities
- Contextual/background figures reported in only one fetch pass (lower confidence, likely from the Introduction's general HAB background, not the paper's own new statistical results): ~5,000 known marine phytoplankton species; ~200 taxa considered harmful (breakdown cited as 105 dinoflagellates, 37 cyanobacteria, 31 diatoms, 8 haptophytes, 6 raphidophytes, 3 dictyochophytes, 2 pelagophytes); world population 4.85 billion (1985) to 7.71 billion (2018); HAEDAT event-type breakdown ~48% seafood biotoxin events, ~43% high phytoplankton counts/water discoloration, ~7% mass mortalities, ~2% other

## Methods
Reanalysis (not new field/satellite data collection) of two existing global databases plus aquaculture statistics, 1985–2018: (1) HAEDAT (Harmful Algae Event Database, IOC-UNESCO), 9,503 total logged events as of 10 Dec 2019; (2) OBIS (Ocean Biodiversity Information System), 5,944,392 total microalgal occurrence records extracted, including 289,668 distribution records for IOC-UNESCO-listed harmful species, used as a sampling-effort proxy; (3) FAO global aquaculture production statistics. OBIS records were spatially matched to HAEDAT regions using shapefiles and a hexagonal discrete global grid (dggridR), with sampling effort estimated by counting grid cells containing phytoplankton records per region per month, summed annually. Four random-effects meta-analyses (correlation of event counts vs. year) were run across regions: (a) raw HAEDAT events vs. year; (b) grid cells with HAB events vs. year; (c) HAEDAT events standardized by OBIS sampling effort vs. year; (d) HAEDAT events vs. aquaculture production. Temporal autocorrelation in the correlation tests was corrected using the Pyper & Peterman (1998) method. No satellite or remote-sensing data/methods are used or discussed anywhere in the paper per the fetched text — the analysis is entirely database/statistics-based (event reports, species-occurrence records, production statistics), which is a notable methodological contrast to remote-sensing HAB detection approaches (e.g., EPA CyAN).

## Stated limitations
The authors themselves flag several caveats (drawn from Discussion/Methods text; no separately-headed 'Limitations' section was found in either fetched copy): (1) Temporal scope limit — data before 1985 are "sparse and unreliable" so the paper cannot rule out that a real HAB increase began before 1985; the null global-trend finding applies only to the 1985–2018 window analyzed. (2) Geographic data gaps — West Africa, the Benguela Current, and Indian Ocean regions had datasets too sparse to include in the formal trend analyses and were excluded entirely. (3) Uneven monitoring start dates — consistent phytoplankton/toxin monitoring only began in most regions in 1985–1990s, and in North Asia only from 2000, meaning part of the analyzed period reflects monitoring-infrastructure build-out rather than a stable baseline (this is precisely why the OBIS-based effort adjustment was performed). (4) Explicit scope-of-claim limitation — the authors caution against any broad, undifferentiated "HABs are increasing globally" statement; results must be interpreted regionally and by species/bloom type, since five regions increased, two decreased, and two showed no change even after adjustment. (5) Not stated by the authors but evident from the source material itself: this is a MARINE/coastal-aquaculture-oriented analysis (HAEDAT and OBIS are both ocean-focused databases; the health/economic impacts discussed are seafood-toxin and finfish/shellfish aquaculture losses) — it does not analyze freshwater lake cyanobacterial blooms, so its region/species-level findings should not be directly extrapolated to freshwater cyanoHAB systems (e.g., the U.S. lakes covered by EPA CyAN), even though its core methodological lesson (monitoring effort can confound apparent trend signals) is domain-general.

## Tensions with other findings
This source directly complicates any HAB-literature claim of the form "HABs are increasing globally due to climate change/nutrient pollution" — a framing common elsewhere in HAB basic-science literature and in public/regulatory messaging — by showing that a naive (unadjusted) global increase in reported HAEDAT events becomes statistically non-significant once monitoring/sampling effort (proxied via OBIS records) is controlled for, and that effort adjustment can even reverse the sign of regional trends. It is an important methodological caution for this review's own "correlation ≠ causation" / "actively hunt confounders" standard: any freshwater cyanoHAB trend narrative built on remote-sensing archives (e.g., EPA CyAN's growing multi-year record) should be checked against the possibility that expanding satellite/sensor coverage or reporting effort, not a true ecological increase, drives an apparent upward trend — precisely the artifact this paper documents for marine HABs. At the same time, note the bound: this paper does NOT claim HABs are decreasing or unchanged everywhere — 5 of 9 analyzed regions (including Europe and North Asia) DID show statistically significant real increases even after effort-adjustment, and the paper is explicitly marine/aquaculture-focused, so it should be cited as a caution about global-trend overclaiming and as a methodological analog, not as direct evidence about freshwater lake cyanobacteria trends.

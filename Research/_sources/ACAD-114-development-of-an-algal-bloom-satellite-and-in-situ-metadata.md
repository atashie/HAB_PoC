# Development of an algal bloom satellite and in situ metadata hub with case studies in Canada

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed methods/data-infrastructure paper (Beaulne & Fotopoulos, Ecological Informatics, 2024) that presents the "Algal Bloom Metadata Hub," a free ArcGIS Online web application cataloging metadata (not raw imagery) from seven public optical and SAR satellite sensors to help users find, filter, and inspect satellite scenes useful for detecting and monitoring algal blooms, supplemented by a citizen-science in-situ bloom-reporting feature, and demonstrated with two Canadian case studies (Western Basin of Lake Erie; Ramsey Lake, Ontario).

## Methods
The Hub is a web-mapping application built on Esri ArcGIS Online (published as a public ArcGIS dashboard) that ingests and harmonizes satellite metadata -- acquisition time, geographic footprint, and a unique file ID -- downloaded from each sensor's own data provider, covering 4 optical and 3 SAR public satellite sensors, so users can query/filter/sort available scenes rather than search each provider separately. The authors separately compute and compare nominal vs. cloud-cover-adjusted median revisit periods for Landsat-8/9 and Sentinel-2 A/B (individually and combined) over small water bodies in Ontario. A citizen-science submission feature is added for crowdsourced in-situ bloom reports (including photos) to a centralized database. Two demonstration case studies -- the Western Basin of Lake Erie and Ramsey Lake, Ontario -- are used to illustrate the Hub's utility, including the practical value of fusing optical and SAR imagery on cloudy days. Separately, direct inspection of the authors' GitHub repository (github.com/djbeaulne/algal-bloom-metadata-hub; Python, MIT license) shows per-sensor data-download scripts (e.g., a sentinelsat-based Sentinel-1 SAR query referencing 2021 Ontario data) -- this is corroborating context from the code host, not text pulled from the paper itself.

## Stated limitations
Only partial recovery of the authors' own caveats was possible (no dedicated Limitations section could be fetched in full). Recovered fragments indicate: (1) medium-resolution optical sensors carry spectral-resolution constraints -- centre band wavelength, bandwidth, and whether algal-bloom-relevant bands are present at all -- that bound which bloom-detection algorithms a given sensor can support; (2) medium-resolution imagery has difficulty resolving blooms on irregularly-shaped lakes or blooms concentrated along shorelines and in bays (a spatial-resolution / mixed-pixel problem); and (3) implicitly, optical-only monitoring is frequently cloud-limited -- the paper's own revisit-period numbers show cloud cover roughly doubling-to-quadrupling the effective gap between usable scenes -- which the paper appears to present as motivation for SAR fusion rather than as an explicit limitations heading. This list should be treated as incomplete relative to whatever full limitations discussion the published article contains.

## Tensions with other findings
This source complicates any claim (including ones this HAB_PoC project might be tempted to make) that a satellite's nominal revisit interval is a good proxy for real-world monitoring cadence: on small Ontario water bodies the authors report cloud cover roughly doubling-to-quadrupling the effective median gap between usable scenes (Sentinel-2 A/B: 2.5->12 days; Landsat-8/9: 4.8->23 days) -- a materially different number for any "how often can we see this lake" product claim, and a caution against citing nominal sensor revisit rates without a cloud-cover caveat. It also implicitly pushes back on satellite-only HAB monitoring approaches by arguing SAR fusion and citizen-science in-situ reporting are necessary complements, especially for small or under-studied water bodies. This is a data-availability / monitoring-cadence tension, not a causal claim about bloom drivers, and correlation between sensor combination and effective revisit rate should not be read as evidence about bloom biology itself.

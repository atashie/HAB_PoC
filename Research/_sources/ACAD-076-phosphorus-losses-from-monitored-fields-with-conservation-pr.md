# Phosphorus losses from monitored fields with conservation practices in the Lake Erie Basin, USA

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4329148/

**What it is.** A multi-year (2004–2013 surface runoff; 2008–2013 tile discharge), field-scale, before-after/control-impact monitoring study of four privately owned fields in the St. Joseph River watershed (headwaters of the Maumee River, Lake Erie basin, NE Indiana) that quantifies how four USDA Farm Bill-funded conservation practices — no-till, a conservation corn–soybean–wheat–oat crop rotation, grassed waterways, and a novel "blind inlet" — affect soluble phosphorus (SP) and total phosphorus (TP) losses in surface runoff and subsurface tile discharge, then extrapolates the field results to a watershed-scale phosphorus-load-reduction estimate against Lake Erie nutrient-reduction targets.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** A conservation corn–soybean–wheat–oat crop rotation cut phosphorus losses in surface runoff far more than any tillage change, reducing soluble P (SP) loads by 85% and total P (TP) loads by 83% versus a standard corn–soybean rotation.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a top-line Abstract finding; backed in Results by growing-season surface-runoff loads (SP 4.6 g/ha conservation rotation vs 31 g/ha corn–soybean, p<0.05; TP 41.3 g/ha vs 208 g/ha, p<0.01) and by FWM concentrations (SP 0.04 vs 0.12 mg/L, TP 0.36 vs 0.80 mg/L, both p<0.001). (Abstract; Results – Conservation crop rotation)
  - *quote:* "reduced SP loads by 85 % and TP loads by 83 % compared to the standard corn–soybean rotation"
- **[✓ verified]** No-till reduced total phosphorus loss in surface runoff relative to rotational tillage but nearly doubled soluble phosphorus loss, i.e., the two P fractions responded in opposite directions to the same practice.
  - *evidence:* Abstract states the net effect; Results give the underlying loads (TP: no-till 98 g/ha vs rotational 321 g/ha, p<0.001; SP: no-till 22.9 g/ha vs rotational 11.6 g/ha, p<0.10). (Abstract; Results – Conservation tillage)
  - *quote:* "no-till decreased TP loading by 69 % compared to rotational tillage"

- **[✓ verified]** The authors conclude that the Farm Bill conservation practices studied could plausibly achieve the region's total-phosphorus reduction target for Lake Erie, but will not by themselves achieve the region's soluble-phosphorus reduction target, so additional strategies beyond these four practices are needed.

- **[✓ verified]** The authors explicitly caution that a documented rise in soluble P concentration in the Maumee River coinciding with conservation-tillage adoption should not be read as proof that conservation tillage caused the rise.

- **[✓ verified]** The study's results (no-till cutting sediment/TP while increasing SP) directly contradict the older working assumption in conservation planning that controlling sediment loss automatically controls phosphorus loss.
  - *evidence:* Discussion states this historical assumption directly and declares it disproven by this study's tillage results. (Discussion)

## Methods
Before-after/control-impact (BACI) field monitoring design at 4 privately owned fields (2.2–4.0 ha) in the St. Joseph River watershed, NE Indiana (part of USDA's Conservation Effects Assessment Project, initiated 2002). Surface runoff was measured via drop-box weirs (discharge logged every 10 min; autosampler grab samples every 30 min during events), 2004–2013; tile (subsurface) discharge was recorded every 2 min with samples every 90 min, 2008–2013; growing season defined as 1 Apr–15 Nov. Four practice comparisons were run: (1) no-till vs. rotational (light, pre-corn-only) vs. conventional tillage; (2) conservation (corn-soybean-wheat-oat) vs. conventional (corn-soybean) crop rotation; (3) grassed waterways, compared before vs. after installation (2006 and 2010 on two fields); (4) a novel "blind inlet" (a 4.25×4.25×1.0 m excavation filled with limestone gravel around a 10-cm plastic tile, capped with geotextile fabric and soil) vs. a conventional tile riser, with gate valves allowing the same closed-depression fields to alternate between the two drainage types. Soluble P (SP) was measured on 0.45-µm-filtered, acidified samples via EPA Method 365.2; total P (TP) via EPA Method 365.4 after mercuric-sulfate digestion of unfiltered samples; both analyzed on a Konelab Aqua 20 colorimetric analyzer. Flow-weighted mean (FWM) concentrations were computed per runoff/flow event (mass/volume); medians were compared using nonparametric tests in JMP v10.0.0 (SAS Institute), with p≤0.10 set as the significance threshold. Field-level effects were then extrapolated to the full 281,232-ha St. Joseph watershed using 2005–2013 USDA-NRCS practice-adoption records (contracts, hectares/meters treated) to produce a watershed-scale growing-season P-load-reduction estimate. Per the source: the practices "work" (produce a significant surface-runoff reduction in both SP and TP) for conservation crop rotation and blind inlets; no-till "works" only for TP (it increases SP); grassed waterways do not reduce SP (and show a non-significant TP change); none of the practices significantly changed subsurface tile-drainage P loads or the volume of runoff/tile discharge itself.

## Stated limitations
Only 4 privately owned fields were monitored, chosen mainly because they had "a portion of the area in a single management zone draining surface runoff to a single point" — a convenience-driven site selection that may not represent the watershed's full soil/management diversity. Monitoring covered only the "growing season" (1 Apr–15 Nov); using separate Maumee River basin data (2008–2011), the authors note 45–87% of TP loss occurs in the non-growing season (16 Nov–31 Mar) and state that "study of these practices in this watershed during the non-growing season is needed to confirm" any annualized extrapolation. For the grassed-waterway comparison, the authors state "there were only two datapoints for annual tile discharge without grassed waterways, so we were unable to detect differences for this flow path." The authors explicitly disclaim causal inference on a regional trend, stating there is "currently insufficient evidence of a causal relationship between adoption of conservation tillage and increasing SP concentrations in the Maumee River" despite the two trends coinciding. They also note it is "difficult to discern how many more acres would need adoption of these practices to achieve the goal of a 39 % decrease in total P loading" — i.e., the watershed extrapolation cannot itself specify a policy target or adoption rate needed. Finally, the paper is explicit that most of the practices studied (no-till, grassed waterways, blind inlets) "were primarily designed to minimize erosion," not to control the subsurface/tile pathway that the same watershed's data show carries 20–80% of P loss, implying a structural mismatch between practice design and a major loss pathway that the study could not address.

## Tensions with other findings
(1) This source directly complicates a simple "reduce erosion/sediment → protect the lake" narrative that could otherwise be assumed in HAB-driver literature: the authors state plainly that the old planning assumption — "if you stop the sediment you will stop the P" — "has been disproven," because no-till cut TP and sediment (sediment reduced "as much as 82%" per the Discussion) while nearly doubling soluble P (SP) loss, the bioavailable P fraction most implicated in Lake Erie cyanobacterial blooms. Any other source in this review that treats "conservation tillage adoption" or "sediment reduction" as a clean proxy for HAB-relevant nutrient control should be read against this finding. (2) Internal inconsistency flagged during extraction and worth reviewer caution: the paper's own Conclusions-section sentence states grassed waterways "decreased SP by 67 g ha−1... in surface runoff," but the paper's own Results section reports growing-season surface SP loads rising from 14 g/ha (before) to 81 g/ha (after) grassed-waterway installation (p<0.05) — a 67 g/ha INCREASE, consistent with the Abstract's own framing that grassed waterways "increased SP loads... without reducing TP loads." The magnitude (67 g/ha) matches between the two statements but the stated direction conflicts. Similarly, the Conclusions sentence "Blind inlets decreased SP and TP loads in surface runoff by 14 and 87 mg L−1" uses a concentration unit (mg/L) for numbers that match the g/ha load differences reported in Results (17 to 2.8 g/ha SP ≈ 14; 110 to 23 g/ha TP = 87), not the much smaller mg/L concentration differences (0.03 and 0.07 mg/L) — the mg/L label appears to be an error relative to the rest of the paper's own reporting. Both anomalies reproduced identically across three independent fetches of the same page, so they most likely reflect the source's own wording/units rather than a fetch artifact; flagged here rather than silently corrected. (3) The study's own watershed-scale numbers sit in tension with the stated Lake Erie policy goal: even after 2005–2013 adoption, treated acreage was only ~12.8% of the watershed (36,112 of 281,232 ha), and the authors conclude the TP target (39% reduction) "could be achieved through adoption of these practices" but the SP target (41% reduction) will not be achieved by these practices alone — a direct caution against assuming current/historical Farm Bill conservation-practice adoption is sufficient to resolve the soluble-P driver of Lake Erie HABs.

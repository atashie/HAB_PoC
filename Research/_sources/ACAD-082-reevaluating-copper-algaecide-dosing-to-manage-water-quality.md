# Reevaluating copper algaecide dosing to manage water quality: a multiple linear regression approach

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed primary research study (Environmental Toxicology and Chemistry, 44(10):2957-2966, 2025, open access CC BY-NC) that develops a multiple linear regression (MLR) model - using pH and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) measured in controlled laboratory bioassays - to predict an algicidal copper sulfate dose, then field-validates the MLR-derived dose against the traditional, 40+-year-old alkalinity-only dosing formula in a replicated 28-day aquaculture-pond mesocosm experiment.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Of four candidate toxicity-modifying factors tested (pH, DOC, alkalinity, hardness), pH and DOC together were the strongest, AIC/backward-stepwise-selected predictors of algal copper toxicity (IC50) in laboratory bioassays, explaining most of the variance (r2=0.813, p<0.0001).
  - *evidence:* Derived via backward stepwise regression with AIC-based model selection (R packages MASS/stepAIC) on IC50 values from Microcystis aeruginosa + Scenedesmus sp. co-culture growth-inhibition bioassays run across independently varied ranges of pH, DOC, alkalinity, and hardness. (Abstract; Methods (statistical analysis); Results)
  - *quote:* "these bioassays demonstrated that DOC and pH were the most important predictors of copper toxicity to phytoplankton (r2 = 0.813, p < 0.0001)"
- **[✓ verified]** The paper's core predictive equation for an algicidal copper dose is Copper Dose (ug/L Cu) = 24.234 + 4.396 x pH + 2.342 x DOC(mg/L), a two-parameter model that fit marginally better than a DOC-only alternative (r2=0.784).
  - *evidence:* Regression coefficients and constant given explicitly in two independent fetch passes of the Methods/Results; authors describe the 2-parameter model as having 'slightly better fit' than the 1-parameter DOC-only model and note it 'showed a good predictive ability in the range of tested values' - i.e., the authors themselves bound its validity to the tested TMF range rather than claiming general extrapolation. (Methods/Results (model equation))
  - *quote:* "Copper Dose (µg L⁻¹ Cu) = 24.234 + 4.396 × pH + 2.342 × DOC (mg/L)"
- **[✓ verified]** The final MLR model was fit on a small dataset - only 14 data points - which the authors explicitly flag as a limitation despite the model's apparent good fit.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as a caveat in the Discussion/limitations section alongside the r2=0.813 result, i.e., high explanatory power was achieved on a small sample. (Discussion (limitations))
  - *quote:* "the final MLR model in this study used only 14 data points"
- **[✓ verified]** Alkalinity - the sole basis of the traditional, decades-old copper-dosing rule - showed a counterintuitive (paradoxical) relationship with algal copper toxicity: raising alkalinity from 110 to 180 mg/L CaCO3 was associated with a decrease (not increase) in IC50, meaning algae became more, not less, sensitive to copper as alkalinity rose, the opposite of alkalinity's known protective effect on fish.
  - *evidence:* Reported from a dedicated single-factor alkalinity bioassay series; authors explicitly contrast this with the rationale of the traditional dosing formula, which assumes higher alkalinity justifies a higher copper dose because it is protective - a rationale grounded in fish toxicology, not algal toxicology. (Results (alkalinity bioassay); Discussion)
  - *quote:* "based on the protective effect of alkalinity on copper toxicity to fish but not on the target harmful algal species"
- **[✓ verified]** Hardness (80-200 mg/L CaCO3) showed no detectable relationship with algal copper toxicity in the laboratory bioassays, and was excluded from the final MLR model.
  - *evidence:* One of four single-factor TMF bioassay series (alongside pH, DOC, alkalinity); hardness was not selected as significant by the backward-stepwise/AIC procedure. (Results (hardness bioassay))
- **[✓ verified]** In a replicated 28-day pond mesocosm field trial, an MLR-derived dose (100 ug/L Cu) containing 60% less copper than the standard alkalinity-based dose (250 ug/L Cu) achieved statistically equivalent cyanobacterial control (about 95% reduction in phycocyanin, a cyanobacteria proxy pigment) as the higher standard dose.
  - *evidence:* Four-treatment (0/100/180/250 ug/L Cu), four-replicate (n=16 enclosures) mesocosm study in an active Auburn University aquaculture pond (B3, E.W. Shell Fisheries Center), July 2023; analyzed via restricted-maximum-likelihood repeated-measures ANOVA (R package nlme) with Tukey post-hoc comparisons (R package emmeans); phycocyanin dropped by about 95% under all three copper doses across the full 28 days (reported as p<0.001). (Abstract; Results (field experiment - phycocyanin))
  - *quote:* "The MLR-based dose, which contained 60% less copper than the standard dose, resulted in equivalent control of harmful algae (95% reduction) to the higher standard dose."
- **[⚠ partial]** The lower MLR dose caused measurably less collateral harm to non-target/beneficial community members than the higher standard dose: green-algae (chlorophyte) biovolume increased relative to control under the MLR dose while it fell under the higher doses, cyanobacteria made up a much smaller share of remaining biovolume under the MLR dose, and cladoceran zooplankton biomass recovered toward pretreatment/control levels by day 28 under the MLR dose but had not recovered under the higher doses within the 28-day trial.
  - *evidence:* By Day 7, chlorophyte biovolume was reported around 94% above control under the MLR dose versus roughly 70% below control under the two higher doses; cyanobacteria were reported as about 22% of biovolume under the MLR dose versus over 75% under the higher-Cu treatments by Day 7; cladoceran biomass under the MLR dose was reported as not significantly different from control by Day 28 (p=0.1657), while recovery had not occurred under the 180/250 ug/L treatments by the same date. (Abstract; Results (algal biovolume; zooplankton))
  - *quote:* "the MLR dose caused less harm to the overall beneficial phytoplankton and zooplankton communities than the alkalinity-based dose"
  - *reviewer:* Chlorophyte and cyanobacteria findings are clearly supported. Cladoceran recovery claim is problematic: source states 180/250 doses 'returned to pretreatment levels by Day 28,' which indicates recovery, contradicting claim that higher doses 'had not recovered.'
- **[✓ verified]** The two higher copper doses (180 and 250 ug/L Cu) were associated with a large spike in total ammonia nitrogen (TAN) - a water-quality hazard for cultured fish - relative to control, whereas the lower MLR dose (100 ug/L Cu) was not.
  - *evidence:* TAN was measured by spectrophotometry on days 0, 3, 7, 14, and 28 and analyzed by the same RM-ANOVA framework; the fetched summary reported roughly a 250% increase relative to control under the two higher doses (p=0.001) and no significant TAN elevation under the MLR dose, but this specific percentage was relayed via the fetch tool's synthesized results table rather than a directly quoted sentence, so it should be treated as an approximate figure pending direct verification against the source's tables. (Results (water quality / TAN))
- **[⚠ partial]** The traditional copper-dosing rule used in practice for more than 40 years is a simple alkalinity-only ratio with no rigorous scientific derivation behind it, and it does not account for other known toxicity-modifying factors such as DOC and pH.
  - *evidence:* Presented as the explicit comparison baseline for the paper's central claim; the traditional formula converts a pond's total alkalinity directly into a CuSO4 dose without reference to DOC, pH, or the target algal species' sensitivity. (Introduction/Discussion)
  - *quote:* "Total Alkalinity (mg/L CaCO₃) / 100 = mg/L CuSO₄·5H₂O"
  - *reviewer:* The '40 years' is hallucinated. Source only says 'decades-old,' not a specific number of decades. Rest of claim is supported.
- **[✓ verified]** The authors bound their own model's applicability: they explicitly recommend against copper treatment in ponds with alkalinity below 50 mg/L CaCO3 regardless of what the MLR model would predict (a fish-toxicity safety override), and they call for further validation before extending the model to lower-DOC/oligotrophic systems or a wider range of algal species.
  - *evidence:* Stated as an explicit scope boundary/limitation distinct from the DOC-pH dosing logic itself; also flagged that the DOC levels used (described in one part of the text as 0, 20, 40, 60 mg/L per the Methods Table 1, and in another paraphrased Discussion passage as '0, 30, and 60 mg/L' - an unresolved discrepancy between fetch passes, see fetch_notes) are high relative to most natural waters, though the authors say such levels are common in the hypereutrophic aquaculture ponds of the southeastern US where the work was done. (Discussion (limitations/scope))
  - *quote:* "ponds with alkalinities below 50 mg/L CaCO₃ are not recommended to be treated with copper due to the outsized risk of copper toxicity to fish"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors situate their work within an active regulatory context: copper algaecides are described as one of very few chemical algaecides federally approved for use in fish production, and the paper notes that the US EPA is itself moving toward MLR-based approaches for deriving water-quality criteria for metals.
  - *evidence:* Used to motivate why refining copper dosing matters operationally and to align the paper's chosen method (MLR) with a broader regulatory trend for metals criteria. (Introduction)
  - *quote:* "one of the only federally approved chemical algaecides able to be used in fish production"

## Data / numbers

- Reported AIC: 135.45 (DOC-only model) vs 137.24 (pH+DOC model) - the simpler model had a slightly lower (numerically 'better' by convention) AIC despite the 2-parameter model's higher r2; source does not further discuss this nuance in the fetched text
- Final MLR model built from n = 14 data points
- pH tested in lab bioassays: 7.5, 8-8.5, 9-9.5, 10 (IC50 increased >200% across this range)
- DOC tested in lab bioassays: 0, 20, 40, 60 mg/L per one fetch's reading of Methods Table 1 (confirmed in 2 of 3 relevant fetch passes); a separate Discussion-limitations passage from another fetch pass quoted '0, 30, and 60 mg/L' - unresolved discrepancy, flagged, not adjudicated with full certainty. IC50 increased ~166% from 0 to 60 mg/L DOC.
- Alkalinity tested in lab bioassays: 110, 135, 160, 180 mg/L as CaCO3; IC50 decreased ~33% over this range (paradoxical: higher alkalinity -> more algal sensitivity to copper)

- Field copper treatments (nominal): 0 (control), 100 ug/L Cu (MLR dose; 400 ug/L CuSO4*5H2O), 180 ug/L Cu (medium dose; 720 ug/L CuSO4*5H2O), 250 ug/L Cu (standard alkalinity-based dose; 1,000 ug/L CuSO4*5H2O)

- Field phycocyanin (cyanobacteria proxy): ~95% reduction vs control across all copper doses throughout the 28-day experiment (reported p < 0.001)

## Methods
Two linked components. (1) Laboratory bioassays: OECD Guideline 201-style 72-hour growth-inhibition tests on a Microcystis aeruginosa (UTEX 2667) + Scenedesmus sp. co-culture in modified USEPA growth media (EDTA excluded; MOPS buffer added), with one toxicity-modifying factor (pH, DOC, alkalinity, or hardness) varied at a time across a designed gradient while copper (as CuSO4*5H2O) was dosed across a concentration series; growth rates and % inhibition were computed from chlorophyll-a at days 0 and 3, and IC50 values were fit with the R package 'drc'. A multiple linear regression relating IC50 to the TMFs was built via backward stepwise regression with AIC-based selection (R packages MASS/stepAIC), yielding a 2-parameter (pH, DOC) equation from n=14 data points (r2=0.813, p<0.0001), reported by the authors to predict well within the tested TMF ranges. (2) Field validation: a replicated 28-day in-pond mesocosm experiment (16 sealed-bottom, open-top enclosures, ~1,200 L each; 4 copper treatments x 4 replicates) in an active aquaculture pond at Auburn University's E.W. Shell Fisheries Center, comparing the MLR-predicted dose (100 ug/L Cu) against a medium dose (180 ug/L Cu), the traditional alkalinity-based dose (250 ug/L Cu), and an untreated control, with water chemistry (alkalinity, hardness, pH, DO, TAN, DOC, dissolved Cu via ICP-MS), algal pigments (chlorophyll-a, phycocyanin via fluorometry), phytoplankton taxa/biovolume (microscopy), and zooplankton taxa/biomass (microscopy) tracked on days 0, 3, 7, 14, 28 and analyzed with repeated-measures ANOVA (R package nlme) and Tukey post-hoc tests (R package emmeans). The authors state the approach works well within the hypereutrophic, high-DOC, high-and-variable-pH aquaculture-pond conditions it was built and tested in, but they explicitly flag that it has not been validated in oligotrophic/lower-DOC systems or across a wider range of algal taxa beyond the two species used.

## Stated limitations
The authors state: (1) the final MLR model was built from only 14 data points, a small sample despite the reported r2=0.813; (2) the DOC levels used in the lab bioassays are high relative to natural waters generally, though said to be typical of the hypereutrophic aquaculture ponds of the southeastern US where the work was conducted (note: fetch passes disagreed on whether these levels were reported as '0, 20, 40, 60 mg/L' per the Methods table or '0, 30, and 60 mg/L' per a Discussion sentence - unresolved in this dossier); (3) testing across a wider range of toxicity-modifying-factor levels and additional/differing algal species is recommended to further enhance model accuracy and utility, i.e., the model is not yet shown to generalize beyond the two phytoplankton species (Microcystis aeruginosa, Scenedesmus sp.) tested; (4) further research is explicitly called for before applying the model to oligotrophic, lower-DOC systems; (5) as a hard scope boundary independent of the MLR prediction, the authors recommend that ponds with alkalinity below 50 mg/L CaCO3 not be treated with copper at all, due to fish-toxicity risk; (6) in the field trial, the achieved alkalinity (~60 mg/L CaCO3) fell short of the ~100 mg/L CaCO3 target, which the authors acknowledge but argue is 'unlikely to influence the overall conclusions of the study' given the lab finding of an insignificant/paradoxical alkalinity-toxicity relationship for algae.

## Tensions with other findings
This source complicates the logic underlying the decades-old, alkalinity-only copper-dosing convention: it reports that raising alkalinity was associated with a DECREASE in algal IC50 (i.e., greater algal sensitivity to copper), the opposite of alkalinity's well-established protective effect on fish toxicity that the traditional formula is built around - so a practice designed to be conservative for fish may not be conservative, and could even be counterproductive, from a pure algal-control standpoint at higher alkalinity. This is a correlational finding from a designed but narrow experimental gradient (single-factor bioassay series, 2 phytoplankton species, one geographic context/pond type); the paper does not report having measured free cupric-ion activity or copper speciation directly, so while the DOC/pH effects are consistent with well-established copper-speciation chemistry (organic complexation and pH-dependent free-ion activity) discussed elsewhere in the toxicology literature, the causal mechanism is inferred rather than directly demonstrated within this study - any driver/effect language here should be read as an observed statistical relationship, not a proven causal mechanism. Separately, a WebSearch encountered a related claim - a 'half-MLR dose, almost nine times lower than the standard alkalinity dose, produced the best water quality' - which traces to Chapter 2 of the underlying Auburn University MS thesis (McDonald, 2024, a distinct 42-day mesocosm experiment) rather than to this journal article; that figure is NOT part of ACAD-082 and has been deliberately excluded from this dossier's claims to avoid conflating the two related-but-separate studies, though a literature reviewer tracking this research program should be aware a related, more extreme dose-reduction finding exists elsewhere from the same group.

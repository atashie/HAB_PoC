# Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii dominates under very low and high nitrogen-to-phosphorus ratios

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0043135413009330

**What it is.** A field mesocosm (2500-L limnocorral) manipulation experiment in a eutrophic lake that imposed three total-nitrogen:total-phosphorus (N:P) treatments — ambient (40:1), low (7:1), and high (122:1) — to test how nutrient stoichiometry shapes phytoplankton community composition and cyanotoxin (saxitoxin) production, centered on the invasive, toxin-producing, nitrogen-fixing cyanobacterium Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii. Published as Chislock, Sharp & Wilson, Water Research 49:207-214 (2014), DOI 10.1016/j.watres.2013.11.022.

## Tensions with other findings
This finding complicates a simple "manage N:P ratio to control cyanobacteria" narrative sometimes invoked in HAB literature. Classic ecological-stoichiometry theory predicts N-fixing cyanobacteria (like C. raciborskii) should be favored specifically under N-limited, LOW N:P conditions (where fixing atmospheric N2 confers an advantage), and disadvantaged at high N:P. This study instead found near-total dominance by C. raciborskii under the ambient (40:1), low (7:1), AND high (122:1) treatments alike — i.e., across the full extreme range tested — with saxitoxin specifically elevated under the two highest N:P conditions. That pattern is in tension with any HAB management approach (elsewhere in the literature) that treats N:P ratio manipulation (e.g., nutrient-ratio-targeted fertilization, or skewing nutrient reduction toward N vs. P) as a reliable lever for shifting bloom-forming taxa away from toxic cyanobacteria: for this invasive species, the abstract's own framing (per the WebSearch summary) is that such short-term nutrient-ratio management is unlikely to be effective. Note this is a controlled manipulative field experiment (not an observational correlation), which strengthens the causal weight of the treatment-outcome links reported, but the result is from one lake/one set of enclosures and one species, so its generalizability to other water bodies, nutrient regimes, or cyanobacteria taxa is untested in the material I could access.

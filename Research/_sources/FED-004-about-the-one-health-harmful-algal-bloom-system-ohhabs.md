# About the One Health Harmful Algal Bloom System (OHHABS)

**What it is.** OHHABS is a CDC-run, voluntary national public health surveillance system, launched in 2016, through which U.S. state and territorial health departments report harmful algal bloom (HAB) occurrences and the associated human and animal illnesses (including foodborne HAB-toxin exposures). CDC frames it explicitly as a 'One Health' system because it links human, animal, and environmental health data in one reporting structure.

## Methods
This is a description of a passive, voluntary national public-health surveillance/reporting system, not a laboratory method, statistical model, or remote-sensing technique. Structure as described on the page: state and territorial health departments (optionally assisted by designated environmental-health or animal-health partners) submit structured reports into a central CDC database across four categories — (1) harmful algal bloom occurrence/description in fresh, salt, or brackish waters, (2) food-source exposure for HAB-toxin-linked foodborne illness where no bloom was directly observed, (3) human illness cases, and (4) animal illness cases (pets, livestock, wildlife). A bloom-only report (no linked illness) is permitted. The page gives no information on case-confirmation protocols, laboratory toxin testing, data validation/QA procedures, or any statistical/predictive modeling applied to the collected data, and states no performance metrics (e.g., completeness, sensitivity, timeliness) for the surveillance system itself.

## Stated limitations
The page has no explicit 'Limitations' section, so nothing is self-labeled by CDC as a limitation. However, several structural facts stated on the page function as implicit caveats about the data: (1) reporting is described as voluntary and channeled only through state/territorial health departments, meaning coverage and completeness across jurisdictions is not characterized or quantified anywhere on this page; (2) the page names no specific organisms, cyanobacterial genera, or toxins, so it cannot support any claim about which HAB agents are captured or how; (3) the page contains zero quantitative output (no counts of blooms, illnesses, or participating jurisdictions), so it cannot itself be used as a data source for prevalence, incidence, or trend claims — only as a citation for the existence, structure, and reporting pathway of the surveillance system.

## Tensions with other findings
As a voluntary, jurisdiction-mediated reporting system with no stated completeness or validation metrics, OHHABS records (if pulled from elsewhere, e.g., a dashboard) would need to be treated as under-ascertained/non-random surveillance data rather than a representative ground truth of true bloom or illness prevalence — a caveat this 'about' page itself does not spell out but that follows from the reporting design it describes, and that matters if OHHABS-derived illness/bloom locations were ever used to validate a satellite- or in-situ-driven HAB risk model. Separately, OHHABS's scope (fresh/salt/brackish-water blooms, plus foodborne seafood exposure and animal illness) is broader than the narrower freshwater-cyanobacteria-chlorophyll focus typical of many remote-sensing-driven HAB risk tools (e.g., EPA CyAN-style satellite cyanoHAB detection), which do not observe marine HAB toxins in seafood or non-aquatic animal exposure routes — a scope mismatch worth flagging if this source is invoked alongside satellite/in-situ chemistry sources in the same analysis.

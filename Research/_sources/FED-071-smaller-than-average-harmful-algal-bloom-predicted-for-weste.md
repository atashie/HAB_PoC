# Smaller-than-average harmful algal bloom predicted for western Lake Erie

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.noaa.gov/news-release/smaller-than-average-harmful-algal-bloom-predicted-for-western-lake-erie

**What it is.** A NOAA/NCCOS news release, issued June 29, 2023, announcing the pre-season (seasonal) severity forecast for the 2023 western Lake Erie cyanobacterial harmful algal bloom: a smaller-than-average bloom (severity index expected at 3, range 2–4.5) compared with 2022 (index 6.8), produced by combining spring nutrient-loading data with precipitation-driven forecast models run by NOAA/NCCOS and partner universities/firms.

## Methods
This is a seasonal (pre-bloom), empirically driven severity forecast for the western Lake Erie cyanobacterial HAB, issued June 29, 2023 by NOAA/NCCOS with Ohio Sea Grant and university/consulting partners. Named inputs: spring nutrient (phosphorus) loading data supplied by Heidelberg University (Ohio), combined with precipitation/river-discharge information, run through models operated by NCCOS, the University of Michigan, North Carolina State University, LimnoTech, Stanford University, and the Carnegie Institution for Science (partner list corroborated via an independent WebSearch that surfaced a University of Michigan News mirror of the identical release). Field-observation/monitoring partners named for in-season support include the Ohio River Forecast Center, NOAA's Center for Operational Oceanographic Products and Services, the Great Lakes Environmental Research Laboratory, the Cooperative Institute for Great Lakes Research, Ohio Sea Grant/Stone Laboratory (Ohio State University), the University of Toledo, and the Ohio EPA. Output is a single expected severity-index value (3) with an explicit uncertainty range (2–4.5) rather than a bare point estimate, followed by an announced "early August" seasonal update and, once the bloom is visible, 5-day nowcast outlooks via an updated forecast website. Neither fetched article specifies the model(s)' statistical/mechanistic form, equations, or any retrospective accuracy/skill score against prior years. A separate WebSearch (not confirmed as verbatim text of this specific release, so treated only as corroborating background) described the general forecast mechanism as driven by total bioavailable phosphorus (TBP) loading from the Maumee River during a March–July "loading season," combined with Ohio River Forecast Center discharge forecasts, drawing on a Heidelberg University monitoring network reported elsewhere to cover 16 rivers since 1974.

## Stated limitations
The release frames its own severity number as a range (2–4.5), not a single point, explicitly because summer precipitation (especially in July) cannot be forecast far in advance, and states that larger rain events would push the index higher. It separately flags that bloom duration (tied to September wind-mixing events) is unpredictable this far ahead. Most importantly for risk communication, it explicitly decouples bloom size/severity from toxicity — a large bloom is not necessarily more toxic than a small one — and states NOAA was still "actively developing tools to detect and predict how toxic blooms will be," implying no operational toxin-concentration forecast existed at the time of this release, only a biomass/severity-index forecast. Neither fetched version of the release provides retrospective accuracy or skill statistics (e.g., an error rate of past forecasts versus observed outcomes), so the reliability of the 2–4.5 range as a calibrated uncertainty interval (versus a qualitative hedge) cannot be assessed from this text alone.

## Tensions with other findings
This is an ex-ante (pre-season) forecast, not an observed outcome — any other source in this review reporting the actual/observed 2023 western Lake Erie bloom severity is a distinct, later data point to compare against, not merge with, this forecast; this document alone cannot establish forecast accuracy. It also explicitly warns that bloom size/biomass (its severity index) is not a proxy for toxin concentration or human-health risk ("the size of a bloom isn't necessarily an indication of how toxic it is"), which complicates any other HAB source in the review that treats chlorophyll-a, cyanobacterial biomass, or remote-sensing-derived bloom extent as a direct stand-in for toxicity/health risk — the two should be kept conceptually separate. Finally, the wide year-to-year swing shown here (index 10 in 2011, 10.5 in 2015, 6.8 in 2022, forecast 2–4.5 for 2023) underscores that a single year's severity figure from any one source should not be read as "typical" for western Lake Erie without multi-year context.

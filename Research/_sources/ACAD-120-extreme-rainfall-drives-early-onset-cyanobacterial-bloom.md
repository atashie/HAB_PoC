# Extreme rainfall drives early onset cyanobacterial bloom

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/570275.full.pdf

**What it is.** A field case study documenting how a single extreme-rainfall/flood event in a eutrophic Ontario reservoir (Conestogo Lake) triggered an atypically early, toxin-producing cyanobacterial bloom, tracked through paired in-situ water-chemistry, phytoplankton-community, and cyanotoxin sampling before and after the storm. It directly links a weather-driven hydrologic/nutrient pulse to a shift in bloom timing (no remote-sensing data are used).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Following a 2017 extreme rainfall/flood event, Conestogo Lake (a eutrophic Ontario reservoir) developed an atypically early, toxin-producing cyanobacterial bloom rather than its usual late-summer bloom.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the paper's core framing sentence describing the study design and headline result. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "an atypically early toxin-producing cyanobacterial bloom"
- **[✓ verified]** Bioavailable phosphorus in surface waters rose more than 27-fold after the flood, preceding a marked Aphanizomenon flos-aquae increase about 2 weeks post-event — roughly 5 weeks earlier than the reservoir's typical bloom onset.
  - *evidence:* This is the paper's central quantitative result tying the nutrient pulse to the timing shift. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "An increase in bioavailable phosphorus by more than 27-fold in surface waters preceded notable increases in Aphanizomenon flos-aquae throughout the reservoir approximately 2 weeks postevent and ∼5 weeks before blooms typically occur."
- **[✓ verified]** The storm was hydrologically extreme: record daily rainfall (78 mm at Conestogo Dam; watershed-wide range 40-120 mm) on 23 June 2017 sharply raised inflow (from a baseline mean of 4.56 m3/s to 219 m3/s, peaking at 521 m3/s), added 3.5 Mm3 to reservoir volume, replaced ~80% of reservoir volume, and cut residence time to 6.04 days versus a normal range of 26-119 days (mean 43 days).
  - *evidence:* Describes the physical magnitude of the flood pulse invoked as the mechanism (dilution/flushing plus nutrient loading) behind the early bloom. (Results (site/hydrology description))
  - *quote:* "Reservoir residence time during this period was 6.04 d, as compared with the normal ranges between 26 and 119 d (mean = 43 d)."
- **[✓ verified]** A. flos-aquae dominated cyanobacterial biomass (95-97% of the cyanobacterial fraction at all sites) and peaked at 10,900 mg/m3 (transitional zone, 19 July) and 4,710 mg/m3 (lacustrine zone, ~25 July), yet total cyanobacterial biomass overall (mean 1550 ± 2920 mg/m3) showed no significant relationship with sampling time or site.
  - *evidence:* Shows the bloom response was taxon-specific and spatially/temporally patchy for the community as a whole, despite a clear species-specific timing signal — an internal null result alongside the positive finding. (Results (phytoplankton biomass))
  - *quote:* "There was no relationship between total cyanobacterial biomass and time or site according to repeated measures ANOVA."
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanotoxins (anabaenopeptin-A, microcystin-LR/-YR/-RR) were present through the 3-5 week bloom period, detectable in 87% of samples (n=13) and quantifiable in 73% (n=11); MC-LR was the dominant congener (highest zone mean 74.46 ± 156.5 ng/L, transitional zone), and total microcystin never exceeded the cited 20,000 ng/L recreational guideline.
  - *evidence:* Gives the toxicological side of the case: toxins were confirmed and dominated by MC-LR, but this particular event stayed under the stated regulatory comparator throughout. (Results (toxins))
  - *quote:* "Total microcystin concentration did not exceed the recreational limit for total microcystins (20 000 ng·L−1)."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors interpret the result as evidence that extreme rainfall can elongate the cyanobacterial bloom/toxicity season by shifting onset earlier, while stating explicitly that the magnitude of this effect is contingent on storm timing and reservoir physical structure.
  - *evidence:* The paper's own generalization plus its own caveat on how far the single-event finding should be extended. (Discussion/Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "extreme rainfall can trigger early cyanobacterial bloom initiation, effectively elongating the bloom season period of potential toxicity"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors draw a forward-looking management implication: because extreme-rainfall events are projected to increase faster than total precipitation under climate change, managing blooms using historical/average conditions may become inadequate.
  - *evidence:* Connects the single-event finding to a broader climate-adaptation argument for reservoir/bloom management. (Discussion/Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "Managers cannot effectively manage a future ecosystem based on averages of the past."

## Methods
Single-reservoir, single-event observational field study (Conestogo Lake, Mapleton Township, Ontario), built around the record 23 June 2017 storm. Per the study's own directly-fetched public code repository (GitHub: biogeochem/formbloom_conestogo_2017HAB, confirmed as "Larsen et al. 2019" Conestogo Lake analysis code): repeated-measures ANOVA via generalized least squares (nlme::gls) with AR(1) correlation structure applied to water-chemistry variables (SRP, TDP, TP, NO3, TDN, chlorophyll-a, dissolved oxygen, temperature, PAR) and to toxin congeners (MC-LR, MC-RR, MC-YR, AP-A) by day-of-year and site (site as a random effect "showed no benefit" over fixed effects); redundancy analysis (RDA) of phytoplankton community composition constrained by z-standardized environmental variables, tested with a 999-permutation test; Kendall rank correlation between cyanobacterial biomass and toxin concentrations. Underlying data: reservoir/sonde chemistry profiles, PAR readings, phytoplankton taxonomy/community matrices, toxin concentrations, precipitation and streamflow records (partly via Grand River Conservation Authority), merged across a riverine-to-lacustrine sampling gradient. Where it works: detects a large, fast, well-resolved nutrient (phosphorus) pulse and an early, species-specific (A. flos-aquae) bloom-timing signal tied to the flood. Where it is limited: total cyanobacterial biomass itself showed no significant time or site effect by repeated-measures ANOVA — the clear timing signal was specific to the dominant taxon, not a uniform whole-community response — and toxin levels, while detected, stayed under the cited recreational guideline throughout. Funded by Global Water Futures (FORMBLOOM) and Genome Canada/Quebec (ATRAPP); data also on Figshare (10.6084/m9.figshare.7811963).

## Stated limitations
The paper frames its own scope narrowly: it states that "effects will vary depending on factors including the timing of rainfall and reservoir physical structure," and that impact on primary production is tied to "event timing (e.g., early vs. late summer) and the zonal gradient created by basin morphometry differences" — i.e., the authors do not claim the Conestogo result generalizes across all reservoirs or storm timings. The study is explicitly motivated by a stated evidence gap ("few studies have directly documented the effects of extreme precipitation events on cyanobacterial composition, biomass, and toxin production"), underscoring this as an early, single-event data point rather than a replicated finding. By design (inferred from the described methods, not a verbatim author admission), this is a single-reservoir, single-storm observational case study with no non-flood counterfactual reservoir-year sampled in parallel, so the phosphorus-pulse-to-early-bloom link, while temporally well-resolved, rests on association plus a plausible nutrient mechanism rather than a manipulated causal test. The results themselves show internal heterogeneity: total cyanobacterial biomass had "no relationship...with time or site" by repeated-measures ANOVA, meaning the clear timing signal was specific to the dominant taxon (A. flos-aquae), not the whole community. ## Tensions with other findings
(1) Correlation vs. causation: despite the causal-sounding title verb ("drives"), the core evidence is a single storm, single reservoir, before/after case study — phosphorus rose and A. flos-aquae biomass rose ~2 weeks later — which is a strong temporal association plus a plausible bioavailable-nutrient mechanism, not a controlled/manipulated causal experiment; this entry treats "extreme rainfall → earlier bloom" as a well-evidenced driver hypothesis from one event, not a proven general causal law. (2) It complicates simple temperature- or calendar-based bloom-timing heuristics common in basic HAB science: here a hydrology/nutrient-pulse pathway shifted bloom onset ~5 weeks earlier than the reservoir's usual late-summer timing, i.e., an acute weather-driven nutrient pulse can override the "typical" seasonal thermal clock for bloom timing — directly relevant to any framing that timing/early-warning matters more than eventual bloom detection. (3) It is consistent with, and adds a mechanistic precipitation pathway to, the broader literature linking nutrient loading and climatic/precipitation change to HAB prevalence, per the paper's own introductory framing. (4) Internally the finding is more nuanced than the headline 27-fold/2-week/5-week statistic suggests: total cyanobacterial biomass showed no significant time or site effect, and toxin concentrations stayed under the cited recreational guideline throughout this event — so the paper documents an earlier-starting but still regulatorily-modest bloom, not a larger or more toxic one. (5) This is a purely in-situ, ground-truth field study (chemistry, sonde, and taxonomy sampling); it contains no satellite/remote-sensing signal, so it can support a bloom-timing/driver narrative but cannot itself speak to remote-sensing detection performance.

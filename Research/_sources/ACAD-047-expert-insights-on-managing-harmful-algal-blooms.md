# Expert insights on managing harmful algal blooms

**What it is.** A qualitative, peer-reviewed research article reporting 28 semi-structured key-informant interviews (31 water managers and researchers across New England, Ohio, and the Mountain West U.S.) on how practitioners perceive current freshwater HAB management — prevention, forecasting, monitoring, response/treatment, and communication — and what they identify as priorities and gaps going forward. It is a perceptions/priorities study, not a quantitative model or remote-sensing analysis.

## Methods
Qualitative study design: 28 semi-structured key-informant interviews (31 informants — environmental planners, laboratory managers/analysts, water-quality managers and program directors, and researchers, mostly from government agencies) across New England (12), Ohio (15), and the Mountain West (4). Participants were identified via purposive sampling through professional contacts, supplemented by snowball referrals from interviewees. Interviews were conducted online via Microsoft Teams in spring/summer 2022, audio-recorded and transcribed, then imported into NVivo (Version 12) for open coding, axial coding, and thematic analysis; intercoder reliability was checked between two coders on 3 interviews before coding the remaining set (agreement >97%), and coding of the full set was then split by region (one coder for all New England interviews; another for Ohio and Mountain West). The study received an IRB exemption from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill (Study #21-1840). This is a purely qualitative/perceptions method — no remote-sensing, in-situ chemistry, or statistical/quantitative modeling was performed by the authors themselves; where such data (e.g., satellite trend statistics, economic-loss surveys) appear, they are cited from other studies (e.g., Fang et al., 2022; Bennett et al., 2020) as background, not generated here. The authors note the method works well for eliciting on-the-ground practitioner themes and priorities with a documented high intercoder-reliability check, but by design it is a small, non-random, three-region sample of subjective perceptions rather than a statistically representative or causal analysis; findings framed as "perceived" trends (e.g., a "7-fold increase" cited by one informant) are explicitly informant impressions, not independently verified counts. Publication note (from the source, not a stated scientific limitation): the publication fee was supported by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, and two of the three authors are EPA employees; the article includes a standard conflict-of-interest disclaimer ("the research was conducted in the absence of any commercial or financial relationships that could be construed as a potential conflict of interest").

## Stated limitations
The authors themselves state: (1) the study covers only three U.S. regions (New England, Ohio, Mountain West), and additional interviews from other U.S. regions and other countries "could create a more holistic view of management practices and identify new transferable best practices for management and risk communication"; (2) there is a broader need for future work on the "human dimensions" of HAB management beyond what this study covers; (3) the research was deliberately framed around recreation-related HABs concerns and did not directly solicit views on drinking-water concerns, yet many informants raised drinking water anyway, which the authors flag as indicating a need for future work focused specifically on drinking water. Beyond these author-stated points, the qualitative design (purposive/snowball sample of 31 individuals) inherently limits statistical generalizability, though the authors frame this as a scope-for-future-work point rather than a flaw.

## Tensions with other findings
As a qualitative perceptions study, its "findings" are the stated opinions/experiences of 31 practitioners, not independently validated causal or efficacy results — any claim about what "works" (e.g., treatment effectiveness, forecasting accuracy) should be read as reported practitioner perception, not as this paper's own controlled evaluation. Two points of note for a HAB treatment/management case study: (1) informants' view that most current chemical/physical surface-water treatments are "not very effective, cause a lot of ecological damage" and are merely symptom-treatment sits in tension with any vendor or product-efficacy claims for algaecide/treatment technologies, and should be weighed as one stakeholder perspective, not a treatment-efficacy trial; (2) the paper's finding that forecasting is "not yet accurate enough at a local scale for widespread use" is a practitioner-skepticism counterpoint to more optimistic framings of satellite/remote-sensing-based cyanoHAB forecasting tools (e.g., EPA CyAN's weekly forecasts), suggesting a gap between forecast-system capability claims and field-manager confidence in applying them locally.

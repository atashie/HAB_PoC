# Biomanipulation as a nature-based solution to reduce cyanobacterial blooms

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed literature-review article in Aquatic Ecology (Triest, Stiers &amp; Van Onsem, Vrije Universiteit Brussel) that synthesizes 48 publications covering 34 whole-lake or large-scale case studies of "biomanipulation" — ecological/food-web interventions such as fish removal, piscivorous or planktivorous fish management, Daphnia/bivalve introduction, and macrophyte restoration — used as nature-based solutions to avoid or reduce cyanobacterial blooms. It proposes a 3-part conceptual scheme (direct bloom abatement; risk reduction; bloom prevention) for when and why these interventions succeed or fail.

## Methods
Not a primary experimental study: a systematic literature review. The authors screened published limnological literature for studies that (a) explicitly report the extent of a cyanobacterial bloom problem, (b) name the cyanobacteria taxa involved, and (c) describe whole-lake or large-scale (as opposed to mesocosm/lab-only) biomanipulation interventions, arriving at a final corpus of 48 publications / 34 whole-lake or large-scale case studies. Interventions synthesized across that corpus: fish removal with water drawdown; stocking of piscivorous fish; addition of filter-feeding planktivorous fish; introduction of Daphnia or bivalves; re-introduction/restoration of submerged macrophytes; and combinations of these. Per the abstract, the review reports that success or failure in controlling blooms of the eight named taxa depended on bottlenecks in fish-removal completeness, stocking densities, trophic-cascade strength, zooplankton grazing/diet shifts, macrophyte recovery, and nutrient/pH status; threshold values distinguishing success from failure are said to be synthesized from prior experiments/monitoring studies into the paper's 3-part conceptual scheme, but the specific numeric threshold values themselves reside in the paper's body/tables, which we could not access (full text is behind a Springer subscription paywall; an open-access-repository PDF mirror we located could not be parsed by our fetch tool - see fetch_notes).

## Stated limitations
The only self-stated limitation we could confirm from the accessible text is the abstract's closing sentence: the authors say more information is needed on the temporal dynamics and abundance of cyanobacteria taxa in whole lakes before and after biomanipulation, in order to properly evaluate the applicability and effectiveness of these nature-based solutions. We could not access the paper's Discussion/Conclusion sections (paywalled full text; an open-access PDF mirror could not be parsed by our tools), so no further author-stated limitations (e.g., on review methodology, publication bias, or cross-lake generalizability) can be confirmed beyond this single sentence.

## Tensions with other findings
This review frames cyanobacteria control as achievable through slow, ecosystem-level food-web restructuring (fish-community and macrophyte management) whose success is explicitly threshold- and context-dependent (nutrient/pH status, stocking density, grazing capacity) and can fail through several named mechanisms. That framing is in tension with any treatment approach presented as a single, fast, broadly reliable fix (e.g., a chemical algaecide application): biomanipulation as described in this abstract is slower-acting, more context-sensitive, and only partially validated - the authors themselves say whole-lake pre/post evidence on cyanobacteria dynamics is still insufficient to fully judge effectiveness. Separately, this source's own bibliographic identity is reported inconsistently across the databases we checked (Springer: Vol. 50 / 106 citations; ouci.dntb.gov.ua: Vol. 49 / 96 citations / 140 references) - worth verifying against the publisher of record before citing specific figures from this paper elsewhere in the dossier.

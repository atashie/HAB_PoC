# Schindler's legacy: from eutrophic lakes to the phosphorus utilization strategies of cyanobacteria

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://academic.oup.com/femsre/article/46/6/fuac029/6617595

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed literature review (FEMS Microbiology Reviews, Nov 2022, DOI 10.1093/femsre/fuac029) that frames itself as a ~50-year update to David Schindler's 1970s whole-lake research establishing phosphorus as a driver of cyanobacterial blooms, synthesizing physiological, molecular/genomic, and modelling literature on how freshwater cyanobacteria measure, take up, store, and scavenge phosphorus (P) and why this makes them strong competitors in eutrophic lakes, while identifying persistent knowledge gaps.

## Methods
This is a literature review/synthesis, not a primary data study. It does not collect new field or lab data itself; it compiles and re-analyzes findings from many prior studies (including physiological measurements gathered into supplementary Tables S1-S3) drawn from: (1) laboratory Pi-uptake kinetics experiments using radioisotope (32P/33P) flux methods and Michaelis-Menten (Kv, Vmax), Monod, and Droop cell-quota kinetic models; (2) field measurements of orthophosphate (Pi) and dissolved organic phosphorus (DOP) in lakes/reservoirs, including critiques of colorimetric (e.g., molybdate/p-NPP) and fluorometric (4-MUP) assay methods; (3) enzyme activity assays for alkaline/acid phosphatase, including taxon-resolved enzyme-labeled fluorescence (ELF); (4) genomic, transcriptomic and metatranscriptomic studies of P-acquisition genes (the Pho regulon: pst, pho, phn gene clusters; ppk/ppx for polyphosphate turnover); and (5) models linking P loading to lake trophic state. The review explicitly cautions that laboratory-derived kinetic parameters are shaped by light, mixing, pH, temperature, sodium availability and prior nutrient history of cultures, and that applying them to natural populations "requires careful consideration of common potential artefacts arising from laboratory methods."

## Stated limitations
The authors state several limitations inherent to the underlying evidence base they review, not just to their own synthesis: (1) "Analytical methods for measuring P are only proxies for bioavailable P," and standard colorimetric Pi methods are "likely to be an inaccurate representation of bioavailable P," especially at low, ecologically relevant concentrations; (2) DOP estimated by subtraction (total P minus Pi) suffers because "small measurement errors are amplified if the difference is close to zero"; (3) phosphatase activity assays are "typically determined at saturating (c.f. in situ) phosphomonoester substrate concentrations," so they measure maximal, not necessarily in-situ, activity; (4) "substantial intra- and inter-specific variations in P utilization strategies have been identified," complicating generalization across species/strains; (5) lab-culture findings need care before being applied to natural field populations because of artefacts such as prior nutrient history of the cultured organisms; (6) P-cyanobacteria relationships are "complicated by nutrients other than P that potentially limit growth, including N, carbon, sulphur, and micronutrients" (co-limitation); and (7) overall, the review concludes that despite decades of physiological measurement, "our understanding of the critical processes that drive species diversity, adaptation and competition remains limited," with "persistent critical knowledge gaps, particularly on the adaptation of cyanobacteria to low nutrient concentrations."

(2) The review argues that "P-related traits are rarely mentioned" among the classic traits invoked to explain cyanobacterial bloom dominance (e.g., N2-fixation, buoyancy, toxin production) — implying a tension between how the field conventionally explains bloom dominance and what this review argues is an under-recognized driver. (3) For the HAB_PoC project specifically: this is a mechanistic/physiological (basic-science) source, not a remote-sensing, monitoring, or predictive-modelling source — it substantiates *why* nutrient/phosphorus covariates should matter biologically as an in-situ driver to fuse with satellite cyanobacteria signals, but it provides no lake-specific dataset, no field-deployable threshold, and no forecasting model; its own caveat is that lab-derived kinetic parameters require care before being applied to a specific natural waterbody's population.

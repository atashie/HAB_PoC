# Oregon DEQ Freshwater Cyanobacteria Harmful Algal Blooms (CyanoHABs) Strategy

**What it is.** A 2023 Oregon DEQ operational strategy document (Version 2.1, October 2023) that lays out the agency's decision points, workflows, and regulatory thresholds for detecting, monitoring, and managing freshwater cyanobacteria harmful algal blooms (CyanoHABs) across three tracks — drinking-water protection, recreational-use public health response, and Clean Water Act (303(d)/TMDL) impairment assessment — in coordination with the Oregon Health Authority. It is explicitly an internal operational manual, not a research or causal-driver study.

## Methods
This is a policy/operations strategy document, not an empirical or modeling study. It presents rule-based regulatory workflows (flowcharts with decision points) rather than a statistical or machine-learning model. Cyanotoxin detection relies on standard laboratory analysis of water samples (via DEQ's Laboratory and Environmental Assessment Division, LEAD, or other accredited labs) for total microcystins and cylindrospermopsin, with a tiered, fixed-threshold sampling cadence (biweekly baseline -> weekly -> daily) keyed to exceedance of numeric action/advisory levels. Drinking-water source "susceptibility" is determined via a qualitative, criteria-based checklist (historical detections, downstream hydrologic position, temperature, nutrient loading, satellite imagery, and cyanotoxin-producing gene presence) rather than a quantified, weighted, or validated predictive model; no accuracy, sensitivity, or performance metric is reported anywhere in the excerpt for this determination or for any other prediction in the document. Waterbody impairment listing and TMDL development follow the Clean Water Act Section 303(d) Integrated Report assessment process, also a regulatory/administrative method rather than a statistical model.

## Stated limitations
The document explicitly scopes itself to freshwater CyanoHABs and states it "does not address harmful algal blooms that occur in estuarine and marine environments" (marine/estuarine material is relegated to a separate background-only Appendix A). It also explicitly states it "does not contain in-depth background information, current areas of research, or policy positions on CyanoHABs," directing readers instead to the 2011 DEQ strategy, US EPA's CyanoHABs website, and the Interstate Technology Regulatory Council for that content. It identifies itself as an operational manual whose "primary audience... is the State of Oregon and DEQ management and staff," not a scientific analysis, and notes that several recommended actions/subprograms "may require additional funding from state, federal, or other applicable sources" that is not guaranteed.

## Tensions with other findings
No direct contradiction of other HAB findings is evident in the extracted text, but two boundary conditions matter for use alongside other sources in this review. First, the document itself disclaims providing "current areas of research" on CyanoHAB causes, so its passing statement that bloom frequency/extent/magnitude "can respond to" temperature, nutrient loading, and hydrologic conditions is an unquantified, associative background remark, not a tested driver analysis — it should not be cited as causal or even correlational evidence, only as motivation. Second, its numeric thresholds (raw-water action levels of 0.20/0.30 µg/L; finished-water advisory levels of 0.3-3 µg/L) are EPA/Oregon regulatory human-health cutoffs for drinking water, not bloom-ecology or remote-sensing detection thresholds — they operate on a different basis than, and should not be conflated with, any satellite-reflectance or cyanobacterial-cell-count threshold used elsewhere in this literature review (e.g., EPA CyAN cyanobacteria index thresholds), since they concern toxin concentration in treated water rather than bloom presence/intensity in the source waterbody.

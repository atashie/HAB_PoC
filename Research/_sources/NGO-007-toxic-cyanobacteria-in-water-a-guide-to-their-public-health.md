# Toxic cyanobacteria in water: a guide to their public health consequences, monitoring and management — Second edition

**What it is.** An 859-page, 15-chapter WHO-edited (Chorus & Welker, 2021) technical guide that synthesizes the scientific and practical basis for identifying, assessing, monitoring, and managing public-health risk from cyanobacteria and their toxins in drinking-water and recreational-water settings. It is explicitly the technical background document underlying the WHO 2020 Guidelines for drinking-water quality and Guidelines for safe recreational water environments.

## Methods
TCiW is not a primary empirical study but a multi-author edited technical guidance volume that synthesizes peer-reviewed limnological, toxicological, and public-health literature into a practical risk-assessment and management framework: hazard identification across the water-use system from catchment to consumer (Ch.6); nutrient-load and waterbody-condition control (Ch.7-8); drinking-water offtake risk management and cyanotoxin-occurrence control (Ch.9-10); monitoring-programme design and fieldwork (Ch.11-12); laboratory analysis of cyanobacteria/water chemistry and cyanotoxin bioassays, including microscopy-based cell-count/biovolume quantification (Ch.13-14); and public health surveillance, communication and participation (Ch.15). Numeric thresholds referenced in this dossier (e.g., TP ~20-50 µg/L) are themselves derived from limnological literature synthesized within individual chapters, not from new data collected by the book itself. Because direct WebFetch of the WHO chapter PDFs (cdn.who.int) returned only unparseable binary/encoded content (not extractable text), the chapter-level claims and numbers in this dossier were recovered via a secondary WHO document (WHO Guidelines on Recreational Water Quality, NCBI Bookshelf NBK572631) that extensively and explicitly cites TCiW chapter-by-chapter with named authors and years — notably, this secondary document is itself the very 2020 recreational-water guideline that the landing page says TCiW was written to support, which increases (but does not guarantee) confidence that its quotations are faithful to the original TCiW text.

## Stated limitations
The WHO landing page itself states no explicit limitations of the guide (it presents the content as current state-of-knowledge background). The one self-stated caveat recovered (via the secondhand citation route, attributed to TCiW, Welker et al., 2021) is that monitoring approaches such as remote sensing, despite existing basic knowledge, "need to be further developed for affordable practical application" — i.e., the guide itself flags a readiness/affordability gap in some monitoring technologies rather than presenting them as fully mature. Because WebFetch could not extract readable text directly from the WHO chapter PDFs (they returned only compressed/encoded PDF streams per the tool's own output), a complete accounting of the guide's self-stated caveats, data gaps, or uncertainty ranges could not be independently verified from primary chapter text in this research pass; only the caveats surfaced through the secondary citing document are reported here.

## Tensions with other findings
Via the Ibelings et al. (2021) chapter (recovered secondhand), TCiW explicitly complicates a simple "warmer water → more cyanobacterial blooms" narrative that appears in some HAB literature and popular framing, arguing that climate change's indirect effects on nutrient and hydrophysical regimes can outweigh the direct temperature-growth-rate effect — relevant caution for any driver/attribution analysis in this project that treats temperature as a primary or simple causal driver of bloom risk (correlation between warming and bloom incidence should not be read as a simple direct causal growth-rate effect per this source). Separately, TCiW's emphasis on microscopy-based cell-count/biovolume quantification (Padisák et al. 2021) and multi-parameter, lab-verified monitoring as the more precise (if labor-intensive) risk-assessment approach sits in tension with satellite/remote-sensing-first approaches such as EPA CyAN that this project's Part A leans on: TCiW itself (Welker et al. 2021, recovered secondhand) states that remote-sensing-based cyanobacteria detection still "need[s] to be further developed for affordable practical application," i.e., it does not present satellite indices as a full substitute for direct biomass/toxin quantification.

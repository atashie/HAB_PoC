# Rapid flotation of Microcystis wesenbergii mediated by high light exposure: implications for surface scum formation and cyanobacterial species succession

**What it is.** A laboratory experimental study comparing two single-cell Microcystis strains (M. wesenbergii FACHB-908 and M. aeruginosa FACHB-905) under high light to identify a non-buoyancy-driven, hours-scale flotation mechanism (photosynthetic micro-bubble trapping plus EPS-mediated cell aggregation) that produces rapid surface scum, distinct from the classically described gas-vesicle buoyancy-regulation pathway.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Under high light (150 μmol photons s⁻¹ m⁻²), M. wesenbergii migrated upward rapidly and formed visible surface scum within 4 hours, whereas M. aeruginosa did not form visible scum for the duration of the experiment.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the abstract and results as the paper's central empirical observation, motivating the rest of the mechanistic investigation. (Abstract; Results)
  - *quote:* "M. wesenbergii exhibited fast upward migration and formed surface scum within 4 hours, while M. aeruginosa did not form visible scum throughout the experiments."
- **[✓ verified]** The rapid flotation is associated with photosynthesis-driven dissolved-oxygen (DO) supersaturation and formation of micro-bubbles (50–200 µm diameter) that become trapped in/on cell aggregates, providing lift to the surface.
  - *evidence:* DO and bubble measurements taken dynamically during the light treatment; authors interpret bubble trapping, alongside EPS aggregation, as the physical lift mechanism for scum formation. (Abstract; Results (DO/bubble section); Discussion)
  - *quote:* "We found supersaturated DO and the formation of micro-bubbles (50–200 µm in diameter) in M. wesenbergii treatments. M. aeruginosa produces bubbles in small quantities and small sizes."
- **[✓ verified]** DO rose far faster under light in M. wesenbergii (5 mg L⁻¹ h⁻¹) than in M. aeruginosa (2 mg L⁻¹ h⁻¹), with M. wesenbergii reaching the DO probe's detection limit (42 mg/L) within about 300 minutes.
  - *evidence:* Quantitative DO time-series comparison between the two species under identical light treatment, used to link photosynthetic oxygen output to bubble formation rate. (Results (DO dynamics))
  - *quote:* "5 mg L⁻¹ h⁻¹"
- **[✓ verified]** M. wesenbergii secreted significantly more extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) - both dissolved and bound polysaccharides and proteins - than M. aeruginosa, and this EPS is proposed to mediate cell-cell adhesion via a 'bridging' network of functional groups.
  - *evidence:* EPS was quantified biochemically (Coomassie protein assay, phenol-sulfuric acid polysaccharide assay) and by FT-IR functional-group analysis; ANOVA reported the between-species EPS differences as statistically significant. (Abstract; Results (EPS quantification); Discussion)
  - *quote:* "The main functional groups in the extracellular substances secreted by Microcystis, such as hydroxyl and carboxyl groups, could have acted as binding sites and bridges with the high-molecular-weight adsorption sites of Microcystis S-EPS to form a network structure."
- **[✓ verified]** Zeta potential of M. wesenbergii cell suspensions became markedly less negative over the light exposure (from below −30 mV toward about −5 mV), consistent with reduced electrostatic repulsion enabling aggregation, while M. aeruginosa showed no significant zeta-potential change.
  - *evidence:* Zeta potential measured dynamically during the light treatment as an indicator of surface-charge-driven cell-cell repulsion/attraction; presented as one of several converging lines of evidence (with EPS and bubbles) for why M. wesenbergii aggregates and M. aeruginosa does not. (Results (zeta potential))
  - *quote:* "significantly increased to a lower value of (−5 mV)"
- **[✓ verified]** M. wesenbergii's surface-layer samples had much higher polysaccharide concentrations (dissolved 41.8 mg/L, bound 72.2 mg/L) than its mixed/water-column samples (dissolved 10.7 mg/L, bound 6.6 mg/L), whereas M. aeruginosa showed no significant difference in polysaccharide content between surface and water-column samples.
  - *evidence:* Direct biochemical measurement comparing surface-scum layer vs. bulk water-column samples for each species, used to argue that EPS accumulates preferentially at the surface specifically in the scum-forming species. (Results (EPS by depth/location))
  - *quote:* "content of polysaccharides secreted on the surface and the water column was basically the same"
- **[⚠ partial]** The observed hours-scale scum formation is faster than the days-to-weeks timescale typically described for conventional buoyancy (gas-vesicle)-driven Microcystis bloom/scum formation, implying an additional mechanism operates under strong light.
  - *evidence:* Framed by the authors as the motivating knowledge gap in the Introduction, contrasting prior buoyancy-regulation literature with their own hours-scale observation. (Introduction)
  - *quote:* "There are exceptions where Microcystis forms scum on a shorter timescale of hours and can persist at the water surface even under high light conditions during the daytime. This may imply the existence of additional mechanisms for the rapid Microcystis flotation and the surface scum formation under strong light conditions."
  - *reviewer:* Source text supports the claim that an additional mechanism operates under strong light and that hours-scale scum formation is exceptional. However, the source does NOT explicitly state that conventional buoyancy-driven formation takes 'days-to-weeks'—that timescale is not present in the provided text and appears hallucinated.
- **[✓ verified]** The authors conclude the mechanism is species-specific and propose it has implications for surface scum formation and succession among cyanobacterial species (i.e., which Microcystis species/morphotypes come to dominate a bloom's surface layer).
  - *evidence:* Stated as the paper's overarching interpretive conclusion; this is an inference drawn from a two-strain laboratory comparison, not a field-validated succession study. (Abstract; Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "Our study reveals a species-specific mechanism for the rapid floatation of Microcystis, providing novel insights into surface scum formation as well as succession of cyanobacterial species."
- **[✓ verified]** The authors' three-part conclusion is that (a) EPS from M. wesenbergii contributes to large aggregate formation, (b) aggregate plus micro-bubble formation together drive surface scum formation, and (c) light and EPS jointly contribute to formation of the large algal aggregate.
  - *evidence:* Explicit bulleted conclusion synthesizing the EPS, zeta-potential, and bubble results into a combined causal narrative for scum formation. (Conclusion)
  - *quote:* "(a) EPS produced by M. wesenbergii can contribute to the formation of large aggregates. (b) The formation of aggregate and micro-bubble can drive the surface scum formation. (c) Light and EPS contributed to the formation of the large algal aggregate."
- **[⚠ partial]** The authors explicitly caveat that their experiments used laboratory strains under simplified indoor conditions and that the mechanism still needs to be tested in natural samples/populations, which may behave differently (e.g., environmental M. wesenbergii forms large colonies with complex vertical-migration dynamics).
  - *evidence:* Stated limitation in the Discussion, explicitly separating what was shown in the lab from what remains to be shown in field/natural populations. (Discussion (limitations))
  - *quote:* "While similar experiments on indoor samples may still be qualitative, it would be interesting to test the mechanisms identified in this study using natural samples."
  - *reviewer:* Source clearly states the need to test in natural samples. However, the parenthetical example '(e.g., environmental M. wesenbergii forms large colonies with complex vertical-migration dynamics)' is NOT in the provided source text and appears hallucinated—the source does not describe environmental M. wesenbergii behavior.

## Methods
Controlled laboratory comparison of two single-cell Microcystis strains (M. wesenbergii FACHB-908, M. aeruginosa FACHB-905) diluted to equal initial chlorophyll-a (~800 μg/L) in triplicate 50 mL glass tubes (20 cm height), under two conditions: strong light (150 μmol photons s⁻¹ m⁻², side-mounted LED) versus darkness, at 25°C. Measured dynamically: dissolved oxygen and zeta potential at the air-water interface; cell/aggregate imaging by camera and confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) with SYTO63 (cells), FITC (proteins), and Calcofluor white (polysaccharides) stains; cell density by hemocytometer under light microscopy. EPS was fractionated into soluble (S-EPS) and bound (B-EPS) pools via centrifugation (11,000 g, 4°C, 15 min) and sonication (25 kHz, 45°C), then quantified for protein (Coomassie brilliant blue) and polysaccharide (phenol-sulfuric acid) content; EPS composition/functional groups further characterized by 3D excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy (Hitachi F4700) and Fourier-transform infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy (Nicolet 6700) on lyophilized samples. Group differences were tested by ANOVA (SPSS v25; p<0.05 threshold), with plots generated in Origin 8.0. This design worked to demonstrate a clear light-dependent, species-specific divergence (M. wesenbergii vs. M. aeruginosa) in DO/bubble output, zeta potential shift, and EPS quantity/location; it was not designed to (and does not) test field/natural bloom populations, mixed-species assemblages, or a broader panel of Microcystis strains/morphotypes, and does not directly isolate the specific EPS chemical substances responsible for binding.

## Stated limitations
The authors state that the underlying mechanisms of cyanobacterial EPS aggregation and binding to form algal blooms were "still poorly understood, and more work is needed to elucidate these binding processes." They explicitly flag that their indoor/laboratory experiments "may still be qualitative" and that testing "the mechanisms identified in this study using natural samples" remains to be done, implicitly acknowledging that environmental M. wesenbergii populations (which form large colonies with complex vertical-migration/glycan-ballast dynamics) may not behave identically to the single-cell lab strains used here. They also state future work should identify "the structure and properties" of the specific EPS substances that facilitate aggregation, indicating the causal chemical agents were not fully isolated in this study. Only two strains (one per species) were tested, limiting generalization across the genus.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper complicates the conventional HAB framework (which it itself cites) attributing Microcystis surface scum mainly to buoyancy regulation via gas vesicles in larger colonies operating over a timescale of days to weeks; here the authors report an hours-scale, non-buoyancy mechanism (photosynthetic micro-bubble trapping plus EPS-driven aggregation) in single-cell strains under strong light. This matters for HAB early-warning/remote-sensing framing: if scum can form within hours under high light, satellite revisit intervals (e.g., Sentinel-3/Landsat-based CyAN products, which are daily-to-weekly) may systematically miss or misdate scum onset relative to what a persistence/buoyancy-only model would assume, and in-situ sampling timed even a few hours off could catch a water body in a very different aggregation state. Also note: any driver language here (light -> photosynthetic O2 -> bubbles/EPS -> aggregation -> scum) reflects a controlled, single-species laboratory manipulation; it is a plausible mechanistic pathway, not a causally validated field-scale process, and a passage in the paper's broader discussion linking global warming to dominance of temperature-adapted cyanobacteria appears to draw on cited literature rather than on a temperature manipulation actually performed in this experiment, so it was excluded from key_claims here.

# Welcome to the IOC Harmful Algal Bloom Website (GlobalHAB Programme)

**What it is.** The official website of the IOC-UNESCO Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) Programme - an institutional portal (not a research paper) that describes the Programme's mission, its governing body IPHAB (IOC-FAO Intergovernmental Panel on Harmful Algal Blooms), the IOC-SCOR GlobalHAB research initiative, the HAIS/HAEDAT information systems, and decades of associated technical publications, training courses, and regional networks aimed at helping coastal member states understand, predict, and mitigate harmful algal blooms.

## Methods
This is an institutional/programmatic web portal, not a primary research study, so it does not itself apply a data-analysis method. It functions as (a) a mission/governance description of the IOC HAB Programme, IPHAB, and GlobalHAB; (b) a gateway to two live data systems it names but does not itself present data from on this page - HAIS (Harmful Algal Information System, hosted at data.hais.ioc-unesco.org: taxonomic names, HAB event/biogeographic information, an expert directory, and an algal-toxin database added in 2021) and HAEDAT (Harmful Algal Event Database); and (c) a bibliographic index (the /publications/ page) of external technical manuals, guides, technical-series reports, and conference proceedings that themselves would contain methods (e.g., ocean-colour radiometry observation guides, toxin-monitoring plan design guides, climate-change-effects study guidelines) - this page does not reproduce those methods itself.

## Stated limitations
The page is an institutional/programmatic portal, not a research article, and contains no self-critical methods/limitations section of its own. Its headline scope claim ("every coastal country in the world is potentially affected") is asserted without any citation, percentage, count, or uncertainty language - it is programme framing/motivational language, not a measured finding, and should not be cited as a quantitative fact. The footer copyright ("© 2021") is stale relative to the live page's actual content, which lists events and training scheduled through 2027 - an internal currency inconsistency worth flagging for anyone citing an "as of" date from this site. Separately, one item in this dossier (GlobalHAB's stated mission/chair) rests on a WebSearch snippet rather than an independently re-verified WebFetch quote of the live page, so it carries lower confidence than the rest of the claims here.

## Tensions with other findings
This source's operational and publications scope is overwhelmingly marine/coastal (shellfish biotoxins, ciguatera, fish kills, ballast-water vectors, ocean-colour radiometry, Sargassum) rather than freshwater/lacustrine. Only one indexed publication ("Solutions for managing cyanobacterial blooms: A scientific summary for policy makers," 2019) squarely touches the freshwater-cyanobacteria domain relevant to a U.S. lake-focused cyanoHAB tool; the flagship data systems named (HAIS, HAEDAT) and governance bodies (IPHAB, GlobalHAB, the listed Task Teams/Regional Networks) are likewise framed around marine/coastal HAB events rather than inland lakes. This complicates treating the page as directly on-point evidence for a freshwater lake cyanotoxin risk model: it is valuable as global institutional/foundational context - establishing HABs as a long-recognized (HAN newsletter since 1992), resourced, actively governed international problem with dedicated monitoring databases and decades of technical publications - but it supplies no lake-specific data, no remote-sensing methodology, and no U.S. freshwater case data usable directly in the Part A analysis. Its broad, uncited scope claim ("every coastal country...potentially affected") also does not itself meet the project's own claim-gate standard (a baseline and an uncertainty for every number), so it should be used only as attributed scope-setting/background language from IOC-UNESCO, never restated as a project finding.

# Surveillance for Harmful Algal Bloom Events and Associated Human and Animal Illnesses — One Health Harmful Algal Bloom System, United States, 2016–2018

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7745959/

**What it is.** A CDC MMWR Surveillance Summary reporting the first three years (2016–2018) of data from the One Health Harmful Algal Bloom System (OHHABS), the first U.S. national public health surveillance system that links reports of harmful algal bloom (HAB) events with associated human and animal illness cases, voluntarily submitted by participating state/jurisdictional health departments. It is a descriptive epidemiological summary (counts, percentages, classifications), not a predictive model.

## Methods
OHHABS, launched by CDC in 2016, uses standardized national case/event report forms and definitions integrating prior 2007–2011 HAB surveillance experience with existing CDC waterborne/foodborne outbreak surveillance infrastructure. HAB events are classified as "suspected" or "confirmed" (confirmed requires laboratory-based HAB data, or observational/environmental data plus ≥1 associated illness case); human and animal illness cases are classified as "suspected," "probable," or "confirmed." Eighteen states voluntarily reported data for January 1, 2016–December 31, 2018. Analysts used SAS (version 9.4) to run descriptive analyses of environmental/event characteristics, human and animal case characteristics (demographics, exposure setting, symptoms, health-care/veterinary care sought, time-to-onset), and toxin testing results, all as self-reported by jurisdictions — the authors performed no independent laboratory confirmation, remote sensing, or statistical/predictive modeling. The report explicitly notes that case/event definitions are "not toxin-specific" and lack numeric thresholds tied to acute health outcomes or public health action levels.

## Stated limitations
The authors state four explicit limitations (quoted from the Limitations section): (1) "these data are for the initial OHHABS data collection period; participation, data completeness, and data quality are anticipated to improve over time"; (2) "the number of reported events or illnesses underrepresents the total that occurred within or across states," because "surveillance capacity and scope (e.g., inclusion of animal case reports) vary across jurisdictions"; (3) "case and event definitions are not toxin-specific and do not yet have thresholds for test results from clinical specimen or environmental samples that correspond to acute health outcomes or public health action levels for toxins"; (4) "harmful algal bloom events can exhibit geospatial, temporal, and toxin production variability, which makes environmental data more difficult to collect, interpret, and report." Additionally, the report itself notes diagnostic testing for HAB toxins "is under development but is not currently available in routine clinical settings," and fewer than 5% of human or animal illness cases were laboratory-confirmed.

## Tensions with other findings
This is passive, voluntary surveillance from only 18 of 50 U.S. states, explicitly stated by the authors to undercount true incidence — so its absolute counts (421 events; 389 human and 413 animal cases) should not be read as a national HAB-illness rate or compared directly against remote-sensing-based bloom-extent products (e.g., EPA CyAN, which detects far more cyanobacterial blooms across many more water bodies/states than are captured here), since the gap reflects reporting/ascertainment limits, not necessarily true geographic/temporal difference in bloom-related harm. Also, because fewer than 5% of cases were lab-confirmed and case/event definitions are explicitly "not toxin-specific," the illness figures reflect epidemiologic association (temporal/spatial linkage of illness to a reported bloom) rather than confirmed toxin exposure or dose-response — a correlation, not a demonstrated causal or quantitative exposure-response relationship. This creates tension with toxin-threshold-based frameworks (e.g., EPA recreational/drinking-water health advisories for microcystin and cylindrospermopsin) that this report itself says its case definitions do not yet map onto, meaning OHHABS illness counts cannot be directly tied to specific measured-toxin-concentration thresholds used elsewhere in the HAB literature.

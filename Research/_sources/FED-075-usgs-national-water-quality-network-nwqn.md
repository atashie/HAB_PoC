# USGS National Water Quality Network (NWQN)

**What it is.** The National Water Quality Network (NWQN) is a USGS national monitoring program, established in 2013, that collects standardized water-quality data from surface-water (streams/rivers) and groundwater sites across the U.S. to answer "What is the water quality of our Nation's streams, rivers, and groundwater?" and "How are water-quality conditions changing over time?", supporting national/regional/state/local water-quality management and policy.

## Methods
Data collection uses "consistent methods" per the USGS National Field Manual for the Collection of Water-Quality Data, applied network-wide. On-site (field) sensors measure temperature, specific conductance, dissolved oxygen, pH, and turbidity at time of visit. Laboratory analyses cover nutrients, major ions, pesticides, PFAS, tritium (an age-dating tracer, groundwater only), radionuclides (groundwater public/domestic supply wells only), suspended sediment (surface water only), microbiological contaminants (groundwater public/domestic supply wells only), and VOCs — but each of these panels is applied only to a defined subset of sites (e.g., pesticides at one-third of groundwater sites and non-reference surface sites; PFAS at all groundwater sites plus a subset of surface sites), not the full network. Design is comparative/stratified: surface-water sites split disturbed (urban/agricultural land use) vs. "minimally disturbed reference sites"; groundwater regional trend networks target shallow groundwater under agricultural/urban land use or the domestic-supply depth zone specifically, explicitly excluding the public-supply depth zone. No statistical/forecast model is described on this page — it documents a monitoring/data-collection network; derived trend statistics are produced through separate linked tools ("Water-Quality Changes in the Nation's Streams and Rivers" and "Decadal Change in Groundwater Quality") rather than detailed on this page itself.

## Stated limitations
The page does not carry a discrete "Limitations" section, but several scope boundaries are stated directly: (1) groundwater regional trend networks are explicitly designed to monitor shallow agricultural/urban groundwater or the domestic-supply depth zone and "do not cover the depth zone used for public supply," so trend results from those networks cannot be extrapolated to public-supply groundwater quality; (2) groundwater is resampled only once per decade (vs. 12–20x/year for surface water) specifically "because groundwater quality changes more slowly than surface-water quality," a coarser temporal resolution than the surface-water side of the network; (3) most laboratory constituent panels (pesticides, PFAS, radionuclides, VOCs, microbiological contaminants) are measured at only a defined subset of sites (e.g., one-third of groundwater/non-reference sites for pesticides and VOCs; public/domestic-supply wells only for radionuclides and microbiological contaminants), so no single contaminant panel has full network-wide spatial coverage; (4) the network as described on this page covers only streams/rivers and groundwater — no lake or reservoir monitoring, and no chlorophyll-a, cyanobacterial cell-count, or cyanotoxin parameter appears anywhere in the enumerated constituent list, despite the page's own citation of the Harmful Algal Blooms and Hypoxia Research and Control Act as an authorizing statute.

## Tensions with other findings
This source is lotic/groundwater-focused (streams, rivers, aquifers), not lentic (lakes/reservoirs), which is the water-body type central to most HAB remote-sensing work (e.g., EPA CyAN targets lakes/reservoirs). Although the page invokes the Harmful Algal Blooms and Hypoxia Research and Control Act of 1998 as a legal authority and describes USGS-authored national/Great Lakes HAB strategies, the constituent list given for NWQN itself contains no bloom-specific analyte (no chlorophyll-a, no cyanobacterial cell counts, no microcystin/cyanotoxin panel) — so, based on this page alone, NWQN appears to contribute to the national HAB effort mainly as a nutrient-loading/context dataset for rivers and estuaries, not as a direct bloom-observation network. Any use of NWQN in a lake-focused HAB PoC would likely need to be as an upstream nutrient/streamflow covariate for tributaries feeding a lake of interest, paired with a lake-specific in-situ source (e.g., Water Quality Portal chlorophyll-a/lake samples) rather than as a stand-alone HAB indicator.

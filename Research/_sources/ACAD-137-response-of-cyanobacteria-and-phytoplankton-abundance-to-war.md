# Response of cyanobacteria and phytoplankton abundance to warming, extreme rainfall events and nutrient enrichment

**What it is.** A 13-month (July 2014-August 2015) full-factorial mesocosm experiment at the UK Centre for Ecology & Hydrology's Aquatic Mesocosm Facility (32 outdoor 3,000-L mesocosms, 54°1'N 2°46'W) testing the individual and combined effects of warming (+4°C), simulated extreme-rainfall 'flushing' events, and nutrient enrichment (eutrophic vs. hypertrophic loading) on total phytoplankton chlorophyll-a and cyanobacterial abundance/composition in a shallow-lake system.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Warming and nutrient enrichment, applied alone, each significantly increased cyanobacteria, but when combined the two stressors produced a weak antagonistic (sub-additive, negative-interaction) effect rather than reinforcing one another.
  - *evidence:* Supported by linear mixed-model coefficients: warming-only effect +2.5±0.8 and nutrient-only effect +2.5±0.8 on cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a, versus a significant negative Nutrient×Warming interaction term of -3.4±1.1 (p<0.05); the same negative-interaction pattern also appeared for total chlorophyll-a (-1.6±0.4, p<0.05). (Results - 'Primary Results (May-August 2015 period)'; Abstract)
  - *quote:* "Warming and nutrient enrichment, as single stressors, resulted in statistically significantly higher cyanobacteria than in ambient (unheated, ambient‐nutrient) mesocosms. However, in combination, these stressors dampened the effect of one another, resulting in a weak antagonism (negative interaction)"
- **[✓ verified]** Occurrence of the toxin-forming genus Microcystis was overwhelmingly concentrated in the heated (warmed) mesocosms.
  - *evidence:* The paper reports that 94% of Microcystis occurrences were in heated mesocosms, with a statistically significant GLMM coefficient for presence in the warming treatment. (Results - 'Warming Effects on Specific Taxa')
  - *quote:* "94% of occurrences were in heated mesocosms"
- **[✓ verified]** Dolichospermum spp. biovolume increased significantly under the warming treatment.
  - *evidence:* Model coefficient of +1.72±0.75 for Dolichospermum biovolume in heated mesocosms, reported as statistically significant (p<0.05). (Results - 'Warming Effects on Specific Taxa')
- **[✓ verified]** Aphanizomenon spp. biovolume increased significantly under nutrient enrichment.
  - *evidence:* Model coefficient of +2.30±0.72 for Aphanizomenon biovolume in nutrient-enriched mesocosms, reported as statistically significant (p<0.05). (Results - 'Nutrient Enrichment Effects')
- **[✓ verified]** Pseudanabaena spp. presence and biovolume both increased significantly under nutrient enrichment.
  - *evidence:* GLMM presence coefficient +1.49±0.40 (p<0.05) and biovolume coefficient +3.45±0.95 (p<0.05) for the nutrient-enriched treatment. (Results - 'Nutrient Enrichment Effects')
- **[✓ verified]** Nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria genera dominated the overall cyanobacterial community by biovolume across the experiment.
  - *evidence:* Composition breakdown by biovolume: nitrogen-fixers 68% of total, with Aphanizomenon spp. alone at 51% (3.64x10^8 mm3/L). (Results - 'Cyanobacteria Composition')
  - *quote:* "Nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria comprised 68% of biovolume"
- **[✓ verified]** In winter, nutrient-enriched and flushed mesocosms showed disproportionate cyanobacterial dominance compared to all other treatment combinations.
  - *evidence:* During Dec 2014-Feb 2015, cyanobacteria made up 55% of phytoplankton abundance in enriched+flushed mesocosms versus 15±14% in the other treatments, suggesting flushing plus nutrient enrichment did not suppress winter cyanobacterial dominance. (Results - 'Winter Cyanobacteria Dominance')
  - *quote:* "55% of winter phytoplankton abundance was accounted for by cyanobacteria"
- **[✓ verified]** The +4°C warming treatment used in the experiment sits within the upper range of IPCC-projected warming under the RCP8.5 scenario.
  - *evidence:* Authors justify their chosen temperature differential by reference to the IPCC 2013 RCP8.5 projected range and note it aligns with other mesocosm climate experiments. (Discussion - 'Temperature Change Context')
  - *quote:* "in the upper range of the predicted increase in temperatures under RCP 8.5 predictions (2.6–4.8°C, IPCC, 2013)"
- **[✓ verified]** The primary cyanobacteria measurement tool (a submersible phycocyanin fluorometer) was cross-validated against, and correlated well with, independent microscope-based biovolume counts.
  - *evidence:* Reported correlation between AlgaeTorch-derived cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a and microscope-derived biovolume was strong and highly significant, supporting the reliability of the fluorometer as the main continuous-time measurement method. (Methods - 'Cyanobacteria Measurement')
  - *quote:* "Cyanobacteria chlorophyll‐a estimated from AlgaeTorch and cyanobacteria biovolume estimated from microscope counts were positively correlated (R² = 0.73, p < 0.001)"
- **[✓ verified]** A fluorometer calibration/interface error meant that cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a data prior to May 2015 were compromised and were explicitly excluded from statistical modeling, even though they are shown and discussed descriptively.
  - *evidence:* The authors state this was a genuine data-quality problem (readings above the 200 ug/L calibration threshold went undetected) rather than a modeling choice, and that the missingness was non-random, so they chose not to statistically model that period. (Limitations - 'Measurement Limitations')
  - *quote:* "Data prior to May are presented (for measurements below the manufacturer's threshold of 200 μg/L) and are discussed but are not statistically analysed."
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacterial cell densities reached under the study's warmed/nutrient-enriched conditions exceeded World Health Organization guideline thresholds for drinking and bathing water safety.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a real-world risk implication of the observed bloom levels, referencing the WHO/Chorus & Bartram (1999) guideline framework rather than a newly derived threshold. (Discussion - 'Critical Threshold Values and Definitions')
  - *quote:* "resulted in cell densities that exceed World Health Organization threshold guidelines for drinking and bathing waters (Chorus & Bartram, 1999)"
- **[✓ verified]** Treatment effects (warming, nutrients, flushing and their interactions) explained only a modest share of variance in cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a; a further, larger share of variance was attributable to differences between individual mesocosms rather than to the manipulated treatments.
  - *evidence:* Marginal R² (fixed effects only) = 0.15 versus conditional R² (fixed+random effects) = 0.43 for the cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a model over 5 May-26 Aug 2015, i.e., ~28 percentage points of additional variance came from mesocosm-level random effects, indicating substantial unexplained/idiosyncratic between-replicate variability even under identical treatments. (Results - 'Model Results and Variance Explained')
  - *quote:* "Marginal R² = 0.15 (fixed effects); Conditional R² = 0.43 (fixed + random effects)"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors explicitly bound the applicability of their findings to enriched, shallow lakes, stating that climate-change responses are expected to differ in deep lakes and that food-web factors not manipulated here (grazing, macrophytes) also shape real-world outcomes.
  - *evidence:* This is a direct scope-of-inference statement by the authors limiting generalization beyond the shallow, nutrient-enriched lake type studied. (Discussion/Conclusion - 'Generalizability Caveats')
  - *quote:* "The combined effects of warming, nutrient enrichment and flushing events on phytoplankton and cyanobacterial abundance observed in this study are relevant to enriched, shallow lakes"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors hypothesize that carbon (dissolved inorganic carbon) limitation may explain the observed warming x nutrient antagonism, having ruled out nitrogen and light limitation as mechanisms, but state this hypothesis could not be directly tested.
  - *evidence:* This is an explicitly flagged untested mechanistic hypothesis, not a demonstrated finding, since the relevant variable (dissolved inorganic carbon) was never measured. (Discussion - 'Key Mechanistic Insights')
  - *quote:* "One explanation could be depletion of dissolved inorganic carbon, that can lead to carbon limitation"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors recommend that nutrient loads may need to be substantially reduced in shallow lakes to counteract both the direct effect of warming on bloom-forming cyanobacteria growth rates and its indirect effect via enhanced nutrient cycling.
  - *evidence:* This is presented as a management implication drawn from the experimental results, not itself a directly measured outcome. (Discussion/Conclusion - 'Management and Research Implications')
  - *quote:* "Nutrients may need to be substantially reduced in shallow lakes in order to: (a) mitigate against the indirect effects of warming‐enhancing nutrient cycling ... and (b) mitigate against the direct effects of enhanced growth rates of common bloom‐forming species of cyanobacteria"
- **[✓ verified]** The authors caveat that their simulated flushing (extreme-rainfall) events were likely too infrequent and too short in duration to fully capture real extreme-rainfall impacts on lake phytoplankton, which may explain the limited long-term flushing effect observed.
  - *evidence:* A self-identified experimental-design limitation regarding external validity of the flushing manipulation. (Limitations - 'Experimental Design Limitations')
  - *quote:* "At three monthly intervals, the flushing events were likely too infrequent"
- **[✓ verified]** Nutrient-enriched (hypertrophic) mesocosms achieved markedly higher total phosphorus and total nitrogen concentrations than ambient-nutrient mesocosms, which sat near the eutrophic-hypertrophic boundary.
  - *evidence:* Reported means with standard deviations and minimums for both treatment groups establish the realized nutrient contrast underlying all other treatment comparisons. (Methods - 'Nutrient Concentrations')

## Methods
Full-factorial mesocosm experiment: 32 outdoor, open-topped, insulated cylindrical mesocosms (1 m deep x 2 m diameter, 3,000 L each) at CEH's Aquatic Mesocosm Facility (54 1'N, 2 46'W), inoculated with sediment, water, phytoplankton, zooplankton, macroinvertebrates and fish (three-spined sticklebacks) sourced from Windermere/local streams, left to settle 13 months before the July 2014-August 2015 experimental period. Design: 4 blocks x 8 treatments (2 warming x 2 flushing x 2 nutrient levels) x 4 replicates. Heated mesocosms held at ambient+4C via a computer-controlled heating element tracking the unheated mesocosm temperature, with automatic mixers preventing stratification. Nutrient treatments: hypertrophic (targeted fortnightly load 510 ug/L N, 70 ug/L P at Redfield ratio) vs. ambient/eutrophic-hypertrophic-boundary (73 ug/L N, 10 ug/L P until 17 Dec, then stopped). Flushing: 1,500 L (50% of volume) replaced with low-nutrient reservoir water (Blea Tarn) at 70-100 L/min over 15-21 minutes, every 12 weeks (5 events total). Sampling every 4 weeks plus targeted post-flushing sampling (immediately, 1 week, 3 weeks after); total chlorophyll-a via cold-ethanol extraction and spectrophotometry; cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a via submersible AlgaeTorch (phycocyanin fluorescence) fluorometer, cross-validated against Utermohl-method microscope biovolume counts (>=400 units/sample) performed only in May and August 2015; nutrients (TP, TN, SRP, NO3-N) via standard wet chemistry (Johnes & Heathwaite 1992; Mackereth, Heron & Talling 1978). Statistics: linear mixed models (LMMs) with quadratic time terms and generalised additive mixed models (GAMMs) with smooth time terms for continuous responses (chlorophyll-a, nutrients), generalised linear mixed models (GLMMs, binomial) for taxon presence/absence, all including a mesocosm-level random intercept; models simplified by removing non-significant higher-order interactions and compared via AIC/BIC; residual temporal autocorrelation checked via ACF plots; Satterthwaite-approximated degrees of freedom used for p-values (significance at p<0.05, with p<0.1 noted separately).

## Stated limitations
The authors state: (1) an AlgaeTorch fluorometer user-interface error meant cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a readings above the 200 ug/L calibration threshold went undetected until May 2015, so pre-May cyanobacteria chlorophyll-a data are shown/discussed descriptively but explicitly excluded from statistical modeling because the missingness was not random; (2) dissolved inorganic carbon, invoked as a candidate explanation for the warming x nutrient antagonism, was not directly measured and could not be estimated from available data, so 'this explanation cannot be fully tested'; (3) the simulated flushing events were likely too infrequent (every 12 weeks) and too short in duration to fully represent extreme-rainfall/flushing impacts, and event timing (season) also mattered; (4) enhanced nutrient loading gave no extra recovery benefit after flushing, likely because no additional nutrients were added to simulate the nutrient-laden run-off that typically accompanies real storm events; (5) mesocosm enclosure/container effects may alter natural processes, though the authors argue replication of treatments vs. controls should account for this; (6) results are stated to be specific to 'enriched, shallow lakes' - the authors explicitly note that climate-change effects on phytoplankton are likely to differ between shallow and deep lakes, and that food-web factors (top-down grazing, macrophytes) important in real lakes were not manipulated in this design; (7) synergistic (rather than antagonistic) warming x nutrient effects may still occur at lower, oligotrophic-to-mesotrophic points on the total-phosphorus gradient where TP-chlorophyll-a and TP-cyanobacteria relationships are more linear, so the antagonism found here may not hold across the full trophic gradient.

## Tensions with other findings
This mesocosm study complicates a common assumption in HAB literature that warming and nutrient enrichment act synergistically to worsen cyanobacterial blooms: under hypertrophic nutrient loading in this shallow-lake mesocosm system, the two stressors combined sub-additively (a 'weak antagonism', with a significant negative interaction term on both total and cyanobacterial chlorophyll-a). The authors themselves caution this may not generalize to lower-nutrient (oligotrophic-mesotrophic) systems, where they expect more linear, potentially synergistic TP-chlorophyll-a/TP-cyanobacteria relationships instead - so this finding should be read as bounding where warming x nutrient synergy claims hold, not as a general refutation of them. The study also nuances the intuitive expectation that extreme-rainfall/flushing (dilution) events straightforwardly suppress blooms: recovery from flushing was rapid in spring/summer (limited net suppressive effect) and slower in autumn/winter, and nutrient-enriched+flushed mesocosms actually showed cyanobacteria dominating a majority of winter phytoplankton abundance (55%, vs. 15+/-14% in other treatments) - a case where flushing combined with nutrient enrichment coincided with, rather than prevented, cyanobacterial dominance. Because this is a temperate-UK, shallow, artificial-mesocosm system with only two discrete nutrient levels and a specific flushing regime (not a field-observed or remote-sensing-derived dataset), any comparison with U.S. lake-based or CyAN-style observational findings should treat the direction and magnitude of these interaction effects as context-dependent experimental hypotheses rather than directly transferable coefficients. Throughout, the paper treats warming/nutrients/flushing as manipulated experimental causes (true causal claims within the mesocosm system), which is a stronger evidentiary basis than typical observational correlation-based HAB driver studies, but external validity to real lakes is explicitly bounded by the authors themselves.

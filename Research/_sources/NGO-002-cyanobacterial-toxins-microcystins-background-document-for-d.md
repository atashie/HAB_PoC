# Cyanobacterial toxins: microcystins — Background document for development of WHO Guidelines for drinking-water quality and Guidelines for safe recreational water environments (WHO/HEP/ECH/WSH/2020.6)

**What it is.** A 2020 WHO background document that reviews toxicokinetic, human, and animal toxicological evidence on microcystins (a family of cyanobacterial cyclic-peptide hepatotoxins) to derive provisional health-based guideline values for microcystins in drinking-water and recreational water, replacing WHO's 2003 microcystin-LR-only background document (WHO/SDE/WSH/03.04/57).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** WHO's provisional guideline values (GVs) for microcystins are 1 µg/L (lifetime drinking-water), 12 µg/L (short-term drinking-water) and 24 µg/L (recreational water), derived from a mouse study of MC-LR corroborated by a similar rat study, with the liver identified as the most sensitive organ.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as the executive-summary conclusion of the document's toxicological risk assessment; the full quantitative derivation (dose-response, uncertainty factors) is described as being in Section 8.1, which is outside the extracted window. (Executive summary, p. 1 (full derivation in Section 8.1, not in extracted text))
  - *quote:* "The provisional guideline values (GVs) for MCs (lifetime drinking-water GV: 1 µg/L; short-term drinking-water GV: 12 µg/L; recreational water GV: 24 µg/L) are based on a study in mice using MC-LR, corroborated by a similar study in rats."
- **[✓ verified]** The short-term drinking-water GV is meant only to bound temporary exceedances (up to 2 weeks) while treatment is upgraded, not repeated seasonal exceedance of the lifetime GV, and is derived for adults; bottle-fed infants and small children are recommended an alternative water source if concentrations exceed 3 µg/L, even briefly.
  - *evidence:* Directly stated qualifier on how the short-term GV should/should not be applied, plus a separate, more conservative numeric threshold for a vulnerable subgroup. (Executive summary, p. 2)
  - *quote:* "the extent to which the lifetime value can be exceeded for periods of up to 2 weeks until water treatment can be augmented... it is recommended, as a precautionary measure, that bottle-fed infants and small children be provided with an alternative water source if concentrations are greater than 3 µg/L, even for short periods."
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystins are described as the most commonly reported cyanobacterial toxins worldwide, produced mainly by freshwater cyanobacteria with wide geographic/ecological distribution; drinking-water is identified as the most likely human exposure route, with recreational contact during blooms as another potentially high-concentration pathway, and limited data suggesting some food accumulation.
  - *evidence:* Direct descriptive/summary statement of exposure pathways in the executive summary; no exposure-frequency dataset or figures are present in this excerpt. (Executive summary, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "MCs are the most commonly reported cyanobacterial toxins worldwide. Drinking-water is the most likely route of exposure to MCs."
- **[✓ verified]** The document attributes high cyanobacterial biomass to nutrient loading from anthropogenic sources (agricultural runoff, wastewater) and, on that basis, recommends nutrient-source control as the primary long-term bloom-management lever.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a summary/expert-consensus statement in the executive summary rather than as a quantified causal or dose-response analysis within this excerpt; should be read as the document's driver framing/association, not a demonstrated attribution statistic (no accompanying data are shown here) — correlation between nutrients and biomass, not a controlled causal test, is what the excerpt supports. (Executive summary, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "The main driver of high amounts of cyanobacterial biomass is nutrients from anthropogenic sources such as agricultural runoff and wastewater. Hence, control of these sources is the primary long-term management option."
- **[✓ verified]** Conventional drinking-water treatment (coagulation, flocculation, filtration, chlorination) is generally sufficient to bring microcystins to acceptable levels; where it is not, ozonation and activated carbon (filtration or powdered addition) can be effective supplementary steps.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a general treatment-performance conclusion in the executive summary; supporting data are presumably in Section 7.4 ('Treatment methods and performance'), whose body text is not present in this excerpt. (Executive summary, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "Drinking-water can usually be treated to acceptable levels by a well-run conventional treatment plant implementing coagulation, flocculation, filtration and chlorination; if this is not sufficient, ozonation and activated carbon filtration or addition of powdered activated carbon can be effective."
- **[✓ verified]** Mechanistically, microcystins are actively imported into cells via organic anion transport proteins (OATPs); because the liver highly expresses OATPs, microcystins act primarily as hepatotoxins, though other OATP-expressing tissues can be affected. Intracellularly they bind protein phosphatases with high affinity, disrupting cytoskeletal, cell-replication, stress-response and DNA-repair pathways, producing acute intrahepatic haemorrhage and, chronically, impaired tumour-proliferation control.
  - *evidence:* Mode-of-action summary given directly in the executive summary; detailed primary evidence is presumably in Section 5.4 'Mode of action', not present in this excerpt. (Executive summary, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "Because the liver has high expression of OATPs, MCs are considered to be primarily hepatotoxins... Acute effects of MC poisoning include intrahepatic haemorrhage as the cytoskeleton within liver cells breaks down, allowing blood to flow between the cells."
- **[✓ verified]** Because the GVs are derived from the single congener MC-LR while environmental exposure is generally to mixtures of MC variants, the document states that comparing total MC concentration (gravimetric or molar) to the GV is 'likely' to be protective in most circumstances — a hedged, not absolute, extrapolation.
  - *evidence:* The document itself flags this as an assumption/extrapolation from single-congener data to real-world mixtures, not a directly tested mixture-toxicity result. (Executive summary, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "comparison of the GVs with total MCs as gravimetric or molar equivalents is likely to be protective of public health in most circumstances."
- **[✓ verified]** The document notes an open scientific disagreement: some recent studies suggest microcystin effects on reproductive organs at lower concentrations than those underlying the liver-based GVs, but these findings are contradicted by earlier studies and are described as requiring corroboration.
  - *evidence:* Self-identified evidentiary caveat/contradiction within the source, presented without resolution — a limitation the document itself flags rather than a settled finding. (Executive summary, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "Although some recent research has suggested that certain reproductive organs may be affected at lower concentrations, these reports are contradicted by earlier studies and so require corroboration."
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystins are cyclic heptapeptides first characterized in the early 1980s (named for Microcystis aeruginosa); more than 250 distinct variants have been identified, with molecular weights of 882–1101 Da; most are hydrophilic and highly water-soluble, with saturation concentrations several orders of magnitude above the WHO guideline values; microcystins and the related nodularins are also described as extremely heat-stable.
  - *evidence:* Direct chemical-identity description from Section 1.1/1.2, citing Spoof & Catherine (2017) and Bouaïcha et al. (2019) as the source for the variant count and molecular-weight range. (Section 1.1 Identity, p. 3; Section 1.2 Physicochemical properties, p. 3-4)
  - *quote:* "more than 250 different MCs have been identified, with molecular weights in the range of 882–1101 Da (Spoof & Catherine, 2017; Bouaïcha et al., 2019)... All structural variants are highly soluble in water, with saturation concentrations several orders of magnitude higher than the World Health Organization (WHO) guideline values."

## Data / numbers
- Lifetime drinking-water guideline value (provisional): 1 µg/L microcystin
- Short-term drinking-water guideline value (provisional): 12 µg/L
- Recreational water guideline value (provisional): 24 µg/L
- Precautionary threshold for bottle-fed infants/small children: 3 µg/L (alternative water source recommended above this even for short periods)
- Short-term GV validity window: up to 2 weeks (temporary, until treatment augmented; not for repeated seasonal exceedance)
- >250 distinct microcystin variants identified to date
- Microcystin molecular weight range: 882–1101 Da
- No explicit numeric uncertainty factors, confidence intervals, or point-of-departure/TDI values are given in the extracted excerpt itself (that quantitative derivation is in Section 8.1, outside the provided window); GVs are explicitly labeled 'provisional'

## Methods
This is a WHO toxicological risk-assessment/background document, not a primary experimental study. Per its Table of Contents (only partially present in the extracted text) it synthesizes: physicochemical/analytical chemistry of microcystins and nodularins; environmental exposure data (air, food, water) and estimated total human exposure; toxicokinetics (absorption, distribution, metabolism, elimination) in laboratory animals and humans; human evidence (case studies of drinking-water, recreational and dialysis exposures; epidemiological studies); animal/in vitro toxicology across acute, short-term/subchronic and long-term exposure (systemic, neurological, reproductive/developmental, genotoxic/carcinogenic, immunotoxic, haematological endpoints) and mode-of-action work (OATP-mediated cellular uptake; protein phosphatase PP1/PP2A/PP5 inhibition); and practical considerations (monitoring, analytical methods and achievability, source control, treatment performance). From this evidence base it derives provisional guideline values, anchored on a mouse oral study of MC-LR (corroborated by a rat study) that identified the liver as the most sensitive organ. The extracted excerpt available for this review includes the front matter, Executive Summary, and Section 1 (Identity in full; start of Physicochemical properties) — it does NOT include the numeric dose-response/uncertainty-factor derivation (Section 8.1), the Section 6.2 'Quality of evidence' assessment, or the human/animal evidence detail (Sections 2-6), so no claims are made here about content in those sections beyond what the Executive Summary itself states.

## Stated limitations
The document itself flags that recent research suggesting reproductive-organ effects of MC-LR at lower concentrations than those underlying the liver-based GVs is contradicted by earlier studies and "require[s] corroboration" — i.e., not treated as settled. All GVs are explicitly labeled "provisional." The short-term drinking-water GV is explicitly bounded: valid only for temporary exceedances of up to 2 weeks while treatment is upgraded, not for repeated seasonal exceedance, and is derived for adults, with a separate, lower (3 µg/L) precautionary threshold recommended for bottle-fed infants and small children because of their higher water intake per body weight. The GVs are derived from single-compound (MC-LR) animal studies even though environmental exposure is generally to mixtures of >250 known MC variants; the document states that comparing total MC concentration to the GV is "likely" (not certain) to be protective "in most circumstances," implying known exceptions are possible. The document's own Section 6 ("Overall database and quality of evidence") is listed in the table of contents but its body text was not present in the extracted excerpt available to this review, so its specific evidence-quality caveats cannot be quoted here.

## Tensions with other findings
The source explicitly flags an unresolved internal tension: some recent studies report microcystin effects on reproductive organs at concentrations below those used to derive the liver-based guideline values, but these are "contradicted by earlier studies" and unconfirmed — a live discrepancy in the toxicology literature as of 2020, relevant to any HAB source that treats the WHO liver-based GVs as the definitive human-health threshold. Separately, the statement that "the main driver of high amounts of cyanobacterial biomass is nutrients from anthropogenic sources" is presented in this excerpt as a summary/expert-consensus framing rather than a quantified causal analysis; it should be read as a driver/association claim (correlation), not a demonstrated causal attribution, when cross-referenced against remote-sensing or in-situ nutrient-bloom studies elsewhere in the dossier. This document also explicitly supersedes WHO's 2003 microcystin-LR-only drinking-water background document (WHO/SDE/WSH/03.04/57), indicating the evidence base and scope (adding recreational-water and short-term values, broader MC-mixture treatment) evolved between 2003 and 2020; other dossier sources citing only the older MC-LR drinking-water figure should be checked against this 2020 update, since the short-term (12 µg/L) and recreational (24 µg/L) values did not exist in the earlier document per this text's framing.

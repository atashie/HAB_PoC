# EPA Drinking Water Health Advisories for Cyanotoxins

**What it is.** EPA's official webpage explaining its non-enforceable Health Advisories (HAs) for cyanotoxins in drinking water, issued under Safe Drinking Water Act authority. It summarizes the 2015 10-day exposure advisory levels for two cyanotoxins (cylindrospermopsin and microcystins), broken out by age group, and points to the underlying Health Advisory documents and Health Effects Support Documents (including one for anatoxin-a, which has no accompanying numeric advisory level on this page).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** EPA may issue non-enforceable federal Health Advisories for drinking water contaminants, including cyanotoxins, that lack a national primary drinking water regulation, under Safe Drinking Water Act authority.
  - *evidence:* Page cites the SDWA statutory basis (42 U.S.C. §300g-1(b)(1)(F)) for EPA's Health Advisory Program and defines what a Health Advisory is (a concentration threshold below which adverse effects are not anticipated over a specific exposure duration). (Main page body, opening/definition section)
  - *quote:* "concentrations of drinking water contaminants at which adverse health effects are not anticipated to occur over specific exposure durations"
- **[✓ verified]** These cyanotoxin Health Advisories are explicitly not legally enforceable federal standards, and are subject to change as new science becomes available.
  - *evidence:* EPA states this directly as a caveat distinguishing HAs from an enforceable drinking-water regulation. (Main page body, regulatory-status statement)
  - *quote:* "These HAs are not regulations and should not be construed as legally enforceable federal standards. HAs may change as new information becomes available."
- **[✓ verified]** In 2015, EPA set a 10-day Health Advisory level for cylindrospermopsin of 0.7 µg/L for bottle-fed infants and pre-school children, and 3.0 µg/L for school-age children and adults.
  - *evidence:* Numeric advisory levels appear in the page's 10-day Health Advisory table/list for cylindrospermopsin, for both age brackets; the same two figures were returned independently by both fetches of the page. (10-day Health Advisory levels table)

- 2015 — year the two numeric Health Advisories were issued

- 42 U.S.C. §300g-1(b)(1)(F) — Safe Drinking Water Act statutory authority cited for EPA's Health Advisory Program

## Methods
This is a regulatory guidance/summary page, not an original study, and presents no data-generating method or model of its own. It states the numeric advisory levels derive from EPA Health Effects Support Documents (HESDs), described on the page only as "a comprehensive review of the published literature on the chemical and physical properties of these toxins, the toxin synthesis and environmental fate, occurrence and exposure information, and health effects" — i.e., a toxicological literature synthesis, not an empirical field or remote-sensing study. No dose-response modeling, numeric uncertainty-factor values, NOAEL/LOAEL figures, or statistical methodology are disclosed on this page itself; that detail would reside in the underlying Health Advisory and HESD source documents (linked from this page but not fetched for this dossier).

## Stated limitations
The page states directly: (1) "These HAs are not regulations and should not be construed as legally enforceable federal standards" — no legal/regulatory force; (2) "HAs may change as new information becomes available" — advisories are explicitly provisional/revisable; (3) only cylindrospermopsin and microcystins receive a numeric 10-day advisory value on this page — anatoxin-a is covered only by a qualitative Health Effects Support Document, with no numeric drinking-water threshold stated for it; (4) the page frames advisories around a 10-day exposure window and references other exposure-duration categories ("one-day, ten-days, several years, and a lifetime") as part of the broader HA program in general, but gives numeric values only for the 10-day duration for these two toxins; (5) the page discloses no quantitative uncertainty factor or confidence interval for the advisory numbers themselves, only the qualitative statement that a "margin of safety" is built in.

## Tensions with other findings
This source sets health-based numeric thresholds specifically for cyanotoxin concentrations in finished/treated drinking water — not for recreational contact, raw source water, or remote-sensing-derived bloom/chlorophyll proxies used elsewhere in HAB literature. The page itself says nothing about recreational criteria or satellite-based detection, so its µg/L thresholds should not be conflated with those other measures without an explicit source bridging them. Also, because the advisories are explicitly framed as non-enforceable and revisable ("may change as new information becomes available"), any other source in this review that treats these (or state-adopted variants) as fixed, binding regulatory limits would be overstating their legal status relative to what this source itself claims.

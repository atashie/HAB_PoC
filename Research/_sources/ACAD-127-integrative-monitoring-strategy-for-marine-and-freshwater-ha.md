# Integrative monitoring strategy for marine and freshwater harmful algal blooms and toxins across the freshwater-to-marine continuum

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed synthesis/recommendations paper (Howard et al., Integrated Environmental Assessment and Management, 2023) arguing that HAB and cyanotoxin monitoring must be redesigned around the hydrological "freshwater-to-marine continuum" rather than isolated waterbodies. It proposes three recommendations — cross-jurisdictional coordination, multiple sampling modalities (beyond grab samples), and routine multi-toxin monitoring — and illustrates them with five California case studies (Klamath River/estuary, San Francisco Bay, Eel/Russian Rivers, a statewide coastwide SPATT survey, and the Santa Clara River Estuary).

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Estuaries and other downstream receiving waters face a 'dual risk' of HAB impacts: blooms originating in the coastal ocean itself, and cyanotoxins produced inland and transported downstream — yet most HAB monitoring programs are not designed to track these hydrological connections.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as the paper's central problem framing in the abstract. (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "Estuaries and other downstream receiving waters now face the dual risk of impacts from harmful algal blooms (HABs) that occur in the coastal ocean as well as those originating in inland watersheds... most HAB monitoring efforts do not account for hydrological connections in their monitoring strategies and designs."
- **[⚠ partial]** The paper's core proposal is a three-part integrative monitoring strategy: (1) coordinated monitoring across hydrologically interconnected waterbodies and across organizational/political/jurisdictional boundaries; (2) combining multiple sampling modalities (grab samples, passive samplers, tissue, benthic mats) rather than relying on discrete grab sampling alone; and (3) routine monitoring for toxin mixtures (both marine algal toxins and cyanotoxins) at the land-sea interface.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the three named recommendations in the abstract, later elaborated as the paper's main section structure. (Abstract; Section "HAB Monitoring Recommendations and Case Studies")
  - *quote:* "First, HAB monitoring would benefit from coordinated and cohesive efforts across hydrologically interconnected waterbodies... Second, a combination of sampling modalities would provide the most effective monitoring for HAB toxin dynamics and transport... Third, routine monitoring is needed for toxin mixtures at the land–sea interface including algal toxins of marine origins as well as cyanotoxins."

  - *evidence:* Direct evidence supporting the paper's recommendation 2 (multiple sampling modalities) and flagging benthic mats as an under-monitored toxin source. (Case study — Eel and Russian Rivers)

## Methods
Synthesis/recommendations paper (not a single controlled experiment) drawing on five California case studies to compare monitoring modalities. Sampling methods discussed: (a) discrete/grab water samples (particulate, dissolved, and total toxin fractions); (b) passive samplers — chiefly SPATT (Solid Phase Adsorption Toxin Tracking), plus POCIS, LDPE and PDMS strips, o-DGT, and SPME — deployed on moorings/buoys, ship flow-through systems, piers, or stakes; (c) shellfish (mussel, oyster) and fish tissue sampling; (d) benthic cyanobacterial mat sampling (composite method per an SOP by Fetscher et al. 2009); and (e) molecular/genetic methods (used in the Klamath case study to confirm Iron Gate Reservoir as the source of downriver Microcystis assemblages). Toxin quantification is referenced as LC/MS/MS. A Toxin Diversity Index (Shannon–Wiener index applied to counts of distinct SPATT-detected toxin compounds) was used to quantify multi-toxin seasonality in the coastwide survey, reportedly showing a significant positive linear correlation with day of year. Where the approach is shown to work: passive samplers (SPATT) substantially out-detected grab samples for the same toxins over the same period in San Francisco Bay (e.g., MC 76% vs. 50%, DA 97% vs. 11%), and combining water + SPATT + benthic-mat sampling in the Eel/Russian Rivers revealed distinct, non-redundant spatial/temporal toxin patterns that no single method captured alone. Where it falls short: passive-sampler results are not readily convertible into numeric concentrations comparable with regulatory health thresholds, and coupled freshwater-to-marine mechanistic/biogeochemical models needed to explain or forecast transport are described as largely absent.

## Stated limitations
The authors state several explicit gaps: (1) passive samplers (their centrally recommended tool) are "limited for regulatory uses at this time because of the difficulty of relating toxin concentrations to health thresholds," and are vulnerable to vandalism, storm debris, and biofouling; (2) "the effects of acute and chronic exposure to mixtures of multiple toxins on the health of human and aquatic life are poorly understood," and most recreational/drinking-water thresholds are single-toxin based even though co-occurrence is common; (3) the "relative frequency of co-occurring cyanotoxins is still poorly defined because the number of comprehensive toxin surveys that measures multiple cyanotoxins is limited"; (4) there are "no federal and state regulations addressing cyanotoxin ingestion by commercially harvested marine shellfish," despite measured bioaccumulation to levels the authors call an "unrecognized risk to human health"; (5) "understanding of the factors that promote benthic blooms is limited"; and (6) modeling capability is immature — "most current models relevant to HABs represent the land and ocean separately," mechanistic models for coastal biogeochemical processes "are not well defined," and there are "few if any examples of coupled model systems" spanning the freshwater-to-marine continuum. The paper is also a synthesis/recommendations piece built on prior published studies and California case studies rather than a single controlled or statistically-tested experiment, so its cross-site generalizations rely on the quality and comparability of the underlying, separately-run monitoring programs it cites.

## Tensions with other findings
This source does not directly contradict satellite/remote-sensing HAB approaches (e.g., EPA CyAN's cyanobacteria index), but it complicates reliance on any single visible-bloom signal as a complete risk indicator. It reports that dissolved domoic acid is "chronically present, even when blooms are not evident," that benthic cyanobacterial mats (which lack a strong water-surface spectral signature) were a toxin source at nearly all sampled river sites, and that toxin transport can carry contamination more than 300 km downstream of — and weeks after — the visible source bloom. Each of these is a case where a surface-reflectance-based bloom signal at a given place and time could be weak or absent even though toxin risk is present, which matters for any tool (like this project's) that infers risk primarily from a satellite-derived surface signal. The paper's own statement that "most current models relevant to HABs represent the land and ocean separately" with "few if any examples of coupled model systems" underscores a gap directly relevant to fusing remote-sensing and in-situ signals across connected waterbodies. Its numbers (bioaccumulation factors, salinity tolerances, SPATT detection rates) are drawn from tidal/estuarine and coastal California systems and the paper explicitly extends the framework to inland lakes/rivers (citing Kansas River and Lake Erie), but these specific figures should not be assumed to transfer to a closed freshwater lake without independent validation.

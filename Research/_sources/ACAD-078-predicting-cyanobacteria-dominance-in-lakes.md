# Predicting Cyanobacteria dominance in lakes

**What it is.** A short (~4-page) peer-reviewed paper in the Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences (2001) that compiles data from 99 well-studied temperate-zone lakes to test whether the nitrogen-to-phosphorus (N:P) ratio, versus absolute total phosphorus, total nitrogen, or standing algal biomass, better predicts cyanobacteria-dominance/bloom risk. The paper concludes the long-standing N:P-ratio-based model is flawed.

## Methods
Cross-sectional, multi-lake correlational analysis: the authors compiled total phosphorus (TP), total nitrogen (TN), N:P ratio, and standing algal/cyanobacteria biomass data across 99 temperate-zone lakes and compared how strongly each predictor correlates with cyanobacteria-dominance/bloom risk. Per the abstract, TP, TN, and biomass show stronger correlation with risk than the N:P ratio, which the authors say invalidates the N:P-ratio model. The specific statistical procedure (regression form, significance thresholds, effect sizes/R²) is not stated in the abstract and could not be confirmed from an accessible full text — the paper is closed-access (confirmed via OpenAlex: is_oa=false, oa_status="closed", no repository has fulltext), and the one full-text PDF mirror located (a Quebec provincial government environmental-review archive copy) is a scanned/image-based file with no extractable text layer, so it could not be parsed for methods/results detail.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper is itself framed as a direct challenge to an earlier, established N:P-ratio paradigm in aquatic ecology (the abstract calls it a "controversial precept... based on... lab and field experiments"), so its conclusion — that absolute nutrient/biomass levels outperform the N:P ratio as correlates of bloom risk — is a known point of contention rather than settled consensus in the HAB literature. It is also worth flagging (though NOT verified as part of this source, and explicitly excluded from key_claims/data_numbers above) that other results encountered in the same search sweep, describing a differently-titled paper on forecasting cyanobacteria dominance in specific Canadian lakes, suggested broad cross-lake statistical predictors can have low predictive power at the scale of a single water body, where local factors (thermal stratification, hypolimnetic nutrient pools, meteorology) may dominate instead — a plausible tension for anyone wanting to generalize this paper's 99-lake cross-sectional pattern to single-lake, near-term forecasting use. I flag this only as context from adjacent literature, not as a claim within ACAD-078 itself, precisely because I could not confirm it belongs to this source rather than to that other paper.

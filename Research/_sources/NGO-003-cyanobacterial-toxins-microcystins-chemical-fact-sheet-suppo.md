# Cyanobacterial toxins: Microcystins — chemical fact sheet supporting WHO Guidelines for drinking-water quality and safe recreational water environments

**What it is.** A WHO chemical fact sheet (Chapter 12 of the Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality) that lays out the health-based provisional drinking-water guideline value, tolerable daily intake, analytical detection limits/methods, monitoring approach, and prevention/treatment options for microcystin-LR, the best-studied cyanobacterial (harmful-algal-bloom) toxin. The same extracted text block also contains fragments of two adjacent, unrelated WHO fact sheets from the same chapter (cyanide and cyanogen chloride), which are excluded from the claims below as out of scope.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Microcystins are presented as the best-researched cyanobacterial toxin group and are said to probably occur most frequently in fresh waters among cyanotoxins.
  - *evidence:* Opening framing statement of the fact sheet; not attributed to a specific cited study within this excerpt. (Microcystin-LR fact sheet, opening paragraph (~p.343-344))
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystins are usually cell-bound (intracellular) and are released into surrounding water mainly upon cell rupture/lysis, whereas other cyanotoxins (cylindrospermopsins, saxitoxins, anatoxin-a, anatoxin-a(s)) tend to occur to a larger extent already dissolved — a distinction the source flags as relevant to drinking-water treatment efficacy.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the key practical difference between microcystins and other cyanotoxin classes for treatment purposes. (Opening paragraph, Microcystin-LR fact sheet)
  - *quote:* "microcystins are usually cell-bound, and substantial amounts are released to the surrounding water only in situations of cell rupture (i.e. lysis), whereas the other cyanotoxins may occur to a larger extent dissolved in water."
- **[✓ verified]** Human exposure to microcystins is largely via drinking-water or recreational contact with cyanobacterial blooms, though microcystins may also occur in fish, molluscs and shellfish from affected water bodies.
  - *evidence:* Background exposure-route statement; descriptive, no numeric threshold given. (Opening paragraph, Microcystin-LR fact sheet)
- **[✓ verified]** More than 80 microcystin variants have been identified, but only a few occur frequently or at high concentration; microcystin-LR is among the most frequent and most toxic congeners and is the only one with a toxicological database sufficient to derive a (provisional) guideline value.
  - *evidence:* Explains why the guideline value applies specifically to MC-LR rather than to microcystins broadly. (Opening paragraph, Microcystin-LR fact sheet)
  - *quote:* "Among the more than 80 microcystins identified to date, only a few occur frequently and in high concentrations."
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacterial genera frequently reported to contain microcystins are Microcystis, Planktothrix and Anabaena.
  - *evidence:* Listed as the taxa relevant to microcystin production. (Opening paragraph, Microcystin-LR fact sheet)
  - *quote:* "Frequently occurring cyanobacterial genera that may contain microcystins are Microcystis, Planktothrix and Anabaena"
- **[✓ verified]** WHO's provisional drinking-water guideline value for total microcystin-LR (free plus cell-bound) is 0.001 mg/l (1 µg/l).
  - *evidence:* Headline numeric guideline value stated in the fact sheet's summary box. (Guideline-value box, Microcystin-LR fact sheet (~p.344))
  - *quote:* "Provisional guideline value Total microcystin-LR (free plus cell-bound): 0.001 mg/l (1 µg/l)"
- **[✓ verified]** The guideline value is explicitly labeled provisional because it covers only microcystin-LR, the underlying toxicological database is limited, and new cyanotoxin toxicity data continue to be generated.
  - *evidence:* Direct caveat given immediately below the numeric guideline value. (Guideline-value box, Microcystin-LR fact sheet)
  - *quote:* "the database is limited and new data for the toxicity of cyanobacterial toxins are being generated."
- **[✓ verified]** The tolerable daily intake (TDI) underpinning the guideline value is 0.04 µg/kg body weight, derived from liver pathology observed in a 13-week oral mouse study, with a 1000-fold uncertainty factor applied because of database gaps, notably the absence of chronic-toxicity and carcinogenicity data.
  - *evidence:* Toxicological derivation basis stated directly in the fact sheet's TDI entry. (Guideline-value box, Microcystin-LR fact sheet (~p.344))
  - *quote:* "TDI 0.04 µg/kg body weight"
- **[⚠ partial]** Three analytical methods for microcystins are compared: HPLC (limit of detection 0.1-1 µg/l), ELISA immunoassay (0.1-0.5 µg/l), and protein phosphatase assay (0.5-1.5 µg/l); HPLC is described as most precise and able to differentiate congeners, while the immunoassay and phosphatase assay are less precise but useful for screening.
  - *evidence:* Method comparison stated directly in the 'Limit of detection' entry of the guideline box. (Guideline-value box, Microcystin-LR fact sheet (~p.344))
  - *quote:* "0.1–1 µg/l by HPLC"
  - *reviewer:* HPLC's ability to differentiate congeners is qualified in the source as conditional: 'will allow differentiation between variants where standards are available.' The claim omits this important caveat that differentiation depends on standard availability.
- **[✓ verified]** WHO's preferred cyanotoxin monitoring approach is visual/microscopic surveillance of source water for rising cyanobacterial cell density or bloom-forming potential, with heightened vigilance once such events occur, rather than routine measurement of finished-water toxin concentration, which the source calls unsatisfactory for safety determination.
  - *evidence:* Stated in the guideline box's 'Monitoring' entry and reinforced in the Practical Considerations section. (Guideline box 'Monitoring' entry; Practical Considerations section (~p.344-345))
  - *quote:* "monitoring finished water against target cyanotoxin concentrations is unsatisfactory for determining whether or not it is safe, because of the large variety of toxins"
- **[✓ verified]** Failing to extract the cell-bound (intracellular) toxin fraction during analysis causes a large underestimate of true cyanotoxin concentration, even though this extraction step is described as easy to perform for microcystins.
  - *evidence:* Explicit analytical caveat given in Practical Considerations. (Practical Considerations section (~p.345-346))
  - *quote:* "neglecting extraction from cells will lead to dramatic underestimation of concentrations."
- **[✓ verified]** Mechanistically, microcystin-LR is a potent inhibitor of eukaryotic protein serine/threonine phosphatases 1 and 2A; the liver is the primary toxicological target because microcystins cross cell membranes chiefly via the bile acid transporter.
  - *evidence:* Mode-of-action statement given directly after the guideline-value-derivation summary. (Mechanism paragraph, Microcystin-LR fact sheet (~p.344-345))
  - *quote:* "Microcystin-LR is a potent inhibitor of eukaryotic protein serine/threonine phosphatases 1 and 2A."
- **[✓ verified]** The guideline-value derivation drew on an oral 13-week mouse study supported by an oral 44-day pig study; the source also notes recorded livestock/wildlife poisonings and published evidence of tumour promotion, and states that in 2006 IARC classified microcystin-LR as a possible human carcinogen (Group 2B).
  - *evidence:* Supporting toxicology and carcinogenicity classification stated directly. (Mechanism paragraph, Microcystin-LR fact sheet)
  - *quote:* "In 2006, IARC classified microcystin-LR as a possible carcinogen (Group 2B)."
- **[✓ verified]** Blooms are described as occurring when phosphorus and nitrogen concentrations are elevated, particularly in stagnant or very slowly flowing water bodies; blooms recur in the same water bodies, can form surface scums or accumulate at the thermocline of stratified reservoirs, can develop rapidly, and vary in duration from hours to weeks, often on a seasonal pattern.
  - *evidence:* General descriptive statement of bloom-occurrence conditions in Practical Considerations; presented as background pattern, not tied to a specific cited study within this excerpt (correlational/associative, not a controlled causal claim). (Practical Considerations section, opening paragraph (~p.345))
  - *quote:* "Blooms occur if concentrations of nutrients (phosphorus and nitrogen) are elevated, particularly in stagnant or very slowly flowing water bodies."
- **[✓ verified]** The most sustainable and effective bloom-prevention measure identified is reducing nutrient loading — particularly phosphorus — into the water body, via control of sewage effluent and catchment sources (erosion, manure, fertilizer); hydrological management such as mixing and flushing can additionally shift the plankton community away from cyanobacteria toward less health-relevant taxa such as diatoms.
  - *evidence:* Presented as WHO's own recommended management priority in Practical Considerations. (Practical Considerations section (~p.345))
  - *quote:* "the most sustainable and effective measure is to reduce nutrient (particularly phosphorus) concentrations in the water body"
- **[✓ verified]** For treatment, particle-removal processes (soil/riverbank filtration, flocculation and filtration, dissolved air filtration) are described as effectively controlling the cell-bound microcystin fraction when optimized and operated to avoid cell rupture; free (dissolved) microcystins are said to be well removed by most activated carbon and by chlorination/ozonation at sufficient dose and contact time; potassium permanganate is effective for microcystins specifically (with limited/no data for other cyanotoxins); oxidation is 'not very effective' for saxitoxins; and chlorine dioxide and chloramine are stated to be ineffective for removing cyanotoxins generally.
  - *evidence:* Treatment-technology efficacy comparison stated directly, differentiated by toxin fraction/class and treatment type. (Practical Considerations section, treatment paragraph (~p.346))
  - *quote:* "Chlorine dioxide and chloramine are ineffective for removing cyanotoxins."

## Data / numbers
- Provisional drinking-water guideline value, total microcystin-LR (free + cell-bound): 0.001 mg/l (1 µg/l) — explicitly labeled provisional/uncertain by the source
- Tolerable daily intake (TDI): 0.04 µg/kg body weight — derived using a 1000-fold uncertainty factor applied to a 13-week oral mouse study (liver pathology as critical effect)
- Limit of detection, HPLC: 0.1–1 µg/l (after methanol extraction or C-18 concentration)
- Limit of detection, ELISA immunoassay: 0.1–0.5 µg/l (less precise than HPLC)
- Limit of detection, protein phosphatase assay: 0.5–1.5 µg/l (less precise than HPLC)
- Guideline-value derivation defaults: 80% of TDI allocated to drinking-water; 60 kg default adult body weight; 2 litres/day default water consumption
- More than 80 distinct microcystin congeners identified to date (only a few reported to occur frequently or at high concentration)
- Supporting toxicology: 13-week oral mouse study (primary basis) plus a 44-day oral pig study (supporting)
- IARC carcinogen classification: Group 2B (possible human carcinogen), assigned in 2006
- Assessment date given for the microcystin-LR guideline value: 2003

## Methods
The source documents WHO's standard chemical risk-assessment/guideline-derivation approach rather than an empirical study of its own: a tolerable daily intake (TDI) is derived from a repeat-dose oral animal toxicity study (13-week mouse study showing liver pathology as the critical effect, supported by a 44-day pig study), divided by a 1000-fold uncertainty factor to cover database gaps (notably missing chronic-toxicity and carcinogenicity data). The TDI is converted into a drinking-water guideline value using default exposure assumptions (80% of TDI allocated to water, 60 kg adult body weight, 2 L/day consumption). For analysis, it compares three detection methods (HPLC, ELISA immunoassay, protein phosphatase assay), stating where each works (HPLC most precise and able to differentiate congeners; the other two "useful for screening" but less precise) and flags that omitting cell extraction causes "dramatic underestimation" of concentration. For operational surveillance, it states that source-water surveillance for bloom/cell-density evidence is the effective approach, while finished-water toxin-concentration monitoring "is unsatisfactory" given toxin diversity and the lack of guideline values/analytical standards beyond microcystin-LR. For treatment, it differentiates efficacy by fraction and toxin type: particle-removal methods (various filtration types) work on the cell-bound fraction if optimized to avoid lysis; oxidation (ozone, chlorine, potassium permanganate) and activated carbon work on free microcystins; but oxidation is "not very effective" for saxitoxins specifically, and chlorine dioxide/chloramine are stated not to work for cyanotoxins generally.

## Stated limitations
The source itself states: (1) the guideline value is provisional — it covers only microcystin-LR (not the >80 other microcystin variants or other cyanotoxin classes), the toxicological database is limited, and new data are still being generated; (2) the 1000-fold uncertainty factor on the TDI explicitly reflects database gaps, especially the lack of chronic-toxicity and carcinogenicity data; (3) the ELISA and protein-phosphatase assays are "less precise in quantification" (and, for the phosphatase assay, also less precise in identification) than HPLC; (4) monitoring finished water directly against toxin targets is called "unsatisfactory" for determining safety, because of toxin diversity, the absence of guideline values beyond microcystin-LR, and the lack of analytical standards for many cyanotoxins; (5) omitting extraction of the cell-bound fraction during analysis causes "dramatic underestimation" of true concentrations; and (6) treatment efficacy is toxin-specific — oxidation is "not very effective for saxitoxins," potassium permanganate has "limited or no data" for toxins other than microcystins, and chlorine dioxide/chloramine are stated to be ineffective for cyanotoxins generally.

## Tensions with other findings
Nothing in this excerpt directly contradicts other HAB sources, but it complicates two things a remote-sensing/in-situ HAB tool might want to claim. First, the source explicitly calls finished-water toxin-concentration monitoring "unsatisfactory" and instead endorses source-water surveillance for bloom/cell-density evidence — this supports using optical/cell-density bloom proxies (e.g., satellite cyanobacteria or chlorophyll signals) as an early-warning input, but the source frames such surveillance as a proxy for bloom presence/potential, not a substitute for a toxin-concentration measurement, so a detected bloom or rising cell density should not be presented as equivalent to a specific microcystin-LR concentration or health-risk level. Second, the only quantitative guideline value given (1 µg/l) applies solely to microcystin-LR — one of >80 microcystin congeners and one of several structurally distinct cyanotoxin classes (cylindrospermopsins, saxitoxins, anatoxin-a, anatoxin-a(s)) that this same source says have materially different treatment-removal efficacy (e.g., oxidation is "not very effective" for saxitoxins, and chlorine dioxide/chloramine are ineffective for cyanotoxins generally). A single risk threshold or treatment recommendation calibrated to microcystin-LR should therefore not be generalized, without qualification, to blooms dominated by other genera/toxins.

# Climate Change and Freshwater Harmful Algal Blooms

**What it is.** An EPA HABs-portal web page that explains, in plain language for a general audience, the mechanisms by which climate change is expected to make freshwater (and adjoining estuarine/marine) cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (HABs) more frequent and severe when combined with nutrient pollution. It is a synthesis/explainer of established climate-science mechanisms (temperature, salinity, CO2, rainfall/nutrient loading, sea level rise, coastal upwelling) rather than an original research study, and cites no specific dataset, statistical model, or peer-reviewed paper of its own.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Climate change effects on freshwaters, estuaries, and marine environments are already being documented by scientists and are predicted to worsen; combined with nutrient pollution, this may make algal blooms more severe and more frequent across more waterbodies.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the page's central framing thesis in the opening paragraph, in hedged/predictive language ("predicted to be exacerbated," "might cause"). (Opening/introductory paragraph (before the "Climate Factors That Affect HABs" section))
  - *quote:* "Scientists continue to document many effects of climate change on freshwaters, estuaries, and marine environments, and those effects are predicted to be exacerbated in the future. These effects, along with nutrient pollution, might cause algal blooms to become more severe and to occur more often in more waterbodies."
- **[✓ verified]** HABs (blooms with potential to harm human health, pets, livestock, or aquatic ecosystems) can also have wide-ranging economic impacts; in freshwater, cyanobacteria are described as the most common HAB producers, and some cyanobacterial HABs produce toxins that cause illness in humans and other animals.
  - *evidence:* Definitional/background framing in the introduction; economic-impact claim is asserted without a figure or citation. (Opening/introductory paragraph)
  - *quote:* "Blooms with the potential to harm human health, pets, livestock, or aquatic ecosystems are referred to as harmful algal blooms, or HABs, and they can also have wide ranging economic impacts."
- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacteria that form HABs thrive in warm, slow-moving water and typically bloom when water temperatures are warmer; climate-driven warming is expected to increase both the magnitude and the duration of cyanoHABs.
  - *evidence:* Presented as the first of six named climate mechanisms, stated as a direct expectation rather than tied to a specific study or number. (Section: "Warming water temperature")
  - *quote:* "Increases in water temperature with climate change are expected to increase the magnitude and duration of cyanoHABs."
- **[✓ verified]** CyanoHABs gain a competitive advantage over other algae in warming water because they can grow faster, can migrate vertically to access nutrients from cool bottom layers while photosynthesizing in the warmer, light-rich upper layer (a trait other algae lack), and absorb sunlight/release heat in ways that further warm the surface water.
  - *evidence:* Explains the biological mechanism behind the temperature-HAB link; no quantitative growth-rate figures given. (Section: "Warming water temperature")
  - *quote:* "Get nutrients from the cooler, dark, bottom layers and use them to grow in the warmer, upper layers where there is more light."
- **[✓ verified]** In some regions, climate change is predicted to reduce freshwater runoff via drought and increased evaporation, raising salinity in inland waters; this has allowed salt-tolerant "golden algae" HABs to recur regularly, expand into lagoons, and kill fish in freshwater lakes of the southwestern and south-central U.S. since 2000.
  - *evidence:* Names a specific regional case and a specific start year for recurring golden-algae kill events, presented as an observed historical pattern rather than a projection. (Section: "Changes in salinity")
  - *quote:* "In some regions, climate change is predicted to reduce freshwater runoff due to drought and increased evaporation... salt-tolerant algal HABs, known as "golden algae," have regularly recurred, expanded into lagoons, and killed fish in freshwater lakes since 2000."
- **[✓ verified]** In other regions, more intense rainfall may increase freshwater flows to coastal areas; because freshwater is lighter and layers atop saltwater, this can prevent mixing between oxygen-rich surface water and oxygen-depleted deeper water (oxygen depleted by decomposition of dead HABs), producing hypoxic zones that harm or kill oxygen-dependent animals, including commercially important species.
  - *evidence:* Describes a density-stratification / hypoxia mechanism linking increased runoff to fish/wildlife harm; no dissolved-oxygen thresholds or species-loss numbers are given. (Section: "Changes in salinity")
  - *quote:* "In other regions, increased runoff from more intense rainfalls may increase freshwater flows to coastal areas."
- **[✓ verified]** Because algae require CO2 to survive, higher atmospheric and aquatic CO2 levels can drive rapid growth of algae, especially cyanoHABs that float to the surface; higher CO2 also raises water acidity, which affects competition among algal species and affects the organisms that graze on algae.
  - *evidence:* States a direct growth-promoting mechanism for CO2 plus a separate, less directional acidification/competition effect; no quantitative CO2 concentration thresholds or growth-rate figures are provided. (Section: "Higher carbon dioxide levels")
  - *quote:* "Higher levels of carbon dioxide in the air and water can lead to rapid growth of algae, especially cyanoHABs that can float to the surface... Increased levels of carbon dioxide also increase the acidity of the water, which affects competition among algal species."
- **[✓ verified]** Climate change is increasing both rainfall intensity and drought duration; increased rainfall causes higher nutrient runoff into waterbodies, which fueled HABs observed in Lake Erie in 2011 and 2015, and subsequent drought lets waterbodies retain those nutrients longer, favoring species that compete well under high-nutrient conditions.
  - *evidence:* Names specific historical Lake Erie bloom years as illustrative examples of the rainfall-to-nutrient-runoff-to-bloom pathway; no loading figures (e.g., tons of phosphorus) are given on this page. (Section: "Changes in rainfall and resulting increases in nutrient loadings")
  - *quote:* "Increased rainfall causes higher nutrient runoff from land into waterbodies fueling HABs like those observed in Lake Erie in 2011 and 2015."
- **[✓ verified]** Increases in extreme rainfall and the resulting increases in freshwater flows may flush large loads of nutrients, freshwater HABs, and their associated toxins into estuaries and marine areas, raising the risk that those waters develop new HABs or see existing HABs worsen.
  - *evidence:* Extends the rainfall/nutrient mechanism from freshwater lakes to downstream estuarine/marine systems; stated as a possibility ("may flush"), not a quantified probability. (Section: "Changes in rainfall and resulting increases in nutrient loadings")
  - *quote:* "Increases in extreme rainfall and subsequent increases in freshwater flows may flush large loads of nutrients, freshwater HABs and their associated toxins into estuaries and marine areas."
- **[✓ verified]** Scientists predict that sea level could rise one meter by the year 2100, which would increase the area of shallow, stable coastal waters that provide favorable conditions for HABs.
  - *evidence:* The only single-number physical projection on the page; presented without a citation to a specific study/model, without a probability range, and without specifying a scenario (e.g., emissions pathway). (Section: "Sea level rise")
  - *quote:* "Scientists predict that sea level could rise one meter by the year 2100."
- **[✓ verified]** Climate change is expected to alter the timing and intensity of coastal upwelling; along the U.S. west coast, excess nutrients delivered by upwelling might lead to more algal blooms, including HABs, especially when combined with increased runoff of land-based nutrient pollution.
  - *evidence:* Final named mechanism; combines a climate-altered ocean-physics process (upwelling) with land-based nutrient pollution as a joint risk factor, stated conditionally ("might lead to"). (Section: "Coastal upwelling")
  - *quote:* "Along the west coast of the United States, excess nutrients delivered by upwelling might lead to more algal blooms, including HABs, especially when combined with increased runoff of nutrient pollution from the land."
- **[✓ verified]** The page illustrates the topic with a real satellite image of a cyanobacterial HAB in the western basin of Lake Erie, captured October 8, 2022, credited to the European Space Agency.
  - *evidence:* This is a documentary illustration (one dated real-world observation), not itself a scientific finding or statistic; useful only as an example image, cross-confirmed verbatim on a follow-up fetch. (Image caption embedded in the page (near top/side of content))
  - *quote:* "A satellite image of a cyanoHAB -taken on October 8, 2022- in the western basin of Lake Erie. (Image credit: European Space Agency)"
- **[✓ verified]** The page cites no specific peer-reviewed study, government report, dataset, or statistical/mechanistic model as the evidentiary basis for any of its six climate-mechanism claims.
  - *evidence:* Absence-of-citation finding, based on reviewing the full extracted text across three separate fetch passes; the only credited source anywhere on the page is the ESA image credit line. (Whole page (all six mechanism sections))

## Methods
Not a primary research study and not a data source in the sense of a dataset, statistical model, or remote-sensing product. It is an EPA public-education synthesis page (part of the same EPA HABs web portal that hosts the CyAN program) that verbally summarizes six climate mechanisms understood to affect freshwater HABs: warming water temperature, changes in salinity, higher CO2 levels, changes in rainfall/nutrient loading, sea level rise, and coastal upwelling. No sampling design, model architecture, statistical test, or specific dataset is described or linked as the direct evidentiary basis for any mechanism; the one concrete artifact on the page is a single ESA satellite image (Oct 8, 2022, Lake Erie) used purely as an illustrative photo, not as analyzed data. The page links out to EPA's broader HABs portal, EPA's "Climate Change's Impacts on Water Resources" page, and NOAA's "Harmful Algal Blooms" page for related information, but the fetched text of this specific page does not itself present or link a specific quantitative study, model output, or dataset that could be traced and reproduced.

## Stated limitations
The page contains no explicit "Limitations" or "Uncertainty" section (consistent with it being a plain-language explainer rather than a scientific paper), but it hedges nearly every mechanism claim with conditional/predictive language rather than asserting certainty: "predicted to be exacerbated," "might cause," "are expected to," "could rise," "may increase," "might lead to." It also explicitly flags that effects are regionally variable rather than uniform ("In some regions... In other regions..." for the salinity/runoff discussion), and it notes for the CO2 mechanism that acidification "affects competition among algal species" without specifying whether that competitive shift favors or disfavors toxin-producing cyanoHABs specifically — i.e., the source itself leaves that sub-mechanism directionally ambiguous.

## Tensions with other findings
This page attributes rising HAB frequency/severity jointly to climate change AND nutrient pollution, which is broadly consistent with (not contradictory to) the wider HAB literature's emphasis on nutrient loading as a primary driver. However, because the page gives no dose-response thresholds, effect sizes, or relative-contribution estimates, it cannot itself be used to apportion how much of any specific bloom (e.g., the 2011 and 2015 Lake Erie events it names) is attributable to climate-driven rainfall intensification versus agricultural/point-source nutrient management practices — a distinction that quantitative peer-reviewed sources on those same Lake Erie blooms (which this dossier entry does not have in hand) may treat very differently. This is a correlational/associational framing throughout ('might cause,' 'may increase') rather than a causal-attribution analysis, and should not be read as establishing causation between any single climate factor and a specific bloom outcome. No direct contradiction with other HAB findings is evident from this page's text alone; the main tension is one of evidentiary weight — this source is useful for conceptual/mechanistic framing and stakeholder narrative, but not as a quantitative citation for a risk model's coefficients or thresholds.

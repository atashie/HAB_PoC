# AquaMatch Chlorophyll a Data from Water Quality Portal: ~1970-2024

**What it is.** A USGS-published, Environmental Data Initiative (EDI)-hosted data release providing a harmonized compilation of chlorophyll-a (and related pigment) records drawn from the multi-agency Water Quality Portal (WQP), covering in-situ measurements from January 6, 1970 to June 20, 2024 across the United States and US Territories. It is presented as one component of a forthcoming "AquaSat version 2" update to the original AquaSat satellite–in-situ water-quality matchup resource (Ross et al., 2019), and organizes records into three data-quality tiers (restrictive, narrowed, inclusive) rather than itself containing satellite matchups.

## Methods
This source is a dataset (data release), not an analytical/methods paper, so there is no statistical model to evaluate. The compilation approach the release itself describes is: (1) source records are drawn from the multi-agency Water Quality Portal (WQP), described in a WebSearch synthesis of this same record as a data warehouse "managed by the Environmental Protection Agency, United States Geological Survey, and the National Water Quality Monitoring Council" covering measurements in the US and US Territories; (2) records are sorted into the three data-quality tiers (restrictive / narrowed / inclusive) described above as the release's stated mechanism for signaling cross-organization and cross-time comparability, rather than asserting all records are equally trustworthy; (3) supplementary "flag columns" are attached to communicate depth- and field-sampling-related complexities; (4) the release is explicitly in-situ only — it "does not contain remote sensing matchups" — and directs users who want satellite pairing to a separate, previously published pipeline (Ross et al., 2019) that adds Landsat surface reflectances. The DOI attached to the Science Data Catalog record (10.6073/pasta/4c795593504d927ac546a36e5dc9fdb0) resolves (302 redirect, confirmed directly) to an Environmental Data Initiative (EDI) repository package (edi.1756.1), indicating EDI is the authoritative host for the actual data files and full metadata. Where it "fails" / falls short for present purposes: the source text retrieved gives no specific unit-harmonization rules, deduplication logic, QA/QC algorithm, or software/code identity, and no validation of the tiering scheme's accuracy — these deeper process-step details live (if anywhere) in the EDI package's full metadata record, which could not be rendered via WebFetch (see fetch_notes for the three access attempts that failed).

## Stated limitations
The release's own text builds two comparability caveats directly into its tiering scheme: "narrowed" records are only believed, not verified, to be self-similar/compatible across data providers ("we have good reason to believe are self-similar, but for which we can not verify full compatibility across data providers"), and "inclusive" records' reliability is merely "assumed," being "harmonized to our best ability given the information available" — i.e., harmonization quality is bounded by whatever metadata each contributing organization happened to supply. The release also states an explicit scope limitation: it "does not contain remote sensing matchups," so satellite pairing requires applying a separate, previously published pipeline (Ross et al., 2019) rather than anything built into this release. Beyond these three points, the landing-page text retrieved contains no dedicated "Limitations," "Data Quality," or "Known Issues" section, no discussion of geographic or temporal sampling gaps/bias, and no accuracy/validation statement for the tiering scheme itself. (Separately, and not a limitation of the source but of this fetch: a fuller limitations statement may exist in the underlying EDI metadata record for package edi.1756.1, which could not be rendered via WebFetch across three attempted routes — see fetch_notes.)

## Tensions with other findings
This source is a raw/harmonized in-situ data compilation, not a HAB trend or risk-modeling study, so it makes no bloom-related predictive or causal claim to directly contradict other findings. Two points are nonetheless worth flagging for the broader review. First, the source's own tiering scheme is itself an implicit caution: it says plainly that only the "restrictive" subset is verifiably self-similar across organizations and time, while "narrowed" and "inclusive" records carry unverified or merely assumed comparability. Any downstream HAB trend/driver analysis that pools chlorophyll-a records across agencies, decades, and methods (as this ~54-year, multi-agency WQP compilation spans) without tier-aware filtering risks mistaking cross-lab/methodological heterogeneity for genuine bloom signal — a measurement-artifact confound rather than a causal environmental one; correlation between pooled chlorophyll-a trends and any driver would not by itself establish causation, and would be especially fragile if tier composition (restrictive vs. inclusive) varies across the very organizations, regions, or time periods being compared. Second, while researching this entry, a WebSearch surfaced a separately titled, differently scoped chlorophyll-a data descriptor associated with overlapping USGS authorship, "Chlorophyll a in lakes and streams of the United States (2005–2022)" (Scientific Data / PMC), reportedly covering ~84,000 sites and >1,374,000 pigment measurements for 2005-2022 — a narrower window than this release's ~1970-2024 span. I did not fetch that paper directly, so I cannot confirm its relationship to (or record overlap with) this AquaMatch release; I flag it only so the two are not conflated or their statistics cross-attributed in the literature review.

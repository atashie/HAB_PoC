# Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) Program Guide (Version 3)

**What it is.** A New York State Department of Environmental Conservation (NYSDEC) program guide (Version 3) documenting how the state's DEC HABs Program defines and classifies cyanobacteria bloom status, coordinates monitoring/reporting/outreach across state agencies and partners, and summarizes approaches to HAB prevention and in-lake control. It is an operational/regulatory reference document, not a peer-reviewed research study.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Freshwater HABs generally consist of cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) that are naturally present at low background abundance in most marine and freshwater systems, and can multiply rapidly into blooms under particular conditions, notably high nutrients and warm temperatures.
  - *evidence:* Stated as general explanatory background in the Executive Summary and repeated in the Introduction/Background section; presented as contextual scientific framing for the program, not as a finding derived from data or a study within this document. (Executive Summary, p. 1; repeated in Section 2.3 Background)
  - *quote:* "particularly high nutrients and warm temperatures, the organisms can begin to multiply rapidly and form blooms"
- **[✓ verified]** The DEC HABs Program assigns each bloom report one of four standardized status levels (No Bloom, Suspicious Bloom, Confirmed Bloom, Confirmed with High Toxins Bloom) using a documented decision-tree that combines visual/photographic evidence, blue-green (BG) chlorophyll concentration, and total microcystins concentration.
  - *evidence:* Presented as the program's core, state-wide operating protocol; the logic is diagrammed in a referenced decision tree figure described as 'updated in 2020.' (Section 3.1 Bloom Status Criteria, p. 9; decision tree in Figure 3.1, p. 10)
  - *quote:* "The DEC HABs Program has established four levels of bloom status"
- **[✓ verified]** "No Bloom" is assigned when, among other allowed criteria, BG chlorophyll is ≤25 µg/L or (absent other criteria) total microcystins is ≤4 µg/L; "Confirmed Bloom" is assigned when BG chlorophyll is ≥25 µg/L, or (absent that) total microcystins is ≥4 µg/L but <20 µg/L together with a photo or field report.
  - *evidence:* Directly quoted operational threshold definitions distinguishing the lowest and mid-level bloom-status tiers. (Section 3.1 Bloom Status Criteria, p. 9)
  - *quote:* "BG chlorophyll levels ≤ 25 µg/L"
- **[✓ verified]** The program's highest alert tier, "Confirmed with High Toxins Bloom," is triggered (in addition to meeting Confirmed Bloom criteria) by total microcystins ≥20 µg/L for shoreline samples, but by a numerically lower threshold of ≥10 µg/L for open-water samples, or by staff judgment of potential exposure risk to anatoxin or another cyanotoxin.
  - *evidence:* Quoted operational threshold definitions; note the source sets a lower µg/L trigger for open-water samples than for shoreline samples, which the text does not explain further. (Section 3.1 Bloom Status Criteria, p. 9)
  - *quote:* "total microcystins ≥ 10 µg/L (open water samples only)"
- **[✓ verified]** DEC states it adopted chlorophyll/microcystin surrogate criteria, rather than requiring microscopy or taxonomic identification on every report, specifically because of practical program constraints: need for rapid turnaround, high volume of reports/waterbodies, limited microscopy/taxonomy capacity, and lags in toxin-lab turnaround.
  - *evidence:* The guide gives this as its own stated rationale for the classification method's design — an explicit, self-acknowledged trade-off between analytical rigor and operational throughput, not a claim that the surrogate method is scientifically optimal. (Section 3.1 Bloom Status Criteria, p. 8)

## Data / numbers
- BG (blue-green) chlorophyll ≤ 25 µg/L → "No Bloom" status criterion (one of several alternative criteria)
- BG chlorophyll ≥ 25 µg/L → "Confirmed Bloom" status criterion
- Total microcystins ≤ 4 µg/L → "No Bloom" criterion, used only if the other (visual/chlorophyll/microscopy) criteria are not met
- Total microcystins ≥ 4 µg/L and < 20 µg/L (plus a photo or field report) → "Confirmed Bloom" criterion, used only if other criteria are not met
- Total microcystins ≥ 20 µg/L in shoreline samples → "Confirmed with High Toxins Bloom"
- Total microcystins ≥ 10 µg/L in open-water samples → "Confirmed with High Toxins Bloom" (a lower numeric bar than the shoreline criterion)
- New York estimated to contain 7,500–16,000 ponded waterbodies (range only; source notes no formal legal definition of "lake" exists, so this is an approximation, not a precise count)
- >200 lakes/year sampled via two DEC ambient monitoring programs (CSLAP and LCI)
- HABs documented in "several hundred" NY waterbodies since 2012 (qualitative, non-precise figure; source explicitly states the true extent is "likely...substantially greater," i.e., a stated known undercount with no numeric uncertainty bound given)
- 2018: 4 regional summits held; Action Plans produced for 12 priority lakes

## Methods
This is a program/policy operating guide, not a statistical or machine-learning modeling study: it documents a rule-based classification protocol. Inputs to the protocol are (1) visual/photographic bloom reports submitted by the public and by trained observers/agency staff, (2) blue-green (BG) chlorophyll concentration — described as the portion of total chlorophyll fluorescence that can be attributed specifically to cyanobacteria (fluoroprobe-based) — and (3) total microcystins concentration, analyzed only by labs certified under the state's Environmental Laboratory Approval Program (ELAP). Where available from a small number of partner programs (Alleghany Reservoir via USACE, Lake Champlain via VT DEC, NYC reservoirs via NYCDEP), quantitative phytoplankton cell counts and qualitative microscopy community-composition data are also used; the source states cell counts are "interpolated using the WHO recreation guidelines (Table 3.2)," though that table's actual numeric content is not present in my excerpt. These inputs are combined via a decision tree (Figure 3.1, "updated in 2020") to assign each report one of four bloom-status labels. The guide states this design works for its intended purpose (rapid turnaround, scalability across a high volume of statewide reports) but explicitly frames it as adopted in place of more rigorous options (universal microscopic/taxonomic identification, full toxin panels) because the program lacks the analytical capacity and turnaround speed to apply those universally; reports with inconclusive photos or descriptions are held in a non-resolving "Suspicious Bloom" category rather than force-classified.

## Stated limitations
The guide itself states: (1) it is not known whether recent increases in reported bloom frequency/duration reflect real environmental change versus improved monitoring/reporting effort ("It is not yet known if recent increases in bloom frequency and duration reflect changing environmental conditions or are a result of improved reporting and monitoring of their occurrence"); (2) documented bloom counts since 2012 ("several hundred" waterbodies) likely understate true occurrence ("it is likely the true extent of bloom occurrence is substantially greater"); (3) the bloom-status classification method was adopted as a practical compromise given limited microscopic/taxonomic analytical capacity and lags in toxin-lab turnaround — presented as an operational proxy, not the most scientifically precise possible measure; (4) reports with insufficient visual/photographic information cannot be resolved and are held indefinitely as "Suspicious Bloom"; (5) there is no formal legal definition of a "lake" in New York, so even the base count of waterbodies (7,500–16,000) is approximate; (6) the guide explicitly excludes marine/coastal/estuarine blooms, which are tracked under a separate DEC Marine Biotoxin Monitoring Program.

## Tensions with other findings
As a state operational/regulatory guide rather than a peer-reviewed research study, this source does not directly engage with or rebut other HAB research, but it raises points relevant to cross-checking other sources in this review: (a) its bloom-severity criteria are built on BG chlorophyll and total microcystins only — other cyanotoxins (e.g., anatoxin) are flagged by qualitative staff judgment of "potential risk" rather than a quantitative threshold, so any severity classification keyed solely to microcystin concentration (as many HAB studies/tools use) may misrepresent risk from blooms dominated by non-microcystin-producing taxa; (b) DEC states health effects "can occur whether toxins are present or not," which is in tension with any risk framework — including a satellite/in-situ fusion tool — that equates "low risk" with "toxin concentration below a lab threshold" rather than with bloom/cyanobacteria presence itself; (c) the guide's own admission that it cannot distinguish a true rise in bloom occurrence from a rise in reporting/monitoring effort is a caution directly relevant to any cross-source time-series or trend analysis that treats historical bloom-report counts as an unbiased proxy for actual HAB frequency.

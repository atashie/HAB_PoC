# Widespread global increase in intense lake phytoplankton blooms since the 1980s

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed Nature research article (Ho, Michalak & Pahlevan, 2019) that uses 30 years (1984–2013) of Landsat 5 satellite imagery to quantify long-term trends in the peak intensity of summertime, near-surface phytoplankton blooms across 71 large lakes in 33 countries on six continents, reporting a widespread global increase in bloom intensity that the authors could not consistently link to any single previously hypothesized driver (temperature, precipitation, or fertilizer use).

## Methods
Core dataset: 30 years (1984–2013) of Landsat 5 Thematic Mapper (TM) imagery at 30 m spatial resolution, covering 71 large lakes in 33 countries across six continents, processed through a Google Earth Engine partnership that handled more than 72 billion Landsat 5 image pixels to derive a near-surface "bloom intensity" signal for each lake's intense summertime blooms. Lakes with at least 14 years of data (n=49) were further grouped into multi-decadal "historical pathway" trajectory categories (Figure 2). Trend/significance testing used a threshold of P < 0.1 to flag statistically meaningful decreases; two independent extractions (and a WebSearch citation-list snippet referencing Mann 1945 and Sen 1968) point to nonparametric Mann-Kendall trend testing with Thiel(Theil)-Sen slope estimation, though this was not read as a direct quoted Methods sentence and so is reported with reduced confidence. The paper also compared bloom-intensity trends against candidate driver time series (temperature, precipitation, fertilizer use) and found no consistent correlation with any single driver. Where the method appears to fail, per an independent 2021 Nature Matters Arising critique (not the original authors' own admission): a single-NIR-band reflectance index is argued to be an unreliable stand-alone bloom proxy, confusable with sediment plumes (potentially affecting ≥82% of the 71 lakes) and submerged aquatic vegetation, degraded by infrequent Landsat 5 revisits, and subject to Fmask cloud/water-masking errors that reportedly caused some of the most severe scum-forming blooms to be excluded as non-water pixels.

## Stated limitations
The paper's own text states that the driver behind the widespread increase is unresolved: bloom-intensity trends "do not track consistently with temperature, precipitation, fertilizer-use trends or other previously hypothesized drivers" — i.e., the authors explicitly could not attribute the trend to a single known cause. The abstract's own framing implicitly scopes the paper to bloom intensity/biomass rather than toxicity, distinguishing "the intensity of a bloom is too high" from cases "when toxin-producing phytoplankton species are present" — this study addresses only the former. The analysis is limited to 71 large lakes (not an exhaustive or random global sample) and to intense, summertime, near-surface blooms specifically. The significance threshold used to flag decreases, P < 0.1, is more permissive than the conventional P < 0.05, which is a fact stated directly in the source's own reported statistic rather than an outside inference. Separately (not a self-stated limitation, but a directly relevant, formally published external critique), a 2021 Nature Matters Arising comment argues the underlying single-NIR-band satellite bloom index can be confounded by sediment plumes and submerged vegetation, weakened by sparse Landsat 5 revisit frequency, and subject to cloud/water-masking (Fmask) errors that dropped some severe scum-forming blooms from the record; the original authors published a reply (doi 10.1038/s41586-021-03255-2) that was not fetched for this dossier, so their rebuttal content is not summarized here.

## Tensions with other findings
A 2021 Nature Matters Arising comment ("Concerns about phytoplankton bloom trends in global lakes," doi 10.1038/s41586-021-03254-3) is a direct, formally published methodological rebuttal of this exact paper, arguing its single-NIR-band Landsat bloom index is confounded by sediment plumes (estimated by the commenters to potentially affect at least 82% of the 71 study lakes) and submerged vegetation, weakened by infrequent Landsat 5 revisits, and subject to cloud/water-masking errors that may have dropped the most severe scum-forming blooms — this is a live, acknowledged controversy in the literature about whether this paper's headline 68%-of-lakes-worsening statistic is measuring bloom intensity or a mix of bloom-and-non-bloom NIR signals. Separately, this paper's own negative/inconclusive result on drivers (no consistent tracking with temperature, precipitation, or fertilizer-use trends) complicates simpler narratives elsewhere in the HAB literature that attribute rising bloom frequency/intensity mainly to warming or nutrient loading; at the same time, its narrower, correlational finding that lakes which "warmed less" were more able to sustain bloom improvement offers qualified (not causal) support for a climate-vs-management-effort tension that recurs across other sources. Any driver-related claims from this paper should be read as correlational only, per the project's correlation-is-not-causation rule.

# Cyanobacterial blooms

**What it is.** A peer-reviewed review article, "Cyanobacterial blooms," by Huisman, Codd, Paerl, Ibelings, Verspagen and Visser, published in Nature Reviews Microbiology (Vol. 16, pp. 471–483; online 26 June 2018; DOI 10.1038/s41579-018-0040-1). It synthesizes evidence that cyanobacterial blooms in freshwater and marine systems are increasing globally in frequency, magnitude and duration, explains the species traits and environmental drivers (eutrophication, climate change, CO2, temperature) that favor them, covers toxin production and human/ecosystem health impacts, and reviews management strategies to prevent or mitigate blooms.

## Methods
This is a synthesis/review article, not a primary single-method study. The extracted text indicates it draws on and cites: (1) paleolimnological sediment-core records (>100 records) to reconstruct centennial-scale (~200-year) trends in cyanobacterial dominance; (2) a cross-sectional comparison of 143 lakes along a subarctic-to-tropical latitudinal transect relating temperature to percent cyanobacterial abundance; (3) multidecadal satellite remote-sensing time series (including MODIS) used for Lake Taihu and Baltic Sea bloom monitoring, with imagery in Fig. 1 credited to the European Space Agency; (4) laboratory selection/evolution experiments demonstrating rapid adaptive change in cyanobacterial CO2-concentrating mechanisms under rising CO2; (5) field/mesocosm experiments on marine warming and CO2 enrichment; (6) coupled hydrodynamic-phytoplankton competition models used to examine heat-wave effects on bloom dynamics; (7) quantitative real-time PCR for quantifying toxic Microcystis genotypes; and (8) long-term sediment/resting-egg-bank reconstructions of Daphnia-Microcystis coevolution in Lake Constance. The extracted text does not describe any of these methods as failing; as a review, it cites them as supporting evidence for its synthesis rather than critiquing their individual performance or giving quantitative error bounds.

## Stated limitations
As a review/synthesis article, the source does not contain a discrete, author-labeled "Limitations" section comparable to a primary research paper. The extracted text does, however, flag several open uncertainties: toxin (e.g., microcystin) production and its environmental/temperature dependence are described as variable and genotype-dependent, with some Microcystis populations containing both toxic and non-toxic strains, which complicates predicting bloom toxicity from bloom presence alone; some proposed community-defence mechanisms (e.g., CRISPR-based antiviral immunity, antiparasitic peptides) are noted as needing further characterization; and projected future bloom expansion is presented as contingent on climate scenarios and lake-specific nutrient/management context rather than as a uniform, universally applicable prediction. No explicit numeric regulatory threshold (e.g., a µg/L guideline value for microcystin in drinking water) appears anywhere in the extracted text, so none is reported here. The review's total reference count could not be reliably confirmed (see fetch_notes) and is not reported as a fact in this dossier.

## Tensions with other findings
The review's emphasis on temperature as a driver of cyanobacterial dominance (e.g., the 143-lake latitudinal-transect result) sits in tension with other HAB literature surfaced during this review's web search — e.g., a paper titled "Nutrients and not temperature are the key drivers for cyanobacterial biomass in the Americas" (ScienceDirect) — which argues nutrients, not temperature, are the dominant predictor of cyanobacterial biomass in some regional datasets. This suggests the temperature-dominance relationship highlighted in this review may not generalize uniformly across all geographies, and the temperature/cyanobacteria correlation should not be read as evidence that temperature alone drives blooms independent of co-varying nutrient conditions (correlation is not causation). Separately, this review's Brazil hemodialysis figure ("more than 50 patients" died) is a rounded figure; other primary literature on the same 1996 Caruaru incident found via search (e.g., The Lancet, 1998) reports different tallies (60 deaths, or 52 of 131 exposed patients with confirmed lethal microcystin exposure), illustrating some variation in reported casualty counts for this widely-cited incident across sources.

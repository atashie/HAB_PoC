# Sediment Resuspension as a System-Wide Driver of Legacy and Bioavailable Phosphorus Release in Lake Erie

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.est.5c17601

**What it is.** A 2026 peer-reviewed primary research article (Dhiman et al., Environmental Science &amp; Technology) that combines in-situ sediment phosphorus fractionation, Beryllium-7 radiotracer analysis, and Sentinel-3 satellite-derived suspended-particulate-matter (SPM) retrievals to produce what the authors call "the first event-resolved, basin-wide estimate of internal TP mobilization and BioP release from sediment resuspension" in western Lake Erie, quantifying how much legacy (previously deposited) phosphorus a single resuspension event remobilizes from lake-bed sediment into the water column.

## Methods
In-situ: sediment cores (GMX-25 Gomex box corer, 4-6 replicate cores/station) and Ponar-grab surface sediments from western Lake Erie stations WLE1, WLE13, WLE14, WLE15 (plus observation site WLE16), sectioned at 1-cm intervals to 5 cm depth, dried at 60°C (~72 h) and sieved to ≤63 μm. A modified five-step sequential extraction partitioned sediment P into loosely-bound, redox-sensitive (NaBD), Al/Fe-oxide-bound (NaOH), Ca-bound (HCl), and residual-organic (persulfate digestion) fractions, analyzed on a Seal AQ2+ Discrete Analyzer per EPA Methods 118A/134A, cross-checked at an independent lab (Univ. of Wisconsin-Milwaukee School of Freshwater Sciences), with 85-105% spike recovery against reference material BCR-684; BioP is defined as the sum of the loosely-bound + redox-sensitive + Al/Fe-oxide-bound fractions. Water-column TP (EPA Method 365.1), SRP, and SPM (gravimetric, 0.7 μm glass-fiber filters) were sampled at 1 m depth in 2023-2024. Beryllium-7 (naturally occurring, atmospherically deposited radiotracer, half-life ~53 days) was measured via high-volume-pumped (~300-800 L) filtered water and sediment cores using gamma spectrometry (EG&G Ortec Ge detectors), feeding a set of 10 steady-state equations to derive sediment mixing rates/depths, particle residence times, settling rates, and mass accumulation rates. Basin-wide scaling used Sentinel-3A/B OLCI top-of-atmosphere radiance (300 m, POLYMER atmospheric correction) processed with a single-wavelength semiempirical algorithm (Nechad et al.) at 665 nm to retrieve SPM and its pre/post-event change (ΔSPM), validated against 117 in-situ sites in the Great Lakes (78 in Lake Erie; NRMSE = 13.9%, the threshold used to flag 'resuspended' pixels). ΔSPM was converted to a per-pixel erosion depth (mass-balance equation using local bulk density/porosity), multiplied by station-derived, depth-weighted sediment P concentrations, and summed basin-wide for the May 18-26, 2023 event, then compared to Maumee River tributary loading/targets. One-way ANOVA with Tukey HSD (α=0.05) tested spatial/temporal differences in sediment P fractions. Where it works: the authors report the sediment-derived theoretical BioP estimates "agreed well" with observed water-column SRP increases, strongest "at directly measured sites, WLE13 and WLE14." Where it is weaker: at peripheral station WLE16, similar satellite-estimated erosion depth to WLE14 did not translate into a comparable SRP rise, attributed to advection of already BioP-depleted resuspended water rather than a failure of the mass-balance method itself — indicating the single-station-to-satellite scaling is sensitive to advective transport away from directly sampled sites.

## Stated limitations
The authors explicitly note: (1) averaging sediment P concentrations across stations and months to build the basin-scaling factor "introduces spatiotemporal uncertainty from spatial and monthly heterogeneity," though they argue this "will be relatively small" when applied over longer satellite time series; (2) "further research is needed to determine the age of sediment overturned within the mixing zone" to distinguish freshly deposited from legacy (older) material being resuspended; (3) the extent to which mobilized P is durably lost/buried versus retained within the actively mixed sediment layer remains uncertain; (4) the study characterizes one resuspension event in this detail — a "~monthly" recurrence is inferred from ⁷Be inventories but not directly measured event-by-event; (5) at peripheral station WLE16, model-consistent erosion depth did not match the (near-absent) observed SRP response, which the authors attribute to sampling water already depleted of BioP rather than a flaw in the mass-balance approach, but this shows the site-level scaling is sensitive to advection and periphery effects; (6) an interpretive caveat that resuspended P "is not 'new' P" — it is recycled, previously deposited material — so "the system may not elicit the same response as from external P inputs," meaning this study does not establish that the internal source is ecologically equivalent in bloom-stimulating potency to the same mass of fresh external P.

## Tensions with other findings
This paper's central quantitative claim — that a single, moderate resuspension event mobilizes internal legacy P equivalent to roughly 4.7-4.8% of TP and ~7.0% of SRP of the current Maumee River spring target loads, at a rate 22-256x the previously assumed aerobic diffusive background flux — sits in tension with nutrient-management frameworks built around controlling external (agricultural) P loading, including the very "spring target loads" the paper itself uses as its comparison baseline. The authors state directly that "most ecological and bloom-forecast models rely on external loading or only consider constant diffusive terms" and that existing mass-balance modeling studies "cannot define or quantify these episodic processes," implying current HAB driver/forecast models may omit a recurring (~monthly, per the ⁷Be data) internal P source. Importantly, the paper does not claim this proves internal loading drives bloom severity or interannual bloom variability — its own language is hedged ("is expected to better constrain interannual HAB variability") — so this is best read as a documented, quantified gap in external-load-centric management/forecasting frameworks (a plausible-mechanism argument), not a demonstrated causal contribution to any specific bloom event; correlation between resuspension-driven P pulses and bloom outcomes is not tested in this paper. Separately, note a minor internal inconsistency: the Abstract states the event contributed "∼4.8%" to TP while the Results section states "∼4.7%" for the same comparison — a small but real discrepancy a careful reader should reconcile rather than assume is fully consistent.

# AI-driven multi-source data fusion for algal bloom severity classification in small inland water bodies: Leveraging Sentinel-2, DEM, and NOAA climate data

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.03808

**What it is.** An arXiv preprint (also published in the peer-reviewed journal Remote Sensing Applications: Society and Environment, vol. 40, article 101800, 2025) by Ioannis Nasios presenting an ensemble machine-learning pipeline — Random Forest, LightGBM, and a multi-input neural network — that fuses Sentinel-2/Landsat-8 optical satellite imagery, Copernicus DEM elevation data, and NOAA HRRR climate data to classify cyanobacterial harmful algal bloom severity into 5 ordinal classes at point locations in small U.S. inland water bodies, developed for a NASA-affiliated machine-learning competition.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** The paper fuses three open remote-sensing/climate data sources – Sentinel-2 optical satellite imagery, Copernicus DEM elevation data, and NOAA HRRR climate data – with an ensemble of AI models to classify cyanobacterial harmful algal bloom severity into 5 ordinal classes at point locations, without relying on in-situ water sampling for the prediction itself (in-situ samples were used only to generate training labels).
  - *evidence:* Stated as the paper's core methodology in the abstract and introduction; corroborated by the detailed data/methods description of the three fused sources. (Abstract / Introduction)
  - *quote:* "This research introduces a high-performing methodology that integrates multiple open-source remote sensing data with advanced artificial intelligence models."
- **[✓ verified]** All three fused data modalities (optical satellite, climate, elevation) contributed positively to model performance; ablations removing any one source performed worse, as did using geomorphology as raw images (vs. engineered statistics) and climate data as summary statistics (vs. a time series).
  - *evidence:* Explicit ablation/discussion statement comparing model variants with and without each data source or feature-representation choice. (Discussion/Conclusion (ablation discussion))
  - *quote:* "All 3 different sources of data contributed to increase modelling performance. Sentinel-2 optical satellite, HRRR climatological data and DEM elevation data, provided valuable features to the models. Experiments conducted without optical satellite or climate or elevation data performed worse."
- **[✓ verified]** The final 3-model ensemble (Random Forest + LightGBM + neural network, simple-averaged) achieved an out-of-fold Region-Averaged RMSE (RA-RMSE) of 0.700 (region mean, 0.711 sample mean) and scored 0.8110 RA-RMSE on the competition's private leaderboard, placing 6th, versus a naive benchmark of 1.5724 and a 1st-place score of 0.7608.
  - *evidence:* Reported in the paper's competition-results table and the out-of-fold regional performance table; the ensemble outperformed each individual model (RF 0.705/0.717, LightGBM 0.739/0.749, NN 0.749/0.773) on the region-mean OOF metric. (Results – Table 2 (private leaderboard) and Table 3 (OOF RA-RMSE by model/region))
  - *quote:* "This method scored 0.8110 on the private LB as trading a small performance loss with a more friendly solution more convenient and simpler was preferred."
- **[✓ verified]** Exact-class classification accuracy across all 5 severity levels was 62.8% (10,720/17,060 correctly classified), but accuracy was much higher, 89.5% (3,228/3,605), when restricted to detecting severe cases (true severity label ≥ 4).
  - *evidence:* Derived from the paper's confusion matrix of out-of-fold final ensemble predictions vs. true severity labels. (Results – Table 4 (confusion matrix))
  - *quote:* "Overall accuracy: 62.8% (10,720/17,060 samples correctly classified to exact severity level)... Severe cases (true label ≥ 4): 89.5% accuracy (3,228/3,605 samples)"
- **[✓ verified]** The 5 severity classes are recovered from a continuous, square-root-transformed cyanobacteria density prediction using optimized cutpoints, not from the raw density units directly.
  - *evidence:* Cutpoints were tuned with SciPy's Nelder-Mead optimizer against out-of-fold predictions, then applied to bin continuous predictions into 5 severity classes. (Methods – target transformation / post-processing)
  - *quote:* "Severity 1: <180; Severity 2: 180–440; Severity 3: 440–979; Severity 4: 979–2,926; Severity 5: >2,926"
- **[✓ verified]** Longitude was reported as the single most important predictive feature overall (notably for the Random Forest model), and the authors found a correlation between geographic location (latitude/longitude) and cyanobacteria severity within their U.S. dataset – but they explicitly caution this pattern is regionally learned and may not transfer outside the U.S.
  - *evidence:* Feature-importance analysis paired with an explicit stated limitation on geographic generalizability; framed here as a correlation, not a mechanistic/causal driver, consistent with the source's own hedge. (Discussion – feature importance and limitations)
  - *quote:* "Data exploration revealed a significant correlation between cyanobacteria severity and both latitude and longitude... The geolocation features that are influential within the U.S. may not have the same impact in other regions."
- **[✓ verified]** NOAA HRRR climate data are limited to the United States, which the authors identify as a structural barrier to building a globally applicable version of the model.
  - *evidence:* Explicitly stated as a geographic limitation of the data source used. (Discussion – limitations)
  - *quote:* "NOAA's climate data are limited to the U.S., which poses a challenge for developing machine learning models intended for use outside the United States."
- **[✓ verified]** Sentinel-2 outperformed Landsat-8 as the optical imagery source, attributed by the authors to Sentinel-2's higher spatial resolution (10 m vs. 30 m) and to more missing data in the Landsat-8 record; only Sentinel-2 was retained in the final solution despite extensive experiments with Landsat-8.
  - *evidence:* Direct comparative statement explaining why Landsat-8, despite being tested extensively, was dropped from the final model. (Discussion – satellite data source comparison)
  - *quote:* "The superior performance observed with Sentinel-2 data, compared to Landsat-8, was likely due to the higher spatial resolution of Sentinel-2 satellites... Greater amount of missing data in the Landsat dataset may have further reduced its effectiveness."
- **[✓ verified]** The test-set label distribution differed from the training-set distribution (test skewed toward higher cyanobacteria severity), which the authors say partly explains why the leaderboard (test) score was worse than the out-of-fold (train) score, and required extra care to avoid model overconfidence.
  - *evidence:* Explicit stated caveat about train/test distribution mismatch and its effect on validation-vs-test score gap. (Discussion – limitations)
  - *quote:* "Test samples distribution is quite different from train samples. Samples in test data are in general of higher cyanobacteria severity... This is also reflected partially in the difference between the OOF score and the LB score as the LB score was worse."

High-severity cyanobacteria estimates could trigger alerts to warn about potential safety hazards"

## Data / numbers
- Total samples: 23,570 (train+test combined), training dates Jan 4, 2013 – Dec 14, 2021, across 4 US regions (West, Midwest, South, Northeast)
- Sentinel-2: 23,130 images retrieved at (64,64,7) px then center-cropped to (32,32,7); 10 m spatial resolution; ~5-day revisit; ≤20-day pre-sampling window; <30% cloud-cover filter
- Landsat-8: 20,035 images at (64,64,7)-equivalent 7-band format; 30 m spatial resolution; ~8-day revisit; used only for dates before mid-2015 or when Sentinel-2 unavailable
- Copernicus DEM: 30 m resolution; geomorphology array shape (23,556, 32, 32); altitude feature vector length 23,556; retrieval footprint 4,000 m × 4,000 m around each sampling point
- NOAA HRRR climate data: 3 km resolution, hourly; array shape (20,502, 28, 5) = 20,502 samples × 28-day window × 5 variables (temperature, rain, gust/wind, snow, geopotential height/HGT); reduced to 3 variables (temperature, rain, wind) in refined analysis
- Random Forest: n_estimators=300, 45 input features, 5-fold cross-validation
- LightGBM: 19 features (RF importance > 0.005); num_rounds=600, learning_rate=0.025, bagging_fraction=0.9, bagging_freq=6, subsample=0.8, num_leaves=20; region sample weights Midwest=1.51, Northeast=1.98, South=1.11, West=1.30
- Neural network: CNN (EfficientNetB3 backbone, 6 Sentinel-2 channels) → 256 features; RNN (5 weather vars × 28 days) → 64 features; Dense (tabular/geo/DEM) → 64 features; concatenated to 384 features; Adam optimizer, cosine-annealing LR from 0.001 over 60 epochs in 2 snapshots; region sample weights Midwest=0.86, Northeast=1.12, South=0.58, West=0.71
- Out-of-fold Region-Averaged RMSE (RA-RMSE), region-mean / sample-mean: Random Forest 0.705 / 0.717; LightGBM 0.739 / 0.749; Neural Network 0.749 / 0.773; Ensemble (simple average of 3 models) 0.700 / 0.711
- Private-leaderboard RA-RMSE: 1st place 0.7608; 2nd 0.7616; 3rd 0.7844; 4th 0.7879; 5th 0.8034; presented approach (6th place) 0.8110; naive benchmark 1.5724
- Overall exact-class classification accuracy: 62.8% (10,720/17,060 samples); accuracy restricted to severe cases (true severity ≥4): 89.5% (3,228/3,605 samples)
- Severity class cutpoints on sqrt-transformed cyanobacteria density: Severity 1 <180; Severity 2 180–440; Severity 3 440–979; Severity 4 979–2,926; Severity 5 >2,926 (max prediction clipped to 4); cutpoints tuned via SciPy Nelder-Mead on OOF predictions

## Methods
Ensemble ML pipeline for 5-class cyanobacterial bloom severity classification in small U.S. inland water bodies, built for a NASA-affiliated machine-learning competition run with NOAA, EPA, USGS and others (one extraction associated it with a "Tick Tick Bloom" challenge referenced via a winners' report, though this label appeared in only one of the two full-text fetches). Three models are trained on the same fused feature set and simple-averaged into an ensemble: (1) Random Forest (scikit-learn, 300 trees, 45 hand-engineered features spanning Sentinel-2 band statistics, spectral indices, date, lat/lon, HRRR climate statistics, and DEM/altitude statistics; 5-fold CV); (2) LightGBM gradient boosting on the 19 RF-important features, with region-based sample weighting to offset severe regional/class imbalance; (3) a multi-input Keras/TensorFlow neural network combining a CNN (EfficientNetB3 backbone) on Sentinel-2 imagery, an RNN on the 28-day HRRR weather time series, and a dense branch on tabular/geo/DEM features, trained on square-root-transformed cyanobacteria density with test-time augmentation. Final severity classes are recovered from the continuous ensemble output via Nelder-Mead-optimized cutpoints. Data were retrieved via Google Earth Engine (Sentinel-2/Landsat-8) and Microsoft Planetary Computer (DEM, HRRR). The paper reports the approach "works" (yields measurable performance gains) when all three modalities are combined, when Sentinel-2 rather than Landsat-8 is used as the optical source, when climate data is used as a time series rather than as summary statistics, and when geomorphology is engineered into statistical features rather than used as raw images. It reports the approach underperforms relative to the top entries in its own competition, which used simpler gradient-boosted-tree-only pipelines with explicit water-body segmentation (K-means clustering or Sentinel-2 scene classification) to remove land pixels; the presented fused approach placed 6th (RA-RMSE 0.8110) versus 1st place (0.7608), and its exact-class accuracy (62.8%) is moderate even though severe-case detection is strong (89.5%).

## Stated limitations
(1) Geographic scope is effectively US-only because the NOAA HRRR climate data source is US-only: "NOAA's climate data are limited to the U.S., which poses a challenge for developing machine learning models intended for use outside the United States," and learned geolocation-feature importance "may not have the same impact in other regions." (2) Sample geolocation inaccuracies can cause land to be misclassified as water; the South region is called "noisy" for this reason, and the West region reportedly suffered because many sample sites were small rivers under 10 m wide, near or below Sentinel-2's 10 m pixel resolution. (3) Image-acquisition constraints (20-day window, <30% cloud cover requirement, satellite revisit cadence) caused missing imagery, "especially for the Landsat satellite," and some NOAA records were unretrievable. (4) Train/test label-distribution mismatch: test data skew toward higher cyanobacteria severity than training data, partly explaining a worse leaderboard score than out-of-fold score, and requiring extra care so models were "not... overconfident." (5) The chosen image footprint deliberately includes land as well as water around each sampling point rather than segmenting out water, a trade-off the authors accept for robustness to shifted geolocation/obstructions at some cost to precision. (6) The 28-day climate window was not rigorously tuned ("subject to further investigation"), though the authors believe alternative window lengths are unlikely to change results much. (7) The fused, more complex approach was benchmarked only within one US competition dataset and placed 6th, behind five simpler gradient-boosted-tree-only pipelines that used explicit water-body segmentation; overall exact-class accuracy (62.8%) is moderate even though severe-case accuracy (89.5%) is high.

## Tensions with other findings
This source defines "bloom severity" via a competition-specific 5-class ordinal label derived from cyanobacteria cell density (square-root transformed, with optimized numeric cutpoints such as <180 and 979-2,926) tied to a U.S. NASA/NOAA/EPA/USGS competition dataset — not via EPA CyAN's cyanobacteria index or Water Quality Portal chlorophyll-a records that the HAB_PoC brief centers on, so these specific thresholds/units are not obviously portable to CyAN or WQP scales without a documented conversion. It also complicates a simple "more data fusion is better" narrative relevant to the HAB_PoC's "explainability over sophistication" principle: the 3-source fused ensemble (Sentinel-2 + DEM + NOAA climate, tree models + neural network) placed only 6th in its own competition, behind entries using simpler, single-modality gradient-boosted-tree pipelines with explicit water-body segmentation — suggesting careful feature/label engineering (e.g., accurate water masking) may matter more than adding data sources or model complexity. Additionally, the paper's own top feature, longitude, is reported only as a correlation ("significant correlation between cyanobacteria severity and both latitude and longitude"), and the source does not claim geography causes bloom severity; per the HAB_PoC's "correlation ≠ causation" and "actively hunt leakage" principles, this is likely a proxy for unmeasured regional climatology/land-use/nutrient regimes, and the fact that regional label-distribution imbalance was strong enough to require region-based sample weighting (weights ranging 0.58-1.98 across regions) is a caution against treating geolocation importance as a transferable driver.

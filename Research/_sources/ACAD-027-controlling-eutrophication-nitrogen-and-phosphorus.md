# Controlling Eutrophication: Nitrogen and Phosphorus

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1167755

**What it is.** A short, highly-cited peer-reviewed commentary in Science (Feb 20, 2009) by eight senior eutrophication/water-quality scientists arguing that effective management of eutrophication in many freshwater and coastal/marine ecosystems requires reducing both nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) inputs together, challenging the traditional lake-management paradigm of phosphorus-only control. It is an argument/synthesis piece, not a primary data study, and it provoked a formal published rebuttal in the same journal months later.

## Tensions with other findings
This source sits on one side of a long-running, still-active scientific and management controversy over whether nitrogen control is necessary in addition to phosphorus control to manage lake/estuary eutrophication. It was directly and formally rebutted by Schindler & Hecky (Science 2009, 324:721-722, with Conley et al. reply at 724-725), and later (per Schindler 2012, PMC3479793) characterized as lacking "ecosystem-scale verification," with Schindler maintaining that phosphorus-only control is sufficient and better supported by long-term whole-lake experiments in many freshwater systems. Any use of this paper's dual-nutrient-control recommendation in a HAB literature review should flag it as contested rather than consensus, and should be read alongside the opposing view (e.g., Schindler's whole-ecosystem N-reduction experiment, PNAS 2008) before being used to support a treatment/management claim.

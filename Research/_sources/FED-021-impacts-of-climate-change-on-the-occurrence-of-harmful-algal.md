# Impacts of Climate Change on the Occurrence of Harmful Algal Blooms

**What it is.** A May 2013 U.S. EPA Office of Water fact sheet (820-S-13-001) that synthesizes existing science on the mechanisms by which climate change — warming, salinity shifts, rising atmospheric CO2, altered rainfall patterns, sea level rise, and changing coastal upwelling — may promote the growth, dominance, and geographic spread of harmful algal blooms (HABs) in freshwater and marine ecosystems. It is explicitly framed as a plausibility/mechanism summary for researchers and decision-makers, not a primary research study, and it states outright that further research is needed to confirm the climate-HAB association.

## Methods
This is a narrative synthesis fact sheet, not a primary research study: it states its purpose is 'to provide climate change researchers and decision-makers a summary of the potential impacts of climate change on harmful algal blooms in freshwater and marine ecosystems.' It presents no original data collection, sample sizes, statistical tests, or predictive model. All mechanisms — thermal stratification physics, viscosity-temperature relationships, CO2 solubility/photosynthesis chemistry, drought-salinity dynamics, coastal-upwelling oceanography — are explained qualitatively/narratively, with no citations to specific underlying studies anywhere in the extracted text. The two concrete empirical/quantitative claims given (the post-2000 Prymnesium parvum spread in SW/south-central U.S. lakes, and the 18cm-1m sea-level-rise projection) are asserted without naming the source study, monitoring program, model, or scenario behind them.

## Stated limitations
The source explicitly caveats its own conclusions in the Summary section: "Although much of the evidence presented in this fact sheet suggests that the problem of harmful algal blooms may worsen under future climate scenarios, further research is needed to better understand the association between climate change and harmful algae." It also qualifies the coastal-upwelling mechanism as variable and not HAB-specific ("[HAB growth in upwelling systems] vary due to atmospheric oscillations... and is subject to water-column stratification, nutrient availability, and the intensity and persistence of upwelling conditions"; intensified upwelling favors "the growth of both harmful algal blooms and benign species"). No uncertainty ranges, confidence intervals, sample sizes, or citations to primary literature are given anywhere in the text for its quantitative figures (77°F threshold, 18cm-1m sea-level-rise range, post-2000 golden-algae trend).

## Tensions with other findings
Within the document itself, the coastal-upwelling mechanism is described as benefiting "both harmful algal blooms and benign species" — an explicitly non-selective effect that sits in tension with the fact sheet's broader narrative (temperature, viscosity, and CO2 mechanisms) that climate change gives HABs a specific competitive edge over non-harmful algae. More generally, because this is a 2013 qualitative mechanism/plausibility review that itself states the climate-HAB association "needs further research," it should be weighed in a literature review as a hypothesis-generating synthesis rather than a validated causal or predictive finding when set against quantitative, site-specific, or attribution-focused HAB studies elsewhere in the dossier; the text provides no data or statistical test capable of separating climate-driven effects from co-occurring drivers such as direct nutrient (nitrogen/phosphorus) loading, which it does not address as an independent or confounding variable.

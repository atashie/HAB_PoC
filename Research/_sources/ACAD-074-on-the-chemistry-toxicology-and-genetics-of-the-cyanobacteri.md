# On the Chemistry, Toxicology and Genetics of the Cyanobacterial Toxins, Microcystin, Nodularin, Saxitoxin and Cylindrospermopsin

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.mdpi.com/1660-3397/8/5/1650

**What it is.** A 2010 open-access, peer-reviewed review article in Marine Drugs (Pearson, Mihali, Moffitt, Kellmann & Neilan) that synthesizes roughly a decade of genetic and biochemical research to summarize the chemistry (structural variants), toxicology (mechanism of action, potency, and health effects), and genetics/biosynthesis (gene clusters and pathways) of the four major cyanobacterial toxin classes: microcystin, nodularin, saxitoxin, and cylindrospermopsin.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Cyanobacterial toxins fall into four toxicological classes—neurotoxins, hepatotoxins, cytotoxins, and dermatoxins (irritant toxins)—despite being structurally very diverse.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the organizing toxicological framework in the abstract for the four toxins covered (microcystin, nodularin = hepatotoxins; saxitoxin = neurotoxin; cylindrospermopsin = cytotoxin/hepatotoxin). (Abstract)
  - *quote:* "the cyanotoxins span four major classes: the neurotoxins, hepatotoxins, cytotoxins, and dermatoxins (irritant toxins). However, structurally they are quite diverse."
- **[⚠ partial]** Microcystin is the largest and most chemically diverse cyanotoxin class: a monocyclic heptapeptide with roughly 90 known structural isoforms, the most common being microcystin-LR (MW 995.17).
  - *evidence:* Both independent fetches of the same article agreed on the isoform count (~90) and the molecular weight of microcystin-LR, cross-validating the figures. (Section 2 (Microcystin), Chemistry subsection)
  - *quote:* "Around 90 microcystin isoforms varying by degree of methylation, hydroxylation, epimerization, peptide sequence and toxicity"
  - *reviewer:* The source supports the structural details, isoform count (~90), and molecular weight, but does not explicitly state that microcystin is 'the largest and most chemically diverse cyanotoxin class.' This ranking is inferrable from isoform counts (90 vs. nodularin's 7 vs. saxitoxin's 30+) but not directly stated.
- **[⚠ partial]** Microcystin-LR is markedly more acutely lethal to mice than the rarer variant microcystin-RR (intraperitoneal LD50 50 µg/kg vs. 600 µg/kg), and both act by inhibiting serine/threonine protein phosphatases 1 and 2A.
  - *evidence:* LD50 values and mechanism reported identically in both fetches; the review traces the mechanism to cytoskeletal degradation, hepatocyte retraction, and hemorrhagic liver damage. (Section 2 (Microcystin), Toxicology subsection)
  - *quote:* "Inhibition of protein phosphatases 1 and 2A"
  - *reviewer:* The LD50 values and PP1/PP2A inhibition are supported, but the source text specifies only 'protein phosphatases 1 and 2A' without the 'serine/threonine' qualifier, which the claim adds.
- **[✓ verified]** The review raises a hypothesis-level (not proven-in-humans) concern that chronic, sub-lethal microcystin exposure could contribute to cancer risk, based on laboratory rodent studies showing tumor promotion.
  - *evidence:* Framed by the source as an animal-model finding plus an inferred human concern, not an established human causal link — correlation/mechanistic hypothesis only. (Section 2 (Microcystin), Toxicology subsection)
  - *quote:* "Chronic exposure to microcystin can indeed promote skin and liver tumors in rats and mice"
- **[✓ verified]** The microcystin biosynthesis gene cluster (mcyS: 55 kb, 10 genes in two divergent operons mcyA–C and mcyD–J, sequenced from Microcystis aeruginosa PCC7806) was the first complete secondary-metabolite gene cluster ever fully sequenced from a cyanobacterium.
  - *evidence:* Stated directly as a discovery/priority claim about the mcy cluster. (Section 2 (Microcystin), Genetics subsection)
  - *quote:* "first complex metabolite gene cluster to be fully sequenced from a cyanobacterium"
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystin gene transcription responds to light in a threshold-like way (distinct dark/low-light vs. medium/high-light regimes), and high light also shifts the toxin-variant mixture toward the more toxic DeLR isoform.
  - *evidence:* Specific numeric light thresholds and fold-changes in isoform ratio are given by the review, describing manipulated-light laboratory experiments rather than field observation. (Section 2 (Microcystin), Regulation subsection)
  - *quote:* "Light threshold 1: 0–16 μmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹ … Light threshold 2: 31–68 μmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹"
- **[✓ verified]** Nodularin (cyclic pentapeptide, MW 619, 7 known natural isoforms) inhibits PP1/PP2A at a similar potency to microcystin (IC50 ≈ 0.1 nM) but binds PP2A non-covalently rather than covalently, which the authors hypothesize may explain its additional carcinogenic potential relative to microcystin.
  - *evidence:* Explicitly flagged by the source as a hypothesis ('hypothesized'), not a demonstrated causal mechanism. (Section 3 (Nodularin), Toxicology subsection)
  - *quote:* "additional carcinogenic properties"
- **[✓ verified]** The nodularin biosynthesis gene cluster (ndaS: 48 kb, 9 ORFs, ndaA–I) was sequenced from Nodularia spumigena NSOR10 in 2004; its tailoring enzyme NdaH shares 71% sequence identity with the microcystin enzyme McyI.
  - *evidence:* Specific cluster size, ORF count, sequencing year, and percent identity given directly. (Section 3 (Nodularin), Genetics subsection)
  - *quote:* "71% identity with McyI"
- **[✓ verified]** Saxitoxin and its more than 30 natural derivatives are potent voltage-gated sodium-channel blockers; in mice the intraperitoneal LD50 is 10 µg/kg, the estimated human lethal dose is as little as 1 mg, and the toxin causes an estimated 2,000 paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP) cases worldwide per year with a 15% mortality rate.
  - *evidence:* Numeric toxicity and epidemiological figures reported consistently in both independent fetches. (Section 4 (Saxitoxin), Toxicology subsection)
  - *quote:* "Annual estimated 2000 cases of PSP globally, with a mortality rate of 15%"
- **[✓ verified]** The saxitoxin (sxt) biosynthesis gene cluster spans more than 35 kb (26 proteins/30 catalytic functions) in Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii T3 and a somewhat smaller ~28 kb in Anabaena circinalis and Aphanizomenon sp.; phylogenetic analysis of the O-carbamoyltransferase gene suggests the cluster's most likely evolutionary origin is an ancestral alpha-proteobacterium, subsequently spread by horizontal gene transfer.
  - *evidence:* Cluster sizes stated directly; evolutionary origin is explicitly an inference from phylogenetic analysis, not a directly observed fact. (Section 4 (Saxitoxin), Genetics/Evolution subsection)
  - *quote:* "most likely origin of the gene was an ancestral α-proteobacterium"
- **[✓ verified]** Whether saxitoxin-producing eukaryotes (e.g., puffer fish, the Panamanian golden frog) actually biosynthesize the toxin themselves or merely bioaccumulate it from other organisms is explicitly unresolved in the literature the review surveys.
  - *evidence:* Directly flagged by the review as a contested, open question — a live tension relevant to any downstream claim about toxin 'production' by these organisms. (Section 4 (Saxitoxin), Chemistry subsection)
  - *quote:* "Whether or not these complex eukaryotic organisms actually produce PSPs or simply accumulate the toxins in their tissues and organs is an issue of contention"
- **[✓ verified]** Some published claims of bacterial (non-dinoflagellate) saxitoxin biosynthesis are considered controversial because the analytical methods used to support them were not definitive and could not be independently reproduced.
  - *evidence:* Explicit methodological caveat stated by the review about prior literature it surveys. (Section 4 (Saxitoxin) narrative)
  - *quote:* "findings are controversial, as the analytical methods used were not definitive and could not be readily repeated by other researchers"
- **[✓ verified]** Cylindrospermopsin — a polyketide-derived alkaloid classed as hepatotoxic, cytotoxic and neurotoxic, and considered a potential carcinogen — caused a well-documented 1979 human poisoning outbreak on Palm Island, Queensland, Australia, hospitalizing 148 people with hepatoenteritis after a bloom of Cylindrospermopsis raciborskii contaminated a drinking-water reservoir.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a historical, documented incident with a specific hospitalization count and named causative bloom species. (Section 5 (Cylindrospermopsin), Toxicology/history subsection)
  - *quote:* "148 people hospitalized with hepatoenteritis"
- **[✓ verified]** The cylindrospermopsin biosynthesis gene cluster (cyr: 43 kb, 15 ORFs, sequenced from C. raciborskii AWT205) includes a proposed—but not fully confirmed—novel pyrimidine-biosynthesis mechanism for forming the toxin's uracil ring, a step the authors state had previously been unexplained.
  - *evidence:* Explicit statement that the uracil-ring-formation mechanism was previously unresolved ('elusive'); the review offers a proposed pathway rather than direct experimental proof. (Section 5 (Cylindrospermopsin), Genetics subsection)
  - *quote:* "a reaction that has been elusive so far and is required for the toxicity of the final cylindrospermopsin compound"
- **[✓ verified]** The review states that research on microcystin (hepatotoxin) regulation lacks standardized experimental growth controls across studies, which it flags as a methodological weakness of that sub-field.
  - *evidence:* Directly stated limitation about lack of standardization across the literature the review surveys. (Section 2 (Microcystin), Regulation subsection)
  - *quote:* "the subject of hepatotoxin regulation remains a somewhat contentious issue"

## Methods
This is a review, so it synthesizes methods used across the primary literature it surveys rather than reporting new experiments. Chemistry/structure work relied on chromatographic purification and mass-spectrometry-based structural characterization of toxin congeners (e.g., distinguishing ~90 microcystin isoforms). Toxicology was established via intraperitoneal (and some oral) LD50 assays in mice, in vitro protein-phosphatase-inhibition assays (IC50) for microcystin and nodularin, oxidative-stress enzyme assays (e.g., GST/CAT activity in flounder liver) for bioaccumulation studies, and chronic rodent exposure studies underlying the tumor-promotion claims. Genetics/biosynthesis work used a 'reverse genetic' cloning approach to identify and fully sequence the mcy, nda, sxt, and cyr gene clusters; degenerate PCR to find genes not contiguous with a main cluster (e.g., the nodularin PPT gene, sxtX); real-time PCR and RNase-protection assays to quantify transcriptional responses to light, iron, and nutrient conditions; and bioinformatic domain/homology analysis plus phylogenetics to assign gene function and infer evolutionary origin (e.g., the proposed alpha-proteobacterial origin of the sxt O-carbamoyltransferase gene). The review reports the reverse-genetics approach as successful for saxitoxin — yielding a complete cluster in C. raciborskii T3 that was subsequently confirmed in two further species — but explicitly states that some other groups' analytical methods for claiming bacterial saxitoxin biosynthesis were 'not definitive and could not be readily repeated,' and that homology-based function assignment failed for several genes (sxtJ, sxtK, cyrO) because no functionally characterized database homologs exist for them.

## Stated limitations
The authors flag several specific evidentiary gaps: (1) microcystin regulation studies lack standardized growth conditions/controls across the field, making the topic "somewhat contentious"; (2) the source of d-Glu in nodularin biosynthesis (inside vs. outside the ndaS cluster) is unresolved; (3) the transport mechanism delivering nodularin into hepatocytes has not been identified ("studies have been unable to identify the specific mechanism of transport"); (4) it is unclear why a putative transposase and a high-light-inducible chlorophyll-binding-protein gene are co-transcribed with the nodularin gene cluster; (5) whether some eukaryotes (e.g., puffer fish, the golden frog) truly biosynthesize saxitoxin-class toxins or merely bioaccumulate them from other organisms is "an issue of contention"; (6) two saxitoxin-cluster ORFs (sxtJ, sxtK) have database homologs but "none of these genes have been functionally characterized"; (7) some published claims of bacterial saxitoxin biosynthesis rest on analytical methods the authors call "not definitive" and "not readily repeated by other researchers"; (8) the mechanism forming cylindrospermopsin's uracil ring (essential for toxicity) "has been elusive so far," with the paper offering only a proposed, not experimentally confirmed, pathway; (9) no promoter has been identified within the cylindrospermopsin gene cluster itself, so its regulation (hypothesized to involve the global nitrogen regulator NtcA) is inferred rather than demonstrated; and (10) the cylindrospermopsin regulatory protein CyrO's function could not be further resolved because of low sequence homology to characterized database proteins.

## Tensions with other findings
This review deliberately scopes itself to only four cyanotoxin classes (microcystin, nodularin, saxitoxin, cylindrospermopsin) and explicitly does not cover other cyanotoxins that appear elsewhere in the HAB literature (e.g., anatoxins); its toxin list should not be treated as exhaustive when read against broader HAB sources. Internally, the source itself surfaces two live scientific controversies relevant to any downstream HAB-toxin claim: (1) whether certain non-cyanobacterial/non-dinoflagellate organisms reported to carry saxitoxin actually synthesize it or merely bioaccumulate it from other organisms ("an issue of contention"), and (2) that some published claims of bacterial saxitoxin biosynthesis rested on analytical methods the authors themselves call non-definitive and non-repeatable — meaning not every 'production' claim in the wider literature should be taken at face value. Finally, as a 2010 synthesis, its isoform/derivative counts (~90 microcystin variants, 7 nodularin variants, >30 saxitoxin derivatives) are counts as of that publication date; this dossier entry does not have evidence of more recent figures and treats these counts as a historical floor rather than a current inventory.

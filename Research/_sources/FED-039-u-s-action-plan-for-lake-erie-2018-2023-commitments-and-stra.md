# U.S. Action Plan for Lake Erie 2018-2023 (Commitments and Strategy for Phosphorus Reduction)

**What it is.** A binational U.S.–Canada policy action plan (finalized February 2018, covering 2018-2023), developed by USEPA's Great Lakes National Program Office with five U.S. states and multiple federal agencies, that lays out phosphorus-load reduction commitments and targets for Lake Erie under Annex 4 (Nutrients) of the 2012 Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement. It documents the HAB/hypoxia problem and its drivers, sets 2008-baseline percent-reduction targets by basin and watershed, and outlines strategy, partners, and an adaptive-management framework for tracking progress.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Lake Erie is a drinking-water source for 12 million people in the U.S. and Canada, and harmful/nuisance algal growth has risen sharply over the past decade, linked to high phosphorus delivery from major rivers during spring storms, threatening a $12.9 billion tourism industry.
  - *evidence:* Stated as the framing/motivation in the plan's Purpose section; asserts association between rising phosphorus delivery and rising algal growth, not a controlled causal test. (PURPOSE, p. ii)
  - *quote:* "Harmful and nuisance algal growth has increased significantly in the past 10 years, in large part because of high levels of nutrients, specifically phosphorus that is delivered from major rivers during spring storms."
- **[✓ verified]** During the 2011 Lake Erie bloom, measured microcystin concentrations reached 50x the WHO safe body-contact limit and 1,200x the WHO safe drinking-water limit.
  - *evidence:* Presented as a measured/observed toxin concentration comparison against WHO reference limits; no measurement method or sample count given in this excerpt. (BACKGROUND > Problem and drivers, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "measurements of microcystin during the 2011 bloom were 50 times higher than the World Health Organization limit for safe body contact, and 1,200 times higher than the limit for safe drinking water"
- **[✓ verified]** In August 2014, more than 500,000 people lost drinking water for three days when algal toxins forced closure of the Toledo, Ohio drinking-water treatment plant.
  - *evidence:* Cited as a real, dated public-health event illustrating HAB consequences; presented as established fact, no dispute noted. (BACKGROUND > Problem and drivers, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "In August 2014, more than 500,000 people were without drinking water for three days when elevated levels of algal toxins forced the closure of the Toledo, Ohio, drinking water treatment plant."
- **[✓ verified]** Lake Erie's Central Basin hypoxic ('dead zone') area has averaged about 4,500 km2 since the early 2000s, with the largest single event of 8,800 km2 in 2012 (the year after the record 2011 bloom); 2015 is cited as the worst bloom year on record as of this report.
  - *evidence:* Trend claim drawing on multi-year monitoring described qualitatively (no cited dataset/method given in this excerpt); '2015 worst on record' is time-bound to the report's Feb-2018 writing date, not necessarily current. (BACKGROUND > Problem and drivers, p. 1)
  - *quote:* "the hypoxic (low-oxygen) area in the Central Basin of Lake Erie has increased to about 4,500 km2, on average, with the largest hypoxic event of 8,800 km2 occurring in 2012"
- **[✓ verified]** In 2012, hypoxic conditions were linked to a large fish kill — tens of thousands of dead fish washing up along roughly a 40 km stretch of Ontario shoreline.
  - *evidence:* Sentence is cut off in the supplied windowed excerpt ('...40 km stretch of Ontario's s...'), so no verbatim quote is given here to avoid completing a truncated word; core facts (magnitude, distance, year, location) are clearly stated before the cutoff. (BACKGROUND > Problem and drivers, p. 1-2 (sentence truncated by source windowing))
- **[✓ verified]** Phosphorus load is unevenly distributed across Lake Erie's three basins: Western 61%, Central 28%, Eastern 11% of the whole-lake annual total-phosphorus load, correlating with different basin depths and algal problems.
  - *evidence:* Stated as measured/estimated load-share figures (source of the underlying loading estimate not specified in this sentence); basins also differ in mean/max depth (Western 7.4 m/19 m; Central 18.3 m/25 m; Eastern 24 m/64 m). (BACKGROUND > Lake Erie's 3 basins, p. 3)
  - *quote:* "The Western Basin receives about 61 percent of the whole lake annual total phosphorus load, while the Central Basin and Eastern Basin receive 28 percent and 11 percent, respectively."
- **[✓ verified]** The Western Basin, being shallow, warm, and phosphorus-rich (fed by the Detroit and Maumee Rivers), regularly hosts blooms of the toxin-producing cyanobacterium Microcystis aeruginosa.
  - *evidence:* Presented as an established ecological pattern; basin physical traits (depth/warmth/nutrient supply) are offered as the explanatory context for why this basin, specifically, hosts toxic cyanobacterial blooms. (BACKGROUND > Lake Erie's 3 basins (Western Basin), p. 3)
  - *quote:* "algal blooms dominated by the blue-green alga (cyanobacteria) Microcystis aeruginosa occur regularly in the summer months"
- **[✓ verified]** The Eastern Basin, despite much lower phosphorus levels than the Western/Central basins, supports excessive growth of the filamentous green alga Cladophora on rocky substrate, causing beach fouling, odor, and clogged intakes rather than cyanotoxin risk.
  - *evidence:* Illustrates that basin-level nutrient-algae relationships differ by algal taxon, not just by phosphorus concentration; described as more frequent on the basin's northern shore. (BACKGROUND > Lake Erie's 3 basins (Eastern Basin), p. 3-4)
  - *quote:* "Mats of Cladophora can cause beach fouling, undesirable odors from decomposing Cladophora, clogged industrial intakes and degraded fish habitat."
- **[✓ verified]** The plan cites at least three different population figures for Lake Erie's human footprint in different contexts: 12 million (binational drinking-water users), 'more than 10 million' (basin residents), and 'over 11 million' (population served, in a cross-Great-Lakes comparison) — figures that should not be treated as interchangeable.
  - *evidence:* This is an internal-consistency observation by the analyst: the three figures appear in three different sections with plausibly different scopes/denominators, but the document does not reconcile them, so a reader should track which figure/scope is being cited. (PURPOSE p. ii; BACKGROUND p. 1; The Need to Control Phosphorus p. 4)
- **[✓ verified]** Since the spread of invasive zebra and quagga mussels (established in Lake Erie in the 1990s), nearshore nutrient recycling has increased via the mussels' filtering/excretion, and greater water clarity has let algae grow at greater depths — factors the plan links to increased nearshore nuisance algal growth, alongside wetland/riparian loss and more intense spring storms.
  - *evidence:* Presented by the source as contributing/associated ecological factors, not as an isolated, experimentally-proven single cause of the algal resurgence; multiple co-occurring factors are named together. (BACKGROUND > The Need to Control Phosphorus, p. 4)
  - *quote:* "Invasive mussels retain and recycle nutrients in nearshore areas through their filtering and excretion activities."
- **[✓ verified]** 'Dissolved phosphorus' (also called soluble/dissolved reactive phosphorus) is more readily taken up by algae than particulate phosphorus, and its share of Lake Erie's total P load has grown since the resurgence of blooms in the late 1990s.
  - *evidence:* Definitional claim with a stated mechanism (bioavailability) for why the dissolved fraction matters more than total load alone. (BACKGROUND > The Need to Control Phosphorus, p. 4, footnote 1)
  - *quote:* "Known as “soluble reactive phosphorus” or “dissolved reactive phosphorus”. These terms tend to be used interchangeably."
- **[✓ verified]** The plan's strategy targets phosphorus alone and assumes P-focused actions will also reduce nitrogen, yet it cites Gobler et al. (2016) for the finding that phosphorus reduction without nitrogen reduction would not reduce bloom toxicity (blooms would shrink in extent but could remain toxic, possibly longer).
  - *evidence:* The plan attributes this toxicity-nitrogen link to a specific cited peer-reviewed paper (Gobler et al. 2016, Harmful Algae 54:87-97), not to its own analysis; framed as an association/mechanism from external research, and as a reason the plan's own science advisors want more nitrogen research. (BACKGROUND > The Need to Control Phosphorus, p. 4-5, footnote 2 (citing C.J. Gobler et al. 2016, Harmful Algae 54:87–97))
  - *quote:* "phosphorus reduction in the absence of nitrogen reduction would not reduce the toxicity of algal blooms"
- **[✓ verified]** Under the 2012 GLWQA, the U.S. and Canada adopted six binational 'Lake Ecosystem Objectives,' including explicitly capping cyanobacterial toxin levels judged to threaten human or ecosystem health.
  - *evidence:* These objectives are the stated policy basis from which the plan's specific numeric phosphorus targets are derived. (GLWQA Commitments, p. 4-5)
  - *quote:* "Maintain cyanobacteria at levels that do not produce concentrations of toxins that pose a threat to human or ecosystem health in the waters of the Great Lakes."
- **[✓ verified]** Central-basin hypoxia target: a 40% reduction in total phosphorus (TP) entering the western and central basins (vs. a 2008 baseline) to reach an annual central-basin load of 6,000 metric tons, split as a 3,316 metric-ton reduction from the U.S. and 212 metric tons from Canada.
  - *evidence:* Stated as a formally adopted binational numeric target following 'a robust science-based process and binational public consultation'; the excerpt does not detail the underlying model runs. (GLWQA Commitments / Phosphorus Reduction Goals, p. 5-6)
  - *quote:* "a 40 percent reduction in total phosphorus (TP) entering the western and central basins of Lake Erie—from the United States and from Canada—to achieve an annual load of 6,000 metric tons to the central basin"
- **[✓ verified]** Nearshore algal-species target: a 40% reduction in spring TP and soluble reactive phosphorus (SRP) loads from named priority watersheds (Canada: Thames River, Leamington tributaries; U.S.: Maumee, River Raisin, Portage, Toussaint Creek, Sandusky, and Huron [Ohio] rivers).
  - *evidence:* A watershed-specific (not lake-wide) target, aimed at the 'maintain healthy nearshore algal species' ecosystem objective. (GLWQA Commitments, p. 6)
  - *quote:* "a 40 percent reduction in spring TP and soluble reactive phosphorus (SRP) loads from the following watersheds where algae is a localized problem"
- **[✓ verified]** Western-basin cyanotoxin target: a 40% reduction in spring (March-July) TP/SRP loads from the Maumee River specifically, equating (vs. the 2008 baseline) to a spring load target of 860 metric tons TP and 186 metric tons SRP.
  - *evidence:* The most HAB-toxin-specific of the three targets; tied explicitly to the Maumee, the river later described as the 'best predictor' of western-basin bloom severity. (GLWQA Commitments, p. 6)
  - *quote:* "a spring (March-July) load of 860 metric tons TP and 186 metric tons SRP"
- **[✓ verified]** The plan states the science is unclear whether phosphorus-load reductions from Eastern-basin sources are warranted for Cladophora control, so it defers firm Eastern-basin targets and commits only to precautionary actions and research, with a re-evaluation planned for 2020.
  - *evidence:* An explicit, source-stated scientific-uncertainty caveat distinguishing the Eastern basin's evidentiary basis from the Western/Central basin targets. (GLWQA Commitments, p. 6)
  - *quote:* "the science remains unclear whether reductions in phosphorus loading from sources in Lake Erie's Eastern basin are warranted"
- **[✓ verified]** Most of Lake Erie's annual phosphorus load comes from a handful of tributaries (Maumee, Detroit, Sandusky in the U.S.; Thames, Grand in Ontario); nonpoint-source runoff is estimated to supply about 72% of the total annual P load lake-wide, and up to about 89% of the annual TP load in the western-basin's tributaries.
  - *evidence:* Tributary loading figures are attributed to Maccoux et al. (2016) for average annual loads 2003-2013 (figure caption); nonpoint-source percentages are given as lake-wide/basin averages, with the source itself noting these loads 'can be highly variable from year to year.' (Major Sources of Phosphorus, p. 7 (loads per Maccoux et al. 2016))
  - *quote:* "runoff from nonpoint sources are estimated to be responsible for about 72 percent of the total phosphorus load entering Lake Erie each year; in the western basin, nonpoint sources are estimated to contribute upwards of 89 percent"
- **[✓ verified]** The plan states Maumee River phosphorus loads are 'the single best predictor' of western-basin bloom severity (attributed to its high P concentration), whereas the higher-flow Detroit River (95% Lake Huron-derived flow) has a much lower TP concentration (0.014 mg/L vs. Maumee's 0.42 mg/L, 2011-2013) that the plan says is 'too low to spur an algal bloom' even though its cumulative load still contributes to downstream algal growth and hypoxia.

## Methods
This is a policy/strategy document, not a primary empirical study; it synthesizes and cites external monitoring and modeling work rather than presenting new statistical analysis. Within this excerpt, its cited evidentiary bases are: (1) binational lake-wide water-quality monitoring cruises by Environment and Climate Change Canada and U.S. EPA, used to characterize total phosphorus concentration trends in the western/central basins; (2) tributary phosphorus load estimates from Maccoux et al. (2016) for average annual TP loads by river, 2003-2013; (3) 'a robust science-based process and binational public consultation,' plus an Annex 4 Objectives and Targets Task Team report and an 'Annex 4 Multi-Modeling Report' (both referenced only by title/URL in a 'Technical resources' list, not detailed in this excerpt) used to derive the percent-reduction and metric-ton targets; (4) an external USEPA Science Advisory Board review that endorsed the phosphorus-focused strategy and recommended further nitrogen-toxin research; (5) one cited peer-reviewed study, Gobler et al. (2016, Harmful Algae 54:87-97), for the nitrogen-toxicity relationship. The document also reproduces (as a figure caption, not an analysis) a NOAA/Ohio Sea Grant satellite-derived 'Bloom Severity Index (2002-2016)' and a 2011 satellite bloom image as supporting visual evidence, without describing the underlying remote-sensing method. Where the plan characterizes its own confidence: it treats the western/central-basin hypoxia and cyanotoxin targets as adequately supported, while explicitly treating the Eastern-basin Cladophora problem as scientifically unresolved, deferring firm targets there pending a 2020 review.

## Stated limitations
The plan itself flags: (1) scientific uncertainty specific to the Eastern basin — "the science remains unclear whether reductions in phosphorus loading from sources in Lake Erie's Eastern basin are warranted" — deferring firm targets there pending a 2020 re-evaluation, and supporting only precautionary actions and targeted research in the interim; (2) that its phosphorus-only reduction strategy rests on an explicit, not-yet-validated assumption ("assumes that management actions to reduce phosphorus will also reduce nitrogen"), with a commitment to keep researching/monitoring nitrogen so "management decisions and actions can be adapted as necessary"; (3) that phosphorus concentrations and nonpoint-source loading "can be highly variable" year to year, meaning single-year loads are noisy relative to the plan's multi-year percent-reduction targets; (4) the excerpt is cut off before the stated rationale for choosing 2008 as the baseline year ("This 2008 Water Year was select[ed]..."), so that justification is not captured here (an artifact of windowed extraction, not necessarily a document omission).

## Tensions with other findings
(1) The plan's chosen management lever is phosphorus-only, and it assumes P-focused action will also lower nitrogen, yet in the same section it cites Gobler et al. (2016) for the finding that phosphorus reduction without nitrogen reduction would not reduce bloom toxicity (blooms would shrink in extent but could remain, or become, toxic longer) — a tension the plan surfaces but resolves only by deferring to future nitrogen research, not through its current targets. (2) Evidence strength is uneven across basins: the Western/Central basin hypoxia and cyanotoxin targets are presented as the output of 'a robust science-based process,' with specific tonnage targets, whereas the Eastern basin's Cladophora problem is explicitly labeled scientifically unresolved with target-setting deferred — so 'phosphorus reduction controls HABs/nuisance algae' is not uniformly evidenced across all of Lake Erie's basins or algal taxa in this source. (3) The source's own tributary data complicate a simple load-based bloom-risk model: the Detroit River carries a high absolute phosphorus load (via very high flow, since Lake Huron water is 95% of its flow) but a comparatively low, and per the plan 'too low to spur an algal bloom,' concentration (0.014 mg/L vs. Maumee's 0.42 mg/L), while the smaller-flow, higher-concentration Maumee is called 'the single best predictor' of western-basin bloom severity — suggesting concentration, not just load, governs bloom risk in this account, a nuance that complicates simpler total-load framings used elsewhere in HAB literature.

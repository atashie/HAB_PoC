# Early Seasonal Increases and Persistence in Relative Abundance of Potentially Toxic Cyanobacteria: Concerning Impacts of Extended Ice-Free Periods in Northern Temperate Lakes

> Note: provisional URL was resolved to a primary source. Original: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2022.12.20.521158.full.pdf

**What it is.** A bioRxiv preprint (posted 2022-12-21, version 1; no peer-reviewed journal version on record) by Cameron, Müller, Stone, Buttle, Leach, Webster and Emelko (University of Waterloo) reporting an 18-month (July 2018-January 2020), 10-event field survey of cyanobacterial community composition via 16S rRNA (V4 region) amplicon sequencing across three interconnected oligotrophic lakes in the Turkey Lakes Watershed, Ontario, paired with an almost-40-year ice-phenology record linking a lengthening ice-free season to earlier and more persistent presence of potentially toxic cyanobacteria.

## Data / numbers
- 3 interconnected oligotrophic lakes studied (Turkey Lakes Watershed, Ontario, Canada)
- "Almost 40 years" of ice phenology data analyzed
- 18-month field sampling period: July 2018 to January 2020
- 10 synoptic sampling events across the 18-month period
- 16S rRNA gene, V4 hypervariable region used as the amplicon-sequencing target (community characterization marker, not a magnitude)

## Methods
Observational field study, not manipulative/experimental. (1) Community-ecology component: 10 synoptic water-sampling events over an 18-month period (July 2018-January 2020) across three interconnected oligotrophic lakes in the Turkey Lakes Watershed (TLW), Ontario (Canadian Shield, sugar-maple-dominated forest catchment); cyanobacterial community composition characterized via amplicon sequencing of the V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene, which allowed detection of both photosynthetic cyanobacteria and the non-photosynthetic basal lineage Melainabacteria. (2) Long-term physical/climate component: analysis of an ice-phenology record spanning almost 40 years for TLW lakes (ice-on/ice-off dates) to quantify trends in ice-free period length and seasonal timing, with an autumn ice-on delay highlighted. (3) Interpretive component: inter-lake differences in cyanobacterial abundance related to each lake's surficial geology and morphometry as proxies for fine-sediment and nutrient supply. The abstract does not name the specific statistical tests (e.g., trend tests, ordination methods, diversity indices) used to establish the "significant" differences reported; those details, if present, are in the Methods/Results sections of the full text, which could not be retrieved for this entry (see fetch_notes).

## Tensions with other findings
The abstract itself frames a tension with the conventional cyanoHAB paradigm it states as typical -- "concern of cyanobacteria blooms (e.g., visible biomass accumulations) occurring in the summer season of eutrophic systems" -- by reporting season-spanning proliferation of potentially toxic cyanobacteria (including winter persistence of a basal lineage, Melainabacteria, and immediate post-ice-off recolonization) in oligotrophic, non-bloom-visible lakes. For any HAB early-warning approach built on visible/satellite bloom signals and/or warm-season, eutrophic-system risk windows, this is a relevant complicating case: it implies a possible nutrient-poor-lake and shoulder-season/under-ice blind spot that summer-only, eutrophic-only, visible-biomass-triggered detection could miss. The authors' overarching synthesis ("convergence of...factors...enable increases") is presented as an association across co-occurring long-term climate/ice trends and community-sequencing results in a single watershed (three lakes), not as an experimentally demonstrated causal mechanism, so it should not be read as proof that longer ice-free seasons cause higher toxic-cyanobacteria abundance in other lakes or regions.

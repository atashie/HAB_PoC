# National Lakes Assessment 2022 Key Findings

**What it is.** The National Lakes Assessment (NLA) 2022 Key Findings page is EPA's summary of "The Fourth Collaborative Survey of Lakes in the United States" — a statistically-based (probability-sample) survey of lake condition across the conterminous U.S., run by EPA with states, tribes, and other partners as part of the National Aquatic Resource Surveys (NARS) program. It reports national percentages of lakes in good/fair/poor condition for nutrients, algae/cyanotoxins, biology, physical habitat, a pesticide (atrazine), and fish-tissue contaminants, plus 2017-to-2022 trend comparisons.

## Key claims
*(each tagged with its blind-review verdict)*

- **[✓ verified]** Half of U.S. lakes are in poor condition for phosphorus and nearly half for nitrogen.
  - *evidence:* National percentage of lakes rated 'poor' condition class for each nutrient indicator, per the survey's condition classification. (Key Findings page — 'Nutrient Pollution' section)
  - *quote:* "50% of lakes were in poor condition with elevated phosphorus"
- **[✓ verified]** Nearly half of lakes have excessive (poor-rated) chlorophyll a, and 30% are hypereutrophic.
  - *evidence:* Chlorophyll a (an algal biomass proxy) rated poor in 49% of lakes; hypereutrophic conditions (excess nutrients, high algae growth, low transparency) found in 30% of lakes. (Key Findings page — 'Algae and Cyanobacteria' section)
  - *quote:* "Chlorophyll a...was in excess and rated poor in 49% of lakes"
- **[✓ verified]** Lakes with elevated phosphorus show a statistical association with degraded benthic invertebrate communities, stronger in natural lakes than reservoirs.
  - *evidence:* Survey-derived relative-likelihood figures comparing high-phosphorus lakes to others; described as an association from the probability survey, not a controlled causal experiment. (Key Findings page — 'Biological Condition' section)
  - *quote:* "1.7 times more likely to be in poor condition"
- **[✓ verified]** Roughly a quarter to a third of U.S. lakes have degraded biological communities nationally.
  - *evidence:* 29% poor / 26% fair by benthic macroinvertebrates; 25% poor / 25% fair by zooplankton, as national condition-class percentages. (Key Findings page — 'Biological Condition' section)
  - *quote:* "29% of lakes were in poor condition"
- **[✓ verified]** Lakeshore disturbance and physical habitat degradation are widespread, and are linked to worse biological condition.
  - *evidence:* Only 16% of lakes rate 'good' for lakeshore disturbance, with 84% showing moderate-to-high human activity/shoreline alteration; shallow-water habitat, riparian vegetation, and habitat complexity each rate 'good' in about half of lakes; poor biological condition is reported as twice as likely when riparian vegetation or habitat complexity is poor. (Key Findings page — 'Lakeshore Disturbance and Physical Habitat' section)
  - *quote:* "Only 16% of lakes were in good condition based on lakeshore disturbance measures"
- **[✓ verified]** The cyanotoxin microcystin is detected in half of U.S. lakes, though only a small fraction exceed the recreational health criterion.
  - *evidence:* 50% detection rate nationally; only 2% of lakes (scaled to an estimated 5,360 lakes) exceed EPA's recreational criterion for microcystins. (Key Findings page — 'Microcystins' section)
  - *quote:* "Microcystins...were detected in 50% of lakes"
- **[✓ verified]** The cyanotoxin cylindrospermopsin is less prevalent than microcystin and did not exceed its health criterion anywhere sampled in 2022.
  - *evidence:* Detected in 12% of lakes nationally; zero lakes exceeded the EPA recreational criterion for this toxin in the 2022 survey. (Key Findings page — 'Microcystins/Cylindrospermopsin' section)
  - *quote:* "did not exceed the EPA recreational criterion in any lakes during the 2022 survey"
- **[✓ verified]** Microcystin detection increased sharply since the last survey cycle even though nutrient levels changed little.
  - *evidence:* The page reports 'little change' nationally in nutrient/biological indicators from 2017 to 2022, but a roughly 30-percentage-point rise in microcystin detection over the same period (exceedance rate itself stayed near 2%, not significantly changed). (Key Findings page — 'Changes since 2017' section)
  - *quote:* "increased by almost 30 percentage points, to 50%"
- **[✓ verified]** The herbicide atrazine is widely detected at low levels, with benchmark exceedance rare but ticking up since 2017.
  - *evidence:* 41% of lakes show low-level atrazine detections (up 11 percentage points since 2017); 0.9% of lakes (an estimated 2,430 lakes) exceed EPA's aquatic-life benchmark. (Key Findings page — 'Atrazine' section)
  - *quote:* "detected at low levels in 41% of lakes"
- **[✓ verified]** Mercury and PCB contamination in lake fish tissue is essentially universal nationally.
  - *evidence:* Every tested fillet sample had detectable mercury and PCBs, scaled to an estimated 58,747 lakes nationally; mercury alone exceeded EPA's tissue-based water-quality criterion in 51% of lakes. (Key Findings page — 'Fish Tissue Contaminants' section)
  - *quote:* "Every fillet tissue sample contained detectable levels of mercury and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs)"
- **[✓ verified]** PFAS ('forever chemicals'), especially PFOS, are nearly ubiquitous in lake fish tissue nationally.
  - *evidence:* An estimated 95% of lakes had fish with detectable PFOS; seven additional PFAS compounds were found in at least 15% of lakes; 16 of 40 PFAS tested were never detected. (Key Findings page — 'Fish Tissue Contaminants / PFAS' section)
  - *quote:* "estimated 95% contained fish with detectable levels of perfluorooctane sulfonic acid (PFOS)"
- **[✓ verified]** The 2022 cycle is the fourth in EPA's repeating national lake probability-survey series, and this page's trend analysis only goes back to 2017.
  - *evidence:* The page's own title/framing identifies it as the fourth cycle, and only 2017-vs-2022 comparisons are shown; no 2007 or 2012 figures appear on this page. (Key Findings page — title/header and 'Changes since 2017' section)
  - *quote:* "The Fourth Collaborative Survey of Lakes in the United States"

## Methods
This is a summary "key findings" page, not the technical methods report. It states the survey is "statistically-based," collaborative (EPA/states/tribes/partners), and covers lakes "from small ponds and prairie potholes to large lakes and human-made reservoirs" in the conterminous U.S. in survey year 2022. It reports in-situ field/lab indicators — water chemistry (total phosphorus, total nitrogen), chlorophyll a, cyanotoxins (microcystins, cylindrospermopsin), an herbicide (atrazine), biological assemblages (benthic macroinvertebrates, zooplankton), physical/habitat metrics (lakeshore disturbance, water-level drawdown, riparian vegetation cover, habitat complexity, shallow-water habitat), and fish-tissue contaminants (mercury, PCBs, PFAS) — each classified into condition/exceedance categories and, where relevant, scaled to national lake-population estimates (e.g., "5,360 lakes," "2,430 lakes," "13,400 lakes," "58,747 lakes"). No sample-size, site-count, statistical-weighting, or indicator-threshold-derivation detail is given on this page itself; it points to a separate "NLA 2022 web report site," indicator manuals, and sampling site maps for full methodology. Only a 2017-vs-2022 trend comparison is shown on this page; no 2007 or 2012 figures are presented here.

## Stated limitations
The page states no explicit statistical/methodological caveats itself (e.g., no discussion of margin of error, non-response, or design-based confidence intervals appears on this summary page — full methodology is deferred to the separate NLA 2022 web report, indicator manuals, and site maps referenced from this page). It does include two health-protective caveats in its own text: for cyanotoxins, "people should check with state, Tribal or local governments before swimming, boating or fishing"; for fish consumption, "People who eat locally caught freshwater fish should consult state and Tribal agencies responsible for issuing fish advisories."

## Tensions with other findings
(1) Nutrient impairment (phosphorus/nitrogen) and biological indicators showed "little change" nationally between 2017 and 2022, yet microcystin detection rose sharply ("increased by almost 30 percentage points, to 50%") over the same interval while its health-threshold exceedance rate (2%) did not change significantly — the source does not explain this divergence, so it should not be read as evidence that nutrient loading alone tracks cyanotoxin prevalence; other factors (e.g., temperature, weather in the sampled year, analytical sensitivity) are plausible but unstated. (2) The reported associations between elevated phosphorus and worse benthic-macroinvertebrate condition ("1.7 times more likely," "2.2 times" in natural lakes) are survey-based statistical associations from a single-cycle probability sample, not a controlled or longitudinal causal test — correlation, not proof of causation, per this source. (3) NLA is purely in-situ/ground-survey data on a multi-year cadence (2007/2012/2017/2022, per the page's "Fourth Collaborative Survey" framing), which is a much coarser temporal resolution than satellite products like EPA CyAN; useful as periodic ground-truth/context but not as a substitute for near-real-time bloom detection.

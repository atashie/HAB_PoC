# Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) Forecasting

**What it is.** A NOAA National Centers for Coastal Ocean Science (NCCOS) project page (ongoing since January 1999) describing NOAA's harmful algal bloom forecasting systems, which fuse satellite ocean-color imagery, NOAA meteorological station data, and state/university field-monitoring data into predictive models that notify coastal managers of bloom location, size, and trajectory before blooms reach shore. It documents one long-standing operational forecast (Florida/Texas Gulf coast Karenia brevis "red tide," delivered as a bi-weekly/weekly emailed HAB Bulletin), a Lake Erie cyanobacteria forecast that transitioned from an "Experimental" to an operational HAB-OFS product, and developmental projects for the remaining regions, out of seven regions NOAA designates nationally as critical HAB problem areas.

## Methods
Operational HAB forecasting is produced by fusing (1) satellite ocean-color imagery, (2) NOAA meteorological station data, and (3) field/in-situ monitoring data from state and university programs into models that predict bloom trajectory over "the next few days." The satellite ocean-color processing step follows a documented methodology (NOAA Technical Memorandum NOS NCCOS 252, 2018, "Ocean Color Satellite Imagery Processing Guidelines"). Forecasts are packaged as an emailed "HAB Bulletin" (bi-weekly active-bloom / weekly non-bloom cadence) plus a public-facing "Operational Conditions Report." The approach is described as working (i.e., mature enough for routine operational/public use) for the Florida/Texas Gulf coast (Karenia brevis) and, per a documented product transition, for Lake Erie cyanobacteria (moving from an "Experimental" 2008–2016 product to an operational HAB-OFS product from 2017 onward). Five further designated regions (western Gulf, Gulf of Maine, Washington Coast, California Coast, Chesapeake Bay) are described only as "developmental forecast projects," i.e., not yet operational per this page. No quantitative skill/accuracy statistics (hit rate, lead-time verification, error metrics) for any region's forecast are reported on this page.

## Stated limitations
The source states two explicit limitations in its own text: (1) a resource/funding constraint — "there are not sufficient funds to sample every event" — meaning ground validation cannot cover every bloom; and (2) an explicit admission of incomplete national coverage — "we still have a long way to go to reach a National Forecast capacity for HABs" — even though "several regions have been or are anticipated to be operational over the next decade." Beyond these two self-stated points, the page provides no quantitative accuracy, skill-score, or forecast-verification statistics for any region's product, and gives no numeric spatial resolution or forecast lead time (only the qualitative "next few days"), which a reviewer should treat as an absence rather than an implied null result.

## Tensions with other findings
As a program-overview webpage rather than a peer-reviewed validation study, this source cannot itself support a specific forecast-skill number (e.g., % correct classification, verified lead-time accuracy) — it documents scope, inputs, and delivery cadence only, so it should be paired with underlying peer-reviewed CyAN/HAB-OFS validation literature before any accuracy claim is used in a product/tool. It also shows that "Lake Erie HAB forecasting" is not one static capability: the page's own linked dataset metadata records a shift from an NCCOS-labeled "Experimental" bulletin (2008-09-04 to 2016-11-01) to a CO-OPS-labeled operational HAB-OFS product (2017-06-25 to 2020-10-20), so other literature citing "the Lake Erie forecast" should specify which system vintage is meant. Separately, current page text uses "Gulf of America (formerly Gulf of Mexico)" terminology in places while the cited historical dataset titles (2017/2018/2021 records) retain "Gulf of Mexico," reflecting a 2025-era U.S. federal renaming applied inconsistently across older vs. newer page content — worth flagging when cross-referencing older HAB-OFS Gulf of Mexico sources under either name. Finally, this NOAA marine/Great-Lakes forecasting program is a close structural analog to EPA CyAN (also filed under remote-sensing in this dossier's source list): both pair a satellite detection signal with in-situ/field data into a forecast product, so the two sources' operational-maturity and limitation statements are worth comparing directly rather than treated as independent data points.

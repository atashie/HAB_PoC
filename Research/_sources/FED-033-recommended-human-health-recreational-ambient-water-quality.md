# Recommended Human Health Recreational Ambient Water Quality Criteria or Swimming Advisories for Microcystins and Cylindrospermopsin

**What it is.** A May 2019 U.S. EPA Office of Water document (EPA 822-R-19-001) that recommends non-binding human-health recreational ambient water quality criteria / swimming-advisory trigger values for two cyanotoxins, microcystins and cylindrospermopsin, for states to use under Clean Water Act Section 304(a) (optionally adoptable as enforceable water quality standards under CWA 303(c)). It derives these values by combining EPA's established criteria-derivation methodology and Exposure Factors Handbook exposure parameters with hazard/dose-response conclusions already published in EPA's 2015 Health Effects Support Documents and Drinking Water Health Advisories for the same two toxins.

  - *evidence:* Stated directly in the document's Foreword, which frames the values as a non-regulatory scientific assessment under 304(a) that becomes an applicable CWA water quality standard only if a state adopts it and EPA approves under 303(c). (Foreword, p. 3)

## Methods
EPA's standard human-health ambient-water-quality-criteria derivation methodology (per U.S. EPA 2000) applied to recreational (swimming) exposure, reusing hazard identification and dose-response conclusions already established in EPA's 2015 Health Effects Support Documents and Drinking Water Health Advisories for microcystins and cylindrospermopsin (U.S. EPA 2015a-d) rather than generating new toxicology data. Exposure assessment draws on recreation-specific parameters (incidental ingestion rate, exposure duration, body weight) from the EPA Exposure Factors Handbook (U.S. EPA 2011), combined through a "combined distribution analysis" (Table 4-1) that, per the appendix/acronym structure, appears to draw on incidental-ingestion datasets (Appendix E) and duration/ingestion data attributed to "DFB" (DeFlorio-Barker et al. 2017), disaggregated by age group (Figures 4-2, 4-3). The document runs comparative screening-level calculations of inhalation dose (Tables 7-7 to 7-9) and dermal-absorbed dose (Tables 7-10 to 7-13) against the incidental-ingestion dose to weigh the relative contribution of non-ingestion routes, and separately compares calculated protective values across lifestages (children under 6, children 6-10, adults; Tables 7-4 to 7-6, Figure 7-4). Cyanobacterial whole-cell/biomass exposure is evaluated as a distinct, apparently less-certain hazard pathway (Section 7.5) alongside dissolved-toxin exposure. Where it "works or fails": the retrieved excerpt does not contain the narrative conclusions of the inhalation-vs-ingestion or dermal-vs-ingestion screening comparisons, nor the final numeric criteria values in Table 6-1 (Recreational Criteria or Swimming Advisory Recommendations for Microcystins and Cylindrospermopsin, p. 76) — those specific outcomes cannot be confirmed from the text captured in this windowed excerpt.

## Stated limitations
The document repeatedly frames itself as advisory rather than binding: "This document provides recommendations only. It does not establish or affect legal rights or obligations. It does not establish a binding norm and cannot be finally determinative of the issues addressed" (Foreword, p. 3-4); states remain free to adopt, modify, or ignore the values. It also allocates a dedicated section to "Enhanced Susceptibility" (7.1), separate from its core lifestage analysis, implying the general-population-oriented derivation may not equally protect all subgroups. Most explicitly, Section 7.5.1 is titled "Health Effects Associated with Cyanobacterial Cells and Uncertainties" and is paired with a dedicated Appendix D, "Review of the State of the Science on Cyanobacterial Cells Health Effects" — signaling that EPA itself regards the evidence linking whole-cell/biomass exposure to health effects as less settled than the purified-toxin dose-response data underlying the core criteria. Note: the retrieved (windowed) excerpt contains these section/appendix titles and the Foreword disclaimer verbatim, but not the full body text of Sections 7.1 or 7.5.1, so the specific substance of these caveats beyond what is quoted/titled here is not confirmed from this excerpt.

## Tensions with other findings
This is a recreational-exposure (swimming) criteria document, explicitly distinct from EPA's parallel 2015 Drinking Water Health Advisories for the same two toxins — different exposure routes/durations are assessed, so any downstream use that treats "the EPA microcystin number" as one universal threshold risks conflating recreational and drinking-water contexts that this source itself keeps separate. Second, the document's own tables (2-1 to 2-3; Appendices A-B) show WHO and many individual U.S. states already had — and, because adoption of this EPA recommendation is voluntary, may continue to have — different numeric action levels than whatever value this document ultimately recommends in Table 6-1 (not captured in the retrieved excerpt), so "the EPA threshold" is not synonymous with "the applicable threshold" in any given state. Third, and most relevant to remote-sensing-based HAB tools: Section 7.5's own title pairing ("Health Effects Associated with Cyanobacterial Cells and Uncertainties") and its dedicated state-of-the-science appendix indicate that the health-effects basis for using cyanobacterial cell density/biomass — the kind of signal satellite products such as EPA CyAN detect — as a proxy for toxin exposure is treated by EPA itself as less certain than toxin-concentration-based criteria. This is a caveat directly relevant to any tool (including this project's) that infers toxin risk from a remote-sensing biomass/pigment signal rather than a measured microcystin/cylindrospermopsin concentration; it does not by itself establish a causal link between cell density and toxin risk, only that EPA flags the relationship as an area of active uncertainty.

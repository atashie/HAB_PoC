# remote-sensing

This category covers optical satellite detection of freshwater cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (cyanoHABs): the spectral indices and algorithms used (Cyanobacteria Index/CI_cyano, phycocyanin retrieval, chlorophyll-a band-ratio and shape indices), the sensors and missions that supply the imagery (MERIS, Sentinel-3 OLCI, Sentinel-2 MSI, Landsat, MODIS, and emerging hyperspectral PACE), the atmospheric-correction step needed to turn top-of-atmosphere radiance into a usable water-leaving signal, and the accuracy/validation literature that tests these products against in-situ ground truth. It also covers the operational federal programs (EPA/NOAA/NASA/USGS CyAN and NOAA's HAB forecasting systems) that package these algorithms into public products, and the resolution/revisit/detection-limit constraints that bound where satellite-only monitoring is and is not viable.

## What's well-established

### The Cyanobacteria Index (CI_cyano) family is the operational backbone, and it works reasonably well as a presence/absence screen for large lakes
EPA/NASA/NOAA/USGS's CyAN program computes a Cyanobacteria Index from MERIS (2002–2012) and, since 2016, Sentinel-3 OLCI imagery at 300 m resolution, distributed as daily GeoTIFFs and 7-day maximum-value composites, currently at processing "Version 6" [FED-008; FED-009; FED-048]. Two independent field-validation studies found the underlying CIcyano algorithm classifies bloom presence/absence with 84% overall accuracy (95% CI 77–87%) against combined microcystin-plus-cell-density ground truth, with 87% precision and 90% recall on same-day matchups [ACAD-045], and separately found CIcyano agreed with state-reported cyanoHAB events at a 60% true positive rate and with state recreation advisories at 69%, with a 76% true-negative rate after advisories were lifted [ACAD-006]. Both studies show these headline numbers are threshold-sensitive: loosening the CIcyano detection threshold changes agreement substantially, and restricting the toxin reference to microcystin-only (rather than microcystin-plus-cell-density) drops accuracy to 72% [ACAD-006; ACAD-045]. EPA's own operational weekly forecasting layer built on CyAN reports a 90% "overall prediction accuracy" for a 7-day cyanoHAB probability forecast across 2,000+ lakes, though the source page itself gives no validation dataset, sample size, or confidence interval — that number traces only to underlying papers (Schaeffer et al. 2024; Myer et al. 2020) not in this pack [FED-060].

### Spectral-shape and peak-height indices exploit specific reflectance features to separate cyanobacteria from other phytoplankton
A family of MERIS/OLCI-era algorithms detects cyanobacteria via distinctive reflectance shape near the 681 nm chlorophyll fluorescence band and the 620 nm phycocyanin absorption band, rather than chlorophyll-a alone. The original spectral-shape metric (S2d/SS at 681 nm) flips from positive to negative during large cyanobacterial blooms because cyanobacterial light scattering overwhelms the fluorescence signal, demonstrated extensively in Lake Erie [ACAD-083; ACAD-023]. After MERIS ended (8 April 2012), a MODIS analogue (S2d(678)) was developed and cross-validated against MERIS over 2008–2011 Lake Erie imagery, agreeing via a linear transform when MODIS bands are not saturated — agreement breaks down under high-biomass surface scums, sun glint, or dense aerosols [ACAD-023]. The Maximum Peak Height (MPH) algorithm, matched against several thousand in-situ chl-a measurements from 40+ lakes worldwide, was found more robust than several competing indices in eutrophic-to-hypertrophic waters (best in 4 of 9 lakes in a head-to-head comparison, R > 0.6), though it systematically overestimates chlorophyll-a and the authors called for global re-calibration [ACAD-062]. A hard physical floor applies to all of these: cyanobacteria-specific (phycocyanin) spectral features only become clearly resolvable above roughly chl-a > 20 mg/m³, so low-to-medium-biomass cyanobacteria detection is described as infeasible for MERIS "or any other currently available satellite imagery" [ACAD-062].

### Phycocyanin is the more specific pigment for cyanobacteria, but is harder to retrieve at scale than chlorophyll-a
Because phycocyanin (absorption peak ~620 nm, target retrieval band 615–630 nm) is a pigment essentially unique to cyanobacteria, it is framed as more diagnostically suitable than chlorophyll-a for cyanoHAB-specific monitoring [ACAD-077]. However, large-scale phycocyanin retrieval remains constrained by a scarcity of imagery with sufficient spatial and spectral resolution, unlike chlorophyll-a [ACAD-077]. Semi-analytic phycocyanin algorithms (the Simis et al. approach) require empirical correction coefficients that are site-dependent rather than universal: a Taihu Lake recalibration (replacing a constant specific-absorption coefficient with a concentration-dependent power function) reached R²=0.55, RMSE=58.89 µg/L, and needed independent validation against a second lake (Dianchi) to demonstrate any generalizability, with retrieval error tied to the ratio of suspended solids to phycocyanin [ACAD-140]. Consistent with this, a case study of Sentinel-3 OLCI on Lake Erie found that none of the traditional retrieval algorithms tested could accurately estimate both phycocyanin and chlorophyll-a jointly, and that simpler empirical band/band-ratio approaches outperformed the traditional algorithms — leading the author to call for OLCI-specific algorithm redevelopment rather than treating "Sentinel-3 can monitor cyanoHABs" as a solved, algorithm-agnostic capability [ACAD-145].

### Sensors and missions form a historical-to-emerging succession, each with a different resolution/coverage trade-off
The satellite lineage runs from Envisat MERIS (2002–2012, 300 m, later reprocessed by CyAN with a MODIS-Terra deep-learning bridge called CyanNet to fill the 2012–2016 gap) to Sentinel-3A/B OLCI (2016–present / 2018–present, 300 m, "best coverage" only once both satellites were flying) [FED-009; FED-048; ACAD-098; FED-011]. Finer-resolution multispectral sensors — Sentinel-2 MSI (10 m, ~5-day revisit) and Landsat-8/9 OLI (30 m, 16-day repeat) — extend coverage to smaller waterbodies that 300 m ocean-color pixels cannot resolve, and a two-sensor "virtual constellation" of Sentinel-2A/2B + Landsat-8 applied to the Floating Algae Index in a small Chinese plateau lake showed the two platforms agree reasonably well with each other (MAPE 19.98%, unbiased percentage difference 17.05%) [ACAD-129; ACAD-091]. A large 103-lake, 28-state U.S. calibration study found Sentinel-2's Maximum Chlorophyll Index (MCI) outperformed the Normalized Difference Chlorophyll Index (NDCI) across all reflectance-processing levels, reached 82% binary trophic-state agreement with in-situ data, and the authors argue S2's finer resolution could in principle extend an operational CyAN-like product's coverage to 98.8% of qualifying U.S. waterbodies — but S2 lacks the ~685 nm band and thermal bands needed to harmonize with MERIS/OLCI-based cyanobacteria-specific indices, and cannot detect phycocyanin at all [ACAD-046; ACAD-088]. The newest platform, NASA's hyperspectral PACE Ocean Color Instrument, was benchmarked in 2026 against the operational CyAN/Sentinel-3-OLCI product across three U.S. lakes and showed strong agreement (R²=0.84 for cyanobacterial cell density vs. the CyAN benchmark) while roughly halving chlorophyll-a retrieval error against in-situ measurements (NRMSE ~21.6% vs. ~38.7% for the operational product) — the first such PACE cyanoHAB validation, and evidence of headroom above the current operational baseline rather than evidence the baseline is unreliable [ACAD-106]. A separate, purely open-ocean/marine methods paper developed 65 empirical "maximum band ratio" chlorophyll-a algorithms (the OC2–OC6 family) across 25 satellite instruments including OLCI and the upcoming PACE, intended to merge and extend the global open-ocean chlorophyll climate record; it does not address cyanobacteria, inland lakes, or the CyAN product at all, but its underlying band-ratio logic and its explicit caveat that the approach assumes non-algal particles and CDOM co-vary with chlorophyll-a is the same class of assumption that inland cyanoHAB chlorophyll algorithms must also confront [FED-046].

### Atmospheric correction is a necessary, imperfect, and non-uniform preprocessing step
Because inland/turbid waters are optically complex, a large multi-processor intercomparison (ACIX-Aqua, a joint NASA-ESA exercise pooling 1,000+ in-situ radiometric matchups across 8 processors and 7 Optical Water Types) found even the best-performing atmospheric correction (AC) processors carry 20–30% median reflectance error at 560/664 nm in freshwater matchups, rising to 25–60% in the blue band, which propagates to 25–70% uncertainty in downstream chlorophyll-a and total suspended solids products [ACAD-007]. No single AC algorithm wins everywhere: iCOR and ACOLITE performed best for diverse/turbid freshwater conditions while OC-SMART and SeaDAS performed best in clearer waters, in that same intercomparison [ACAD-007], and a separate six-algorithm benchmark for Landsat-8/9 (440 matchups) similarly found OC-SMART best inland overall, L2GEN best in coastal waters, and ACOLITE/iCOR best specifically in highly turbid Optical Water Types — with performance always turbidity-class-dependent, not universal [ACAD-109]. A cheaper alternative is available for time-series work: a 53-image Karla Reservoir (Greece) study found a fast, partial Rayleigh-only correction correlates extremely highly with full iCOR correction for derived chlorophyll/cyanobacteria indices (r>0.95) while running ~25× faster (5 minutes vs. ~2 hours per image), leading the authors to propose it as adequate for long seasonal time-series monitoring specifically — while explicitly not endorsing it as a general AC replacement, and noting they performed no in-situ validation of the resulting pigment values [ACAD-025]. This aligns with why CyAN and the 25-year Great Lakes multi-mission fusion both build on Rayleigh-corrected (not fully atmospherically corrected) reflectance as a pragmatic workaround, since standard AC routinely fails over turbid, productive inland waters [ACAD-098; ACAD-062].

### The field organizes prediction approaches into three families, layered on top of the remote-sensing signal
Two independent 2025 review papers converge on the same three-way taxonomy for how remote-sensing-derived indicators (chlorophyll-a concentration, surface bloom area, phycocyanin) feed into HAB prediction: data-driven/machine-learning models (ANN, random forest, LSTM) that learn statistical relationships between environmental variables and bloom events from historical data; process-based models that mechanistically simulate photosynthesis, nutrient uptake, and cell division; and hybrid models combining both, which the reviews state further improves prediction of bloom timing and magnitude [ACAD-084]. One of these reviews [ACAD-084] identifies three core unresolved challenges facing this modeling layer — selecting optimal input variables, model transferability across diverse water bodies, and interpretability of complex machine-learning models — and recommends explainable/interpretable AI (XAI) techniques and adaptive hybrid structures as the way forward, rather than treating any single family as solved. (Per the blind synthesis review, this specific XAI-and-transferability framing is supported by [ACAD-084] only; the companion review's accessible text emphasizes machine-learning performance and applications and does not, in what could be accessed, address these interpretability/transferability challenges — so the claim is attributed to the single supporting source rather than presented as a cross-review consensus.) Neither review states a comparative verdict on remote-sensing-only versus remote-sensing-plus-in-situ/weather fusion approaches specifically, leaving that question open for downstream work [ACAD-084].

### Small-waterbody detection is the field's most persistent, best-documented limitation
Multiple independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion: coarse ocean-color sensors (MERIS/OLCI, 300 m) cannot resolve small lakes, and applying their signal to smaller waterbodies degrades accuracy in ways large-lake validation studies don't capture. A national-scale validation study found roughly 80% of state-reported cyanoHAB records had to be discarded because they occurred in waterbodies too small to contain three 300-m satellite pixels [ACAD-006]. An Oklahoma study directly comparing ground-based hyperspectral, Landsat-based, and the standard satellite Cyanobacteria Index found ground-based sensing "far superior" to both satellite approaches in small-to-medium, highly productive reservoirs — though this qualitative comparison reports no R², RMSE, or sample-size statistics [ACAD-055]. A three-satellite (PlanetScope, Sentinel-2, Landsat-8), 20-algorithm comparison study concluded that reliable satellite HAB detection in small-to-medium waterbodies remains an unsolved problem, that available spectral bands (not spatial resolution per se) is the single most important driver of detection skill, and recommended combining regular field sampling with satellite data rather than relying on satellite data alone for these smaller systems [ACAD-135]. A 2019/2020 South African case study using four small inland reservoirs found even the best OLCI chlorophyll-a algorithm (MPH on Bottom-of-Rayleigh Reflectance) reached only R²=0.55 with RMSE(%)=99, and a standard adjacency correction (SIMEC) failed to improve results at all [ACAD-014]. Adjacency effects — stray light scattered from bright adjacent land into the sensor's field of view — can contribute more than 50% of at-sensor near-infrared radiance in spatially constrained waters and can be observed many kilometers offshore [ACAD-062].

## Contested / uncertain / poorly-established

**Does finer spatial resolution reliably translate into better retrieval accuracy, or does band availability matter more?** A Baltic-lakes validation study found Sentinel-2 MSI (10–20 m) measurably and substantially outperformed Sentinel-3 OLCI (300 m) across every tested water-quality product — chlorophyll-a (MSI R²=0.84 vs. OLCI R²=0.83, but OLCI worst specifically in Clear/oligotrophic water at R²=0.34), total suspended matter (MSI R²=0.89 vs. OLCI R²=0.81), CDOM (MSI R²=0.91 vs. OLCI R²=0.76), and especially Secchi depth (MSI R²=0.97 vs. OLCI R²=0.69) — and the authors recommend MSI for national operational monitoring [ACAD-099]. Yet a large multi-satellite HAB-detection comparison (PlanetScope/Sentinel-2/Landsat-8) found Landsat-8 and Sentinel-2 actually outperformed the higher-native-resolution PlanetScope, and concluded spectral band availability — not spatial resolution — was the single most important driver of detection skill, with detection performance showing little relationship to waterbody size across the satellites/algorithms tested [ACAD-135]. These are not strictly contradictory (different sensors, products, and lake sets), but they pull in different directions on how much weight "go finer-resolution" deserves as a fix, and neither source in the pack reconciles them directly.

**Is satellite remote sensing a genuine substitute for in-situ monitoring, or a structurally different, complementary signal that will never fully agree with it?** A Lake Okeechobee/St. Lucie Estuary case study found in-situ and satellite bloom-frequency rankings agree strongly and significantly at monthly resolution (Kendall's tau=0.85, p=0.0002) but are statistically indistinguishable from no agreement at annual resolution (tau=0, p=1), and found point-based in-situ sampling recorded zero blooms at quadrant scale in periods where satellite imagery detected blooms in over 10% of observations — the authors frame this as a structural point-vs-areal mismatch rather than an error in either stream, and explicitly caution the two "should not be expected" to match [ACAD-088]. EPA's own national validation study similarly found only 60% agreement with state-reported events and flagged a diel-timing mismatch (cyanobacteria migrate vertically on a daily cycle; satellite overpasses near solar noon may not align with morning field sampling) plus a shoreline-resolution mismatch as root causes, not simply satellite error [ACAD-006]. Against this, EPA's operational forecast page asserts 90% prediction accuracy with no accompanying validation detail on this page, an assertion in tension with the more granular validation literature's lower, more caveated agreement figures [FED-060]. A commercial vendor (CyanoLakes) separately claims up to three weeks of advance warning versus routine in-situ monitoring and 80%/70% forecast accuracy at 1-/2-week lead times, but discloses no methodology, validation sample, or comparison baseline for these figures — they should be read as marketing claims, not peer-reviewed validation [PVT-003].

**Does satellite biomass/pigment detection meaningfully track toxin risk, or are the two decoupled?** A field-validation study explicitly states there is no established quantitative relationship between cyanobacterial biomass and cyanotoxin concentration, that satellite sensors cannot detect cyanotoxins directly, and that dissolved microcystin can persist after a bloom has optically dissipated (a source of apparent false negatives) — restricting the toxin reference to microcystin-only measurably lowers apparent satellite accuracy (72% vs. 84%) because the satellite also detects genera producing non-microcystin toxins or no toxin at all [ACAD-045]. This is a meaningful bound on any product claim that treats "satellite detects a bloom" as equivalent to "satellite detects toxin risk," and it is not contradicted elsewhere in the pack — but it also means the field lacks a demonstrated way to close this gap with current sensors.

**Does machine learning consistently outperform traditional retrieval algorithms, or is the evidence weaker than review-level language suggests?** One 2025 review states machine-learning methods "consistently outperformed" traditional algorithms for phycocyanin estimation, but this is characterized in the pack itself as a strong review-level generalization from a synthesis paper, not a new head-to-head experiment, with a caution that primary studies elsewhere often report more mixed results [ACAD-085]. A separate, independent 2025 review flags that ML-based bio-optical retrieval models for inland water quality carry four specific, named validation risks — low generalizability, low dimensionality, spatial/temporal autocorrelation, and information leakage — that can inflate apparent skill unless specifically guarded against [ACAD-141]. Because none of the individual ML retrieval studies in this pack report the spatial/temporal cross-validation checks ACAD-141 calls for, ML superiority claims for pigment retrieval should be treated as plausible but not yet rigorously demonstrated across the literature as a whole.

**Do current retrieval algorithms generalize across lake types, or is local recalibration typically required?** A 2025 review (abstract-only access) explicitly names "poor generalizability of retrieval models across different lake types" as one of three persistent, unresolved technical limitations of the field [ACAD-013]. This is corroborated by primary-study evidence: phycocyanin correction coefficients were found to be lake-dependent rather than transferable in the Taihu/Dianchi comparison [ACAD-140], and a 103-lake Sentinel-2 chlorophyll study found its own fitted algorithm was validated on only 3 samples from a single western U.S. lake, explicitly limiting confidence the algorithm generalizes outside the Midwest/Eastern U.S. lakes that dominated its training data [ACAD-046]. No source in this pack demonstrates a retrieval algorithm validated as portable across a broad, representative range of lake optical types without local recalibration.

**How much does a reprocessing-version change vs. a real ecological change explain any observed trend in CI_cyano values?** NASA's own documentation states CyAN's "Version 5" reprocessing (May 2023) produced CI_cyano values averaging 15–20% higher than Version 4 for the same historical imagery purely from recalibration, and that NASA explicitly prohibits comparing or trending CI values across different processing versions [FED-066]. This is a single-source (FED-066) but authoritative (NASA's own release notes) caution that is highly consequential for any long time-series trend claim built on CyAN data spanning a version boundary; it is not itself contradicted elsewhere, but no other source in the pack independently re-derives or cross-checks the 15–20% figure.

**Is Great Lakes cyanoHAB severity uniformly trending in one direction?** A 25-year multi-mission fusion study found Western Lake Erie is the only one of six studied Great Lakes basins showing an overall increasing trend in bloom intensity and extent since 2000 (Sen's slope 0.098 CI/yr, Kendall's tau=0.33), while Sandusky Bay and Saginaw Bay show slight decreasing trends and Green Bay/Lake Winnebago show negligible change — directly complicating any narrative that treats Great Lakes (or freshwater) cyanoHABs as uniformly worsening [ACAD-098]. This single study also reports a striking correlational finding — Sandusky Bay bloom extent fell ~29% and intensity ~62% in the 3 years after a dam removal (Ballville Dam, Oct. 2018), alongside a shift in dominant cyanobacterial taxa — that is plausible but rests on one study's before/after comparison without a stated counterfactual or control basin, so it should be read as an association, not a demonstrated causal effect of the dam removal alone [ACAD-098].

**How large is the true socioeconomic value of satellite early detection, and does it generalize?** The one study in this pack that monetizes satellite-detection value (Utah Lake 2017, ~$370,000 central estimate, sensitivity range $55,000–$1,057,000) explicitly states its own estimate is not generalizable to a "typical" U.S. lake because Utah Lake's unusually high visitor counts likely inflate the benefit relative to other lakes, and that Utah's 2019 shift to permanent year-round advisories at that lake reduces the future decision-relevant value of early detection there specifically [ACAD-133]. This is the only economic-impact estimate in the pack; it should not be extrapolated to other lakes or events without qualification.

**Access-level caveat:** Several sources most relevant to algorithm mechanics and reviews (ACAD-007, ACAD-013, ACAD-023, ACAD-055, ACAD-077, ACAD-083, ACAD-084, ACAD-085, ACAD-091 partially, ACAD-109, ACAD-129, ACAD-135, ACAD-140, ACAD-141, ACAD-145, ACAD-106) were accessed at abstract level only (or, per pack metadata, in one case blocked entirely — PVT-008). Their claims are reported as stated in the abstract/summary but could not be cross-checked against full methods/results tables in this pack, so quantitative specifics from these sources should be treated as provisional pending full-text confirmation.

## Data-feasibility & coverage

**EPA/NASA/NOAA/USGS CyAN (Cyanobacteria Index product).** Spatial resolution: 300 m sensor pixels (MERIS/OLCI native), with a 50 m CONUS land mask vs. a coarser 500 m Alaska land mask [FED-009; FED-048]. Temporal coverage: MERIS 2002–2012 (~10-year archive); Sentinel-3A OLCI 2016–present; Sentinel-3B OLCI 2018–present, with "best coverage" only from 2018 once both Sentinel-3 satellites were operating [FED-009; FED-048]. Revisit: daily imagery plus 7-day maximum-value composites [FED-009]. Detection limits: valid CI_cyano output spans ~10,000 to 7,000,000 cells/mL; DN=0 means below detection, DN=254 is land, DN=255 is no-data (cloud) — meaning the product cannot quantify anything under cloud cover, over land, or below its detection floor for a given pixel/day [FED-048; FED-009]. Coverage scope: 2,000+ of the largest U.S. lakes and reservoirs currently, with a stated aspiration (not yet achieved) to expand to over 270,000 lakes and add direct chlorophyll-a detection [FED-069; FED-010]. Practical minimum waterbody size is roughly 1 km² [FED-010]. Access: CyAN File Search API (region/date/cadence query, GeoTIFF output), SeaDAS, RS_Toolbox for ArcGIS, EPA's CyANWeb browser tool (successor to the discontinued CyAN Android app, support ended Dec 31 2024), and a mobile app [FED-047; FED-010; FED-048]. Versioning caveat: currently "Version 6" (Aug 2025 release notes); NASA explicitly prohibits trend comparisons across processing versions because recalibration alone shifted values 15–20% at the V4→V5 transition (May 2023) [FED-009; FED-066]. **Remote-sensing/in-situ overlap:** this is the crux data-fusion issue for the category. Validation work shows in-situ and satellite bloom-frequency rankings agree well at monthly aggregation (Kendall's tau=0.85) but not at all at annual aggregation (tau=0) in a detailed case study, and point-based state monitoring is structurally biased toward bloom-presence periods (opportunistic, event-triggered sampling) while satellite observation is systematic but time-of-day-fixed (near solar noon, which may miss diel vertical migration patterns) [ACAD-088; ACAD-006]. Roughly 80% of state-reported cyanoHAB records nationally fall in waterbodies too small for the satellite product to resolve at all, meaning the two modalities' *feasible* spatial overlap is much smaller than either dataset's raw record count suggests [ACAD-006].

**NOAA NCEI Great Lakes 25-year archive (Accession 0312614).** Coverage: Lake Erie, Sandusky Bay, Saginaw Bay, Green Bay, Lake Winnebago, 2000-06-01 to 2024-11-15 [FED-011]. Built by fusing MERIS, Sentinel-3 OLCI, and a CyanNet-derived MODIS-Terra bridge into one harmonized time series [FED-011; ACAD-098]. Distribution: CSV and GeoTIFF via HTTPS/FTP, CC0 public domain [FED-011]. No resolution, revisit frequency, or accuracy statistics are stated in the archive's own metadata page; the fuller validation detail (CyanNet MedAD figures, trend statistics) lives in the companion peer-reviewed paper [ACAD-098], not the archive page itself [FED-011].

**Sentinel-2 MSI.** Resolution: 10–20 m depending on band (60 m for some bands) [ACAD-129]. Revisit: ~5 days at the equator with the twin S2A+S2B constellation, better at higher latitudes [ACAD-129]. Radiometric resolution: 12-bit [ACAD-129]. Coverage limitation: lacks thermal bands (limits cloud masking) and lacks a ~685 nm band needed to harmonize with MERIS/OLCI cyanobacteria-specific indices; cannot detect phycocyanin [ACAD-046; ACAD-088]. Detection floor: ~10 µg/L chl-a in a 103-lake U.S. calibration, below which MCI/NDCI retrievals frequently go negative [ACAD-046].

**Sentinel-3 OLCI.** Resolution: 300 m [multiple, e.g. FED-048]. Revisit: 1–2 days [ACAD-129]. Radiometric resolution: 14-bit, 21 bands 400–1020 nm, ~1270 km swath [ACAD-129]. Practical lake-size floor: 300 m resolution is stated to restrict practical water-quality monitoring to roughly the 1,000 largest lakes on Earth [ACAD-099].

**Landsat-8/9 OLI.** Resolution: 30 m [ACAD-129]. Revisit: 16-day repeat cycle [ACAD-129]. Cannot compute NDCI at all (lacks red-edge 700–720 nm bands); fallback OC3 chlorophyll algorithm loses accuracy in optically complex coastal/inland waters with non-covarying CDOM and suspended matter [ACAD-129].

**MODIS-Terra.** Resolution: ~1.1 km nominal [ACAD-098]. Continuous coverage from ~2000, used as a temporal bridge (via CyanNet) between the MERIS and OLCI eras rather than as a primary standalone HAB product in this pack [ACAD-098; FED-011].

**NASA PACE OCI (hyperspectral).** First validated for cyanoHAB monitoring in 2026 against three lakes (Lake Erie, Green Bay, Clear Lake), summer 2024 imagery only; no long-term archive or resolution/revisit figures are given in the pack for this newly launched sensor beyond the comparative accuracy statistics above [ACAD-106].

**NOAA HAB-OFS / seasonal forecast products (Lake Erie and others).** Lake Erie: seasonal (July–October) severity outlook normalized to 2005 (floor) and 2011 (ceiling, "to date") reference years, ≥96-hour bloom position/extent and vertical-mixing forecasts fusing OLCI/true-color imagery with hydrodynamic models, Maumee River discharge, and modeled phosphorus loads [FED-024]. A "Demonstration" 20 m-resolution weekly imagery product exists for Maumee Bay but is explicitly labeled demonstration-only and not corroborated by more than one source pass in the pack [FED-024; FED-061]. Broader NOAA HAB forecasting (since Jan 1999) spans 7 nationally designated problem regions, but only the Florida/Texas Gulf coast has a mature operational forecast; Lake Erie has transitioned from an "Experimental" to an operational HAB-OFS product; the remaining regions remain developmental [FED-020]. No source in the pack gives a numeric lead-time accuracy or skill score for these forecast products beyond the EPA's uncorroborated 90% figure [FED-020; FED-060].

**Commercial (CyanoLakes).** Premium tier 10 m resolution (EU Copernicus/NASA imagery, sensor unspecified), medium tier 300 m; update cadence 5–6 days/week generally, 2–3×/week for high-definition product; measurement range 1–1000 µg/L chl-a; 6 months of included historical data, up to 6 years purchasable [PVT-003]. No independent (non-vendor) validation of its accuracy claims appears in this pack [PVT-003].

**Commercial (EOMAP).** A separate commercial provider states its satellite water-quality products (chlorophyll, turbidity, Secchi depth, CDOM, and a qualitative HAB/cyanobacteria proxy indicator built from the 550 nm vs. 650 nm reflectance/absorption discrepancy) can be delivered at 1–8 day revisit frequency, within 3–12 hours of image acquisition, drawing on satellite archives extending back up to 40 years, with a minimum mappable water body of at least 5×5 water pixels (e.g., ≥50×50 m using 10 m Sentinel-2 imagery) after excluding a 1–2 pixel shoreline buffer [PVT-008]. As with CyanoLakes, this vendor's own whitepaper supplies no independent validation baseline or uncertainty figure for its HAB indicator or other retrieved parameters, so it cannot itself satisfy this project's claim-gate standard (traceable, regenerable, baselined, bounded) — it is included here only to characterize what a commercial provider states about coverage and resolution, not as validated accuracy evidence [PVT-008].

**State-level screening tools (California SWAMP/FHAB program, as one example).** Beyond the federal CyAN infrastructure, at least one state program (California) layers its own satellite-imagery screening tool for larger waterbodies on top of a voluntary citizen bloom-report form and an interactive incident map, as part of a broader regulatory monitoring/advisory system built out under state legislation (AB 834, 2019) [SLG-012]. This source is a program/regulatory overview rather than a technical or validation study, so it documents that such state-level satellite screening tools exist and how they fit into a state's monitoring stack, but gives no resolution, algorithm, sensor, or accuracy detail for its satellite component, and its own claim that blooms are "increasing statewide in incidence, duration, and toxicity" is asserted without accompanying trend statistics in the fetched text [SLG-012].

## Gaps & open questions

- **No source in this pack demonstrates a cyanoHAB retrieval algorithm that generalizes across a broad, representative set of lake optical types without local recalibration** — phycocyanin correction coefficients, Sentinel-2 MCI/NDCI calibration, and MPH coefficients are all reported as needing site- or region-specific tuning [ACAD-140; ACAD-046; ACAD-062], and a 2025 review names this "poor generalizability" explicitly as an unresolved limitation [ACAD-013].
- **The quantitative link between satellite-detected biomass/pigment and actual cyanotoxin concentration remains unestablished** — the pack contains no algorithm or validated relationship translating CI_cyano or chlorophyll-a into a toxin-risk estimate; the one study that tested this directly found no established quantitative relationship exists [ACAD-045].
- **How to reconcile point-based in-situ sampling with areal satellite composites at fine temporal/spatial scale is not solved** — the monthly-vs-annual scale-dependence finding (tau=0.85 vs. tau=0) in ACAD-088 is from one lake system; no source in the pack tests whether this scale-dependence pattern holds generally across other lakes.
- **Version-to-version CI_cyano reprocessing effects on long time series are flagged as a risk (NASA prohibits cross-version trending) but no source in this pack independently quantifies how much of any published multi-year CI_cyano trend (e.g., the Great Lakes 25-year study) might be attributable to reprocessing artifacts versus real ecological change**, beyond the general 15–20% V4→V5 shift figure [FED-066; ACAD-098].
- **Small-waterbody detection is universally flagged as a limitation, but the pack contains few quantified, cross-validated solutions** — ground-based hyperspectral sensing is reported as superior for small Oklahoma reservoirs but with no reported statistics [ACAD-055]; Sentinel-2/Landsat approaches show promise but the multi-satellite comparison found detection skill was not clearly resolution-driven, complicating a simple "go finer" prescription [ACAD-135].
- **Economic/decision value of earlier satellite detection has been quantified in only one lake-event case (Utah Lake 2017), which the authors themselves say does not generalize** [ACAD-133] — there is no broader, multi-lake economic-value literature in this pack to establish typical or bounding values.
- **Diel-timing mismatch (satellite overpass near solar noon vs. cyanobacterial vertical migration cycles) is identified as a source of satellite/field disagreement but is not itself directly measured or corrected for by any algorithm or product in this pack** [ACAD-006].
- **Full-text access gaps limit confidence in several review-level claims** — a number of reviews and methods papers relevant to algorithm performance and ML use were accessible only at abstract level in this pack (see the access-level caveat under Contested/uncertain above), so their more specific quantitative claims (where present) could not be cross-checked against full methods.
- **Training/curriculum materials in the pack are explicitly dated and self-flagged as possibly superseded** — a 2017 NASA ARSET introductory course on remote sensing of HABs (covering MODIS, VIIRS, Sentinel-2A, Sentinel-3, Landsat 8, plus decision-support demos of California's C-HARM and freshwater tools like "HAB Tracker") carries its own disclaimer that its technical guidance may no longer reflect current methods, illustrating how quickly this sub-field's practical tooling recommendations age relative to the peer-reviewed literature [FED-062].

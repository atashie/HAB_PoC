Part 1 · The problem & the decision
The problem

Forecasting harmful algal blooms before they take hold.

A harmful algal bloom can turn a lake toxic in days. By the time one looks like this, the water is often already unsafe and the window to act has narrowed. This project forecasts where blooms will appear one to four weeks ahead, so a response can be planned before then.

A cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) bloom on Lake Okeechobee, Florida — the largest lake in our pilot. The satellite view of this same 2016 event appears later, in the data section. ↓

Terms
Harmful algal bloom (HAB) — a rapid overgrowth of algae; in these freshwater lakes it is typically cyanobacteria (blue-green algae), some of which produce toxins that threaten drinking water, recreation, livestock, and aquatic life.
Cyanobacteria — the blue-green “algae” (really bacteria) behind most freshwater HABs; the green paint-like scum in the photo.
Why someone would want to know about a bloom

Different use cases require different tools.

“Know about HABs” is not a single problem. Each reason a person might care implies a different data set and a different kind of model.

Monitor for the record

Regulatory / legal evidence that a bloom occurred, where and when.
Needs: auditable in-situ grab samples + lab toxin assays; chain-of-custody, not a model.

Map severity

How bad is it, in cell counts or toxin level, across the water body.
Needs: calibrated satellite/optical retrieval + in-situ calibration; a severity regression.

Map extent

How far the bloom reaches — which reaches, which intakes.
Needs: higher-resolution imagery + hydrodynamic transport; a spatial model.

Assess impact

Ecological / economic damage; drivers to manage long-term.
Needs: integrated watershed + nutrient-loading models; causal, not just predictive.
Our use case

Predict to prevent

Where will a bloom appear next, early enough to pre-position treatment.
Needs: a satellite bloom signal fused with weather + in-situ drivers; a short-horizon risk forecast.
Why this one: it is a use case that SePRO’s products and field specialists can directly address (timing and targeting a treatment), and it is buildable from public data with an explainable, benchmarkable model.
Terms
In-situ / grab sample — a measurement taken in the water itself (sensor or lab), as opposed to from a satellite.
Pre-position treatment — stage crews / products at a water body ahead of a predicted bloom, rather than reacting after one appears.

What we set out to do

High level goals:

  • Meaningfully forecast HABs using only public data.
  • Match or beat the only public forecast we can benchmark head-to-head — EPA’s CyanoHAB forecast.
  • Forecast at 0–4 weeks, extending EPA’s single 1-week horizon.
  • Make every driver’s contribution explainable and physically sensible — show why, not just what.
  • Keep it operationally runnable — reproducible, on public feeds, at sensible cost.

The question, in one sentence (each highlighted word is a deliberate filter):

Forecast the probability of a WHO Alert-Level-1 bloom, one to four weeks ahead, for the 133 satellite-resolvable lakes in Florida, each week, benchmarked against the EPA forecast, using only public data.

Each highlighted word is a unique filter — and a built-in limitation:

  • WHO Alert-Level-1 — a satellite chlorophyll/dominance proxy, not a toxin measurement; biomass and toxin risk are decoupled.
  • one to four weeks ahead — h≥1 assumes an unrealistic “perfect weather forecast” that does not account for uncertainty in weather forecasts (an upper bound, not operational skill).
  • 133 satellite-resolvable lakes — excludes the small/narrow waterbodies CyAN can’t see (it needs ≥3×3 of its 300 m pixels, so ~900 m across) — often SePRO’s real customers.
  • Florida — one state, not CONUS (too compute-heavy for a PoC); FL has robust data and a relatively easy seasonal signal (no snow/ice).
  • each week — weekly aggregation caps temporal resolution; we do it mainly for an apples-to-apples EPA comparison (revisable).
  • benchmarked against the EPA forecast — EPA’s forecast is itself CyAN-derived and carries its own uncertainty: an unknown ceiling on achievable skill.
  • only public data — no lab toxins, no proprietary feeds.
Sources & data
WHO Alert Level 1 — WHO recreational-water guidance (lake-wide median chlorophyll-a ≥ 12 µg/L with cyanobacteria dominance); operationalized on CyAN by Schaeffer et al. (2024).
“Only benchmarkable public forecast” — from the 12-tool inventory: EPA is the sole public freshwater product that forecasts ahead in a comparable per-lake weekly-probability form (keys FED-060, ACAD-050); NOAA products are marine or single-lake; vendors paywalled/unvalidated.
Lake set — the satellite-resolvable lakes in EPA’s CyAN set (NHDPlus V2 COMID), filtered to Florida (133 lakes).

From pixels to lakes — masking

Why we mask to lake polygons.

The raw raster mixes lakes, rivers, wet land and shoreline. We keep only pixels inside a resolvable lake polygon (the 133 outlined here), then take the per-lake median index each week — that median is what defines a bloom. Masking is what turns a noisy image into a clean per-lake signal.

Result: one number per lake per week, tied to a known COMID — joinable to every other data source.
CyAN limitations — the load-bearing ones
  • Coarse 300 m pixels — reliable only for water ≥ ~900 m across (≥3×3 pixels); only ~5% of U.S. lakes are resolvable, and ~80% of state-reported cyanoHAB records fall in waterbodies too small/ill-shaped for the 3-pixel analysis.
  • A spectral proxy, not a direct measure — CI_cyano is a reflectance-shape index, not a cyano count; turbidity, sediment, CDOM, thin cloud and shoreline adjacency can bias it, and low-biomass cyano (below ~chl-a 20 µg/L) is hard to see.
  • Not toxins — sensors can’t measure cyanotoxins, and biomass↔toxin risk are decoupled (our CyAN↔lab matchup, next slide: CI missed toxins in 70 lakes and over-called 65).
  • Cloud, ice and shorelines cause gaps and false readings; cloud alone lost the target week in 595 of 929 lakes in that matchup.
  • Weekly max-composite, ~1-week lag; single sensor since 2016 (OLCI), no long baseline; values are version-dependent (no trending across reprocessings).
  • “Below detection” ≠ zero — it is below the sensor’s ~10⁴ cells/mL floor, not an absence.
  • Our bloom label is CyAN-derived, so CyAN is used antecedent-only (never the target week) to avoid circularity.
We put numbers on this by matching CyAN to independent EPA lab samples — the next slide.

The aggregation unit — HydroBASINS

HydroBASINS: how we place point data.

Before the in-situ sources: many gages and stations aren’t inside a lake. We use HydroBASINS / BasinATLAS level-12 sub-basins (outlined here, one per lake) as the unit to attach point measurements to — a station inside the lake joins directly; otherwise we take the median of stations in the lake’s containing sub-basin. BasinATLAS also supplies static basin traits (land use, climate normals).

Limitations — basins
  • Watershed-median attribution is coarser than an in-lake reading; a sub-basin can hold conditions the lake doesn’t.
  • Partial matching: many waterbodies span more than one sub-basin, so a single-L12 match is only partial — resolving multi-basin lakes is future work.
  • Static traits are fixed in time — they encode which lakes differ, not when a bloom happens.
  • Between-lake static gradients are compressed — all Florida lakes are broadly eutrophic, so only 1 static feature survived multiple-comparison correction.
Sources & data
Basins & static traits — HydroATLAS / BasinATLAS L12 (Linke et al. 2019, Sci. Data 6:283, doi). Shown: the L12 sub-basins containing our 133 lakes (real polygons).
Terms
HydroBASINS / BasinATLAS — a global set of nested drainage sub-basins, each with pre-computed environmental attributes.
Sub-basin (L12) — the finest level; the local watershed we attach point data to.
In-situ hydrology — NWIS

USGS streamflow gages (NWIS).

The USGS National Water Information System adds discharge, gage height and water temperature — proxies for loading and flushing. Most linked points carry no usable data; the dark points are streamflow (discharge) gages — the robust, continuous ones — usually on a river in the lake’s basin, not the lake itself. Each carries a staleness (how old the reading is).

Coverage: 395 gages link in, but only ~17 carry data (13 of them streamflow gages); just 25 of 133 lakes end up with usable NWIS signal, and per-variable coverage is lower still (water-temp 12, gage-height 24, discharge 21 lakes). Many Florida lakes are seepage/closed-basin and ungauged.
Limitations — NWIS
  • Sparse: 25/133 lakes have any NWIS signal (per-variable 12–24); gages often sit on a river, not the lake.
  • Lake vs streamflow gages: streamflow (discharge) gages give robust, continuous flow but sit on rivers in the basin; in-lake stage/temperature gages are far rarer — so “coverage” overstates lake-relevant data.
  • Datum artifacts: each gage’s zero differs → raw gage height needs within-site/anomaly forms.
  • Signal is weak-to-modest: higher stage → slightly less bloom (dilution), within-lake only.
Sources & data
Streamflow — USGS NWIS. waterdata.usgs.gov. Points = the 395 linked FL sites (models/prepare/pull_link_nwis_fl.py).
Terms
Staleness — days between the last in-situ reading and the forecast week; a stale value is used, but flagged.
Discharge / gage height — how much water flows / how high it stands.
In-situ water quality — WQP

Water-quality stations (WQP).

The Water Quality Portal (USGS / EPA / 400+ agencies) adds the chemistry the satellite can’t see: nutrients (phosphorus, nitrogen), chlorophyll-a, temperature, turbidity, DO, pH. Dark burnt-orange dots sample ~monthly-or-better; faded dots are sparser / one-off — so most values are stale at forecast time. Each analyte joins as-of the forecast week with its own staleness.

Freshness: 123 of 133 lakes have some WQP data, but it is sparse and mostly stale — only ~262 of 8,726 stations sample at least ~monthly and just ~16 sample weekly, so at a given forecast week most values are weeks-to-months old; the (value, staleness) pair carries that age.
Limitations — WQP
  • Irregular & sparse in time — many analytes ~monthly; carried forward with staleness, not interpolated.
  • Not unit/method-harmonized — mixes total/dissolved phosphorus and chlorophyll methods; nutrient signals are indicative until harmonized.
  • Chlorophyll-a is partly circular with the target — coincident chl-a barred; only lagged chl-a kept as a modest independent lead.
  • TN & pH undercounted by characteristic-name misses — need alias discovery.
Sources & data
Water quality — Water Quality Portal (USGS/EPA/NWQMC). waterqualitydata.us. Points = the 8,726 linked FL stations (models/prepare/pull_link_wqp_fl.py).
Terms
Analyte — a measured quantity (e.g. total phosphorus).
Nutrients (N / P) — nitrogen and phosphorus; the fertilisers that feed blooms.
Weather — ERA5

ERA5 reanalysis weather.

ERA5 gives temperature, precipitation, solar radiation, wind and evaporation everywhere — all 133 lakes, no gaps. It is the densest and most forecast-eligible driver layer. The grid shows its native resolution: a coarse ~0.25° (≈28 km) cell, much larger than most lakes.

The tension: one weather cell can blanket several lakes — good coverage, coarse in space; and our forecasts assume a “perfect weather forecast” that ignores uncertainty in actual forecasts (real forecast weather is future work).
Limitations — ERA5
  • Coarse grid (~28 km) → nearest-cell extraction blurs lake-scale differences.
  • Reanalysis, not observations — a model’s best estimate of past weather.
  • Used as a “perfect weather forecast” (coincident) input for h≥1 — an upper bound on predictability, not operational skill.
Sources & data
Weather — ECMWF ERA5 reanalysis (Hersbach et al. 2020, Q. J. R. Meteorol. Soc. 146:1999–2049, doi); Copernicus Climate Data Store. Grid drawn at the real 0.25° spacing.
Terms
ERA5 — a global hourly weather reanalysis on a ~28 km grid.
Perfect weather forecast — pretending we know the target week’s weather exactly; an idealisation that ignores real forecast uncertainty, clearly labelled.

Part 2c · Trusting the signal — CyAN vs lab samples
Signal check — CyAN ↔ EPA National Lakes Assessment

How far can we trust the signal the forecast runs on?

We matched CyAN pixels to independent EPA National Lakes Assessment (NLA) lab samples — chlorophyll-a and microcystin — to put numbers on CyAN’s reach and its limits. Two things fall out: a steep coverage funnel, and a biomass-yes / toxin-no gap.

Temporal matching is free (weekly mosaics are continuous — every visit fell inside a week). The attrition is entirely size + cloud: 290 lakes are sub-resolvable at 300 m, then 595 of the 929 resolvable lakes lost their specific week to cloud. From analysis/cyan_nla_matchup/outputs/matchup_report.md.
  • CI vs lab chlorophyll-a: ρ = 0.62 (n = 334) — moderate and robust. A floor of lakes have chlorophyll but no cyano signal, because chl-a is total algae while CI is cyanobacteria-specific.
  • CI vs microcystin: ρ = 0.56 (n = 184 detections) — weaker, and a genuinely different target.
  • Poor toxin screen, both directions (~60% agreement): 65 usable matches where CI sees cyano but no toxin (over-prediction), and 70 where CI sees nothing yet microcystin was detected (missed toxins).
Usable set skews large (median 515 ha) — a resolution-induced bias that survey weights can’t fix, so it is not representative of the lake population.
Verdict: the CyAN↔in-situ bridge is real but thin and cloud-limited — usable mainly for larger lakes, moderately good for biomass, and not a trustworthy toxin screen. So we treat CI as a screening / biomass signal (never a toxin claim), carry the coverage limits explicitly, and use NLA’s lab toxins to bound the CI→risk translation rather than assume it. NLA isn’t a forecast input — it tells us how far to trust the signal the forecast runs on.

Part 2b · From data to features
Feature engineering

Feature families: raw signals, and what we derive from them.

Every model input, by family. Raw = as measured; derived = the lags, trailing windows and anomalies that carry the forecast. Every temporal feature is stored as a (value, staleness) pair, so irregular sampling is handled without interpolation.

Processing: the tree models (HistGBM / XGBoost) take features as-is — they handle missing values (NaN) natively and don’t need scaling. For the linear / logistic models, features are median-imputed and z-scored (mean/SD fit on the training years only, then applied to validation and test).

Part 3 · Findings, defensibility, platform & the ask
The bar to clear

Baselines first — and what “good” even means.

Five reference predictors on the same 4,527 shared Florida 2025 lake-weeks (132 lakes × 35 weeks; base rate 28.8%; W−2 / h1 — the latency-fair 1-week-ahead scenario): naïve persistence, a CyAN autoregressive ladder, per-lake climatology, and the EPA forecast (both cutoffs). The onset columns are the decision metric — skill on positive flips (currently-clear lakes that bloom next), not the both-direction transition metric that also counts blooms clearing.

What we optimize vs how we judge: we tune and report MCC as the headline balanced metric (robust to the ~29% imbalance, fair to hard 0/1 baselines). But aggregate AUC is near-saturated by CyAN autocorrelation, so it flatters everyone. The metric that matters is onset (positive-flip) skill — and the order there is real: at 1 week, fusion leads (onset-MCC 0.47) > climatology 0.39 > CyAN-ladder 0.35 > EPA ≈0.24 > persistence 0 (persistence can never call a flip).
Terms — the metrics
AUC-ROC — ranks a random bloom above a random non-bloom this often; 0.5 = chance, 1 = perfect.
AUC-PR — like AUC-ROC but focused on the rare positive class.
Brier — mean squared error of the probability; lower is better.
MCC — a balanced correlation between prediction and truth; −1…+1, fair under imbalance.
Accuracy — share of lake-weeks called correctly (weak under imbalance).
onset (positive flip) — restrict to currently-clear weeks; score catching the ones that become a bloom — the early-warning test. Distinct from a both-direction “transition” metric that also counts blooms clearing.
Limitations — the head-to-head
  • EPA values are a dashboard snapshot (2026-07-02), not proven as-issued.
  • Structural edge to us: persistence/ladder/climatology are derived from our exact labels; EPA’s model is independent — a gap is not a clean skill test.
  • Timing (your question): the join is exact on the target week — EPA’s Saturday-ending week == our week, same COMID — so we score the same dates. But EPA is a current-week forecast (released Tue/Wed, valid that week), so it sees fresher data than our 1-week-ahead issue, and the snapshot may have been revised since issuance. Same dates, different information sets — not a clean apples-to-apples.
  • We do not claim to beat EPA: EPA vs persistence ΔAUC = +0.010, 95% CI [−0.000, +0.022] — includes zero.
  • Seasonal: EPA ~Apr–Nov, so 35 of 52 weeks (peak season); base rate 28.8% vs 26.6% full-year.

Model families

Which model families we trial — and which we don’t.

We trial — explainable, robust:

  • Regularized logistic GLM — transparent, signed coefficients as log-odds.
  • Gradient-boosted trees — XGBoost / HistGBM — handle sparse in-situ NaNs natively, robust to outliers and mixed scales, strong on tabular data, still explainable (SHAP / partial dependence).
  • (A logistic GAM and an SVC round out the explainable spread in the design.)

We don’t trial (here):

  • LSTM · RNN · other neural nets · transformers.
Why: the data regime and the goal both point away from deep nets.
  • Wide feature set, limited/rare targets — many heterogeneous features but relatively few bloom onsets; deep nets are data-hungry and would overfit, while boosted trees and regularized linear models are robust to that ratio and to outliers.
  • Explainability is a hard requirement — we must show why each driver matters; neural nets are far harder to interpret defensibly.
  • Sparse, irregular in-situ with many NaNs — GBDTs handle missingness natively; NNs need imputation that invents structure.
  • Compute / time — for a PoC the marginal accuracy of a tuned NN doesn’t justify the cost, and EPA’s own comparison shows NNs don’t beat simpler models on this task.
All-feature model families vs baselines and the CyAN-only ladder (h=1, held-out 2024–2026): AUC-ROC and MCC on all weeks. The families are near-identical on aggregate; the GBDTs (HistGBM/XGBoost) edge the GLM. From models/outputs/experiments.md.

Skill vs lead time (0–4 weeks)

Aggregate skill decays gently; onset skill is where the models separate.

Feature importance & selection

The fusion is a negative result: CyAN does the work.

Each bar is the full-model test AUC (0.983); the bright-red segment is the AUC lost when that block is shuffled. CyAN is nearly all of it. The 27-cluster permutation test (exp_perm_importance.md) corroborates.
  • CyAN is ~100% of the skill. Correlation-clustered permutation: the real-time-CyAN cluster carries Δonset-MCC +0.46 and Δpooled-AUC +0.47; scrambling it collapses the model to a coin flip (onset-MCC 0.466→0.002, pooled AUC 0.980→0.514) — on every architecture.
  • Every other family is small and inconsistent. Weather, in-situ, static, season each ≤ +0.038 on onset-MCC, ≈0 on AUC, and flip sign across GLM/HistGBM/XGBoost — no robust incremental held-out skill. (Not “no signal”: the univariate screens did find weather/nutrient associations — they just add nothing on top of CyAN.)
  • A 2-feature model matches the 44-feature one. Greedy backward elimination strips to cyan_median + area with no significant held-out AUC loss — massive redundancy (47 feature pairs at |r| > 0.8).
  • clim is a memorization shortcut. The per-lake seasonal base rate is leaned on as hard as all real-time CyAN (Δonset-MCC +0.351) yet removing it costs only 0.068 — kept as a baseline, not a feature; the deployable model is clim-free and generalizable.
This is a good outcome, honestly reported. Aligns with the literature (slide 4): temperature and nutrients explain why lakes bloom, but for a short-horizon forecast of an autocorrelated CyAN target, the target’s own recent level answers when. A well-validated simple model beat the complex one — exactly what the brief prizes.
Limitations
  • Importance is associational and inflated by autocorrelation — not causal.
  • Same-lake temporal validation only; static + nearest-cell weather can still fingerprint place.
  • “Small / inconsistent” ≠ “no signal” — sparse in-situ (NWIS 25, WQP 123 lakes) and coarse weather likely understate drivers; a denser/harmonized pull could change this.

Defensibility

Where the leakage is, and how we guarded it.

  • Circularity: the target is CyAN-derived, so CyAN features are antecedent-only (never the target week); persistence uses the same latency-corrected week, so the model never sees fresher CyAN than the baseline it must beat.
  • Temporal split by held-out year (train ≤2023 / tune 2024 / test 2025), not a random shuffle on autocorrelated lake-weeks; normalization and feature selection fit on train only.
  • “Perfect weather forecast” caveat (coincident ERA5 for h≥1 — an upper bound, not operational skill); known-lake-only claim (a blocked-lake stress test is the check for transfer); AL1 is a satellite proxy, not a toxin measure.
  • Clustered confidence intervals (block bootstrap by lake/week), never row-level.

ii · Choose a direction

Go deep · go wide · go narrow · go home.

Go deep

Improve and generalize the current framework: better feature cleaning, CONUS-wide data for generalization (and enough data to justify richer architectures).

Go wide

A clear pathway to global. Use Copernicus “Lake Water Quality” v2 for a ready global spatial footprint (external / web-research — confirm), and supplement it with CyAN for the longer temporal record (Copernicus is only 2024→present). Copernicus for spatial coverage + CyAN for history → robust global modeling — rather than rebuilding a global CyAN from scratch.

Go narrow

300 m is coarse. Sentinel-2 / Landsat (10–30 m) lack S3’s cyano-specific bands but may carry enough signal to downscale to the small waterbodies CyAN can’t see — SePRO’s actual customers.
Leading option

Go home

EPA’s forecast is already reasonably accurate. Put it in our internal clients’ hands for a few months: is this information useful at all, and how do they want to consume it (probabilities, alerts)?

Takeaway: even the full model is tens of dollars a month — the weather-forecast pull is the main recurring time cost, storage the main recurring dollar cost, and both are modest. So the decision is driven by value to the user, not by cost. (Back-of-envelope; a costed spike would firm these up.)
Estimate assumptions
  • Compute: AWS on-demand (us-east-1, 2026 order-of-magnitude): c6i.4xlarge ≈ $0.68/hr, c6i.large ≈ $0.085/hr, t3.micro ≈ $0.01/hr. CyAN CONUS mosaic ~0.4 GB (measured); raster reprojection + per-lake aggregation + weather extraction fit in ~2–3 hr on one instance.
  • Weather: ERA5 (Copernicus CDS) and ECMWF open-data forecasts (IFS/AIFS) are free to pull, but the download + processing is the slow step — refreshed every run for both reanalysis and forecast.
  • Storage: S3 Standard ≈ $0.023/GB-mo; transfer-in free, egress ≈ $0.09/GB (minimal here). Keeping raw archives is optional — process-and-discard cuts storage to near-zero.
  • Excludes one-time build and human QA time — often the actual dominant costs.
iv · Monitoring

Issue forecasts, then keep ourselves honest.

  • Weekly forecasts + a rolling hindcast: issue the next n weeks every week, then score them as the truth lands — how does the operational forecast actually perform, and when and where are the big misses?
    • Was last week’s forecast within the typical range of performance?
    • Are our predictions substantially diverged from climatology — and if so, why (which features are anomalous)?
    • Are we more or less accurate in certain regions — and if so, why (regional weather patterns)?
  • Drift detection: is live performance still inside the validation/test window we signed off on? If it drifts outside, ping to investigate and retrain — with CyAN and the EPA forecast as continuously-ingested references.
Why this matters: a forecast you don’t score is a claim you can’t defend. The hindcast is the audit trail; drift monitoring is what keeps a deployed model from silently going stale.
Claim boundary

Where this applies — and where it doesn’t.

  • It forecasts a satellite bloom proxy (WHO AL1 on CyAN), for known Florida lakes, into future weeks.
  • It does not measure toxins, does not claim treatment efficacy, and is not yet validated on unseen lakes or sub-resolvable waterbodies.
  • Every driver statement is associational and model-relative, not causal.
v · The recommendation

Start small and honest, then let users choose the direction.

Stated suggestively, not prescriptively — my honest recommendation:

  • Ship the simplest useful thing first: a dashboard that makes the EPA forecast more accessible and tailored — alerts on positive flips (clear → bloom) and monitoring of ongoing blooms, from regular CyAN + EPA-forecast ingests.
  • Gather feedback for a few weeks/months: is this information useful, and how do users want to consume it?
  • Then revisit go deep / go wide / go narrow / go home — with real user signal instead of guesses.
Why this over “build more model”: our own results say fusion’s edge on new blooms is modest and the EPA forecast is already a credible baseline. The binding uncertainty isn’t the model — it’s whether and how the information is useful. Answer that cheaply first.

Lead Data Scientist Finalist Case Study
SePRO Corporation | Water-Resource & Plant-Protection Science
Why we're asking you to do this
You're a finalist for the Lead Data Scientist role at SePRO. This will be SePRO's first data
science hire, so it's a foundational role: the person who builds the analytics capability, sets the
standards, and earns trust across R&D, Commercial, and Operations. This case study is the
capstone of our process and your chance to show how you'd do exactly that.
We'd rather see how you think and build than ask you to grind through several exercises, so this
is the one prepared deliverable in our process. You'll present it in person to the team you'd be
working with. What we hope to see is your judgment, your rigor, your scientific credibility, and
your instinct as a builder.
Time and scope: Please plan for a focused effort, in the range of 6 to 8 hours across the week
we've given you. We've kept this to a single deliverable on purpose. A crisp design and a thin
working slice will tell us far more than an exhaustive build, so once you've shown your thinking,
please stop. Feel free to use whatever public data, libraries, and AI or coding tools you'd
normally reach for.
Presentation: You'll present in person to a cross-functional panel that spans scientific, platform,
commercial, and operations stakeholders. Plan for roughly 45 minutes to present, followed by
about 30 minutes of discussion and Q&A.
About the role, briefly
The Lead Data Scientist builds SePRO's data science capability across three areas:
strengthening how research and field-trial data connect to real-world efficacy; building
diagnostic and prescriptive tools that turn data into recommendations; and bringing predictive
analytics to commercial and operational decisions. Across all three, the throughline is the same:
turn complex, fragmented data into decision-ready insight that holds up to scrutiny, and put it
into tools people actually use.
This case study centers on the diagnostic and prescriptive part of that mandate, where
SePRO's science, data, and customer impact meet most directly.
The challenge
Design an approach to a water-health diagnostic and treatment recommendation
that fuses remote-sensing or satellite signals with in-situ aquatic data, is
explainable and defensible enough to support a product claim, and could
realistically be built into a tool SePRO and its customers would use. Show your

thinking with real public data, build a small working version, and sketch how you

The most natural problem to anchor on is the harmful algal bloom (HAB): cyanobacteria blooms
that threaten drinking-water sources, recreational waters, and aquatic ecosystems, and that
SePRO's customers need to get ahead of. Public satellite programs such as EPA, NASA, and
NOAA's CyAN detect bloom signals from space, and public in-situ networks report the water
chemistry underneath. Fusing the two is exactly the kind of spectral-signal-meets-aquatic-
science diagnostic this role is built around, and the operational truth is that timing and targeting
matter most: a recommendation made two weeks early, in the right water body, is worth far
more than a perfect one made after a bloom is visible.
You have latitude in how you frame the specific question. A few directions that work well:
• Risk scoring or forecasting: which water bodies are most likely to bloom in the coming
weeks, and what drives that risk?
• Early-warning detection: flagging when a water body's combined satellite and in-situ
readings depart from its normal pattern.
• Driver and treatment analysis: which conditions best explain bloom activity, and what
that implies for where, when, and how to intervene.
If you'd rather anchor on an adjacent SePRO-relevant water or plant problem, you're welcome
to. We'd just ask that you share your reasoning, still follow the structure below, and keep the
spectral-plus-aquatic, claims-defensible spirit of the prompt.
What to deliver
Part A: The analysis
Take the problem through the full data science arc, with your reasoning visible along the way.
Our team thinks about this in roughly these stages: define, acquire, prepare, explore, model,
evaluate, communicate, and deploy. You don't need to belabor each, but we'd love to see the
judgment behind them.
1. Define it. The specific question you chose and the decision it informs. Who uses the
answer, and what do they do differently because of it?
2. Acquire and integrate. Bring together at least two distinct public sources, ideally pairing a
remote-sensing or satellite signal with in-situ aquatic or weather data. Tell us where it
came from and how you accessed it.
3. Inspect and prepare. Show us the data's rough edges, such as gaps, biases, sampling
artifacts, and detection limits, and how you handled them. Thoughtful data handling
matters to us as much as a clean result.
4. Explore. The patterns and relationships you found, and what they ruled in or out. A little
care around correlation versus causation goes a long way.

5. Model. A method suited to the question. Fit and rigor count for more than sophistication,
and a well-validated simple model is very welcome. Be clear about your assumptions, how
you validated, and where data leakage or overfitting could creep in.
6. Evaluate. How you know the result is meaningful, the metrics that fit the decision, and
how confident you are.
7. Land the “so what.” Translate it into a recommendation a SePRO user or customer could
act on, with a clear-eyed view of where it applies and where it reaches its limits.
A word on defensibility: because SePRO's recommendations can sit behind product claims,
we care a lot about whether your approach is explainable and auditable. Wherever you can,
show why the model says what it says, not just what it predicts.
Part B: The tool
Build a small, working, interactive tool that puts your analysis in the hands of a non-technical
user. Picture a SePRO field or technical specialist, or a customer, who needs the answer rather
than the code.
• It should run and be ready to demo live. Running locally or sharing a link are both fine.
• It should draw directly on your Part A analysis, so the two tell one story. A user might
select a water body or set of conditions and get back a risk readout, a recommended
action window, or the key drivers.
• Please use the stack and tools you'd naturally reach for, whether that's Streamlit, Gradio,
a notebook-as-app, a plain web page, a React app, or anything else that lets you move
quickly. AI-assisted development is very welcome. We're looking for builder instinct, not a
finished product. (Please note: SePRO will reimburse you up to $150 of token usage
for the tool build should you wish to vibe code with your preferred LLM.)
Part C: From prototype to platform
Finally, step back and tell us how you'd make this real. A short, practical view (a few slides or a
sketch) on:
• Data architecture: how you'd connect and scale the inputs, given that SePRO's data lives
across research, field-trial, lab, sensor, satellite, distributor, and commercial systems.
• Production and monitoring: how you'd deploy it, keep it explainable and auditable, and
watch for model drift over time.
• The first 6 to 12 months: where you'd start if you were standing up this capability at
SePRO, and what you'd build first.

Pitch your story to a mixed audience: the panel spans scientific, platform, commercial, and
operations stakeholders, so part of what we're watching is how you translate between the
domain, the data work, and the business. Plan for roughly 45 minutes to present, then about 30

The technical deep dive
After your presentation, we'll set up a shorter, recorded follow-up: a relaxed, screen-sharing
walkthrough session where you take us through your code, your data sources, and the
analytical choices behind your results. Think of it as a conversation about how you built things
rather than a test. With your consent, we’ll record the session so the full panel can review your
technical depth, since not everyone can join live.
A few ground rules
• Public data only. Please use only public data, and steer clear of anything proprietary,
confidential, or personal, or anything you don't have the right to use. Using LLM’s to curate
data is also fine, and you are welcome to synthesize some data if it helps backstop your
work in some meaningful way that couldn’t be achieved via public real data sources. A
citation for each source is all we need.
• AI and coding tools are welcome. Use them the way you normally would, and feel free
to tell us what you used and where your own judgment shaped the result. We're hiring an
analytic builder, and using good tools well is part of that.
• Reasonable assumptions are fine. Where SePRO-specific detail would help and you
don't have it, make a sensible assumption and tell us what you assumed.
• Tell us about your trade-offs. If you took shortcuts or set things aside for time, we'd like
to hear about them. Noting a known limitation reflects well on you.

Suggested public data sources
These are entirely optional, offered simply so you don't spend your budget hunting for data:
• EPA Cyanobacteria Assessment Network (CyAN): satellite-derived cyanobacteria
detection for U.S. lakes (the spectral signal), including weekly cyanoHAB forecasting
research and links to the underlying Sentinel-3 and Landsat imagery.
https://www.epa.gov/water-research/cyanobacteria-assessment-network-cyan
• Water Quality Portal: in-situ water chemistry from USGS, EPA, and more than 400
agencies, covering nutrients, chlorophyll-a, temperature, and more.
https://www.waterqualitydata.us/
• USGS National Water Information System (NWIS): streamflow, gage, and water data,
with dataRetrieval packages available for R and Python. https://waterdata.usgs.gov/
• EPA National Aquatic Resource Surveys / National Lakes Assessment: standardized
lake condition and nutrient data. https://www.epa.gov/national-aquatic-resource-surveys
• NOAA / National Weather Service: temperature, precipitation, and climate data to pair
with water data. https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/
• State environmental and DEQ lake-monitoring programs: many publish open bloom
advisories and monitoring data.
What we're hoping to see
We'll be paying attention to how sharply you framed the problem and tied it to a real decision,
the judgment in how you'd source and architect the data, the rigor and honesty of your modeling
and validation, whether your approach is explainable and defensible enough to stand behind a
claim, the builder instinct behind your tool and your plan to operationalize it, and how clearly you
carry a mixed-audience panel and defend your choices. Because this is SePRO's first data
science hire, we weight the ability to build production-grade tools and to engage credibly with
the science especially highly.
There's no single right answer here. We're far more interested in how you reason and build your
way through an open problem than in a polished result.
A few questions you might have
Do I need a background in aquatics or HABs? Not at all. Part of the role is reasoning about
an unfamiliar domain and earning scientific credibility quickly, so that's part of what we hope to
see. A little reading tends to go a long way.

Can I use a model, library, or AI tool I haven't tried before? Absolutely. Learning quickly and

What if my analysis doesn't turn up a strong result? That's common and completely fine. A

How polished should the tool be? Function comes well before beauty. A thin proof-of-concept

Will I need to send you my code or data? No. You keep full ownership of everything you
create. You'll walk us through your code and methods live in a recorded screen-share after your

How should I split my time? Lean toward the analysis and the thinking. The tool and the
prototype-to-platform view should each be a focused, lightweight slice that supports the story.
